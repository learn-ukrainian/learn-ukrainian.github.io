What Would You Do?

Real conditionals deal with possible futures. Now let's talk about unreal situations - things that aren't true or might never happen:

The particle "–±–∏/–±" transforms any sentence into hypothetical territory. It comes after the verb and attaches to it. This single particle does the work of "would," "could," and "might" in English conditionals!

üìú History Bite: Slavic Conditional

The particle "–±–∏" comes from an old form of "–±—É—Ç–∏" (to be). In Old Church Slavonic, conditionals used full auxiliary verbs. Over centuries, these shortened to just "–±–∏/–±" - a perfect example of grammaticalization!

–Ø–∫—â–æ vs –Ø–∫–±–∏

–Ø–∫—â–æ (if) - possible/real:
