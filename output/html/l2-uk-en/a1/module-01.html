üí° Did You Know?
The Cyrillic alphabet was NOT invented by Russians! It was created in the late 9th century in the First Bulgarian Empire. It is named after Saint Cyril, a missionary from Byzantium, but it was likely developed by his students, particularly Saint Clement of Ohrid. They based it largely on the Greek alphabet, which explains why so many "math symbols" (like $\pi$ and $\rho$) appear as letters here. Ukraine adopted it over a thousand years ago, long before modern Russia existed.

There are seven of these honest letters: –ê, –ï, –Ü, –û, –ö, –ú, –¢.

Think of the word –ê–¢–û–ú . In Ukrainian, it is –ê–¢–û–ú. Same letters, same meaning.
Think of –ú–ê–ú–ê . It's –ú–ê–ú–ê.
Think of –ö–û–¢ . It sounds like "cot", but it means "cat" (specifically a male cat).

The Second Layer: False Friends

Now we enter the danger zone. These letters are the "tricksters". They look incredibly familiar‚Äîyou see a "B", a "P", an "H", a "C", an "X", and a "y". But if you pronounce them like English letters, you will be speaking gibberish. These are "False Friends".

Why do they look like this? Because they come from Greek.

  • The letter –† looks like a "P", but it is the Greek Rho. It makes the R sound (rolled, like in Spanish or Italian).
  • The letter –• looks like an "X", but it is the Greek Chi. It makes the heavy H sound (like in "Loch Ness").

üîç Myth Buster
Myth: "Ukrainian is just a dialect of Russian."
Fact: Ukrainian is a separate East Slavic language with a distinct history. While they share an alphabet (mostly), the sounds are different. For example, the Ukrainian –ì (H) is soft and breathy, while the Russian G is hard (like in "Go"). The Ukrainian –û is always pronounced "O", whereas in Russian it often turns into "A" when unstressed. They are as different as Spanish and Portuguese.

The Third Layer: New Friends

Finally, we meet the strangers. These letters don't look like English letters at all. They are unique shapes that you simply need to memorize. But the good news is: they usually make very simple sounds.

  • –ë looks like a 6 or a b with a hat. It is B.
  • –ì looks like a gallows or a hook. It is the breathy H (like in "home").
  • –î looks like a fortress or a table. It is D.
  • –ó looks like the number 3. It creates the Z sound.
  • –õ looks like an upside-down V or a lambda. It is L.
  • –ü looks like a mathematical Pi ($\pi$) or a doorway. It is P.
  • –§ looks like a circle with a line through it. It is F.
