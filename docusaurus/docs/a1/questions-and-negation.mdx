# Questions & Negation

## Вступ: Мистецтво запитань

### Ключі до розмови
Imagine we are neighbors chatting over the fence. You want to ask if I am home, or if I have a hammer. Statements like **«Я тут»** [jɑ tut] (I am here) are useful, but questions unlock real conversation. Today, we invite you to the world of interaction. The good news is that Ukrainian questions are much simpler than in English. You do not need "do", "does", or "did". We build questions with simple blocks.

:::tip[💡 Tip]
**No "Do" Gymnastics**
In English, you say "You read" but "**Do** you read?".
In Ukrainian, you just say "**Ти читаєш**" [tɪ t͡ʃɪtˈɑjeʃ] (You read) vs "**Ти читаєш?**" [tɪ t͡ʃɪtˈɑjeʃ] (Do you read?).
The words stay the same. Only the music of your voice changes.
:::


### Інструмент №1: Частка «Чи»
To make a clear Yes/No question, we often use the small word **чи** [t͡ʃɪ]. **Воно діє як прапор.** [ʋɔnˈɔ dˈijɛ jɑk prˈɑpɔr] (It acts like a flag.) It signals: "Attention! A question is coming." Think of it as an upside-down question mark at the beginning. **Це ввічливо і популярно.** [t͡sɛ ʋʋˈit͡ʃlɪʋɔ i pɔpulʲˈɑrnɔ] (It is polite and popular.)

_Приклад:_
*   **Ти знаєш.** [tɪ znˈɑjeʃ] — You know. (Statement)
*   **Чи ти знаєш?** [t͡ʃɪ tɪ znˈɑjeʃ] — Do you know? (Question)
*   **Чи це кіт?** [t͡ʃɪ t͡sɛ kit] — Is this a cat?
*   **Чи це правда?** [t͡ʃɪ t͡sɛ prˈɑʋdɑ] — Is this true?
*   **Чи він тут?** [t͡ʃɪ ʋin tut] — Is he here?

### Інструмент №2: Інтонація
In casual talk, neighbors often drop **чи** [t͡ʃɪ]. We just raise our voice at the end of the sentence. This is the "lazy" but friendly way to ask. Your voice goes up, like a slide.

_Приклад:_
*   **Він там.** [ʋin tɑm] — He is there. (Flat voice)
*   **Він там?** [ʋin tɑm] — Is he there? (Voice goes up ↗)
*   **Мама вдома?** [mˈɑmɑ ʋdˈɔmɑ] — Is Mom at home?
*   **Ти читаєш?** [tɪ t͡ʃɪtˈɑjeʃ] — Do you read?

### Інструмент №3: Заперечення «Не»
Finally, to say "not", we use the particle **не** [nɛ]. It is strong and simple. You place it strictly before the word you want to negate. Usually, it stands right before the action (the verb).

_Приклад:_
*   **Я не знаю.** [jɑ nɛ znˈɑju] — I do not know.
*   **Він не тут.** [ʋin nɛ tut] — He is not here.
*   **Ми не спимо.** [mɪ nɛ spɪmˈɔ] — We are not sleeping.
*   **Це не кава.** [t͡sɛ nɛ kˈɑʋɑ] — This is not coffee.


## Теорія: Питання, відповіді та заперечення

### Мистецтво відповіді: «Так» і «Ні»
Now that we have the tools to ask questions, we need to know how to answer them. Imagine my neighbor Petro asks me over the fence: «**Чи ти вдома?**» [t͡ʃɪ tɪ ʋdˈɔmɑ] (Are you at home?). I cannot just stand there silently! I need the two most powerful words in any language: Yes and No.

**Українська згода — це просто.** [ukrɑjˈinʲsʲkɑ zɦˈɔdɑ t͡sɛ prˈɔstɔ] (Ukrainian agreement is simple.) We say **так** [tɑk]. It is short, crisp, and positive.
*   **— Ти тут?** [tɪ tut] — Are you here?
*   **— Так.** [tɑk] — Yes.
*   **— Це правда?** [t͡sɛ prˈɑʋdɑ] — Is this true?
*   **— Так, це правда.** [tɑk t͡sɛ prˈɑʋdɑ] — Yes, this is true.

To disagree, we say **ні** [ni]. Note the soft "n" sound. It is not a heavy "knee", but a gentle **ні** [nʲi].
*   **— Чи це кіт?** [t͡ʃɪ t͡sɛ kit] — Is this a cat?
*   **— Ні, це пес.** [ni t͡sɛ pɛs] — No, this is a dog.

Here lies a small trap for English speakers. Do not confuse the answer **ні** [ni] (No) with the negation particle **не** [nɛ] (Not).
*   **Ні** stands alone as an answer.
*   **Не** sticks to the verb to stop the action.

_Приклад:_
*   **— Ти спиш?** [tɪ spɪʃ] — Are you sleeping?
*   **— Ні, я не сплю.** [ni jɑ nɛ splʲu] — No, I am not sleeping.

### Залізне правило «Не»
We briefly touched on **не** [nɛ] earlier, but let us look closer at its "Iron Rule." In English, negation is messy. You need "do not," "does not," "is not," "are not." In Ukrainian, **не** [nɛ] is a loyal guard dog. It sits strictly *before* the word it negates.

Most often, it sits before the verb (the action). You do not need any helper words. You just place **не** [nɛ] and the action stops.
*   **Я знаю.** [jɑ znˈɑju] — I know.
*   **Я не знаю.** [jɑ nɛ znˈɑju] — I do not know.
*   **Він читає.** [ʋin t͡ʃɪtˈɑje] — He reads.
*   **Він не читає.** [ʋin nɛ t͡ʃɪtˈɑje] — He does not read.

You can also negate descriptions. If the coffee is not tasty, you simply say:
*   **Кава не смачна.** [kˈɑʋɑ nɛ smɑt͡ʃnˈɑ] — Coffee is not tasty.
*   **Він не вдома.** [ʋin nɛ ʋdˈɔmɑ] — He is not at home.

:::warning[⚠️ Warning]
**The "Not" Location**
Never put **не** [nɛ] after the verb.
*   Wrong: **Я знаю не.** (I know not.)
*   Correct: **Я не знаю.** [jɑ nɛ znˈɑju] (I not know.)

Also, never use **ні** [ni] (No) to negate a verb.
*   Wrong: **Я ні знаю.**
*   Correct: **Я не знаю.**
:::


### Хто це? (Люди)
Sometimes a Yes/No question is not enough. You see a shadow in the window and you want to ask: "Who is that?" For people, we use the word **хто** [xtɔ].

Pronouncing **хто** [xtɔ] can be tricky. It starts with a strong **х** [x] (like in "loch"), followed immediately by **т** [t]. Imagine breathing out heavily while tapping your teeth. **Хто** [xtɔ].

**«Хто» — це про людей.** [xtɔ t͡sɛ prɔ lʲudˈɛj] ("Who" is about people.)
*   **Хто це?** [xtɔ t͡sɛ] — Who is this?
*   **Це мама.** [t͡sɛ mˈɑmɑ] — This is Mom.

You can also ask about the subject of an action.
*   **Хто там?** [xtɔ tɑm] — Who is there?
*   **Хто читає?** [xtɔ t͡ʃɪtˈɑje] — Who is reading?
*   **Хто знає?** [xtɔ znˈɑje] — Who knows?

Notice that **хто** [xtɔ] acts like "he" or "she" for the verb. We use the same verb ending as for "Він/Вона" (He/She).

### Що це? (Речі)
If you see an object, not a person, you ask **що** [ʃt͡ʃɔ] (What).

Listen closely to the sound: **щ** [ʃt͡ʃ]. It is a combination of "sh" and "ch" mashed together, like in "fre**sh ch**eese." It is long and soft. **Що** [ʃt͡ʃɔ].

**«Що» — це про речі.** [ʃt͡ʃɔ t͡sɛ prɔ rˈɛt͡ʃi] ("What" is about things.)
*   **Що це?** [ʃt͡ʃɔ t͡sɛ] — What is this?
*   **Це телефон.** [t͡sɛ tɛlɛfˈɔn] — This is a phone.
*   **Що там?** [ʃt͡ʃɔ tɑm] — What is there?
*   **Там стіл.** [tɑm stil] — A table is there.

You will use this word constantly to ask about actions.
*   **Що ти робиш?** [ʃt͡ʃɔ tɪ rˈɔbɪʃ] — What are you doing?
*   **Що вона каже?** [ʃt͡ʃɔ ʋɔnˈɑ kˈɑʒɛ] — What is she saying?

:::note[🏺 Culture]
**The Street Sound «Шо»**
In textbooks and formal speech, the word is **що** [ʃt͡ʃɔ]. But on the street, at the market, or in a taxi, you will often hear just **шо** [ʃɔ]. It sounds simpler, like "sh-o."
*   **Шо це?** [ʃɔ t͡sɛ]

This is very common, but it is considered casual or colloquial. As a learner, it is safer to say **що** [ʃt͡ʃɔ], but do not be surprised when you hear **шо** [ʃɔ]. It is the same word!
:::


### Де ти? (Місце)
When you are looking for someone or something, you need the word **де** [dɛ] (Where). It is short and simple.

Imagine I cannot find my cat. I walk around the house asking:
*   **Де кіт?** [dɛ kit] — Where is the cat?

**«Де» — це про місце.** [dɛ t͡sɛ prɔ mˈist͡sɛ] ("Where" is about place.)
*   **Де мама?** [dɛ mˈɑmɑ] — Where is Mom?
*   **Мама тут.** [mˈɑmɑ tut] — Mom is here.
*   **Де таксі?** [dɛ tɑksˈi] — Where is the taxi?
*   **Таксі там.** [tɑksˈi tɑm] — The taxi is there.

Notice the pattern. In English, you must say "Where **is** the cat?". In Ukrainian, we drop the "is." We just say "Where cat?". **Де кіт?** [dɛ kit]. It is much faster!

### Коли це буде? (Час)
To ask about time, we use **коли** [kɔlˈɪ] (When). Note the stress on the last syllable: ko-LY. **Коли** [kɔlˈɪ].

Use this to check schedules or plan meetings.
*   **Коли сніданок?** [kɔlˈɪ snidˈɑnɔk] — When is breakfast?
*   **Коли автобус?** [kɔlˈɪ ɑʋtˈɔbus] — When is the bus?

You can also combine it with verbs to ask about habits or plans.
*   **Коли ти читаєш?** [kɔlˈɪ tɪ t͡ʃɪtˈɑjeʃ] — When do you read?
*   **Коли ми тут?** [kɔlˈɪ mɪ tut] — When are we here?

Remember the "is" rule again. "When **is** breakfast?" becomes simply "When breakfast?". **Коли сніданок?** [kɔlˈɪ snidˈɑnɔk].

### Пастка «Do» та «Does»
We must end this section with a very important warning. This is the most common mistake for English speakers.

In English, you are addicted to the helper verb "Do." You say: "Do you know?", "Does he sleep?", "I do not read." You feel naked without it.

In Ukrainian, **"Do" does not exist** as a helper. Never try to translate it!
If you try to translate "Do you know?" word-for-word, you might say something like "Робиш ти знати?". This sounds absurd to a Ukrainian. It sounds like "Make you to know?".

Relax and forget about "Do."
*   English: **Do** you know?
*   Ukrainian: You know? (**Ти знаєш?** [tɪ znˈɑjeʃ])

*   English: He **does** not sleep.
*   Ukrainian: He not sleeps. (**Він не спить.** [ʋin nɛ spɪtʲ])

The verb carries the whole weight. It does not need help. This makes your life easier—you have fewer words to learn!


## Практикум: Інтонація та слова

### Сила голосу
Now that we have cleared away the clutter of helper words like "do" and "does," your voice becomes the most important tool. In English, you usually change the word order to ask a question. In Ukrainian, the words often stay in the exact same place. You simply raise the pitch of your voice sharply at the end.

*   **Це правда.** [t͡sɛ prˈɑʋdɑ] — This is true. (Statement)
*   **Це правда?** [t͡sɛ prˈɑʋdɑ] — Is this true? (Question)

The sentence looks identical on paper, but it sounds completely different. It is like musical notation.

*   **Він знає.** [ʋin znˈɑje] — He knows.
*   **Він знає?** [ʋin znˈɑje] — Does he know?

If you use a question word like **хто** [xtɔ] (who) or **де** [dɛ] (where), the listener already expects a question, but your voice should still carry that curious energy.

*   **Хто там?** [xtɔ tɑm] — Who is there?
*   **Де ти?** [dɛ tɪ] — Where are you?

:::tip[🔍 Pattern Discovery]
**The Melody of Speech**
Imagine drawing a line with your voice. For a statement, the line goes down at the end (`\`), showing certainty. For a question, it curves up (`/`), showing curiosity or surprise.
*   **Мама вдома.** (`\`) — Mom is at home.
*   **Мама вдома?** (`/`) — Is Mom at home?
:::



## У реальному житті: Діалоги

Now that you understand the "music" of Ukrainian questions, let's see how this plays out in the real world. You will switch between the polite structure and the casual melody depending on where you are and who you are talking to.

### At the Café: The Polite «Чи»
Imagine you are entering a cozy café in Lviv. When you speak to the barista or a stranger, you want to sound polite and clear. This is where the particle **чи** [t͡ʃɪ] is your best friend. It acts like a gentle warning signal that says, "I am asking a question."

In these formal or semi-formal situations, using **чи** makes your Ukrainian sound softer and more respectful. It creates a nice rhythm to the conversation.

:::info[ℹ️ Fact]
**The Politeness Marker**
While you *can* ask questions with just intonation, using **чи** in public places (shops, banks, cafés) shows that you are making an effort to be clear and polite. It is the linguistic equivalent of a gentle knock on the door before entering.
:::


*   **— Чи це кава?** [t͡ʃɪ t͡sɛ kˈɑʋɑ] — Is this coffee?
*   **— Так, це кава.** [tɑk t͡sɛ kˈɑʋɑ] — Yes, this is coffee.
*   **— Чи ви тут працюєте?** [t͡ʃɪ ʋɪ tut prɑt͡sʲˈujɛtɛ] — Do you work here?
*   **— Так, я тут працюю.** [tɑk jɑ tut prɑt͡sʲˈuju] — Yes, I work here.

### With Friends: The Casual Melody
Now, imagine you see your friend Andriy on the street. You do not need the formal warning signal. You want to be quick and direct. In casual conversation, Ukrainians often drop the **чи** and rely entirely on that rising intonation we practiced.

This style is faster and feels more intimate. It assumes the listener is already tuned in to your voice.

*   **— Ти знаєш?** [tɪ znˈɑjɛʃ] — Do you know? (Casual)
*   **— Так, я знаю.** [tɑk jɑ znˈɑju] — Yes, I know.
*   **— Він тут?** [vin tut] — Is he here?
*   **— Ні, він не тут.** [ni ʋin nɛ tut] — No, he is not here.

### The Art of «No»: Correcting and Clarifying
Sometimes the answer is not a simple "yes." You often need to correct a misunderstanding. In Ukrainian, we do this by combining the answer **ні** [ni] (no) with the negation particle **не** [nɛ] (not).

Notice how **не** always stands like a guard right before the word it is saying "no" to. This is very useful when you need to clarify what something is *not*.

*   **— Це чай?** [t͡sɛ t͡ʃɑj] — Is this tea?
*   **— Ні, це не чай. Це суп.** [ni t͡sɛ nɛ t͡ʃɑj. t͡sɛ sup] — No, this is not tea. This is soup.

*   **— Він знає?** [ʋin znˈɑjɛ] — Does he know?
*   **— Ні, він не знає.** [ni ʋin nɛ znˈɑjɛ] — No, he does not know.


## 🏺 Культурний контекст: «Чи» та Альф

As we saw in the dialogues, asking a question is more than just grammar. It is about your relationship with the person you are talking to. Let’s look closer at how a tiny word and a funny alien changed how Ukrainians speak.

### Шкала ввічливості (The Politeness Scale)
Ви вже знаєте два методи. (You know two methods.) Але де їх використовувати? (But where to use them?) Частка **чи** — це офіційна краватка. (Particle chi is a formal tie.)

Незнайомець. Шеф. Бариста. Тут офіційний стиль. (Stranger. Boss. Barista. Here is formal style.) Використовуйте **чи**. Це знак поваги. (Use chi. This is a sign of respect.)

Друзі. Сім'я. Стиль — простий. (Friends. Family. Style is simple.) Тут **чи** не треба. (Here chi is not needed.) Просто інтонація. (Just intonation.)

:::tip[💡 Tip]
**Safe Harbor Rule**
If you are unsure, use **чи**. It is never wrong to be too polite. But dropping it with a stranger might sound too casual.
:::


*   **Чи ви тут працюєте?** [t͡ʃɪ ʋɪ tut prɑt͡sʲˈujɛtɛ] — Do you work here? (Polite)
*   **Ви тут працюєте?** [ʋɪ tut prɑt͡sʲˈujɛtɛ] — Do you work here? (Casual)
*   **Чи це ваш кіт?** [t͡ʃɪ t͡sɛ ʋɑʃ kit] — Is this your cat? (Polite)

### Інопланетянин-учитель (The Alien Teacher)
*ALF* — це серіал з 90-х. (ALF is a TV show from the 90s.) Для українців це легенда. (For Ukrainians this is a legend.)

Український дубляж — це шедевр. (Ukrainian dubbing is a masterpiece.) Актори показали: українська мова — весела і сучасна. (Actors showed: Ukrainian language is funny and modern.)

**Альф і Кіт (Alf and the Cat):**

*   **Альф:** Привіт, кицю! Ти тут? [prɪʋˈit kˈɪt͡sʲu tɪ tut] (Hello, kitty! Are you here?)
*   **Кіт:** Мяв. [mʲɑʋ] (Meow.)
*   **Альф:** Чи ти смачний? [t͡ʃɪ tɪ smɑt͡ʃnˈɪj] (Are you tasty?)
*   **Кіт:** Ні, я не смачний! Я друг! [ni jɑ nɛ smɑt͡ʃnˈɪj jɑ druɦ] (No, I am not tasty! I am a friend!)

### The Famous Cat Quote
There is one line from *ALF* that every Ukrainian knows. It perfectly demonstrates how to use **не** (not) to make a negative statement.

Alf is surprised that someone does not like cats. He explains why.

*   **Ти не любиш котів?** [tɪ nɛ lʲˈubɪʃ kotˈiʋ] — You do not like cats?
*   **Ти просто не вмієш їх готувати!** [tɪ prˈosto nɛ ʋmˈijɛʃ jix hotuʋˈɑtɪ] — You just do not know how to cook them!

Look at the grammar here. The particle **не** stands strictly before the verb **любиш** (like) and **вмієш** (know how). It blocks the action. This simple structure is the key to saying "no" to any action.

*   **Я не готую.** [jɑ nɛ hotˈuju] — I do not cook.
*   **Ми не знаємо.** [mɪ nɛ znˈɑjɛmo] — We do not know.
*   **Він не тут.** [ʋin nɛ tut] — He is not here.

## 📋 Підсумок (Summary)

Ми вивчили питання і слово «ні». (We learned questions and the word "no".)

| Concept (Поняття) | Ukrainian (Українська) | English (Англійська) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Question Particle** | **Чи** [t͡ʃɪ] | Used for Yes/No questions (polite). |
| **Intonation** | **Голос вгору ↗** | Used for casual questions (no "Do"). |
| **Negation** | **Не** [nɛ] | "Not" - always before the verb. |
| **Answer: Yes** | **Так** [tɑk] | "Yes" |
| **Answer: No** | **Ні** [ni] | "No" (as an answer). |
| **Who** | **Хто** [xtɔ] | For people. |
| **What** | **Що** [ʃt͡ʃɔ] | For things. |
| **Where** | **Де** [dɛ] | For location. |
| **When** | **Коли** [kɔlˈɪ] | For time. |

**Важливе (Important):**

1.  **Забудьте про «Do»** (Forget about "Do"). Українська мова — проста. (Ukrainian language is simple.) Тут немає «helper verbs». (Here are no helper verbs.)
    *   **Ти знаєш?** (Do you know?)
2.  **Правило «Не»** (The "Not" Rule). **Не** + Verb. (Not + Verb.)
    *   **Я не знаю.** (I do not know.)
3.  **Ввічливість** (Politeness). Незнайомці = **Чи**. (Strangers = Chi.)
4.  **Спеціальні питання** (Special questions). **Хто**, **що**, **де**, **коли**.

Тепер ви знаєте все! (Now you know everything!)
