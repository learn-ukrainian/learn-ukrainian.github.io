# Checkpoint: First Contact

## Skill 1: Вступ: Читання кирилиці та рід іменників

### Model: Phonetic and Structural Foundations
We have traveled far in our Ukrainian journey. It is time to look back at our progress. Let's begin with our foundation: the **алфавіт** (alphabet). The Ukrainian alphabet has 33 letters. Most letters are simple for English speakers. However, the vowels often cause confusion for new students. We must focus on the letters **и** and **і**. The letter **і** — [i] sounds like the «ee» in «see». The letter **и** — [ɪ] is a bit lower. It sounds like the «i» in «bit». This small change changes the meaning of words. You must hear this difference clearly. We also have letters like **ґ** and **г**. The **ґ** — [ɡ] is hard like in «goat». The **г** — [ɦ] is soft like in «help». You must read these letters correctly in every **текст** (text). We are here to help you.

Next, we review how to identify the **рід** (gender) of a word. In Ukrainian, every **іменник** (noun) is masculine, feminine, or neuter. You usually look at the last letter of the word. **Чоловічий рід** (masculine gender) nouns usually end in a consonant. **Жіночий рід** (feminine gender) nouns usually end in **-а** or **-я**. **Середній рід** (neuter gender) nouns usually end in **-о** or **-е**. This pattern helps you choose the right verb forms later. You already know many words for these categories. Practice these rules with our cafe words. For example, the word **кава** (coffee) is feminine. The word **молоко** (milk) is neuter.

Rules are great, but some words are special. We have biological gender exceptions in Ukrainian. Some words for men end like feminine or neuter words. For example, the word **тато** (dad) ends in **-о**. Usually, **-о** means a neuter noun. However, a dad is a man. Therefore, **тато** is a masculine noun. We also have the word **батько** (father). It also ends in **-о**. You must remember these specific family words. They follow masculine patterns for verbs and adjectives. Do not let the last letter fool you! Even the name **Михайло** (Mykhailo) follows this rule. It is a man's name. It is masculine.

### Practice: Phonetics and Noun Genders
Let's see a short dialogue with these letters.
**— Хто це?** (Who is this?)
**— Це мій син.** (This is my son.)
**— Він знає алфавіт?** (Does he know the alphabet?)
**— Ні, він не знає.** (No, he does not know.)
**— Так, він знає все.** (Yes, he knows everything.)

`[!tip]`
Think of **и** as a middle sound. It is not «ee» and not «oo». Your tongue stays flat in the mouth. Try to say «bit» with a smile.

Look at this interaction in our cafe.
**— Де моя кава?** (Where is my coffee?)
**— Ось вона.** (Here it is.)
**— Дякую.** (Thank you.)
**— Будь ласка.** (You're welcome.)

## Skill 2: Дієвідмінювання дієслів теперішнього часу

### Model: The Two Conjugation Families
As we see with **батько** and **тато**, grammar often has patterns that reflect reality, and this logic extends to how we describe actions. Just as nouns follow clear gender rules, Ukrainian verbs follow organized patterns called conjugations. Understanding these groups helps you predict the ending of almost any action you want to describe.

Verbs in the **Перша дієвідміна** (First Conjugation) group are the most common in the language. You can easily recognize them by the **-ати** ending in their basic form. To use them in a sentence, you drop the **-ти** and add specific personal endings like **-ю**, **-єш**, and **-ють**. For example, the verb **читати** — [t͡ʃɪˈtatɪ] (to read) becomes **я читаю** — [ja t͡ʃɪˈtaju] (I read). Other important verbs like **знати** — [znatɪ] (to know) and **писати** — [pɪˈsatɪ] (to write) also belong here. While most are regular, **писати** changes its sound to **пишу** — [pɪˈʃu] (I write), where **ш** — [ʃ] is like «sh» in «shoe». The most important feature is the plural «they» ending, which is usually **-ють**.

The **Друга дієвідміна** (Second Conjugation) group typically end in **-ити** or **-іти**. A vital verb in this category is **говорити** — [ɦoʋoˈrɪtɪ] (to speak). While the first group uses an «e» sound in the middle of endings, this group prefers the «y» sound. The biggest distinction appears in the «they» form. Instead of the **-ють** ending we saw before, these verbs use **-ять**. For instance, we say **вони говорять** — [ʋoˈnɪ ɦoˈʋɔrʲatʲ] (they speak). Mastering this difference between the plural endings is a key milestone.

To build a correct sentence, you must understand the **підмет** (subject) and the **присудок** (predicate). The **підмет** is the noun or pronoun that performs the action. The **присудок** is the action itself, which we express with a verb. In Ukrainian, these two parts must always agree in person and number. This agreement ensures that the listener always knows exactly who is doing what.

### Practice: Conjugation and Syntax
Observe the conjugation patterns in these examples:

*   **Я читаю текст.** — [ja t͡ʃɪˈtaju tekst] (I read the text.)
*   **Ти знаєш відповідь.** — [tɪ ˈznajeʃ ˈʋidpoʋidʲ] (You know the answer.)
*   **Вони знають все.** — [ʋoˈnɪ ˈznajutʲ ʋsɛ] (They know everything.)
*   **Він говорить повільно.** — [ʋin ɦoˈʋɔrɪtʲ poˈʋilʲno] (He speaks slowly.)
*   **Ми говоримо добре.** — [mɪ ɦoˈʋɔrɪmo ˈdɔbrɛ] (We speak well.)
*   **Вони говорять.** — [ʋoˈnɪ ɦoˈʋɔrʲatʲ] (They speak.)

Syntactic role examples:
*   **Студент читає.** — [stuˈdent t͡ʃɪˈtaje] (The student reads.) — **Студент** is the підмет.
*   **Я пишу.** — [ja pɪˈʃu] (I write.) — **Я** is the підмет.
*   **Друзі знають.** — [ˈdruzʲi ˈznajutʲ] (The friends know.) — **Знають** is the присудок.

:::danger[🛡️ Myth Buster]
Many students think that the verb ending is just an extra detail. In reality, the ending is so strong that you can often drop the pronoun. **Читаю** — [t͡ʃɪˈtaju] already means «I read»!
:::

## Skill 3: Комунікація: Питальні речення та замовлення в кафе

### Model: Asking and Navigating the Cafe
This handshake between a **підмет** (subject) and a **присудок** (predicate) forms the backbone of your speech, but a living dialogue truly begins when you start asking questions. In Ukrainian, a **питальне речення** (interrogative sentence) is your primary tool for gathering information. These questions usually start with a specific **питальне слово** (question word) that tells your listener exactly what type of answer you are looking for: **хто** (who), **що** (what), **де** (where), and **коли** (when).

To form these specific questions, you simply place the question word at the very beginning of the sentence. The verb stays right where it is, agreeing with its subject. When you just need a simple "yes" or "no", Ukrainian relies entirely on the music of your voice. You keep the words in the exact same order but apply a rising intonation to the final word. When the answer is "no", we use the negation particle **не** (not), which always sits directly before the verb it is negating.

In the cafe, the most common way to place an order is to use the phrase **я хочу** (I want) or the polite **можна** (may I). In Ukrainian culture, always bookend your requests with **будь ласка** (please) and respond with **дякую** (thank you).
