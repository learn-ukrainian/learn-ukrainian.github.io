# Food & Drinks

> **Note:** You've mastered verbs! Now let's fill them with meaning ‚Äî talking about food! This is pure vocabulary, using the verbs you already know.

## Warm-up: The Universal Language

Food is perhaps the most universal topic. No matter where you go in the world, people love talking about what they eat and drink. In Ukraine, food is not just sustenance ‚Äî it's **culture, history, and hospitality** wrapped into one delicious package.

When you visit Ukraine, you'll encounter dishes that have been perfected over centuries. From the legendary **–±–æ—Ä—â** (borscht) that every grandmother makes differently, to the beloved **–≤–∞—Ä–µ–Ω–∏–∫–∏** (dumplings) that appear at every celebration, food tells the story of the Ukrainian people. These aren't just recipes ‚Äî they're family traditions passed down through generations.

Ukrainian cuisine reflects the country's agricultural heritage. The rich black soil (—á–æ—Ä–Ω–æ–∑–µ–º) produces some of the best grain, vegetables, and fruits in Europe. That's why bread, potatoes, and beets are central to the diet. Every dish has a story.

In this module, you'll learn the vocabulary you need to navigate Ukrainian cuisine ‚Äî from breakfast to dinner, from ordering at a restaurant to shopping at a market. By the end, you'll be able to read a menu, order your meal, and compliment the cook! Get ready to eat your way through Ukrainian!

> üí° **Cultural Tip:** Ukrainians take food seriously. Refusing food is considered rude. When offered, always accept at least a little! The phrase "–Ø –≤–∂–µ –Ω–∞—ó–≤—Å—è/–Ω–∞—ó–ª–∞—Å—è" (I'm already full) might not work ‚Äî expect to hear "–©–µ —Ç—Ä–æ—Ö–∏!" (A little more!)

## Cultural Insight: –ë–æ—Ä—â ‚Äî More Than Soup üá∫üá¶

**–ë–æ—Ä—â** is not just soup ‚Äî it's a symbol of Ukrainian identity. In 2022, UNESCO added Ukrainian borscht to its list of Intangible Cultural Heritage, recognizing it as a uniquely Ukrainian tradition.

Every Ukrainian grandmother (–±–∞–±—É—Å—è) has her own recipe. Some add beans, some add prunes, some serve it with meat, others without. But all agree: –±–æ—Ä—â must have **–±—É—Ä—è–∫** (beets), which gives it the famous red color.

The proper way to eat –±–æ—Ä—â:
1. Add a generous spoonful of **—Å–º–µ—Ç–∞–Ω–∞** (sour cream)
2. Eat with **–ø–∞–º–ø—É—à–∫–∏** (garlic bread) or dark bread
3. Say **"–°–º–∞—á–Ω–æ–≥–æ!"** before eating

> üìú **Myth Buster:** Some people call it "Russian borscht." This is false. –ë–æ—Ä—â is **Ukrainian** in origin, documented in Ukrainian cookbooks for centuries. The UNESCO recognition confirms this cultural ownership.

> üìú **History Bite:** –ë–æ—Ä—â sustained Ukrainian families through famines and wars. The Holodomor (1932-33) was a deliberate famine that killed millions of Ukrainians. Today, every bowl of –±–æ—Ä—â carries this memory ‚Äî and the resilience of the Ukrainian people.
