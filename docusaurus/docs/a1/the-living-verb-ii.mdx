# The Living Verb II

## Warm-up

Welcome back, neighbor! Today is a beautiful day to learn something new. Today, we are opening a new door in our language journey together. So far, we have met many useful words. But today we focus on the engine of the sentence: the **дієслово** (verb) [dʲijeˈslɔwɔ]. In our previous lessons, we looked at the first group of verbs, like **читати** (to read) [tʃɪˈtatɪ]. We call them **Перша дієвідміна** (First Conjugation) [ˈpɛrʃɐ dʲijeˈwidmʲinɐ].

Now, it is time to meet their cousins in the **Друга дієвідміна** (Second Conjugation) [ˈdruɦɐ dʲijeˈwidmʲinɐ]. You can think of them as the "и-family". The letter **и** appears everywhere! These verbs are essential for your daily life. They help us describe how we **говорити** (to speak) [ɦɔwɔˈrɪtɪ]. They help us describe what we **бачити** (to see) [ˈbatʃɪtɪ]. They even help us describe who we **любити** (to love) [lʲʊˈbɪtɪ]. **Це дуже важлива тема.** (This is a very important topic) [tsɛ ˈduʒɛ wɐʒˈlɪwɐ ˈtɛmɐ].

### Aspect Overview: Ongoing Actions
Before we look at the patterns, let’s talk about how these verbs behave in time. In Ukrainian, every action has a specific aspect. The verbs we are studying today belong to the **недоконаний вид** (imperfective aspect) [nɛdɔˈkɔnanɪj wɪd]. This is a very friendly aspect for beginners! This simply means the action is currently happening, is a habit, or is not finished yet.

When you speak in the present tense, you are using this aspect. Use it to tell your neighbor what you do every day or what you are doing right now.

_Приклад:_
**Ми говоримо повільно.** [mɪ ɦɔˈwɔrɪmɔ pɔˈwilʲnɔ] — We speak slowly.

## Presentation

How do you know the family of a verb? The secret is usually in the vowel of the endings. Class I verbs usually have the letter **е** or **є**. Class II verbs prefer the letter **и**. Let’s look at a side-by-side comparison of **читати** [tʃɪˈtatɪ] and **говорити** [ɦɔwɔˈrɪtɪ].

| Person | Class I (читати) | Class II (говорити) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Я** (I) | чита**ю** | говор**ю** |
| **Ти** (You) | чита**єш** | говор**иш** |
| **Він / Вона** (He / She) | чита**є** | говор**ить** |
| **Ми** (We) | чита**ємо** | говор**имо** |
| **Ви** (You pl.) | чита**єте** | говор**ите** |
| **Вони** (They) | чита**ють** | говор**ять** |

Notice the clear difference. Class II keeps the **и** sound strong. This vowel harmony is a great guide for your ears. It helps you practice speaking.

:::tip[💡 Tip]
Think of Class II as the "High Tongue" family. The vowel **и** requires you to lift your tongue high. Whenever you see a verb ending in **-ити** in the dictionary, prepare your tongue!
:::


### The Present Tense Paradigm
To build these verbs, we start with the **Інфінітив** (infinitive) [infʲiniˈtɪw]. This is the basic form ending in **-ти**. To conjugate it, we remove the **-ти** to find the root. Then, we add our new neighborly endings. Let's practice with the verb **бачити** (to see) [ˈbatʃɪtɪ].

- **Я бачу** [ˈbatʃʊ] — I see. (We use **-у** here after the **ч**).
- **Ти бачиш** [ˈbatʃɪʃ] — You see.
- **Він / Вона бачить** [ˈbatʃɪtʲ] — He / She sees.
- **Ми бачимо** [ˈbatʃɪmɔ] — We see.
- **Ви бачите** [ˈbatʃɪtɛ] — You see.
- **Вони бачать** [ˈbatʃatʲ] — They see.

These endings (**-у/ю, -иш, -ить, -имо, -ите, -ать/ять**) are the keys to clear communication. They tell everyone who is doing the action.

_Приклад:_
**Ти бачиш парк?** [tɪ ˈbatʃɪʃ park] — Do you see the park?

### Consonant Mutations: The 1st Person Shift
Sometimes, a verb changes its face a little bit when you talk about yourself! This is called **чергування приголосних** (consonant mutation) [tʃɛrɦʊˈwanʲnʲɐ ˈprɪɦɔlɔsnɪx]. In Class II, this only happens in the **Я** form. For all other people, the verb is normal.

Here are common shifts:
- **д** becomes **дж** [dʒ]: **сидіти** (to sit) [sɪˈdʲitɪ] → **Я сиджу** [sɪˈdʒu].
- **т** becomes **ч** [tʃ]: **платити** (to pay) [plɐˈtɪtɪ] → **Я плачу** [plɐˈtʃu].
- **с** becomes **ш** [ʃ]: **просити** (to ask) [prɔˈsɪtɪ] → **Я прошу** [ˈprɔʃʊ].

These shifts help the words flow naturally. This small change helps you sound like a native speaker!

_Приклад:_
**Я сиджу тут.** [jɐ sɪˈdʒu tut] — I am sitting here.

### The Labial L: A Helping Sound
When a verb root ends in a sound made with your lips, we add a letter **л** [lʲ] in the **Я** form. We call these "labial" sounds. These consonants are **б, п, в, м, ф**. If your verb root ends in one of these, add the **л**!

Look at these very common verbs:
- **любити** (to love) [lʲʊˈbɪtɪ] → **Я люблю** [lʲʊˈblʲʊ].
- **робити** (to do) [rɔˈbɪtɪ] → **Я роблю** [ˈrɔblʲʊ].

This extra letter only appears when you say "I". For everyone else, the **л** is not there.

:::warning[⚠️ Warning]
Don't forget the **л**! A common mistake is to say **я робю**. Always remember to add that smooth **л** sound after the lip consonants in the **Я** form.
:::


### Error Prevention: The Conjugation Trap
The biggest trap is using the **-ати** endings for everything. We often learn Class I first. It feels like the rule. But remember, a verb like **говорити** follows its own path!

If you say **ти говореш** instead of **ти говориш**, it sounds clumsy. Imagine putting a hat on your foot. Check the **Інфінітив** in your mind. If it ends in **-ити**, you are in the "и-family". You will be a pro in no time!

_Приклад:_
**Ви вчите мову.** [wɪ ˈwtʃɪtɛ ˈmɔwʊ] — You are learning the language.

## Practice

Now you can tell the families apart. Let's see how these verbs behave in real life.

### Identifying the Conjugation Families
Think of verbs as neighbors. Some belong to the **Перша дієвідміна** family. Others are part of the **Друга дієвідміна**. To help them, look at the **інфінітив** ending. If a verb ends in **-ати**, it is group one. If it ends in **-ити**, it is our family.

_Приклади:_
**Я роблю.** [ja rɔˈblʲʊ] — I am doing.
**Ти бачиш.** [tɪ ˈbatʃɪʃ] — You see.
**Він ходить.** [ʋin ˈxɔdɪtʲ] — He walks.

:::tip[🔍 Pattern Discovery]
Look at the verb **робити** (to do). Can you see the **-и-** in almost every form? This is the signature of our new family!
:::


### High-Frequency Verb Pairings
In Ukrainian, some words are best friends. These are collocations. For example, you say **говорити повільно** (to speak slowly). Another pair is **робити помилки** (to make mistakes). Don't worry, everyone makes them!

_Приклади:_
**Ми говоримо багато.** [mɪ ɦɔˈwɔrɪmɔ bɐˈɦatɔ] — We speak a lot.
**Я роблю фото.** [ja rɔˈblʲʊ ˈfɔtɔ] — I am taking a photo.
**Вона платить гроші.** [ʋɔˈna plɐˈtɪtʲ ˈɦrɔʃi] — She is paying money.

## Production

The third person forms are he, she, it, and they. In the **Друга дієвідміна**, the singular ending is usually **-ить**. When you move to the plural "they", it changes to **-ять**. It is a small shift. It helps your listener.

| Singular (Він/Вона) | Plural (Вони) | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Він любить** [ʋin ˈlʲʊbɪtʲ] | **Вони люблять** [ʋɔˈnɪ ˈlʲʊblʲatʲ] | He loves / They love |
| **Вона бачить** [ʋɔˈna ˈbatʃɪtʲ] | **Вони бачать** [ʋɔˈnɪ ˈbatʃatʲ] | She sees / They see |
| **Воно стоїть** [ʋɔˈnɔ stɔˈjitʲ] | **Вони стоять** [ʋɔˈnɪ stɔˈjatʲ] | It stands / They stand |

### Common Sentence Blueprints
Now, let’s put everything together. You can use simple blueprints. A popular one is **Я люблю...** followed by another verb. You can also describe what you see using **Ти бачиш...**. These patterns are like Lego blocks.

_Приклади:_
**Я люблю пити каву.** [ja lʲʊˈblʲʊ ˈpɪtɪ ˈkawʊ] — I love to drink coffee.
**Ти бачиш парк.** [tɪ ˈbatʃɪʃ park] — You see the park.
**Ми бачимо море.** [mɪ ˈbatʃɪmɔ ˈmɔrɛ] — We see the sea.

:::note[🏺 Culture]
Українці люблять приймати гостей. Сусід бачить вас і каже **«Ходіть у гості!»** [xɔˈdʲitʲ ʊ ˈɦɔsʲtʲɪ], він запрошує вас на чай та розмову. Це наріжний камінь нашої гостинності!
:::


### Special Guests: Eating and Drinking
We have two verbs that are vital: **їсти** (to eat) and **пити** (to drink). **Пити** follows the rules of the First Conjugation. **Їсти** is an ancient bonus verb. It has its own unique rhythm and is very old. But you will learn it quickly!

**Conjugation of їсти (to eat):**
- **Я їм** [ja jim] — I eat
- **Ти їси** [tɪ jiˈsɪ] — You eat
- **Він/Вона їсть** [ʋin ˈjistʲ] — He/She eats
- **Ми їмо** [mɪ jiˈmɔ] — We eat
- **Ви їсте** [ʋɪ jisˈtɛ] — You (plural) eat
- **Вони їдять** [ʋɔˈnɪ jiˈdʲatʲ] — They eat

**Conjugation of пити (to drink):**
- **Я п'ю** [ja pju] — I drink
- **Ти п'єш** [tɪ pjeʃ] — You drink
- **Він/Вона п'є** [ʋin pje] — He/She/It drinks

## Cultural Insight

These two verbs are essential for hospitality. Keep them close!

:::note[🏺 Cultural Context]
In Ukrainian culture, offering food and drink is a duty. You will hear **“Прошу до столу!”** (Please come to the table!). Then you will hear **“Ви будете їсти?”** (Will you eat?). Knowing how to answer with **“Я їм...”** or **“Я п’ю...”** is the first step.
:::


When you enjoy a meal with your Ukrainian friends, you join a tradition. The words you use — verbs ending in **-ити** — are echoes of the ancient language of Kyivan Rus’. These structures have remained stable since the 10th century.

### Historical Stability
The **-ити** ending is a descendant of the Proto-Slavic form. Other parts of the language have shifted. But this specific class of verbs remained a linguistic anchor. In the era of Kyivan Rus’, speakers used these same patterns. You find these familiar verb forms in ancient manuscripts.

For a beginner, this history is a comfort. The system is not a modern invention. This tried-and-tested engine has served millions of people for a millennium.

| Period | Form | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Proto-Slavic (Ancient) | `*lubiti` | to love |
| Kyivan Rus' (11th c.) | **любити** — [lʲuˈbɪtɪ] | to love |
| Modern Ukrainian | **любити** — [lʲuˈbɪtɪ] | to love |

### Etymology: Love and People
A beautiful connection is in the word **любити** (to love). The root is connected with the word **люди** (people). In the Slavic worldview, loving is connected with being among people. To love is to recognize humanity in others.

This connection reflects social traditions in Ukraine. Loving is expressed through community. When you tell someone **«Я люблю тебе»**, you use a word rooted in the idea of being human.

:::info[ℹ️ Fact]
The verb **любити** is used for people and things you enjoy. For deep romantic love, Ukrainians also use the verb **кохати**. As a beginner, **любити** is your all-purpose word!
:::


### Slavic Family Bridge
These **-ити** verbs are a bridge to other Slavic languages. If you travel to Poland or Czechia, your Ukrainian knowledge helps you. The stems and endings look like cousins. This kinship makes learning Ukrainian rewarding. It opens doors to many cultures.

| English | Ukrainian | Polish | Czech |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| He/She speaks | **Він говорить** | *On mówi* | *On mluví* |
| They speak | **Вони говорять** | *Oni mówią* | *Oni mluví* |
| He/She does | **Він робить** | *On robi* | *On robí* |

:::danger[🛡️ Myth Buster]
People say Slavic languages are impossible. But look at the patterns! Whether in Kyiv or Warsaw, the **-ити** family follows a rhythm. Once you find the beat, you can dance!
:::

Крім того, ми запам'ятали важливі слова **їсти** та **пити**. Тепер ви можете описати ваші дії. Ви можете сказати "Я люблю" або "Я бачу".
