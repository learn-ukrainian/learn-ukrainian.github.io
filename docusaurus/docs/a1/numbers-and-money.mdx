# Numbers & Money

## Розминка: Числа в повсякденному житті

### Числа для виживання (Numbers for Survival)
Imagine you just arrived in a beautiful Ukrainian city like Lviv or Kyiv. Everything is new and exciting! You want to buy a fresh **хліб** (bread) at the local bakery. You see a sign with the price. You want to take **автобус** (a bus) to the city center. You look for the correct number. Numbers are your first survival tools in a new country. They help you navigate the world without feeling lost.

**Числа** (Numbers) help you with time, money, and transport.
Ми бачимо числа всюди (We see numbers everywhere).
Вони допомагають нам кожного дня (They help us every day).

### Числа навколо нас (Numbers Around Us)
Take a walk through a typical Ukrainian neighborhood. Look at the local cafe. The menu has **ціни** (prices). Look at the phone in your hand. It shows the **дата** (date) and the **час** (time). Look at the houses. Every building has a **номер** (number). Even at the pharmacy, numbers tell you the dosage of your vitamins. Numbers are not just math. They are the language of your daily life.

У кафе є меню (There is a menu in the cafe).
Ми бачимо ціни (We see prices).
Тут є номер будинку (There is a house number here).

:::tip[💡 Tip]
Numbers in Ukraine use a comma for decimals instead of a period. For example, a price might look like 25,50 instead of 25.50. Keep an eye out for this small but important detail!
:::


### Рахуємо від нуля до десяти (Counting from Zero to Ten)
Let's start our journey with the building blocks. These are the **кількісні числівники** (cardinal numbers) from 0 to 10. They are very common and very useful. Listen to the rhythm of the words. Some words have soft endings, and some have strong stresses. Pay close attention to the IPA guides to get the sounds just right. Stress is very important in Ukrainian!

- **нуль** — [nulʲ] (zero)
- **оди́н** — [oˈdɪn] (one)
- **два** — [dwa] (two)
- **три** — [trɪ] (three)
- **чоти́ри** — [tʃoˈtɪrɪ] (four)
- **п'ять** — [pjatʲ] (five)
- **шість** — [ʃistʲ] (six)
- **сім** — [sim] (seven)
- **ві́сім** — [ˈʋisʲim] (eight)
- **де́в'ять** — [ˈdɛʋjatʲ] (nine)
- **де́сять** — [ˈdɛsʲatʲ] (ten)

Це число один (This is number one).
Ось число п'ять (Here is number five).
Там число десять (There is number ten).

### Один та два: Спеціальні випадки (One and Two: Special Cases)
In Ukrainian, some numbers change their form to match the gender of the noun. Don't worry, it is quite simple for now! The number **один** (one) has three forms: **один** (masculine), **одна** (feminine), and **одне** (neuter). The number **два** (two) has two forms: **два** (masculine and neuter) and **дві** (feminine). Using the correct form makes your Ukrainian sound natural and clear to your neighbors.

Один квиток (One ticket).
Одна кава (One coffee).
Одне вікно (One window).
Два квитки (Two tickets).
Дві кави (Two coffees).

| Number | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | оди́н | одна́ | одне́ |
| 2 | два | дві | два |
## Презентація: Рахуємо до ста та правила узгодження

### Числівники від 11 до 19: Магія суфікса -надцять
In the previous section, we mastered the basics from zero to ten. Now, imagine you are at a local bakery and the price is slightly higher than ten. To count from 11 to 19, Ukrainian uses a very logical system based on the number ten. We take the base digit (1–9) and add the suffix **-надцять** — [nadtsʲatʲ]. This suffix literally means «on ten» (на десять), reflecting how these numbers were originally formed by adding units on top of a ten. 

When building these numbers, pay close attention to the soft sign (**ь**). In the base numbers like **п'ять** — [pjatʲ] (five) or **шість** — [ʃistʲ] (six), the soft sign is at the end. However, when we add the suffix, the soft sign from the base number disappears, and it only remains at the very end of the word. For example, **п'ять** becomes **п'ятнадцять**.

| Number | Ukrainian | IPA | Components |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 11 | одина́дцять | [odɪˈnadtsʲatʲ] | один + надцять |
| 14 | чотирна́дцять | [tʃotɪrˈnadtsʲatʲ] | чотири + надцять |
| 15 | п'ятна́дцять | [pjatˈnadtsʲatʲ] | п'ят + надцять |

_Приклади:_
- Це коштує одина́дцять гривень. (It costs eleven hryvnias.)
- Мені потрібно чотирна́дцять яблук. (I need fourteen apples.)
- Квиток коштує п'ятна́дцять гривень. (The ticket costs fifteen hryvnias.)

:::warning[⚠️ Warning]
**Soft Sign Rule**: One of the most common mistakes is keeping the soft sign in the middle. Remember: **п'ятна́дцять** — [pjatˈnadtsʲatʲ] has NO soft sign after the «т». The only soft sign is at the very end!
:::


### Десятки: Від двадцяти до ста
Once you reach twenty, the pattern changes slightly. For 20 and 30, we use the suffix **-дцять** — [dtsʲatʲ]. For 50, 60, 70, and 80, we use the suffix **-десят** — [desjat]. Notice that 40, 90, and 100 have unique forms that you simply need to memorize. Like the "teens," these tens do not have a soft sign in the middle — even for **п'ятдесят** — [pjatdesjat] (fifty) and **шістдесят** — [ʃistdesjat] (sixty).

To form compound numbers (like 25 or 48), we simply place the ten first and then the single digit. It is exactly like English "twenty-five." You say **два́дцять** (twenty) and then **п'ять** (five). 

| Tens | Ukrainian | IPA | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 20 | два́дцять | [ˈdwadtsʲatʲ] | Base for 20-29 |
| 40 | со́рок | [ˈsɔrok] | Unique form |
| 50 | п'ятдеся́т | [pjatdeˈsʲat] | No soft sign in middle |
| 100 | сто | [stɔ] | The big goal! |

_Приклади:_
- Це коштує два́дцять п'ять гривень. (It costs twenty-five hryvnias.)
- Со́рок гривень — це дешево. (Forty hryvnias is cheap.)
- Сто гривень за каву — це дорого! (One hundred hryvnias for coffee is expensive!)

### Секрет Базарного Бухгалтера: Правило 1-2-5
Now we get to the most important part for your wallet! In Ukrainian, the word for "hryvnia" (**гривня**) changes its ending depending on the number before it. Think of this as the «1-2-5 Rule.» As a "Bazaar Accountant," you must categorize numbers into three groups to get the grammar right.

First, the number **1** (**один/одна**) always takes the Nominative Singular form: **одна́ гри́вня**. Second, the numbers **2, 3, and 4** take a special plural form: **дві/три/чоти́ри гри́вні**. Finally, for numbers **5 through 20**, and for any round tens (like 30, 40, 50), we use the Genitive Plural: **п'ять гри́вень**.

| Number Group | Form | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Nominative Singular | одна́ гри́вня | 1 hryvnia |
| 2, 3, 4 | Nominative Plural | дві гри́вні | 2 hryvnias |
| 5–20 | Genitive Plural | п'ять гри́вень | 5 hryvnias |

_Приклади:_
- Одна́ гри́вня — це мало. (One hryvnia is a little.)
- Три гри́вні за воду. (Three hryvnias for water.)
- Де́сять гри́вень, будь ласка. (Ten hryvnias, please.)

### Узгодження з великими числами
What happens when the numbers get bigger, like 21 or 54? The rule is simple: the noun **гривня** always looks at the **last digit** of the number. If the number ends in 1 (but not 11!), use the singular form. If it ends in 2, 3, or 4 (but not 12, 13, 14!), use the plural form. For everything else, use the "5+" form.

This logic also applies to **копійка** — [kopijka] (kopeck), which is the smaller unit of currency. Although you rarely see physical coins today, prices in supermarkets still show them. 

_Приклади:_
- Два́дцять одна́ гри́вня. (Twenty-one hryvnias.)
- Со́рок дві гри́вні. (Forty-two hryvnias.)
- П'ятдеся́т п'ять копі́йок. (Fifty-five kopecks.)

:::tip[💡 Tip]
**The 11-14 Exception**: Numbers 11, 12, 13, and 14 are "rebels." They always take the **п'ять** form (**гривень**), even though they look like they end in 1, 2, 3, or 4. Always say: **одина́дцять гри́вень**.
:::

## Практика: Покупки та ціни

### Скільки це коштує?
Now that you can count your hryvnias correctly, let's go to the market! I always say: a good accountant knows the price of everything. Let's walk to your local neighborhood bazaar. You see fresh bread. You also smell hot coffee. It is time to talk to the sellers. **Ми йдемо́ на ри́нок.** (We go to the market). **Ми ба́чимо ка́ву.** (We see coffee). To ask for the **ціна́** (price), we use a very simple question. You say: **Скільки це коштує?** (How much does this cost?). This phrase works in any shop or bazaar. The verb **коштува́ти** (to cost) is essential for every shopper. We use it to find out if an item is **до́рого** (expensive) or **де́шево** (cheap). This helps you manage your budget. It also helps you compare different options. You can use it for anything you see.

_Приклади:_
- Скі́льки кошту́є ка́ва? (How much does the coffee cost?)
- Ка́ва кошту́є со́рок гри́вень. (The coffee costs forty hryvnias.)
- Це до́рого чи де́шево? (Is this expensive or cheap?)
- Ціна́ — сто гри́вень. (The price is one hundred hryvnias.)
- Скі́льки кошту́є цей хліб? (How much does this bread cost?)

**Нові слова:**
- **ціна́** — [tsʲiˈna] — price
- **скі́льки** — [ˈsʲkʲilʲkɪ] — how much
- **коштува́ти** — [koʃtuˈʋatɪ] — to cost
- **до́рого** — [ˈdoroɦo] — expensive
- **де́шево** — [ˈdɛʃɛʋo] — cheap

### Рахунок та Здача
Are you ready for the payment? You need to know the total amount. **Ми раху́ємо гро́ші.** (We count the money). **Гро́ші — це важли́во.** (Money is important). A friendly way to ask for the total is **Скільки з мене?** (How much from me?). This phrase is common at bazaars and small shops. It shows that you are ready to pay. Sometimes you pay with a large bill. You might use **сто гри́вень** (one hundred hryvnias). In this case, you will receive **зда́ча** (change). Another word for this is **ре́шта** (change/the rest). Sellers usually count the bills out loud for you. I always check my change too! It is a good idea to count along. This practice helps you recognize the numbers quickly. It also builds trust with the seller.

_Приклади:_
- Скі́льки з ме́не? (How much is my total?)
- Ва́ша зда́ча — де́сять гри́вень. (Your change is ten hryvnias.)
- Ось ва́ша ре́шта. (Here is your change.)
- Да́йте, будь ла́ска, зда́чу. (Give the change, please.)
- Ми раху́ємо гро́ші. (We count the money.)

**Нові слова:**
- **зда́ча** — [ˈzdatʃa] — change
- **ре́шта** — [ˈrɛʃta] — change/the rest
- **сто** — [sto] — hundred
- **гро́ші** — [ˈɦrɔʃʲi] — money

### Рольова гра: На ринку
Let's practice a real conversation between a buyer and a seller. You want to buy bread. You also want to buy water. A loaf of bread often costs around 35 UAH. A bottle of water is about 20 UAH. You use simple sentences to communicate your needs. The seller is patient. The seller is also helpful during the transaction. They appreciate your effort to speak Ukrainian in their shop. This small interaction makes your day better. It also improves your language skills. **Прода́вець ка́же ціну́.** (The seller says the price). **Поку́пець дає́ гро́ші.** (The buyer gives the money).

## Культурний огляд: Світ української гривні

Carrying your bread and your fresh change from the bazaar, you might look at the bills in your hand and wonder about their origin. The paper and coins in your pocket have a story that reaches back much further than the modern market. Understanding the history and symbols of the money you use every day makes your shopping trips in Ukraine feel much more like a journey through time!

### Історія назви: Давня гривна

The word **hryvnia** [ˈɦrɪu̯nʲɐ] is far more than just a label on a modern banknote. It carries a history that spans over a thousand years, going all the way back to the medieval state of Kyivan Rus. In those ancient times, a **hryvna** [ˈɦrɪu̯nɐ] was actually a heavy, beautiful piece of jewelry. Imagine a solid ring made of pure silver or gold worn around the neck! The name itself comes from the Old Slavic word for «neck» or «nape». Because these rings had a consistent weight and value, people began to use them for important trade deals and accounting. Eventually, the heavy jewelry was replaced by smaller coins and paper bills, but the name stayed as a proud symbol of Ukrainian heritage.

- **На́зва гри́вня ма́є ста́ру істо́рію (history).** [ˈnɐzwɐ ˈɦrɪu̯nʲɐ ˈmɐje ˈstaru isˈtɔrʲiju]
- **Рані́ше гри́вна була́ як прикра́са (jewelry).** [raˈnʲiʃe ˈɦrɪu̯nɐ buˈla jɐk prɪˈkrasɐ]
- **Вона́ була́ срі́бна або́ золота́.** [woˈna buˈla ˈsrʲibnɐ aˈbɔ zoloˈta]
- **Лю́ди носи́ли її на ши́ї (neck).** [ˈlʲudɪ noˈsɪlɪ iˈji nɐ ˈʃɪji]
- **Зо́лото та срі́бло — це дорогі́ мета́ли.** [ˈzɔlotɔ tɐ ˈsrʲiblɔ t͡sɛ doroˈɦi meˈtalɪ]

_Note:_ Be careful with the spelling! In modern Ukrainian, the currency is **гри́вня** (with an -я), while the ancient neck jewelry is called **гри́вна** (with an -а).

### Символ ₴ та сучасна валюта

When you look at a menu or a price tag in a Ukrainian shop, you will see a unique symbol: **₴**. This sign was officially adopted by the National Bank in 2004 to give the currency its own identity on the global stage. It is based on a lowercase cursive letter «г», which is the first letter of «hryvnia». The two horizontal strokes across the middle are not just for decoration; they represent the stability and strength of the national economy. You might also encounter prices that include the **kopeck** [koˈpʲii̯kɐ]. Although 1 or 5 kopeck coins are now quite rare in daily cash payments, they remain a vital part of bank calculations and digital prices on your phone.

- **Ми ма́ємо спеціáльний си́мвол (symbol) — ₴.** [mɪ ˈmajemo spet͡siˈalʲnɪj ˈsɪmwɔl]
- **Він схо́жий на бу́кву «г».** [win ˈsxɔʒɪj nɐ ˈbukwu ɦɛ]
- **Бу́ква ма́є дві лі́нії (lines).** [ˈbukwɐ ˈmɐje dwi ˈlʲinʲiji]
- **Вода́ кошту́є три гри́вні.** [woˈda koʃˈtuje trɪ ˈɦrɪu̯nʲi]
- **Ка́ва кошту́є со́рок гри́вень.** [ˈkawɐ koʃˈtuje ˈsɔrok ˈɦrɪu̯nʲeu̯]
- **Це деше́во або́ до́рого для вас?** [t͡sɛ ˈdɛʃɛwɔ aˈbɔ ˈdɔroɦo dlʲɐ was]

_Note:_ Even if you don't see small coins in your hand, you will see them on your smartphone screen every time you pay with a card or check your balance.

### Народна мудрість: Копійка береже гривню

Ukrainians have a deep respect for hard work and careful living, and this is perfectly captured in our folk wisdom. One proverb in particular is a favorite among local families: **«Копі́йка береже́ гри́вню»**. It translates as «A kopeck saves a hryvnia». It is the local version of the idea that if you take care of the small change, the larger amounts will take care of themselves. This proverb is a great reminder for any visitor that every little bit counts! It reflects a cultural value of being mindful and appreciative of what you have, whether it is a single coin or a large bill in your wallet.

- **В Украї́ні є до́бре прислі́в'я (proverb).** [w ukrɐˈjinʲi jɛ ˈdɔbre prɪˈslʲiu̯jɐ]
- **Копі́йка береже́ гри́вню.** [koˈpʲii̯kɐ bereˈʒɛ ˈɦrɪu̯nʲu]
- **Мале́нька моне́та — це теж гро́ші.** [mɐˈlɛnʲkɐ moˈnɛtɐ t͡sɛ tɛʒ ˈɦrɔʃi]
- **Кожна гри́вня ма́є зна́чення (meaning).** [ˈkɔʒnɐ ˈɦrɪu̯nʲɐ ˈmɐje ˈznat͡ʃennʲɐ]
- **Мала́ копі́йка ро́бить вели́ку гри́вню.** [mɐˈla koˈpʲii̯kɐ ˈrɔbɪtʲ weˈlɪku ˈɦrɪu̯nʲu]
- **Оща́дність (frugality) — це важли́ва ри́са.** [oˈʃt͡ʃadnʲisʲtʲ t͡sɛ wɐʒˈlɪwɐ ˈrɪsɐ]

_Note:_ This proverb is very common. You might hear it from a friendly neighbor or a shopkeeper if you are searching for a small coin in your pocket to pay exactly!

`[!culture] — National Heroes`
The Ukrainian banknotes are like a small history book in your pocket. The 100 UAH bill features **Taras Shevchenko** [tɐˈras ʃɛu̯ˈtʃɛnkɔ] (the most famous poet), while the 20 UAH bill shows **Ivan Franko** [iˈwan frɐnˈkɔ] (a legendary writer). Look closely at the faces on your bills next time you go shopping!

1.  Числа від 0 до 100.

    *   5-20 гривень (Родовий множини).
4.  Корисні фрази для покупок: **«Скільки це коштує?»**, **«Скільки з мене?»**.
5.  Як ввічливо просити **решту** (здачу).
