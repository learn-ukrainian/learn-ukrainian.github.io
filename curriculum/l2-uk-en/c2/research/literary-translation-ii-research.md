## Cultural Hooks

**1. Микола Лукаш (1919–1988) — the principled standard-bearer**
Source: Wikipedia (uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Лукаш_Микола_Олексійович)

Lукаш knew 22 languages and translated from 18 — always from the original, never via a Russian intermediary translation. This was a principled political act under Soviet conditions, where national minorities were required to translate world classics *from Russian*, not from originals. His *Decameron* (Boccaccio) used archaic 17th–19th-century Ukrainian vocabulary, finding precise Ukrainian equivalents for every Boccaccio proverb. He spent 18 years on the *Faust* translation (published 1955). Repressed 1973–1979 after publicly defending Ivan Dziuba. Died 1988; his major collection *Від Бокаччо до Аполлінера* published posthumously (1990). The journal *Всесвіт* established the annual **Ars Translationis prize** (1989) in his honour. Lукаш's approach is the lived embodiment of the module's core thesis: translation is an act of cultural sovereignty, not a neutral linguistic operation.

**2. 2024 boom in Ukrainian literary translation abroad**
The plan references the "2024 boom" in translations of Ukrainian literature. This is verifiable: following the full-scale invasion (2022), Ukrainian literature entered major Western publishing houses at an unprecedented rate — Oksana Zabuzhko, Serhiy Zhadan, Victoria Amelina's posthumous works. This created a new professional challenge: how do Ukrainian translators (working into English, German, French) preserve national identity without exoticizing, and how do English-to-Ukrainian translators avoid the "colonial gaze" embedded in source-language assumptions? This contemporary frame directly energizes the module's section on representing Ukrainian authors to foreign readers (§5 of the outline).

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Translationese / калькована мова** → copying English SVO word order (Subject–Verb–Object) rigidly into Ukrainian prose, which defaults to verb-second and topic-comment structure. *Example*: "Він повільно підійшов до вікна" is natural; a calque of "He slowly walked up to the window" tempts learners to stack adverbs before verbs as in English. **Root cause**: insufficient internalization of Ukrainian word-order pragmatics; not a grammatical error but a rhythmic and stylistic one.

2. **Leveling of sociolect markers** → rendering all character voices in uniform literary Ukrainian, erasing class, regional, and educational differentiation. A working-class character from Donbas in the original speaks differently from a Kyiv intellectual; collapsing both into neutral standard Ukrainian makes dialogue flat and reduces characterization to plot-level information. **Root cause**: learners conflate "correct Ukrainian" with "uniform Ukrainian."

3. **Over-explanation of realia disrupting authorial tempo** → inserting parenthetical explanations ("борщ — traditional Ukrainian soup") within the flow of prose, destroying the rhythm that characterizes the author's style. The professional solution (transcription + footnote) is not intuitive; learners default to in-text explanation. **Root cause**: anxiety about the target-language reader's comprehension, prioritized over fidelity to source-text texture.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **Lукаш quote anchor**: the plan's claim that Lукаш said translation must be "дуже по-українськи" even when the original is "дуже по-іспанськи" is a pedagogically powerful formulation — verify the exact wording against a primary source before attributing as direct quote; the Wikipedia extract paraphrases his philosophy but doesn't give this as a verbatim quote. Use attributively: "Лукаш наголошував, що..." rather than guillemets.
- **Vlakhov & Florin** (Влахов і Флорін): Their taxonomy of реалії (from *Непереводимое в переводе*, 1980) is the academic backbone for §4 of the outline. Their framework (транскрипція, калька, генералізація, функціональний аналог) is standard in Ukrainian translation studies programs. No need to introduce it cautiously — C2 learners can handle the full taxonomy.
- **TTT pedagogy** (§5): The workshop section uses Test-Teach-Test. This means the module should present a prose fragment *before* introducing theoretical models, ask learners to attempt a translation, then use their errors as the teaching moment. This is a structural requirement, not optional flavor.
- **Decolonized framing**: The Soviet practice of translating via Russian intermediary is a concrete, historically documented form of cultural erasure — Lукаш's refusal to use this method is the decolonization argument in miniature. Frame the module's ethics of translation section around this, not around abstract principles.
- **Frequency caveat for ідіолект/соціолект**: These terms (0.02–0.04 IPM) are below typical C2 productive vocabulary threshold. The vocabulary section should present them with multiple concrete examples and usage patterns so they become productive, not merely recognized.
