## Cultural Hooks

**1. Григорій Кочур (1908–1994) — патріарх українського перекладу** *(verified: Wikipedia uk)*
- Arrested 1943, sentenced to 10 years GULAG (Inta, Komi ASSR) for "Ukrainian bourgeois nationalism." Continued translating *in the camp*, learned Estonian, Latvian, Armenian, Georgian from fellow prisoners.
- After release (1953) and rehabilitation (1962) settled in Irpin; his home became a centre of oppositional creative intelligentsia.
- Corpus: 26 centuries of poetry, 3 continents, ~30 literatures. Author of theoretical articles on translation.
- First received foreign academic invitations only at age 82 (Illinois, 1991; Poland and Czech Republic, 1992) — he was banned from travel earlier.
- Annual "Kochur-Fest" festival in Irpin; chair of translation studies at Lviv University named after him; State Literary Prize named after him for best Ukrainian translations.
- **Decolonization hook**: Kochur was a student of Mykola Zerov — he embodied the neoclassicist tradition of direct translation *from the original*, bypassing Russian intermediary texts.

**2. Микола Лукаш (1919–1988) — «Моцарт перекладу»** *(verified: Wikipedia uk)*
- Knew 22 languages, translated freely from 18. Translated *Faust* (18 years of work), *Decameron* (in archaic 17th–19th c. Ukrainian), *Don Quixote*, *Madame Bovary*, Schiller's lyrics.
- **Critical decolonization fact**: In the USSR, minority-language translations were mandated to go through Russian intermediary translations. Lukash consistently translated *directly from the source language* — a political and artistic act of resistance.
- Quote (Dmytro Pavlychko): «Лукашева мова — як небеса, побачені в телескоп. Міріади слів, які годі спостерігати простим оком, виступають у його мові зі своїм глибинним світлом.»
- Expelled from the Writers' Union 1973 for defending Ivan Dziuba; lived in poverty for years, unable to publish. Reinstated 1986, died 1988. Posthumous collection *«Від Бокаччо до Аполлінера»* (1990).

**3. Максим Рильський on translation** *(verified: Grade 11 Borzenko textbook, chunk `11-klas-ukrlit-borzenko-2019-prof_s0049`)*
- Rylsky: artistic translations «виходять далеко за межі літератури й культури. Це „знаряддя спілкування між народами, знаряддя поширення передових ідей і обміну культурними цінностями, знаряддя зміцнення і зросту інтернаціональної свідомості"».
- This quote is from a Ukrainian school textbook — **safe to use as a module epigraph or opening framing**.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Syntactic calquing of English passive** → Ukrainian active construction
   - Error: «Книга була написана ним» (direct passive calque from English "The book was written by him")
   - Correct: «Книгу написав він» (natural Ukrainian active with fronted accusative object)
   - Why: English overuses passive voice; Ukrainian strongly prefers active constructions. Learners transfer English syntactic patterns wholesale.

2. **False friends (фальшиві друзі перекладача)**
   - *pathetic* (Eng.) = «жалюгідний», NOT «патетичний» (патетичний = «stirring/impassioned»)
   - *sympathy* (Eng.) = «співчуття», NOT «симпатія» (симпатія = «liking/fondness»)
   - *accurate* (Eng.) ≠ «акуратний» (акуратний = «neat/tidy»; accurate = «точний»)
   - Why: Shared Latin/Greek roots create false cognate expectations; particularly dangerous for intermediary translation work.

3. **Overtransliterating реалії instead of contextualizing**
   - Error: rendering every culture-specific term as a transliteration (e.g., «борщ» → *borshch*) without any explanation, even when compensation or descriptive translation would serve the target reader better.
   - Correct: choosing the strategy — transliteration with gloss, calque, or descriptive equivalent — based on the term's cultural salience and target-language context.
   - Why: learners default to the "safe" (visible) strategy of transliteration rather than evaluating communicative effect.

## Notes for Content Writing

1. **Decolonization framing is central, not peripheral**: The history section must foreground the Soviet policy of *obligatory Russian relay translation* as the ideological context that made Lukash's and Kochur's direct-source approach explicitly subversive. This is not just cultural background — it explains why Ukrainian translation theory has its own distinct identity.

2. **Venuti attribution**: «Одомашнення» and «очуження» are Lawrence Venuti's terms from *The Translator's Invisibility* (1995). Use «Л. Венуті» as the theorist name; this is consistent with Ukrainian academic convention.

3. **Equivalence terminology**: Eugene Nida's *dynamic equivalence* vs. *formal equivalence* should be named as «динамічна еквівалентність» (Ю. Найда) and «формальна еквівалентність». Nida's name is standard in Ukrainian translation studies.

4. **Rylsky quote** from the textbook is public domain and usable; it anchors the module in a known figure from the Ukrainian literature curriculum.

5. **Kochur vs. Andrukhovych on Hamlet**: The plan calls for a comparative analysis. Kochur's translation represents classical poetic tradition; Andrukhovych's (2008) is a contemporary, demotic, deliberately non-canonical version. This pairing illustrates the domestication/foreignization axis with a canonical Ukrainian literary text. Verify: Andrukhovych's Hamlet translation was published by Folio, 2008.

6. **Word count architecture**: With a 5000-word target and 7 sections, the intro section (500 words) and summary (200 words) are deliberately short; the three middle sections (1000+900+900 words) carry the primary pedagogical load. Distribute examples generously — at C2, example sentences are analytical demonstrations, not drills.

7. **Розстріляне відродження hook**: The plan names Zerov and Pidmohylny. Zerov was Kochur's teacher — this is a verified biographical link. Pidmohylny translated Balzac (*Батько Горіо*, *Євгенія Гранде*) and Maupassant directly from French, contributing to the European-orientation project of 1920s Ukrainian culture.
