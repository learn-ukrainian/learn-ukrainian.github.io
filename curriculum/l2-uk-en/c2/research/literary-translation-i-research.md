## Cultural Hooks

1. **Микола Лукаш (1919–1988)** — Ukrainian translator, linguist, polyglot, шістдесятник (Wikipedia confirmed). Most celebrated translations: *Faust* (Goethe), *Decameron* (Boccaccio), *Madame Bovary* (Flaubert), *Don Quixote* (Cervantes), Schiller's lyrics. Distinguished by his use of archaic Ukrainian vocabulary layers, dialectalisms, and lexical stratification to render the stylistic richness of originals — a technique not available to translators working into flatter literary languages. His approach demonstrated that Ukrainian, far from being a limited dialect, possesses exceptional expressive range.

2. **Микола Зеров (1890–1937)** — leader of the Kyiv Neoclassicists (Wikipedia confirmed), master of the sonnet form, translator of ancient Greek and Roman poetry. Executed during the Rozstriliane Vidrodzhennia (Executed Renaissance). His principle *ad fontes* — returning to the classical sources — positioned Ukrainian literature as a direct heir to European tradition, not a branch of Russian letters. Zérov translated Virgil, Horace, Ovid — asserting that Ukrainian was fully capable of rendering classical antiquity without mediation through Russian.

3. **Григорій Кочур (1908–1994)** — translator, poet, literary scholar, шістдесятник. Imprisoned in Soviet labor camps 1943–1951. Continued translating in conditions of censorship and political persecution; his choice of texts was itself an act of cultural resistance. His theoretical writings on translation practice established systematic approaches to fidelity vs. domestication that are still cited.

4. **Максим Рильський (1895–1964)** — poet-academician, one of the Neoclassicists, Academician of the AN URSR (1943) and AS USSR (1958), master translator. Wikipedia confirms him as Ukrainian Soviet poet-academician (навіть у складних умовах радянської доби зберіг поетичну майстерність). His translation theory concept of "competition with the original" (*змагання з оригіналом*) is pedagogically central to this module.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Синтаксична калька** → Re-order Ukrainian sentence to reflect natural intonation contour — *Why it happens*: English SVO word order is copied directly; Ukrainian allows (and often demands) different constituent ordering for rhythmic and stylistic effect. E.g., "Осінь прийшла тиха" not *"Тиха осінь прийшла"* (English calque of "Quiet autumn came").

2. **Рима заради рими** (rhyme for rhyme's sake) → Preserve semantic content; sacrifice imperfect rhyme rather than meaning — *Why it happens*: learners prioritize auditory fidelity over semantic fidelity; they translate the surface sound not the image system. Result: rhyme preserved, metaphor destroyed.

3. **Ігнорування милозвучності при відтворенні метру** → Apply у/в, і/й, з/із alternations even when this shifts syllable count — *Why it happens*: learners treat metric constraints as absolute and override Ukrainian euphony rules; in practice, slight metric adjustment is preferable to producing phonetically awkward Ukrainian. E.g., "у великому" is correct; forcing "в великому" to preserve a syllable violates Ukrainian phonological norms.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **Decolonization angle is strong here**: Zérov's *ad fontes* and Luka's' lexical stratification both demonstrate Ukrainian's independent European pedigree. Frame as: "Ukrainian translation school accessed European classics *directly*, not via Russian intermediaries." Do NOT frame as "unlike the Russian tradition."
- **Rylsky's "competition" concept** (*змагання з оригіналом*) is the philosophical spine of the intro section — present it as asserting translator's creative agency, not mere servitude to the original.
- **Luka's' *Faust* and *Decameron*** are the canonical examples for showing lexical stratification; use specific Ukrainian archaisms/dialectalisms from these translations if possible.
- **Зеров's execution (1937)** should be mentioned — he was shot at Sandarmokh. Context: Soviet censorship suppressed Ukrainian translation culture; the school survived through Kochur's underground continuation. This is not tangential history; it is the reason Ukrainian translation theory was forced to develop in resistance.
- **§4.3.3 tropes list**: the content section on "Stylistics and Imagery per State Standard" should explicitly work through epithet → metaphor → personification → periphrasis → metonymy → synecdoche in a *translation* context, not just definition.
- **звукопис frequency = 0.06 ipm** confirms it is highly specialist vocabulary — define clearly on first use; do not assume learner recognition even at C2.
- **строфіка** (1 VESUM match) and **евфонія** (1 VESUM match): use with explicit context; both are low-frequency specialist terms.
- Word target = 5000; overshoot to 5500–5600 recommended. Heaviest sections: "Техніка відтворення форми" (1100) and "Стилістика та образність" (1100) — these are the mechanically dense sections and should carry the bulk.
