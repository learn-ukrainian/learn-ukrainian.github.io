## Cultural Hooks
1. **The "Golden Age" of Terminology (1920s)**: The Institute of Ukrainian Scientific Language actively developed Ukrainian medical terms (e.g., *м'яз* instead of *мускул*, *городина* context). Later, during the Soviet era (1930s+), these were often repressed and replaced with Russian-like calques to "harmonize" the languages. Modern medical Ukrainian is often a return to these purer roots.
2. **Mykola Amosov (Микола Амосов)**: A legendary heart surgeon and cybernetician working in Kyiv. He pioneered heart surgery in Ukraine and popularized the "Amosov system" of physical health. His books are excellent sources of natural medical narration.
3. **Agapit of Pechersk (Агапіт Печерський)**: Historical reference to the 11th-century monk-physician in Kyiv Pechersk Lavra, representing the deep roots of healing traditions.

## Common Learner Errors
1. **Gender of "Біль"**: Learners often treat *біль* (pain) as feminine (like Russian *боль*), saying "сильна біль". Correct: *сильний/гострий біль* (masculine).
2. **Лічити vs Лікувати**: Confusing "to count" (*лічити*) with "to treat" (*лікувати*). "Лікар лічить пацієнта" implies the doctor is counting the patient. Correct: "Лікар лікує пацієнта".
3. **Серцевий приступ vs Напад**: *Приступ* is often a Russian calque or archaic in this context. Modern standard is *серцевий напад* (heart attack) or *інфаркт*.
4. **Осложнення vs Ускладнення**: Calque from Russian *осложнение*. Correct: *ускладнення*.
