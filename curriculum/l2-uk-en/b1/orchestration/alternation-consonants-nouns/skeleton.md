## Що таке чергування приголосних? (~500 words total)
- P1 (~120 words): Bridge from vowel alternations (M08). Explain that while vowels change in the center of the root, consonants change at the boundary of the root and the suffix/ending. Define "чергування" as a systematic sound substitution within a single root (корінь).
- P2 (~130 words): Introduce the three velar consonants (задньоязикові): [г], [к], [х]. Explain that each has two "alternate faces" depending on the grammatical context, creating a triple pattern: [г]—[з]—[ж], [к]—[ц]—[ч], [х]—[с]—[ш].
- P3 (~130 words): Historical logic (brief). Explain how front vowels like [і] and [е] in old Ukrainian caused these back-of-the-mouth consonants to shift forward, creating "palatalization." Emphasize that this isn't random; it's a phonetic law turned into a grammatical rule.
- P4 (~120 words): Metalanguage setup. Define "перша палаталізація" (shift to hushing sounds) and "друга палаталізація" (shift to sibilants). Set the stage for predicting which one appears where (Vocative vs. Dative/Locative).

## Перша палаталізація: [г/к/х] -> [ж/ч/ш] (~750 words total)
- P1 (~130 words): Definition of first palatalization. Focus on the result sounds: [ж], [ч], [ш]. Explain the primary triggers: the Vocative case ending -е and specific suffixes like -к- or -н-.
- P2 (~150 words): Deep dive into the Vocative case (Кличний відмінок) for masculine nouns of the II declension (hard group). Explain the rule: -е triggers the change. Examples: друг — друже, козак — козаче, пастух — пастуше, чоловік — чоловіче.
- P3 (~150 words): Word formation (словотвір). Explain how suffixes create these changes automatically. Examples with diminutive -к- (нога — ніжка, рука — ручка, вухо — вушко) and adjective suffix -н- (книга — книжковий, молоко — молочний).
- P4 (~120 words): Adjectives derived from geographic names using -ськ-. Show the transformation: Прага — празький, Рига — ризький, Норвегія — норвезький. Contrast with nouns that don't end in velars.
- P5 (~100 words): Cultural context and proverbs. Analyze "Терпи, козаче, отаманом будеш" and "Вчися, юначе, знання — то сила." Explain how ignoring the alternation makes the address sound unnatural or like a foreign calque.
- P6 (~100 words): Dialogue: "Львівська книгарня." A customer addresses a bookseller as "друже" and asks for a "книжка" or "ручка," noticing the [г]->[ж] and [к]->[ч] changes in real speech.

## Друга палаталізація: [г/к/х] -> [з'/ц'/с'] (~750 words total)
- P1 (~120 words): Introduction to second palatalization. Result sounds: soft sibilants [з'], [ц'], [с']. Explain the primary trigger: the ending -і in Dative (Д.в.) and Locative (М.в.) cases.
- P2 (~150 words): Feminine nouns (I Declension) in singular Dative and Locative. Provide full paradigms: нога — на нозі, рука — у руці, муха — мусі. Contrast these with Genitive (ноги, руки) where the [г/к] stays hard because the ending is not -і.
- P3 (~150 words): The logic of the [і] trigger. Explain why "книга" becomes "у книзі" (Locative) but "ноги" (Plural/Genitive) remains "г". Differentiate the phonetic [і] that causes softening from other vowels.
- P4 (~130 words): Geographic names in Locative. Practice common travel destinations: у Празі (Prague), у Ризі (Riga), в Америці (America), в Африці (Africa). Note the consistency of the rule across all nouns.
- P5 (~100 words): Common pitfalls: "на нозі" vs "на ножі." Explain that "ніж" (knife) already has [ж], so "на ножі" is just locative without a *new* alternation, whereas "нога" must shift to "з" to follow the second palatalization rule.
- P6 (~100 words): Natural examples from folklore and idiomatic speech: "Що на умі, те й на язиці," "Просили на дорозі, щоб не були на порозі."

## Чергування [ц'] -> [ч] та інші (~500 words total)
- P1 (~150 words): The special case of [ц'] -> [ч] in the Vocative case. Explain that soft [ц'] acts like a velar in this specific context. Examples: хлопець — хлопче, молодець — молодче, швець — шевче.
- P2 (~150 words): Rare or unproductive alternations. Mention [з] -> [ж] in specific titles (князь — княже) and the historical shift in words like "колесо — на колішні." Emphasize these are exceptions to be memorized as chunks.
- P3 (~100 words): The "Why" behind [ц'] -> [ч]. Briefly explain that [ц'] itself is a historical product of a palatalization, and shifting it to [ч] in the Vocative follows the same "forward-shifting" logic as [к] -> [ч].
- P4 (~100 words): Summary of triggers for [ж/ч/ш] vs [з/ц/с]. Reinforce that [ж/ч/ш] is for addressing (Vocative) or making new words, while [з/ц/с] is for locations or giving (Locative/Dative).

## Чергування у відмінюванні іменників II відміни (~550 words total)
- P1 (~150 words): Systematic overview of masculine nouns (II Declension). Contrast the "Nominative vs Vocative" relationship. Show that for masculine nouns, alternation is almost exclusively a Vocative phenomenon (друг-друже).
- P2 (~150 words): Locative case in the II Declension. Explain that masculine nouns with [г/к/х] can take two endings: -і (causing alternation: у порозі) or -у (no alternation: на порогу). Discuss when to use which (Авраменко: -і is more traditional/literary).
- P3 (~150 words): Detailed table based on Litvinova Grade 6: how endings differ by stem group (тверда vs мішана). Show how "козак" (hard) alternates in Vocative but "товариш" (mixed/hushing) does not need to, as it already ends in a hushing sound.
- P4 (~100 words): Recap of singular vs plural. Remind students that these alternations are primarily a singular phenomenon (однина). In plural (множина), we see "ноги", "руки" with no change.

## Чергування у власних назвах і географічних іменах (~500 words total)
- P1 (~150 words): Personal names. Explain that Ukrainian names follow the rules strictly: Ольга — Ользі, Марійка — Марійці, Одарка — Одарці. Masculine names in Vocative: Олег — Олеже (common) or Олегу (allowed).
- P2 (~150 words): Geographic integration. Practice with Ukrainian cities and regions: Кременчук — у Кременчуці, Бердянськ — у Бердянську (no velar at end), Волноваха — у Волновасі. Contrast with foreign names that might resist (though most follow: Гаага — у Гаазі).
- P3 (~100 words): Social etiquette. Why saying "Привіт, Олег!" sounds like a Russianism or an English-style address. Teaching "Привіт, Олеже!" as the authentic Ukrainian standard.
- P4 (~100 words): Reading practice: Identify names and places in a short text about a trip through Ukraine, noting every consonant shift.
