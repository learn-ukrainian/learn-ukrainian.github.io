# Дослідження: Вид у запереченні

## State Standard Reference
§4.2.2.2.1: "об’єкт дії під час заперечення: Не зрозумів слóва. Не побачив друзів;"
Alignment: The State Standard explicitly mandates the use of the Genitive case for direct objects in negative sentences. This module addresses both this critical morphosyntactic rule and the aspectual shift—the semantic preference for the Imperfective aspect for general negation, and the Perfective aspect for specific expectations ("ще не").

## Cultural Hooks
1. The "Polite Refusal" Ritual: A direct "ні" can be perceived as blunt in Ukrainian hospitality. It is culturally expected to soften the refusal with gratitude ("Дякую, але ні") or regret ("На жаль, ні").
2. The Expectation of Completion: The phrase "ще не" (not yet) intrinsically carries the cultural and conversational expectation that the action *will* be completed, which aligns logically with its pairing with the Perfective aspect.

## Common Learner Errors
1. Missing Double Negation → "Я ніколи був там" (Incorrect) vs "Я ніколи не був там" (Correct) — caused by English syntactic interference.
2. Perfective for General Prohibitions → "Не прочитай!" (Incorrect) vs "Не читай!" (Correct) — caused by misunderstanding the semantics of prohibition versus warning.
3. Accusative instead of Genitive object → "Не бачив книгу" (Incorrect) vs "Не бачив книги" (Correct) — failing to apply the negative object rule mandated by the State Standard.

## Notes for Content Writing
- Decolonized framing: Present the Ukrainian negation system (like double negation and the genitive object) as its own logical, self-contained ecosystem. Avoid comparing it to Russian.
- Immersion: 100% Ukrainian. Do not use English scaffolding in explanations.
- Ensure all phonetics or IPA (if mentioned in vocab tables later) strictly use Ukrainian [ɔ] and [ɛ].
- Allocate sufficient depth to the "ще не" + perfective vs "не" + imperfective distinction, as this is the core pedagogical hurdle.
- Include a Mermaid flowchart for the aspect decision algorithm in negative contexts.
