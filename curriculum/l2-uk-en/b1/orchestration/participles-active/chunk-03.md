## Активні дієприкметники минулого часу (-л-) (~770 words total)

While the present forms discussed previously are heavily restricted and often stylistically awkward, a past **активний дієприкметник** (active participle — subject performs the action) is entirely natural and highly productive. They are as fundamental to Ukrainian syntax as a standard **пасивний дієприкметник** (passive participle — subject receives the action). Every properly formed **дієприкметник** (participle — verb form expressing attribute by action) of this type is authentic and stylistically neutral. You will encounter them constantly in literature, news, and everyday conversation.

This authenticity makes them completely different from artificial Russian calques that must be replaced by proper adjectives. For example, you must always replace the calque діючий with **чинний** (current, valid — replaces Russian calque діючий). You must also replace існуючий with **наявний** (existing, available — replaces Russian calque існуючий). Past active participles do not suffer from this problem. They are formed exclusively from perfective verbs, which makes logical sense because they describe an action that has already reached its conclusion.

To form them, you isolate the infinitive base of a perfective verb by removing the final **-ти**, and then add the suffix **-л-** followed by standard adjective endings. Let us look at a straightforward example of this formation process. Take the perfective verb **посивіти** (to go grey). You remove the infinitive marker to get the base, and then add the past active participle suffix to create **посивілий** (gone grey — past active participle).

You can now use this to describe someone as a «посивілий чоловік» (a man who has gone grey). Another excellent example is the verb **змарніти** (to become emaciated or to waste away). Following the exact same pattern, it becomes **змарнілий** (emaciated). These forms seamlessly blend the completed action of the verb into a permanent descriptive trait for the noun they modify.

There is one critical phonetic rule you must memorize when forming these participles, known as the "nu-drop" rule. Many perfective verbs in Ukrainian denote a sudden or single completed action using the suffix **-ну-**. When you form a participle like **зів'ялий** (wilted — past active participle) from these specific verbs, the entire **-ну-** suffix drops out and disappears before you add the **-л-** suffix. This phonetic simplification makes the resulting words flow much more smoothly and prevents awkward consonant clusters. The seventh-grade textbook by Avramenko explicitly highlights this morphological change as a key rule for proper word formation.

Відповідно до правил сучасної української мови, якщо основа інфінітива має цей суфікс перед закінченням, він обов'язково зникає. Візьмемо дієслово зів'янути. Коли ми утворюємо від нього дієприкметник, суфікс випадає, і ми отримуємо слово зів'ялий. Так само працює дієслово розквітнути, яке перетворюється на розквітлий. Якщо на вулиці дуже холодно, людина може змерзнути, і тоді ми назвемо її змерзлий.

> *According to the rules of the modern Ukrainian language, if the infinitive base has this suffix before the ending, it necessarily disappears. Let us take the verb to wilt (зів'янути). When we form a participle from it, the suffix drops out, and we get the word wilted (зів'ялий). The verb to blossom (розквітнути) works the same way, turning into blossomed (розквітлий). If it is very cold outside, a person might freeze (змерзнути), and then we will call them frozen (змерзлий).*

It is essential to internalize this dropping mechanism because failing to drop the suffix will result in words that sound immediately foreign to a native speaker. You will never hear someone say «розквітнулий»; the only correct form is **розквітлий** (blossomed — past active participle). You might use a natural present form like **квітучий** (blooming — natural active participle) or **палаючий** (blazing — natural active participle) to describe an ongoing state. In contrast, **розквітлий** emphasizes the fully completed transition into that state.

Similarly, the verb for falling or shedding, such as leaves in autumn, is **опасти**. Its past active participle simply drops the infinitive marker to become **опалий** (fallen, shed — past active participle, leaves). The leaves have finished falling, so they are now characterized by that completed action.

This brings us to the core semantic meaning of the past active participle: it always expresses a fully completed change of state. The subject underwent a specific action in the past, and it now carries the result of that action as an inherent characteristic. The focus is entirely on the result, not the process. Understanding this temporal boundary is crucial for choosing the right word when describing the world around you.

Уявіть собі осінній ліс. Коли ви бачите пожовклий листок, ви розумієте, що процес зміни кольору вже повністю завершився. Листок колись був зеленим, потім він змінив колір, і тепер він остаточно жовтий. Цей стан є результатом минулої дії. Натомість форма жовтіючий означала б, що листок змінює свій колір саме зараз, у момент розмови.

> *Imagine an autumn forest. When you see a yellowed leaf, you understand that the process of changing color has already fully completed. The leaf was once green, then it changed color, and now it is definitively yellow. This state is the result of a past action. In contrast, the form turning yellow (жовтіючий) would mean that the leaf is changing its color right now, at the moment of conversation.*

Another very common example of this completed state semantic is the verb **зникнути** (to disappear). Using the "nu-drop" rule, we form the word **зниклий** (disappeared — past active participle). A «зниклий собака» is a dog that has already vanished; the act of disappearing is over, and the dog is currently in the state of being missing. This is a precise and economical way to convey complex temporal information inside a single descriptive word. You do not need to use an **описовий зворот** (descriptive phrase — alternative to participle) like "the dog that disappeared yesterday" when you can simply use the participle.

From a grammatical perspective, integrating these words into your sentences is incredibly straightforward. Once you have formed the past active participle with its **-л-** suffix, it behaves exactly like a standard hard group adjective. It must agree with the noun it modifies in gender, number, and case. There are no special declension rules or exceptions to memorize here. Below is the complete declension table for the word «зів'ялий» across all genders and the plural form.
