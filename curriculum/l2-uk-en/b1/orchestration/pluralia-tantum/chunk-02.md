## Семантичні групи (~700 words)

The first and most intuitive group of *pluralia tantum* nouns refers to paired objects (*парні предмети*). These are items that inherently consist of two identical parts functioning together. The most common examples are **окуляри** (glasses), **ножиці** (scissors), and **штани** (trousers). In English, we often say "a pair of glasses" or "a pair of scissors," acknowledging their dual nature. In Ukrainian, these words exist exclusively in the plural. You cannot have just one "scissor blade" functioning as a tool, so the language does not provide a singular form for it.

The same logic applies to **ворота** (gate) and **сани** (sleigh). Even though a gate might seem like a single object to an English speaker, historically and structurally, it was viewed as two swinging doors. For these words, the grammatical concept of **однина** (singular number) is completely absent.

Найпростіший спосіб перевірити, чи є слово *pluralia tantum*, — це спробувати додати до нього числівник «один» або «одна». Наприклад, слово «шкарпетки» позначає парний предмет, але ми легко можемо сказати «одна шкарпетка». Це означає, що воно має форму однини й не належить до цієї групи. Натомість для слова «ножиці» форми «одна ножиця» в сучасній літературній українській мові просто не існує.

> *The simplest way to check if a word is pluralia tantum is to try adding the numeral "one" to it. For example, the word "шкарпетки" (socks) denotes a paired object, but we can easily say "одна шкарпетка" (one sock). This means it has a singular form and does not belong to this group. Conversely, for the word "ножиці" (scissors), the form "одна ножиця" simply does not exist in modern standard Ukrainian.*

:::info
**Grammar box**
Collective and mass nouns (*збірні та масові поняття*) form another major group of *pluralia tantum*. Unlike paired objects, these words describe substances, collections of items, or abstract masses that are treated as an indivisible plural entity.
:::

The second major category encompasses collective and mass concepts. These are words where the boundary between a single item and a group is blurred. A prime example is **гроші** (money). You cannot count "one money" in Ukrainian; the concept itself is inherently plural. Another crucial word for your daily life is **меблі** (furniture). Notice a key difference here: in English, "furniture" is an uncountable singular noun ("the furniture is heavy"), but in Ukrainian, these nouns are always plural.

Other important words in this group include **дріжджі** (yeast), **парфуми** (perfume), and **ліки** (medicine). You must use plural adjectives and verbs with all of these nouns, regardless of how much of the substance you are referring to. 

Як зазначає відомий мовознавець Олександр Авраменко, ці слова позначають речовини, які складаються з безлічі дрібних частинок або крапель, тому сприймаються як множинність. Особливу увагу варто звернути на слово «ліки». Коли ви йдете в аптеку, ви завжди купуєте ліки у формі множини, незалежно від того, чи це одна таблетка, чи ціла упаковка.

> *As noted by the famous linguist Oleksandr Avramenko, these words denote substances consisting of a multitude of tiny particles or drops, therefore they are perceived as a plurality. Special attention should be paid to the word "ліки". When you go to the pharmacy, you always buy medicine in the plural form, regardless of whether it is a single pill or a whole package.*

The third group includes time periods, recurring events, and certain games. These concepts often imply a duration made up of multiple days or a complex process involving multiple actions. A word you will use frequently is **канікули** (holidays/vacation). Because a vacation stretches over several days, it naturally takes a plural form. Similarly, traditional agricultural events like **жнива** (harvest) are *pluralia tantum*, reflecting the prolonged, collective effort required to gather crops. 

Games that involve multiple pieces, such as *шахи* (chess), also fall into this category. Another fascinating cultural example is the concept of **іменини** (name day). 

Слово «іменини» позначає традиційне свято на честь святого, ім'я якого носить людина. Це свято завжди має форму множини. Його часто плутають зі словом «день народження». Пам'ятайте, що «день народження» — це два окремих слова, де головне слово «день» має форму однини. Тому ми кажемо «мій день народження», але «мої іменини».

> *The word "іменини" denotes a traditional holiday in honor of the saint whose name a person bears. This holiday always takes the plural form. It is often confused with the word "день народження" (birthday). Remember that "день народження" is two separate words, where the main word "день" has a singular form. Therefore, we say "мій день народження" (my birthday) but "мої іменини" (my name day).*

Finally, the fourth group consists of geographical names (*географічні назви*). A surprising number of cities, regions, and mountain ranges in Ukrainian are *pluralia tantum*. The most iconic example is the **Карпати** (Carpathians). When you travel to the mountains, you are literally traveling to a plural destination. Therefore, when discussing your trips, you must ensure your verbs and adjectives reflect the plural number, known as **множина** (plural number). 

Let's see how these words operate in a natural conversational setting. When using *pluralia tantum* nouns, remember that grammatical **узгодження** (agreement) is strictly plural. Adjectives, pronouns, and verbs must all reflect the plural number, even if the object being discussed seems singular in your mind. In the following dialogue, a resident is packing boxes for a move to a new apartment, and a neighbor drops by to help. 

Notice how words like *окуляри*, *ножиці*, *гроші*, and **двері** (door) dictate the forms of the surrounding words. We will look at how to form the **родовий відмінок** (genitive case) for these tricky nouns in the next section, but for now, focus on mastering their basic Nominative agreement.

> — **Сусід:** Привіт! Тобі допомогти з речами? *(Hi! Do you need help with your things?)*
> — **Мешканець:** Привіт! Так, дякую. Де мої **окуляри**? Вони лежали на столі. *(Hi! Yes, thank you. Where are my glasses? They were lying on the table.)*
> — **Сусід:** Я поклав їх у цю коробку. А де **ножиці**? Мені треба розрізати скотч. *(I put them in this box. And where are the scissors? I need to cut the tape.)*
> — **Мешканець:** Вони у тій великій коробці, де **меблі**. Обережно, там ще старі мамині **парфуми**. *(They are in that big box where the furniture is. Careful, my mom's old perfume is there too.)*
> — **Сусід:** Зрозумів. А що робити з цим конвертом? *(Understood. And what should I do with this envelope?)*
> — **Мешканець:** Там **гроші**. Поклади їх у мій рюкзак, будь ласка. *(There is money there. Put it in my backpack, please.)*
> — **Сусід:** Добре. Ми все зібрали? *(Good. Have we gathered everything?)*
> — **Мешканець:** Здається, так. Перевір, чи зачинені **двері**. *(I think so. Check if the door is locked.)*
> — **Сусід:** Так, **двері** зачинені на ключ. Можемо їхати. *(Yes, the door is locked with a key. We can go.)*
