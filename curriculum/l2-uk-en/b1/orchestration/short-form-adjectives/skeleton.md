## Повні та короткі форми прикметників (~800 words)
- P1 (~150 words): Introduction to the concept of adjective length in Ukrainian. Define "Short Adjectives" (короткі прикметники) as truncated qualitative forms like *зелен* or *ясен*, contrasting them with the standard "Full Contracted" forms (*зелений*, *ясний*) that dominate modern speech.
- P2 (~200 words): Morphological breakdown of the short form's lack of case endings. Explain that while *зелений* follows a full paradigm of cases, genders, and numbers (*зеленого*, *зеленій*), the short form *зелен* is frozen in the nominative case and only exists in specific gender/number variations (mostly masculine singular).
- P3 (~150 words): Historical context of these forms as remnants of the Old East Slavic nominal declension (коротка відміна). Explain how, over centuries, the "full" pronominal forms pushed out the short forms, leaving them as stylistic relics or specialized predicative tools.
- P4 (~300 words): Decolonization focus: Contrast with Russian grammar. Explain that in Russian, short forms are a productive and often mandatory category for predicates (*он болен*, *книга интересна*), whereas in Ukrainian, the full form is the universal standard for both description and predicates (*він хворий*, *книжка цікава*). Emphasize that Ukrainian short forms are a "closed group" and creating new ones (*цікав*, *гарн*) is a common L2 error (calque).

## Які прикметники мають короткі форми? (~800 words)
- P1 (~150 words): The Golden Rule: Only *якісні* (qualitative) adjectives can form short versions. Explain why relative (*залізний*, *цегляний*) and possessive (*мамин*) adjectives cannot have these forms, providing examples of why *дерев'ян* is impossible.
- P2 (~200 words): Categorizing the "Poetic/Folk List." List and define the traditional short forms found in literature: *зелен*, *ясен*, *повен*, *дрібен*, *білен*, *чорен*. Explain their emotional value—how they make a description feel more dynamic and less static.
- P3 (~250 words): Categorizing the "Active Modern List." Focus on forms used in standard modern Ukrainian: *потрібен* (needed), *певен* (certain), *годен* (fit), *ладен* (willing), *варт* (worth), *рад* (glad), *жив* (alive). Explain that these are not archaic but are essential for expressing states and needs.
- P4 (~200 words): Morphological behavior of the modern list. Explain how *потрібен* (masc) changes to *потрібна* (fem), *потрібне* (neut), and *потрібні* (plural). Note that while they lack the *-ий* ending in the masculine, they still agree with the subject in gender and number.

## Короткі форми у сучасній мові (~700 words)
- P1 (~200 words): Detailed breakdown of *потрібен* in the predicative position. Explain the syntax: *Мені потрібен час* (I need time) where *потрібен* is the predicate agreeing with *час*. Contrast this with the English "I need" (verb-based) to show how Ukrainian uses an adjective-based construction for necessity.
- P2 (~200 words): The nuances of *певен* and *певна*. Explain the construction *Я певен, що...* (I'm certain that...). Discuss why *певен* feels more decisive and assertive in speech than the full form *певний*, which often feels more descriptive (e.g., *певна кількість* - a certain amount).
- P3 (~150 words): Semantic roles of *годен* and *ладен*. Explain their combination with infinitives: *Він годен це зробити* (He is capable of doing this) and *Вона ладна допомогти* (She is willing to help). Mention that *годен* can also mean "fit" or "worthy" in some dialects.
- P4 (~150 words): Usage of *варт* and *рад*. Explain that *Це варто побачити* is one of the most common ways to recommend something. Clarify that *рад/рада* is restricted to personal feelings of being glad/pleased, usually in greeting contexts like *Рад знайомству*.
