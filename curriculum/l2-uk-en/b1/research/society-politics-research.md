## Cultural Hooks

**Hook 1 — Конституція Пилипа Орлика (1710)**
Source: Ukrainian Wikipedia — «Конституція Пилипа Орлика»

- Full name: «Договори і Постановлення Прав і вольностей Війська Запорозького»
- Signed: 5 (16) April 1710, Bender (now Moldova), between Hetman Pylyp Orlyk, Cossack elders, and Zaporozhians
- Comprises a preamble + 16 articles
- Established: legislative power (Генеральна Рада acting as parliament, meeting 3× per year), executive power (Hetman with limited authority — could not control treasury, lands, or conduct independent foreign policy), protection of rights and freedoms
- Significance: Recognized as a landmark of Ukrainian and European constitutionalism of the 18th century — predating Montesquieu's formal articulation of separation of powers (1748)
- **Decolonized framing:** This document emerges from within Ukrainian political tradition (Cossack statehood), not as a reaction to Russian/Soviet rule. It demonstrates Ukraine's long-standing democratic and constitutional traditions operating independently of Russia.

**Hook 2 — «Українська хвиля» / Живий ланцюг (21 January 1990)**
Source: Ukrainian Wikipedia — «Живий ланцюг», section «Українська хвиля»

- Date: Sunday, 21 January 1990 — 71st anniversary of the Act of Zluka (unification of UNR and ZUNR)
- Scale: millions of people joined hands from Ivano-Frankivsk (via Lviv) to Kyiv — chain length over 770 km
- One of the largest human chain events in world history
- Inspired by the Baltic Way (1989)
- Today commemorated annually on Соборності день (January 22)
- **Decolonized framing:** Frame as an expression of соборність — a distinctly Ukrainian concept of national unity and wholeness — not merely as a protest against Soviet rule. The chain connected the historically western (ZUNR) and eastern (UNR) Ukrainian lands, symbolizing the unity of a people who had long maintained their own political aspirations.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Громадський vs. Громадянський**
→ *Correct use:* «громадський транспорт» (public transport), «громадянське суспільство» (civil society)
→ *Error:* Using «громадянський транспорт» or «громадський суспільство»
→ *Why it happens:* Both share the root «громада» and look/sound similar. English speakers see "civic/civil/public" as near-synonyms and translate loosely. Key drill: громадський = relating to public space/infrastructure; громадянський = relating to citizenship, rights, civil law.

2. **Цивільний vs. Громадянський (false calque from Russian)**
→ *Correct use:* «цивільний шлюб» / «Цивільний кодекс» (civil marriage, civil code — legal domain)
→ *Error:* «громадянський шлюб» (Russian calque: «гражданский брак»)
→ *Why it happens:* Calque from Russian, where «гражданский» covers both civic AND civil law registers. In Ukrainian, цивільний handles the legal/civil sense, while громадянський stays in the civic/rights domain.

3. **Політика vs. Політик (stress and register confusion)**
→ *Correct use:* «Політика — це мистецтво можливого» (politics/policy); «відомий політик» (a politician)
→ *Error:* Using «політика» to mean "a politician" or mispronouncing stress: поліТИка (noun, f.) vs. поліТИк (noun, m.)
→ *Why it happens:* English "politics" and "politician" don't share the exact same root in daily use, while Ukrainian makes both from the same root with a gender/suffix change. The stress pattern is identical (поліТИ-), which does not help disambiguation — the final syllable does the work.

4. **Verbal noun collocations: прийняти vs. ухвалити закон**
→ *Correct:* «ухвалити закон» (parliament adopts a law — formal legislative register)
→ *Common substitution:* «прийняти закон» (also correct but less precise in formal discourse; more neutral register)
→ *Why it matters:* Textbook source (Grade 10, Glazova) and Grade 11 history materials consistently use «ухвалити» for formal legislative acts. B1 learners from the news/media module (b1-70) need to know this distinction for authentic comprehension of news Ukrainian.

## Notes for Content Writing

1. **Соборність framing:** The Живий ланцюг hook should introduce the word «соборність» — a high-value Ukrainian cultural concept that B1 learners will encounter in political and cultural discourse. Define it inline: «єдність і цілісність українського народу та його земель».

2. **Three branches of power:** Ukrainian uses a clear triad — «законодавча влада» (Верховна Рада), «виконавча влада» (Кабінет Міністрів + Президент), «судова влада» (суди) — these labels are high-frequency in news and should be presented as a set.

3. **Register contrast section:** The plan calls for formal news vs. «kitchen table» contrast. Concrete pairs to demonstrate: «ухвалити закон» (formal) / «прийняти закон» (neutral); «формування уряду» (news) / «новий уряд» (casual); «здійснювати право голосу» (formal) / «іти голосувати» (casual).

4. **Verbal noun pattern:** The plan's grammar focus (noun collocations, deverbal nouns) aligns with §4.3.4 (B1 deverbal nouns -ння/-ення). Use «формування», «прийняття», «голосування», «порушення» as examples — all high-frequency in political discourse and all VESUM-verified.

5. **Anti-Russianism check for content phase:** Watch for potential Russianisms in political vocabulary — «получати владу» → «отримувати владу»/«здобувати владу»; «кошти» vs. «гроші» (not relevant here); «звертатися» is fine. Main risk: calques on «власть» (use «влада»), «законодательство» (use «законодавство»).

6. **Word target:** 4000 words. Recommend overshoot to 4500–5000 to pass gate. The six sections have targets ranging from 200 to 900 words — the intro and summary are tight; all weight is in the four core sections. Consider expanding the law/legislation section slightly (plan: 700) to include more example sentences with the legal collocations.
