## 2. Vocabulary frequency

Since this is a grammar checkpoint, the core vocabulary consists of **grammatical terminology** and **high-frequency verbs** used to demonstrate these patterns.

### High-Frequency Verbs (for morphology drills)
*   **Читати / Прочитати** (read) → *читаючи* (reading), *прочитавши* (having read), *прочитаний* (read - passive).
*   **Писати / Написати** (write) → *пишучи*, *написавши*, *написаний*, *написано*.
*   **Робити / Зробити** (do/make) → *роблячи*, *зробивши*, *зроблений*, *зроблено*.
*   **Відкривати / Відкрити** (open) → *відкритий*, *відкрито* (common in signs).

### Diminutives (High Frequency in Speech)
*   **Мама** → *Матуся, мамуся, мамця* (top frequency).
*   **Кава** → *Кавка*.
*   **Вода** → *Водичка*.
*   **Сонце** → *Сонечко*.
*   **Трохи** → *Трішки*.

### Collective Numerals
*   **Двоє, троє** (very high frequency).
*   **Четверо, п'ятеро** (medium frequency).
*   **Шестеро+** (lower frequency).

## 3. Cultural hook

### The "Polite" Diminutive
Ukrainian culture ("Mentalitet") relies heavily on diminutives to soften requests and show intimacy. Unlike English, where "little water" sounds specific to size, **"водичка"** implies politeness and warmth. It is ubiquitous in service industries (waiters asking *"Вам кавку чи чайок?"*).
*   **Verified Fact**: The Ukrainian anthem refers to enemies as **"воріженьки"** (little enemies/dear enemies), a unique stylistic choice that diminishes the threat through irony or condescension rather than aggression.

### The "Bureaucratic" Passive
The impersonal passive forms ending in **-но/-то** (e.g., *вирішено*, *зроблено*, *заборонено*) are the hallmark of official notices and bureaucratic language in Ukraine. Seeing a sign *"Вхід заборонено"* (Entry forbidden) is a daily visual experience. This contrasts with the active voice preferred in folk songs and casual speech.

## 4. Pedagogical notes

### Key Differences from English
1.  **Gerund Confusion**: English "-ing" covers both *reading* (noun) and *reading* (adverbial). Ukrainian strictly separates them: *читання* (noun/process) vs. *читаючи* (while reading).
2.  **Sequence of Actions**: Learners struggle to distinguish **Simultaneous** (*читаючи* - while reading) vs. **Sequential** (*прочитавши* - having read). English speakers often overuse the present participle.
3.  **Active Participles Avoidance**: Modern standard Ukrainian discourages forms like *читаючий хлопець* (the reading boy), considering them artificial (calques from Russian). The pedagogical preference is to teach **"той, що читає"** (relative clause) as the primary spoken form, treating active participles as passive recognition vocabulary for literature.

### Common Learner Errors
*   Using *двоє* with feminine nouns (e.g., *двоє дівчат* - technically acceptable but rare/colloquial; *дві дівчини* is standard).
*   Using *-ючи* forms for completed actions (*приходячи додому, він з'їв вечерю* - Wrong! Should be *прийшовши*).
*   Overusing diminutives in formal business contexts where they sound childish.

## 5. Scope boundaries

### IN Scope (B1 Level)
*   **Adverbial Participles**: Formation and basic usage for subject-consistent actions (*Я йшов, співаючи*).
*   **Passive Participles**: Focus on Past Passive (*зроблений*) and Impersonal (*зроблено*).
*   **Collective Numerals**: *Двоє, троє, четверо* + usage with male persons, children, and plural-only nouns (*двоє дверей*).
*   **Diminutives**: Recognition of suffixes and production of common forms (*-к-, -ок, -ець*).

### OUT Scope (Reserved for B2/C1)
*   **Active Present Participles**: *Квітучий, знаючий* (teach as adjectives, not productive grammar).
*   **Complex Syntax**: Dangling participles correction (stylistics).
*   **Archaic/Poetic forms**: Short forms of adjectives.
*   **Rare Collectives**: *Обоє, обидва* (can be mentioned but not drilled).
*   **Declension of Fractions**: Deep drill of fractional cases (intro only).
