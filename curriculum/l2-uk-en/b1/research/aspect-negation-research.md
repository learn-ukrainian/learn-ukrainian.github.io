## Cultural Hooks

1. **Ukrainian hospitality and the ritual of refusal** — Ukrainian folk tradition distinguishes *ввічлива відмова* (polite refusal) from *справжня відмова* (genuine refusal). Hosts typically offer food/drink 2–3 times before accepting a guest's refusal; a first "ні" is often understood as politeness, not finality. This maps directly to the lesson's dialogue: the learner must understand that "Дякую, я не голодний" + "Я взагалі не їм так пізно" uses imperfective (general fact) to make the refusal sound culturally natural. Source: folk hospitality patterns documented in ethnographic sources on Ukrainian customs.

2. **Double negation as a Ukrainian grammatical norm** — Ukrainian requires grammatical double negation (ніколи не, ніхто не, нікуди не), unlike some other European languages where negative pronouns cancel each other. This is a defining structural feature of Ukrainian, attested across all periods of the written language and fully codified in the State Standard §4.2.1.4 (negative pronouns: ніхто, ніщо). The construction appears regularly in proverbs: *Ніколи не кажи «ніколи»* — a recognizable cultural hook that also demonstrates the double-negation rule in action.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Perfective with general negation** → *Я не прочитав цієї теми* (intended: "I haven't studied this topic at all") should be **Я не читав цієї теми** — happens because learners extend the "perfective = completed" logic into negative contexts where no completion was expected; the imperfective is needed for undirected non-action.

2. **Dropping не with negative pronouns** → *Я ніколи бачив таке* should be **Я ніколи не бачив такого** — L1 English interference ("I never saw such a thing" has single negation); Ukrainian grammatically obligates double negation, and learners omit the verbal не.

3. **Accusative instead of Genitive after не** → *Я не бачив книгу/друга* should be **Я не бачив книги/друга** — the Genitive trigger at §4.2.2.2.1 requires case shift on the direct object; learners default to Accusative by analogy with affirmative sentences.

4. **Imperfective with ще не** → *Я ще не читав цю книгу* (ambiguous) vs. the expected **Я ще не прочитав цієї книги** — "ще не" semantically encodes an expected-but-incomplete result, which selects for perfective; learners miss the expectation component and default to imperfective.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **The Genitive trigger is the syntactic anchor**: §4.2.2.2.1 gives just one line in the Standard; Phase B must develop the full morphological mechanics. Every negative sentence example should model Genitive on the direct object — learners see it in every example, not just in the dedicated section.

- **Flowchart pedagogy**: The plan's decision algorithm (Is it general negation? Is it ще не? Is it a command?) is pedagogically strong. Phase B should make this visual — a numbered sequence of yes/no questions, not a prose paragraph.

- **Double negation framing**: Frame as a Ukrainian structural feature, not a "quirk" or contrast to another language. "Українська мова вимагає подвійного заперечення — це норма, а не виняток."

- **Aspect asymmetry in negation**: Affirmative sentences favor perfective for completed events; negative sentences favor *imperfective* for the same events (Я прочитав книгу / Я не читав книги). This reversal is the core conceptual challenge — Phase B should name it explicitly as *видова асиметрія в запереченні*.

- **Contrastive minimal pairs**: The Я не читав / Я не прочитав contrast needs its own dedicated section with semantic glosses — "fact of non-action" vs. "failed attempt / missed result." These are genuinely different communicative acts in Ukrainian.

- **Avramenko Grade 11 textbook** provides imperative examples with negation (не ходіть, не залишайте, не залишайте — imperfective for ongoing prohibition). Grade 11 Voron textbook explicitly states "прямі додатки вживаються у родовому відмінку при запереченні" — this can be cited as textbook backing.

- **Register note**: "На жаль, ні" and "Я б з радістю, але..." are conversational softeners operating at a neutral-to-formal register boundary — appropriate for B1.1 immersion contexts. Phase B should introduce these in a dialogue before explaining the grammar behind them.
