## Cultural Hooks

1. **Антиканцелярит як деколонізація мови (post-2014)** — Wikipedia confirms: «Канцелярит — особлива мова (жаргон) канцелярських працівників, що представляє собою спотворену модифікацію літературної мови, що базується на канцеляризмах.» The anti-cancelyarit campaign gained major momentum after 2014 as part of broader Ukrainian language decolonization efforts, targeting Soviet-era bureaucratic syntax calques — including active present participles used as nominal constructions — that entered Ukrainian via Russian administrative language. Organizations like «Мово! Мово!» and language councils popularized accessible guides to natural Ukrainian alternatives.

2. **Народна мудрість і лексикалізовані форми** — The proverb «Не бійся гостя сидячого, а бійся стоячого» (attested in Ukrainian folklore collections) demonstrates that сидячий/стоячий are fully lexicalized; no living speaker perceives them as participial. This is pedagogically powerful: the same suffix that marks a "forbidden" present active participle in official prose is invisible in everyday proverb usage — because lexicalization erases the form's verbal origin.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Gender/number/case agreement failure** → *палаючий очі* instead of *палаючі очі* — Why: learners treat participles as uninflected modifiers (L1 English transfer; English "burning eyes" has no agreement). Agreement is shared with adjective paradigm but learners underextend.

2. **Using active present participles as nominal heads** → *бажаючі прийти* instead of *охочі прийти* / *ті, хто хоче прийти* — Why: calque from bureaucratic administrative Ukrainian that absorbed Soviet-era Russian syntax. Forms like *бажаючий, завідуючий, головуючий* are widespread in pre-2014 administrative texts and learners encounter them passively.

3. **Producing Russian-pattern active past participles with -ш-** → *сказавший, привівша* instead of *той, що сказав; та, що привела* — Why: direct Russian morphology transfer. This error also appears at C1 level as a persistent hypercorrection in formal writing. Grade 11 Glazova calls it a «грубе порушення мовної норми».

## Notes for Content Writing

- The VESUM non-registration of **завідуючий** is a strong pedagogical anchor: "the dictionary itself rejects this form" — use this as authoritative evidence.
- **палаючий** not being in VESUM as a lexicalized adjective lemma means it must be framed carefully in content: acknowledge it appears in poetic/literary texts (*палаючі очі* in Shevchenko-influenced register) but should never be used in official or everyday prose. Do NOT present it as fully normative.
- The Grade 7 NUS textbooks (Avramenko, Litvinova) already teach the replacement strategy — this is established curriculum knowledge by B1, so frame the B1 module as systematizing and deepening what learners encountered at a lower level, adding the stylistic/decolonization dimension that secondary school treats lightly.
- The anti-cancelyarit frame is pedagogically strong and historically grounded — do not soften it. This is not "preference" but normative correctness per Ukraine's language culture post-2014.
- Section on «Боротьба з канцеляритом» has the most culturally loaded content — ensure examples are verified (all the Zabolotnyi table entries are safe: *мийний, охочі, панівне, розбурхане, омолоджувальний* all exist in VESUM from the earlier search).
- Lexicalized "safe list" (співучий, квітучий, родючий, сидячий, стоячий, пекучий) — all VESUM-confirmed; frame as "these have crossed the line from participle to adjective — the language absorbed them fully."
- Word target: 4000 words. With 5 sections (600+900+1100+900+500), the content is well-distributed. The канцелярит section at 1,100 words is the richest — ensure it includes the full normative table, transformation drills, and decolonization framing.
