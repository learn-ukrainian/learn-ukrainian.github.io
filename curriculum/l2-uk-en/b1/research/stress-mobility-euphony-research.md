## Cultural Hooks
1. Ukrainian mobile stress is a deeply archaic feature that preserves traces of the Proto-Slavic pitch accent system. Unlike languages with fixed stress (like Polish, where stress is almost always penultimate), Ukrainian stress is a dynamic morphological tool that can change the entire meaning of a word (зáмок/замóк) or indicate a shift in grammatical case or number.
2. "Милозвучність" (euphony) is not just a grammatical rule but a core aesthetic philosophy of the Ukrainian language. It actively avoids consonant clusters to maintain a rhythmic, flowing, and musical cadence, heavily relying on the alternation of vowels and consonants (у/в, і/й, з/зі/із) depending on the phonetic environment.

## Common Learner Errors
1. *рóблю instead of роблю́ (and similar verbs like ходжу́, пишу́) — Learners often default to stem stress in the 1st person singular, failing to apply the stress shift that occurs in specific verb conjugations.
2. *він в школі instead of він у школі — Failing to apply euphony rules at word boundaries, especially after a consonant and before a consonant cluster, leading to unnatural stress clashes and difficult pronunciation.
3. *рукú instead of рýки (Nominative plural) — Overgeneralizing end-stress in nouns, failing to recognize the predictable end-to-root stress shift in the plural forms of many feminine nouns.

## Notes for Content Writing
- Frame the concept of mobile stress not as a set of random exceptions, but as a systematic, predictable morphological engine.
- Avoid comparing Ukrainian stress patterns to Russian. If comparisons are needed to highlight the uniqueness of mobile stress, contrast it with fixed-stress languages like Polish, French, or Czech.
- Ensure that the "Advanced Euphony" section clearly distinguishes between strict literary norms and acceptable conversational variations, as register sensitivity is a key B1/B2 skill.
- Provide clear, visually distinct markings for stressed vowels (e.g., using the acute accent ´) in all examples to reinforce visual memory.
