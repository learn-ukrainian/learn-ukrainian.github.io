# Research: How to Talk About Grammar (Metalinguistic Competence)

**Module:** B1 M01 "Як говорити про граматику"  
**Level:** B1 (Bridge from A2)  
**Topic:** Metalinguistic Terminology & Language Awareness

## 1. Grammar: State Standard 2024 Reference

The **State Standard of Ukrainian as a Foreign Language (2024)** explicitly requires metalinguistic competence at the B1 level, enabling learners to discuss language phenomena, understand instructions, and ask for clarification using appropriate terminology.

> **Section: Linguistic Competence / Grammatical Subsystem**
> The standard mandates that a learner at the B1 level must:
> *   "Understand and use basic grammatical terminology to facilitate the learning process."
> *   "Identify and name parts of speech (*частини мови*) and grammatical categories (*рід, число, відмінок*) in standard texts."
> *   "Monitor their own speech for basic grammatical accuracy (*самоконтроль*)."

*Note:* While A1/A2 focuses on *using* the forms, B1 requires the ability to *name* and *analyze* them (Language Awareness).

## 3. Cultural Hook

**The "Culture of Speech" (Культура мови):**
In Ukraine, correct grammar and stress are highly valued and often seen as a marker of education and patriotism, especially in the context of switching from Russian to Ukrainian.
*   **Fact:** There is a popular genre of "Express Ukrainian" lessons (e.g., by Oleksandr Avramenko) on TV and social media where grammatical nuances are explained to the general public. Knowing the "names" of cases and parts of speech allows a learner to participate in this shared cultural striving for linguistic perfection (*вдосконалення мови*).
*   **Fact:** Unlike English speakers who rarely discuss "subjunctive mood" in daily life, Ukrainians frequently discuss *наголос* (stress) and *закінчення* (endings) because the highly inflected nature of the language requires constant attention to form.

## 4. Pedagogical Notes

### Differences from English
*   **Conceptual Gap:** English native speakers often do not know what a "direct object" or "dative case" is even in English.
    *   *Teaching Strategy:* Do not assume they know the *concept*. Re-explain the concept briefly when introducing the Ukrainian label (e.g., "Знахідний implies finding or seeing something -> Accusative").
*   **Naming Convention:** English learners usually learn "Case 1, Case 2... Case 7". Ukrainians learn them by name (*Називний, Родовий...*).
    *   *Goal:* Transition the student from "Genitive" to "*Родовий*" to enable full immersion.
*   **"Article" Confusion:** Students often ask for the word for "the/a". We must explain that *артикль* does not exist in Ukrainian structure, though the word exists to describe English grammar.

### Common Learner Errors
*   Confusing **Буква** (Letter) and **Звук** (Sound).
*   Using *слово* when they mean *речення* (sentence).
*   Mixing up case names (e.g., calling Genitive "Possessive").

## 5. Scope Boundaries

**IN SCOPE (Module B1.01):**
*   **Names of Parts of Speech:** Іменник, дієслово, прикметник, займенник, числівник.
*   **Names of 7 Cases:** Називний through Кличний (just the labels and recognition).
*   **Basic Structure:** Слово, речення, літера, звук.
*   **Gender Labels:** Чоловічий, жіночий, середній.
*   **Number Labels:** Однина, множина.

**OUT OF SCOPE (Save for later B1/B2):**
*   **Complex Syntax Terms:** *Підмет* (Subject), *Присудок* (Predicate), *Додаток* (Object) — stick to "noun/verb" for now to avoid overload.
*   **Advanced Morphology:** *Дієприкметник* (Participle), *Дієприслівник* (Gerund) — these are taught later in B1.
*   **Stylistics:** *Сленг, діалектизм* — too early for deep analysis.
*   **Linguistic Theory:** Deep classifications of declension groups (I, II, III, IV) — teach patterns, but don't force memorization of "Second Declension Hard Group" labels yet.
