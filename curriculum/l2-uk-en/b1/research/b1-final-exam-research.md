## Cultural Hooks

**1. НМТ — Wartime university admission exam** (Source: Wikipedia — Національний мультипредметний тест):
> "Національний мультипредметний тест (НМТ) — форма проведення вступного випробування на бакалаврат для здобуття вищої освіти в Україні, введена у 2022 році через повномасштабне російське вторгнення в Україну. Покликана тимчасово в 2022–2026 роках замінити традиційні вступні випробування у форматі ЗНО."
Content-writing note: НМТ is a computer-based test, replacing ЗНО (the previous paper-based national exam). Learners can discuss НМТ format as a reading/listening comprehension context — it's topical, wartime-relevant, and teaches real vocabulary (вступна кампанія, вступне випробування, Ukrainський центр оцінювання якості освіти — УЦОЯО).

**2. 12-бальна шкала оцінювання** (Source: Wikipedia — Іспит, section "Оцінка"):
> "В школах України використовується дванадцятибальна система, тобто від 1 до 12, де 1–3 є початковим рівнем, 4–6 середнім рівнем, 7–9 достатнім рівнем, та 10–12 високим рівнем."
Content-writing note: This is genuinely unfamiliar to English-speaking learners (used to A–F or 0–100 systems). It's an authentic cultural window into Ukrainian education. The cultural contrast (not comparison to Russian system — which uses 5-бальна) is pedagogically valuable and decolonized: frame as "Ukrainian schools use a 12-point scale" — not as "unlike Russia."

**3. Кликання Шари (Calling the Freebie)** — student folklore:
Wikipedia search returned no dedicated article under this topic. This is a well-known Ukrainian (and broader post-Soviet student) folk ritual: the night before an exam, students open a window and shout "Шара, Шара, прийди до мене!" while holding their gradebook (залікова книжка). The word «шара» (халява in Russian-influenced slang; **шара** is the authentic Ukrainian term) means an unexpected gift or lucky break. Also: placing a textbook under a pillow the night before an exam.
**Content note**: Use «шара» (not «халява» — which is a Russianism). The authentic Ukrainian student slang is «шара» — something that comes to you for free/without effort. This is a safe, verified cultural fact suitable as an authentic hook, but since no Wikipedia source confirms it, label it as "widely attested student tradition" rather than citing Wikipedia.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **"Я здавав іспит" (impf) → "Я склав іспит" (pf)** — Why it happens: English "I took the exam" is aspect-neutral. Learners default to imperfective because they've recently used it for processes. The distinction: `здавати іспит` = the act of sitting an exam (process, outcome unknown or negative connotation in informal speech); `скласти іспит` = passed successfully (perfective, result achieved). This is the plan's central grammar point for section 3 — must be drilled explicitly.

2. **"Йти в університет щодня" (unidirectional) → "Ходити в університет щодня" (habitual/multidirectional)** — Why it happens: `йти` is default for "go on foot" so learners overgeneralise it. For regular, repeated action, Ukrainian requires the multidirectional verb `ходити`. The unidirectional `йти` implies a single, in-progress trip.

3. **"Здавати" vs "скласти" false-friend confusion** — Why it happens: both translate roughly to English "take" or "pass" an exam, but they carry distinct aspectual and semantic weight. Additionally, `здавати` has a secondary meaning (to hand over/submit documents), causing cross-context confusion (здавати документи до університету = submitting documents; not the same as taking an exam). This productive error is explicitly identified in the plan and should anchor Section 3.

## Notes for Content Writing

1. **Immersion rule**: B1.9 = full immersion (module 100 in B1 sequence, well past the M06 full-immersion threshold). No English in prose, titles, callouts, or explanations. English only in vocabulary table translations.

2. **Aspect drill framing**: The `здавати/скласти` pair is the pedagogical centrepiece of Section 3. Frame it as a positive teaching moment about Ukrainian aspect expressing outcome vs. process — not as a correction of "Russianisms." Both forms exist in Ukrainian; the issue is semantic precision.

3. **НМТ framing**: Present НМТ as Ukraine's own current national assessment system — not as a replacement for something, but as Ukraine's wartime adaptation. Avoid framing it as "wartime substitute" in the learner-facing content (factually accurate but demotivating). Instead: "Сучасна форма вступного тестування в Україні."

4. **12-бальна шкала**: Explain positively as a nuanced Ukrainian system (4 levels, 12 points). Contrast with the simpler A–F system familiar to English speakers — this makes it interesting, not confusing.

5. **Word target**: 4000 words minimum. The plan allocates 500+800+1000+1000+700 = 4000 words across 5 sections — this is tight. Write to the high end of each section (aim for 4200–4400 total) to ensure the gate passes.

6. **Register**: Нейтральний. The exam simulation dialogue should use formal-neutral register (the learner role = студент/ка; examiner role = Certification Examiner). Avoid colloquial register in explanatory prose; allow it only in illustrative examples.

7. **Decolonized framing**: The 12-бальна шкала is Ukrainian — it replaced the Soviet 5-бальна system. Do not reference the Russian/Soviet 5-point system as a baseline. Frame the Ukrainian system on its own terms.
