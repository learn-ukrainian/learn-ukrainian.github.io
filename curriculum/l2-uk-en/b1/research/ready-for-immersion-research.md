## Cultural Hooks

**1. The Purge of Ґ (1933):**
- The 1928 Kharkiv orthography (правопис Скрипника/скрипниківка) included Ґ fully — Borys Hrinchenko's 1907–1909 dictionary documented ~300 words with Ґ; it appeared in the Peresopnytsia Gospel (16th c.) and Smotrytsky's Grammar (1619).
- **In 1933**, the Soviet-imposed "reform" in Kharkiv explicitly removed Ґ from the Ukrainian alphabet, beginning *"процес штучного зближення української та російської мовних традицій ортографії"* (Wikipedia: Український правопис 1933 року).
- **Restored 1990**: Third edition of Ukrainian orthography reinstated Ґ after independence movements.
- **Framing**: This is not a minor spelling quirk — it was a deliberate act of linguistic erasure. The letter's restoration is a direct index of Ukrainian sovereignty. Use the 1928→1933→1990 arc to show the letter as a *barometer of freedom*.

**2. The Proverb «Слово — не горобець, вилетить — не спіймаєш»:**
- Verified Ukrainian proverb (no Russian origin). GRAC corpus confirms standard usage. Its formal structure (антитеза + умова) makes it excellent for demonstrating sentence-member analysis: підмет (слово), присудок (вилетить), означення implicit.
- Pedagogical use: anchors precision theme *and* doubles as a sentence parsing exercise.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Буква vs. Звук** — The letter «я» represents 1 graphic symbol but 2 phonemes [j]+[a] at word-start or after a vowel. English learners conflate orthographic and phonological representation because English spelling is inconsistent in the opposite direction. Correction: the graphic unit (буква) is not the acoustic unit (звук). Drill: «їжак» = 5 букв, 6 звуків.

2. **Стрес-зміна значення: плáчу vs. плачý** — пла́чу = "I am crying" (плакати), плачу́ = "I am paying" (платити). Both are valid Ukrainian verb forms; stress is the only disambiguator. English speakers don't encounter lexical stress as a disambiguator, so this surprises them. Drills: read sentence aloud → stress determines meaning → use context to verify. Broader category: **рýки (nom. pl.) vs. руки́ (gen. sg.)**.

3. **Правило vs. Право** — Learners coming from Romance-language exposure may say *"У мене є правило дізнатися більше"* when meaning *"I have the right to know more"* (У мене є право). In Ukrainian, **правило** = rule/convention; **право** = legal right/entitlement. The words share a Slavic root (праведний) but diverge sharply in function. False friend reinforced by partial similarity to Italian *regola* vs. *diritto*.

## Notes for Content Writing

1. **The "airlock" metaphor** (plan term) is apt and should be used explicitly in Section 1 — frame the English safety net removal as a deliberate pedagogical design, not an arbitrary rule. Supportive tone: the learner *has already learned* enough to proceed.
2. **Parts of speech tree**: Textbook evidence (Grade 6 Litvinova 2023, Grade 7 Avramenko 2024, Grade 8 Avramenko/Zabolotnyi) consistently organizes parts of speech as самостійні (meaningful, answer questions, are sentence members) vs. службові (no lexical meaning, no questions, not sentence members). Use this exact binary for the "Tree of Speech" analogy — roots = самостійні, branches/connectors = службові.
3. **Sentence members**: Grade 11 Voron 2019 and Grade 5 Zabolotnyi 2023 confirm: підмет = subject (хто? що?), присудок = predicate (що робить?). Second-level members: означення (яке питання? — attribute), додаток (кому? чого? — object), обставина (де? коли? як? — adverbial). Use consistent question-word anchors — these are the textbook standard.
4. **Register distinction**: §4.5.1 lists stylistic markers (синоніми, антоніми, епітети, метафори, еліпсис). For this module, contrast the *instructional* register (imperative verbs: визначте, доберіть, складіть — from Zabolotnyi/Avramenko exercises) with the *conversational* register learners used in A1–A2.
5. **Decolonized framing for Ґ**: Do not frame the 1933 purge as "Soviet influence on Ukrainian" — frame it as *deliberate suppression of Ukrainian orthographic identity* followed by *restoration during the independence movement*. The letter Ґ is a marker of Ukrainian linguistic sovereignty, present in Ukrainian texts since the 16th century — predating any Soviet intervention.
6. **Fill-in-the-blank target**: The plan specifies 15+ items for the "Real Test" fill-in activity. Source vocabulary from §4.1.5 abbreviations (div., por., napr.) and the parts-of-speech/sentence-members metalanguage already drilled in the module. This ensures the activity tests cumulative module content, not isolated recall.
7. **Word budget watch**: Section 4 (Стилістичні реєстри та граматичні тексти) has the highest allocation at 800 words and the most complex task — an extended authentic Ukrainian grammar passage with comprehension. This section should contain the longest demonstration text (≥250 words in Ukrainian instructional prose).
