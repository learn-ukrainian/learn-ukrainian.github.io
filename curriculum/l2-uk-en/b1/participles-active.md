## Що таке дієприкметник? (~650 words total)

Welcome to the world of participles! In Ukrainian grammar, a **дієприкметник** (participle) is a unique, hybrid part of speech. According to the standard middle school curriculum, such as Litvinova's seventh-grade textbook, it perfectly combines the core features of two completely different word classes. From a verb, it inherits the fundamental idea of an action or a state, along with the grammatical categories of tense and aspect. From an adjective, it takes the syntactic ability to agree with a noun in gender, number, and case. To understand this hybrid nature conceptually, think of a base verb like «квітнути» (to bloom). This verb simply tells us what the subject is doing. But when we transform that verb into the active participle **квітучий** (blooming), it acts exactly like an adjective describing the subject, while proudly retaining its core action. 

Українська мова має дуже чітку структуру для цієї частини мови. Дієприкметник — це особлива форма дієслова, яка означає ознаку предмета за дією або станом. Він завжди відповідає на типові питання прикметника: «який?», «яка?», «яке?» або «які?».

> *The Ukrainian language has a very clear structure for this part of speech. The participle is a special verb form that signifies an attribute of an object based on an action or state. It always answers the typical questions of an adjective: "which?", "what kind of?".*

To truly grasp how a **дієприкметник** functions, we must look at the philosophical difference between permanent qualities and qualities that arise directly from an action. Let us compare two distinct phrases describing a garden. If we say «красивий сад» (a beautiful garden), we are using a standard, everyday adjective. The beauty of the garden here is a static, permanent characteristic. It does not tell us what the garden is doing at this exact moment. However, if we change our phrasing and say «квітучий сад» (a blooming garden), we are now using a participle derived from the active process of blooming. The garden is actively performing an action, and we are using that very action to describe it dynamically. As defined in Zabolotnyi's eighth-grade grammar textbook on page fifty-one, a participle expresses an «ознака за дією» (attribute by action). 

:::info
**Grammar box**
Always remember the core difference: a regular adjective describes *what* something is generally like (a static quality). A participle describes what something is actively *doing* or what was directly *done* to it (a dynamic quality).
:::

Participles in the Ukrainian language are logically divided into two main categories, based entirely on who or what is performing the action. These categories are the **активний дієприкметник** (active participle) and the **пасивний дієприкметник** (passive participle). An active participle is used exclusively when the subject of the phrase is performing the action itself. For example, in the phrase «палаючий вогонь» (a blazing fire), the fire itself is doing the physical burning. It is the active agent of the verb's meaning. Conversely, a passive participle is required when the subject merely receives the action from someone or something else. In the phrase «прочитана книга» (a read book), the book did not read itself; someone else performed the action of reading upon it. This specific module will focus exclusively on the active scope. We will learn how to describe subjects that act on their own, leaving the passive forms for a dedicated future lesson.

Because participles look and behave so much like standard adjectives, learners of Ukrainian very often confuse them. To systematically avoid this common pitfall, you can employ a simple "Triple Check" logic to successfully distinguish a participle from a regular adjective and a verb. Let us thoroughly test this diagnostic logic using three closely related words: «зелений» (green), «зеленіючий» (turning green), and «зеленіє» (turns green). Step one is to ask the syntactic question: does the word answer "what kind?" like an adjective? Both «зелений» and «зеленіючий» successfully answer this, but the verb «зеленіє» answers "what is it doing?". Step two is to look deeply for a verb root. Both «зеленіє» and «зеленіючий» explicitly share the verbal root indicating the active process of becoming green, whereas «зелений» is just the static base color. Step three is the ultimate proof: does the word have a specific tense suffix attached directly to that verb root? Only «зеленіючий» contains the specific present tense participle suffix. Therefore, if a word answers «який?», explicitly derives from a verbal action, and contains a specific grammatical suffix, it is definitively a participle.

## Активні дієприкметники теперішнього часу (-уч-/-юч-, -ач-/-яч-) (~880 words total)

To form an **активний дієприкметник** (active participle) in the present tense from a first conjugation verb, we must start with the foundation of its present tense paradigm. The **пасивний дієприкметник** (passive participle) relies on the infinitive stem, but the active present forms rely on the third-person plural form, which translates to "they do something". For first conjugation verbs, this form invariably ends in either «-уть» or «-ють». Once we have this base, the mechanical transformation is quite straightforward. We simply drop the final «-ть» from the verb ending and attach the adjectival suffix «-чий». Because the preceding vowel is preserved, the resulting participle suffix effectively becomes either «-учий» or «-ючий». Let us look at how this morphological shift operates in practice to create a dynamic description.

Якщо ми візьмемо дієслово першої дієвідміни «синіти», то його форма для третьої особи множини буде «синіють». Ця форма є базою для нашого наступного граматичного кроку. Ми відкидаємо закінчення і додаємо новий суфікс, утворюючи слово «синіючий». Так само від дієслова «знати» ми беремо форму «знають», щоб утворити дієприкметник «знаючий».

> *If we take the first conjugation verb "to turn blue", its third-person plural form will be "they turn blue". This form is the base for our next grammatical step. We drop the ending and add the new suffix, forming the word "turning blue". Similarly, from the verb "to know" we take the form "they know" to form the participle "knowing".*

For verbs belonging to the second conjugation, the morphological process follows the exact same underlying logic, but utilizes different vowels because of the distinct present tense endings. Second conjugation verbs form their third-person plural with either «-ать» or «-ять». When we apply the same transformation rule — dropping the final «-ть» and appending «-чий» — the resulting participle suffix is naturally either «-ачий» or «-ячий». This consistent pattern allows learners to confidently predict the shape of the participle if they know the verb's conjugation class. According to Oleksandr Zabolotnyi's seventh-grade grammar textbook on page ninety-five, this is the standard suffixal method for creating these specific forms.

Наприклад, дієслово другої дієвідміни «лежати» у множині має форму «лежать». Ця форма показує нам правильну голосну літеру перед закінченням. Відкидаючи закінчення, ми отримуємо активний дієприкметник «лежачий». Дієслово «тремтіти» дає нам форму «тремтять», з якої ми утворюємо слово «тремтячий», що описує предмет у стані тремтіння.

> *For example, the second conjugation verb "to lie" has the plural form "they lie". This form shows us the correct vowel before the ending. Dropping the ending, we get the active participle "lying". The verb "to tremble" gives us the form "they tremble", from which we form the word "trembling", describing an object in a state of trembling.*

Now that we have established the mechanical rules for creating these words, we must immediately introduce a crucial caveat regarding their actual usage in modern Ukrainian. While it is grammatically possible to construct a present active participle for almost any imperfective verb, doing so freely is highly uncharacteristic of natural Ukrainian speech. As noted by Oleksandr Avramenko in his eleventh-grade textbook, the widespread use of participles ending in «-учий» or «-ачий» to describe temporary actions is severely restricted. When overused, these forms sound excessively bookish, artificial, or like direct, clumsy calques from the Russian language, where such participles are ubiquitous and stylistically neutral. Instead of using a participle like "the boy reading a book", Ukrainians vastly prefer an **описовий зворот** (descriptive phrase).

Замість того, щоб використовувати активний дієприкметник для опису тимчасової дії, сучасна українська літературна мова надає перевагу підрядним реченням. Цей вибір робить наше мовлення набагато легшим та природнішим для сприйняття. Ми уникаємо важких конструкцій, які можуть звучати як переклад з іншої мови. Наприклад, ми не кажемо «читаючий хлопчик», а використовуємо описовий зворот і кажемо «хлопчик, який читає».

> *Instead of using an active participle to describe a temporary action, the modern Ukrainian literary language prefers subordinate clauses. This choice makes our speech much lighter and more natural to perceive. We avoid heavy constructions that might sound like a translation from another language. For example, we do not say "the reading boy", but we use a descriptive phrase and say "the boy who is reading".*

Despite this general restriction, there is a very important and distinct category of present active participles that are not only acceptable but are considered beautiful, authentic Ukrainian vocabulary. These are lexicalized forms, such as **квітучий** (blooming) or **палаючий** (blazing). A participle becomes lexicalized when it loses its strong connection to a temporary, ongoing verbal action and instead comes to represent a permanent, static characteristic. Over time, these specific words effectively transitioned into regular adjectives. Because they now describe enduring traits rather than momentary actions, they bypass the stylistic restrictions mentioned earlier. They are deeply embedded in folklore, literature, and everyday idioms, showcasing the expressive capacity of the language.

Ми можемо вільно використовувати слова квітучий або палаючий, тому що вони стали звичайними прикметниками, які позначають постійну ознаку. Ці слова стали невід'ємною частиною нашої літератури та повсякденного словника. Ми кажемо «пекучий біль», коли біль є надзвичайно сильним, або «киплячий чайник», коли вода досягла певної температури. Знамените українське прислів'я каже, що під лежачий камінь вода не тече.

> *We can freely use the words "blooming" or "blazing" because they have become regular adjectives that denote a permanent attribute. These words have become an integral part of our literature and everyday vocabulary. We say "burning pain" when the pain is extremely strong, or "boiling kettle" when the water has reached a certain temperature. A famous Ukrainian proverb says that water does not flow under a lying stone.*

:::info
**Grammar box**
Always ask yourself: is the trait permanent or temporary? A "blooming garden" (квітучий сад) is a state of being for the season, so the lexicalized participle is perfect. A "boy reading a book" is a temporary action, so you must use a descriptive phrase with «який» (who).
:::

Understanding when to use a present active participle and when to replace it with a descriptive clause is ultimately a matter of register awareness. The few productive present participles that have not become permanent adjectives are generally confined to specialized scientific terminology or elevated, poetic literary descriptions. In a medical context, a doctor might appropriately refer to the "wandering nerve" as «блукаючий нерв». However, taking that same grammatical structure into colloquial, everyday speech results in an unnatural utterance. You cannot refer to a "screaming child" as «кричаща дитина»; doing so violates the established norms of spoken Ukrainian. Furthermore, many present participles are actually Russian calques that must be entirely replaced by proper Ukrainian adjectives. For instance, instead of using the calque «діючий закон» (acting law), you must use the adjective **чинний** (current, valid). Similarly, «існуючий» (existing) is replaced by **наявний** (existing, available).

У щоденному спілкуванні українці завжди обирають прості та природні лексичні відповідники замість неприродних кальок. Мова постійно розвивається, але вона зберігає свої унікальні синтаксичні традиції. Штучні конструкції роблять текст важким, тому їх краще уникати під час розмови. Якщо ви хочете звучати грамотно, вивчайте правильні українські прикметники та уникайте штучних форм.

> *In everyday communication, Ukrainians always choose simple and natural lexical equivalents instead of unnatural calques. The language is constantly developing, but it preserves its unique syntactic traditions. Artificial constructions make the text heavy, so it is better to avoid them during conversation. If you want to sound grammatically correct, learn the proper Ukrainian adjectives and avoid artificial forms.*

## Активні дієприкметники минулого часу (-л-) (~770 words total)

While the present forms discussed previously are heavily restricted and often stylistically awkward, a past **активний дієприкметник** (active participle — subject performs the action) is entirely natural and highly productive. They are as fundamental to Ukrainian syntax as a standard **пасивний дієприкметник** (passive participle — subject receives the action). Every properly formed **дієприкметник** (participle — verb form expressing attribute by action) of this type is authentic and stylistically neutral. You will encounter them constantly in literature, news, and everyday conversation.

This authenticity makes them completely different from artificial Russian calques that must be replaced by proper adjectives. For example, you must always replace the calque діючий with **чинний** (current, valid — replaces Russian calque діючий). You must also replace існуючий with **наявний** (existing, available — replaces Russian calque існуючий). Past active participles do not suffer from this problem. They are formed exclusively from perfective verbs, which makes logical sense because they describe an action that has already reached its conclusion.

To form them, you isolate the infinitive base of a perfective verb by removing the final **-ти**, and then add the suffix **-л-** followed by standard adjective endings. Let us look at a straightforward example of this formation process. Take the perfective verb **посивіти** (to go grey). You remove the infinitive marker to get the base, and then add the past active participle suffix to create **посивілий** (gone grey — past active participle).

You can now use this to describe someone as a «посивілий чоловік» (a man who has gone grey). Another excellent example is the verb **змарніти** (to become emaciated or to waste away). Following the exact same pattern, it becomes **змарнілий** (emaciated). These forms seamlessly blend the completed action of the verb into a permanent descriptive trait for the noun they modify.

There is one critical phonetic rule you must memorize when forming these participles, known as the "nu-drop" rule. Many perfective verbs in Ukrainian denote a sudden or single completed action using the suffix **-ну-**. When you form a participle like **зів'ялий** (wilted — past active participle) from these specific verbs, the entire **-ну-** suffix drops out and disappears before you add the **-л-** suffix. This phonetic simplification makes the resulting words flow much more smoothly and prevents awkward consonant clusters. The seventh-grade textbook by Avramenko explicitly highlights this morphological change as a key rule for proper word formation.

Відповідно до правил сучасної української мови, якщо основа інфінітива має цей суфікс перед закінченням, він обов'язково зникає. Візьмемо дієслово зів'янути. Коли ми утворюємо від нього дієприкметник, суфікс випадає, і ми отримуємо слово зів'ялий. Так само працює дієслово розквітнути, яке перетворюється на розквітлий. Якщо на вулиці дуже холодно, людина може змерзнути, і тоді ми назвемо її змерзлий.

> *According to the rules of the modern Ukrainian language, if the infinitive base has this suffix before the ending, it necessarily disappears. Let us take the verb to wilt (зів'янути). When we form a participle from it, the suffix drops out, and we get the word wilted (зів'ялий). The verb to blossom (розквітнути) works the same way, turning into blossomed (розквітлий). If it is very cold outside, a person might freeze (змерзнути), and then we will call them frozen (змерзлий).*

It is essential to internalize this dropping mechanism because failing to drop the suffix will result in words that sound immediately foreign to a native speaker. You will never hear someone say «розквітнулий»; the only correct form is **розквітлий** (blossomed — past active participle). You might use a natural present form like **квітучий** (blooming — natural active participle) or **палаючий** (blazing — natural active participle) to describe an ongoing state. In contrast, **розквітлий** emphasize the fully completed transition into that state.

Similarly, the verb for falling or shedding, such as leaves in autumn, is **опасти**. Its past active participle simply drops the infinitive marker to become **опалий** (fallen, shed — past active participle, leaves). The leaves have finished falling, so they are now characterized by that completed action.

This brings us to the core semantic meaning of the past active participle: it always expresses a fully completed change of state. The subject underwent a specific action in the past, and it now carries the result of that action as an inherent characteristic. The focus is entirely on the result, not the process. Understanding this temporal boundary is crucial for choosing the right word when describing the world around you.

Уявіть собі осінній ліс. Коли ви бачите пожовклий листок, ви розумієте, що процес зміни кольору вже повністю завершився. Листок колись був зеленим, потім він змінив колір, і тепер він остаточно жовтий. Цей стан є результатом минулої дії. Натомість форма жовтіючий означала б, що листок змінює свій колір саме зараз, у момент розмови.

> *Imagine an autumn forest. When you see a yellowed leaf, you understand that the process of changing color has already fully completed. The leaf was once green, then it changed color, and now it is definitively yellow. This state is the result of a past action. In contrast, the form turning yellow (жовтіючий) would mean that the leaf is changing its color right now, at the moment of conversation.*

Another very common example of this completed state semantic is the verb **зникнути** (to disappear). Using the "nu-drop" rule, we form the word **зниклий** (disappeared — past active participle). A «зниклий собака» is a dog that has already vanished; the act of disappearing is over, and the dog is currently in the state of being missing. This is a precise and economical way to convey complex temporal information inside a single descriptive word. You do not need to use an **описовий зворот** (descriptive phrase — alternative to participle) like "the dog that disappeared yesterday" when you can simply use the participle.

From a grammatical perspective, integrating these words into your sentences is incredibly straightforward. Once you have formed the past active participle with its **-л-** suffix, it behaves exactly like a standard hard group adjective. It must agree with the noun it modifies in gender, number, and case. There are no special declension rules or exceptions to memorize here. Below is the complete declension table for the word «зів'ялий» across all genders and the plural form.

## Чого уникати: русизми у дієприкметниках (~660 words total)

In Ukrainian, using the suffixes **-вш-** and **-ш-** to form past active participles is a critical violation of morphological norms. Words like *сказавший* meaning "having said", *прийшовший* meaning "having arrived", and *посинівший* meaning "having turned blue" are not Ukrainian. They are direct structural borrowings from Russian and are categorized as severe grammatical errors, or *суржик*. The authoritative school textbook by Glazova (Grade 11, p. 72) explicitly identifies these forms as a gross violation of the literary standard. Historically, the Ukrainian language developed its own distinct methods for expressing these concepts, favoring descriptive clauses or completely different morphological structures. When you use a word ending in **-вший**, it immediately signals that you are applying foreign grammar rules to Ukrainian vocabulary. Decolonizing your speech means entirely eliminating these suffixes from your active use. Instead of trying to force these unnatural participles into your sentences, you must rely on the authentic Ukrainian strategies that we will explore below.

Найкращий і найприродніший спосіб замінити штучний активний дієприкметник — це використати описовий зворот. Ця синтаксична конструкція складається із займенника «який» або «що» та звичайного дієслова у відповідному часі. Наприклад, замість кальки «читаюча дівчина» ми завжди говоримо «дівчина, яка читає». Замість помилкового «сказавший чоловік» потрібно казати «чоловік, який сказав». Цей підхід є нормативним для нейтрального та розмовного стилів сучасної української мови. Він робить ваше мовлення живим, легким і динамічним, уникаючи важких канцелярських конструкцій. Українська мова віддає перевагу дії, вираженій повноцінним дієсловом, а не скам'янілим формам. Тому використання підрядних речень є вашим головним інструментом для створення автентичних висловлювань.

> *The best and most natural way to replace an artificial active participle is to use an **описовий зворот** (descriptive phrase). This syntactic construction consists of the pronoun "який" (which/who) or "що" (that) and a regular verb in the appropriate tense. For example, instead of the calque "читаюча дівчина" meaning a reading girl, we always say "дівчина, яка читає" meaning the girl who is reading. Instead of the erroneous "сказавший чоловік", you must say "чоловік, який сказав". This approach is absolutely normative for the neutral and conversational styles of the modern Ukrainian language. It makes your speech lively, light, and dynamic, avoiding heavy bureaucratic constructions. The Ukrainian language prefers action expressed by a full verb rather than fossilized forms. Therefore, the use of subordinate clauses is your main tool for creating authentic utterances.*

Another powerful strategy for avoiding unnatural active participles is replacing them with dedicated nouns or adjectives. Many present active participles ending in **-учий** or **-ячий** have become deeply entrenched bureaucratic calques. For instance, the management word *керуючий* should always be replaced with the noun **керівник**. Similarly, instead of saying *завідуючий* for a department head, the correct Ukrainian noun is **завідувач**. When expressing the concept of something being currently in effect, you must avoid the Russian calque *діючий* and use the authentic Ukrainian adjective **чинний** (current, valid). We also see this distinction clearly in everyday household items. The word *миючий* implies the active process of washing right now, whereas **мийний** denotes the intended purpose of the item. Therefore, you buy a cleaning detergent called a **мийний засіб**, not a *миючий засіб*. By choosing precise nouns and purpose-driven adjectives over clumsy participles, your vocabulary becomes sharper and much more aligned with the natural elegance of the Ukrainian language.

:::info
**Grammar box**
Always double-check words ending in **-учий**, **-ючий**, **-ачий**, and **-ячий**. If you can replace the word with a noun, a regular adjective, or a descriptive clause, that replacement is almost always the preferred Ukrainian standard.
:::

To speak authentic Ukrainian, you must memorize a few common false friends. These are direct calques from Russian that disguise themselves as Ukrainian active participles. They are widespread in careless speech and often sound correct to beginners, but they represent severe stylistic errors. Purging these specific calques from your vocabulary is a crucial step in decolonizing your language. Using their natural replacements immediately elevates your proficiency and proves that you are learning the true literary standard. | *лідируючий* | **провідний** | leading |
| *існуючий* | **наявний** | existing, available |
| *оточуючий* | **довколишній** | surrounding |

By consciously substituting clunky, bureaucratic words like *існуючий* with the elegant adjective **наявний** (existing, available), you align your speech with the natural rhythms of the language. This attention to detail shows deep respect for authentic Ukrainian morphology.

## Дієприкметник у тексті (~880 words total)

In documentaries, travelogues, and nature programs, writers carefully balance descriptive phrases with active participles to create a rhythmic, engaging narrative. Because the Ukrainian language inherently limits the use of active present participles, a skilled narrator must alternate between natural adjectives derived from participles and descriptive subordinate clauses. This deliberate stylistic choice keeps the text flowing rhythmically without sounding artificial, bureaucratic, or grammatically strained. Let us look at a script excerpt from a nature documentary about the Carpathian Mountains to see how this balance is achieved in practice.

> — **Оповідач:** Камера повільно піднімається над горами. Олень, що біжить через галявину, раптом зупиняється. *(The camera slowly rises above the mountains. A deer running across the meadow suddenly stops.)*
> — **Режисер:** Добре, тепер показуємо гірську річку зблизька. *(Good, now we show the mountain river up close.)*
> — **Оповідач:** Падаючий водоспад розбивається об каміння. Навколо розкинулися квітучі луки, сповнені життя. *(The falling waterfall crashes against the stones. Blooming meadows, full of life, stretch around.)*
> — **Режисер:** Чудово. Зверни увагу на контраст: ми кажемо «олень, що біжить», але залишаємо «квітучі луки». *(Excellent. Notice the contrast: we say "a deer that runs", but we leave "blooming meadows".)*

Notice how the narrator uses the natural active participle **квітучий** (blooming) to describe a permanent, inherent state of the spring landscape. However, they deliberately switch to the descriptive phrase **олень, що біжить** (a running deer) to depict a temporary, dynamic action. 

While the phrase «падаючий» is sometimes tolerated in highly poetic or cinematic contexts to emphasize continuous motion, many rigorous editors would still prefer the construction «водоспад, що падає» to maintain absolute grammatical purity. Recognizing when to use a participle and when to use a clause is the mark of an advanced speaker.

When we look at classic Ukrainian literature, we see that writers masterfully employ the past active participle to convey a completed change of state in nature. These forms, which are formed with the suffix **-л-**, are deeply authentic, historically rooted, and evoke incredibly vivid sensory details. They allow the author to paint a picture of a landscape that has already undergone a transformation.

Українські письменники часто використовують активні дієприкметники минулого часу для створення атмосферних пейзажів. Наприклад, у творах Михайла Стельмаха ми знаходимо такі ліричні рядки: «Солодким хлібом пахнуть стерні, і чуйно соняшник поверне пожовклу голову на схід». А видатний класик Михайло Коцюбинський описує осіннє поле так: «Стиха лущиться зерно з перестиглого колоса». У цих текстах слова «пожовклу» та «перестиглого» показують дію, яка вже повністю завершилася, залишаючи після себе яскравий візуальний результат.

> *Ukrainian writers often use past active participles to create atmospheric landscapes. For example, in the works of Mykhailo Stelmakh we find such lyrical lines: "The stubble smells of sweet bread, and the sunflower sensitively turns its yellowed head to the east." And the prominent classic Mykhailo Kotsiubynsky describes an autumn field like this: "Quietly the grain peels from the overripe ear." In these texts, the words "yellowed" and "overripe" show an action that has already completely finished, leaving behind a vivid visual result.*

In these beautiful literary examples, the **активний дієприкметник** (active participle) expresses an attribute defined by an action that the subject performed entirely on its own. The sunflower turned yellow naturally, becoming **пожовклий** (yellowed), and the ear of grain ripened fully over the summer, becoming **перестиглий** (overripe). Using the past active participle instead of a longer descriptive clause makes the prose tighter, more elegant, and highly evocative. 

The narrative focus shifts beautifully from the active process of aging or ripening to the melancholic aftermath of the season. You will frequently encounter similar descriptive forms like **опалий** (fallen, shed) or **зів'ялий** (wilted) when reading poems about autumn leaves. You will also see words like **посивілий** (gone grey) when authors introduce a wise, elderly character. 

Furthermore, lexicalized forms expressing intense states, such as **палаючий** (blazing), are commonly used to describe a bright fire illuminating the dark scenery.

The sharp contrast between natural Ukrainian phrasing and unnatural Russian calques becomes especially obvious in professional, academic, or technical writing. In the corporate and business register, relying on incorrect active participles is a common marker of poor stylistic editing and a lack of linguistic awareness. Let us compare two short versions of a corporate analytical report to see exactly how vocabulary choices drastically affect the perceived quality and authority of the text.

Версія А містить типові стилістичні помилки: «Існуючий ринок показує, що домінуючий продукт стрімко втрачає позиції. Оточуюче середовище вимагає від нас нових рішень». Цей текст звучить дуже штучно і перевантажений кальками. Версія Б демонструє ідеальну та грамотну українську мову: «Наявний ринок показує, що панівний продукт стрімко втрачає позиції. Довколишнє середовище вимагає від нас нових рішень». Тут використані правильні прикметники, які роблять звіт по-справжньому професійним.

> *Version A contains typical stylistic mistakes: "The existing market shows that the dominating product is rapidly losing ground. The surrounding environment requires absolutely new solutions from us." This text sounds very artificial and is overloaded with calques. Version B demonstrates perfect and correct Ukrainian: "The available market shows that the dominant product is rapidly losing ground. The surrounding environment requires absolutely new solutions from us." Here, the correct adjectives are used, which make the report truly professional.*

A meticulous professional writer knows that the calque «існуючий» must always be replaced with the standard word **наявний** (existing, available). Similarly, the clumsy word «домінуючий» should seamlessly be replaced with **панівний** (dominant). 

When you draft formal emails, analytical reports, or academic essays at the B1 level and beyond, you must actively and consciously filter out these bureaucratic words from your vocabulary. An **описовий зворот** (descriptive phrase) or a dedicated standard adjective is always preferred over awkward present active participles. By choosing the standard Ukrainian adjectives, you demonstrate a profound respect for the morphological rules of the language.

:::info
**Grammar box**
In official documents, legal texts, and business contracts, you must be extremely precise with terminology. You should always use the adjective **чинний** (current, valid) when referring to laws or regulations that are currently in effect. Never write «діючий закон»; the only correct phrasing is «чинний закон».
:::

Understanding how to correctly form these words and when to avoid them is only the first step in mastering the **дієприкметник** (participle). You must also know how to integrate them smoothly into complex sentences. Syntactically, a participle almost always acts as an attribute, modifying a specific noun and directly answering the question "which one?". When a participle gathers its own dependent words around it to form a larger descriptive unit, it creates a powerful syntactic structure known as a participial phrase.

У реченні дієприкметник найчастіше виступає означенням і підкреслюється простою хвилястою лінією. Якщо він має залежні слова, то утворює дієприкметниковий зворот. Розгляньмо такий класичний приклад: «Гори, побілілі від снігу, виднілися вдалині». У цьому реченні слово «побілілі» є дієприкметником, а фраза «від снігу» — його залежним словом. Разом вони детально описують гори і обов'язково виділяються комами на письмі.

> *In a sentence, the participle most often acts as an attribute and is underlined with a simple wavy line. If it has dependent words, it forms a participial phrase. Let us consider such a classic example: "The mountains, whitened by snow, were visible in the distance." In this sentence, the word "whitened" is a participle, and the phrase "by snow" is its dependent word. Together they describe the mountains in detail and are obligatorily set off by commas in writing.*

This elegant syntactic structure is incredibly useful for adding descriptive depth and sophisticated detail to your sentences without creating overly long, repetitive, and complex subordinate clauses. While you will learn the comprehensive punctuation rules and the deeper mechanics for the participial phrase in the upcoming modules, it is vitally important to recognize its core function right now. Whether you are using a **пасивний дієприкметник** (passive participle) to show an action done to an object, or an active one to show an action performed by the subject itself, the phrase allows you to compress complex information elegantly. 

For instance, instead of writing "The mountains, which had turned white from the snow, were visible," you simply state "The mountains, whitened by snow." This concise, rhythmic phrasing is a true hallmark of advanced Ukrainian syntax and is highly characteristic of both literary and scientific texts. You will see similar advanced structures with words like **розквітлий** (blossomed) or **зниклий** (disappeared), where the participle gracefully anchors a much broader description of the subject in the narrative.

## Підсумок та перехід до M58 (~550 words total)

We have reached the end of our comprehensive exploration of the **дієприкметник** (participle). By now, you should confidently recognize that this unique grammatical category perfectly blends the dynamic properties of a verb with the descriptive nature of an adjective. It directly answers the question "which one?" while simultaneously pointing to a specific action or state that the noun is experiencing. An **активний дієприкметник** (active participle) specifically describes a subject that performs the action itself, without any external force acting upon it. Throughout this module, we observed how these forms are meticulously constructed depending on the tense and the underlying verb's conjugation class.

Для теперішнього часу ми використовуємо суфікси -уч- або -юч- для дієслів першої дієвідміни. Якщо дієслово належить до другої дієвідміни, ми додаємо суфікси -ач- або -яч-. Щоб утворити форму минулого часу, ми беремо основу інфінітива доконаного виду та додаємо суфікс -л-. Так ми отримуємо правильні та питомі українські слова.

> *For the present tense, we use the suffixes -уч- or -юч- for verbs of the first conjugation. If the verb belongs to the second conjugation, we add the suffixes -ач- or -яч-. To form the past tense, we take the infinitive base of the perfective aspect and add the suffix -л-. This is how we get correct and native Ukrainian words.*

Remember that many present tense active forms have simply become standard adjectives in modern usage. When you see a **палаючий** (blazing) fire illuminating a campsite or a **квітучий** (blooming) garden in early spring, you are looking at lexicalized adjectives that denote a permanent, inherent state rather than an ongoing, temporary action. You must always be wary of newly minted present active participles, as they often clash with the natural, established rhythm of Ukrainian syntax.

The most critical takeaway from this entire module is the decolonization checklist. You must actively avoid artificial words by choosing native replacements. For instance, always use **чинний** (current, valid) instead of a bureaucratic calque, and select **наявний** (existing, available) when describing something that is present. Embracing these native terms ensures your Ukrainian sounds truly authentic and polished.

Ви ніколи не повинні використовувати суфікси -вш- або -ш- для минулого часу, оскільки це груба граматична помилка, яка одразу видає ненормативне мовлення. Також уникайте штучних слів, таких як «діючий» або «існуючий», замінюючи їх на відповідні українські прикметники. Замість канцеляризму «бажаючий» завжди краще сказати «охочий» або просто використати підрядне речення.

> *You must never use the suffixes -вш- or -ш- for the past tense, as this is a gross grammatical error that immediately betrays non-standard speech. Also, avoid artificial words such as "діючий" (acting) or "існуючий" (existing), replacing them with the appropriate Ukrainian adjectives. Instead of the bureaucratic "бажаючий" (wishing), it is always better to say "охочий" (willing) or simply use a subordinate clause.*

When an active participle feels clunky or forced in your writing, your best grammatical tool is the **описовий зворот** (descriptive phrase). Using the relative pronoun «який» combined with a regular conjugated verb creates a smooth, highly idiomatic sentence that native speakers overwhelmingly prefer. 

The past tense forms beautifully capture a completed change of state without sounding unnatural. Whether you are describing a **посивілий** (gone grey) man, a **розквітлий** (blossomed) tree, or a **зів'ялий** (wilted) flower, these participles elegantly compress complex information into a single descriptive word.

While past active participles like **опалий** (fallen, shed) autumn leaves or a **зниклий** (disappeared) person are highly productive, the present forms demand your strict grammatical scrutiny before every use.

:::tip
**Did you know?**
The resistance to unnatural active participles is not a recent linguistic trend. Prominent Ukrainian linguists and classic writers have championed the use of natural descriptive phrases and native adjectives for over a century, actively preserving the melodic rhythm and clarity of the language.
:::
