## Дієприслівниковий зворот: будова (~770 words)

Welcome to a new level of expressive Ukrainian. When you want to describe actions with precision, showing how, when, or why something happened in relation to the main event, you need advanced structures. Imagine you are listening to a popular life coach podcast recorded in a modern Kyiv studio. The guest is sharing strategies for personal growth, and to make their points sound authoritative and flowing, they rely heavily on a specific grammatical tool. Let's tune into the conversation.

> — **Коуч:** Працюючи над собою щодня, ви обов'язково побачите позитивні зміни у своєму житті. *(Working on yourself daily, you will definitely see positive changes in your life.)*
> — **Ведучий:** Це звучить дуже надихаюче. Але як саме почати цей процес? *(That sounds very inspiring. But how exactly to start this process?)*
> — **Коуч:** Усе дуже просто. Прочитавши цю книгу, ви зрозумієте основні принципи успіху. Не зробивши першого кроку, ви ніколи нічого не досягнете. *(It's very simple. Having read this book, you will understand the basic principles of success. Not having made the first step, you will never achieve anything.)*
> — **Ведучий:** Отже, зміни починаються з дії. *(So, changes begin with action.)*

The coach in our studio is not just giving advice; they are demonstrating how to build complex, sophisticated sentences. They use phrases like *працюючи над собою щодня* (working on yourself daily), *прочитавши цю книгу* (having read this book), and *не зробивши першого кроку* (not having made the first step). These phrases expand a simple action into a detailed descriptive scenario. They add depth, context, and a professional rhythm to the speech, setting the stage for our exploration of expanded gerund structures.

In previous modules, we explored the concept of the single gerund, known in Ukrainian grammar as the *одиничний дієприслівник*. This is a special, unchangeable verb form that denotes an additional action accompanying the main verb. For example, if someone smiles while talking, you might say they spoke *усміхаючись* (smiling). However, actions rarely occur in total isolation. They usually interact with objects, have specific locations, or involve other people. This is where the concept of the gerund phrase comes into play.

Українська граматика визначає дієприслівниковий зворот як дієприслівник разом із залежними від нього словами. Залежні слова — це ті слова, до яких ми можемо поставити питання безпосередньо від дієприслівника. Разом вони утворюють єдину змістову та синтаксичну конструкцію.

> *Ukrainian grammar defines a **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase) as a gerund together with its dependent words. Dependent words are those words to which we can ask a question directly from the gerund. Together they form a single semantic and syntactic construction.*

To understand this structure, let's contrast it with what you already know. Consider the single gerund in the sentence: *Спілкуючись, вона дізналася багато нового.* Here, *спілкуючись* (communicating) stands alone. Now, let's expand it into a full phrase: *Спілкуючись із відомими журналістами, вона дізналася багато нового.* The gerund has acquired dependent words: *із відомими журналістами* (with famous journalists). We can ask a question from the gerund: communicating with whom? This entire block of words now functions as a unified grammatical unit, enriching the sentence with specific details.

:::info
**Grammar box**
A **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase) is simply a gerund that has gathered other words around it to form a more detailed description of an action. The gerund acts as the anchor of this phrase, dictating the grammatical relationship with the dependent words.
:::

Every element in a Ukrainian sentence plays a specific role. Because the core function of a gerund is to describe the circumstances of the main verb, the entire phrase inherits this responsibility. In syntactic terms, the whole gerund phrase functions as a single **обставина** (adverbial modifier). This means that regardless of its length, you treat the phrase as one logical unit when analyzing the sentence.

У реченні дієприслівниковий зворот завжди виступає обставиною. Він відповідає на питання «як?», «коли?», або «чому?». Ця конструкція допомагає нам зрозуміти умови, за яких відбувається головна дія.

> *In a sentence, the gerund phrase always acts as an **обставина** (adverbial modifier). It answers the questions "how?", "when?", or "why?". This construction helps us understand the conditions under which the main action takes place.*

Let's analyze a classic literary example from the Ukrainian poet Levko Borovykovskyi to see this in practice: *Край річки, ставши на плотину, рибалка вудочку закинув.* (By the river, having stood on the dam, the fisherman cast his fishing rod.) The main action here is *закинув* (cast). To understand the circumstances of this action, we look at the gerund phrase *ставши на плотину*. 

Let's break this phrase down into its components. The core is the perfective gerund *ставши* (having stood). From this core, we ask the question: having stood where? The answer is *на плотину* (on the dam). These dependent words attach to the gerund, forming the complete phrase. The entire block *ставши на плотину* answers the question "under what circumstances did he cast the rod?" or "what did he do before casting the rod?", fulfilling its role as an adverbial modifier.

The true power of this construction lies in its logical efficiency. By using dependent words, you can pack a significant amount of context into a single sentence without needing to create multiple, separate clauses. This is particularly useful when describing dynamic events, such as sports competitions or fast-paced actions, where you want to highlight a sequence of events smoothly.

Випередивши сильного суперника на останніх метрах дистанції, молодий плавець здобув золоту медаль. Скориставшись неочікуваною помилкою захисника, нападник нашої команди забив вирішальний гол.

> *Having overtaken the strong opponent in the final meters of the distance, the young swimmer won a gold medal. Having taken advantage of the defender's unexpected mistake, our team's striker scored the decisive goal.*

In the first example, the phrase sets up the precise condition for the victory. The main verb is *здобув* (won), and the gerund phrase explains exactly how and when this victory was secured. In the second example, *скориставшись неочікуваною помилкою захисника* explains the opportunity that led to the main action *забив* (scored). The dependent words detail whose mistake it was, giving a complete picture of the circumstances surrounding the goal. Without these phrases, the narrative would sound choppy.

Finally, the choice of gerund within the phrase dictates the timeline of events. Just like single gerunds, gerund phrases can be built using either imperfective or perfective forms, and this choice radically alters the meaning of the sentence. 

Дієприслівникові звороти недоконаного виду означають дію, яка відбувається одночасно з головною дією. Звороти доконаного виду вказують на дію, яка завершилася перед початком головної дії.

> *Imperfective gerund phrases denote an action that happens simultaneously with the main action. Perfective phrases indicate an action that was completed before the start of the main action.*

Consider the difference between two similar phrases. If we say *Читаючи нову книгу, він пив каву*, the imperfective phrase *читаючи нову книгу* (while reading a new book) shows two actions happening at the exact same time. The reading and the drinking are parallel. However, if we return to our podcast coach's advice, they said: *Прочитавши цю книгу, ви зрозумієте основні принципи успіху.* Here, the perfective phrase *прочитавши цю книгу* (having read this book) establishes a strict sequence. The action of reading must be fully completed before the understanding can happen. Recognizing this temporal logic is crucial for mastering the nuances of Ukrainian storytelling.

## Правила відокремлення (~990 words)

Let's talk about the mechanics of punctuation. If you recall our previous lessons on participles, you might remember that placing commas required careful attention to where the phrase was located in relation to its noun. With gerunds, the grammar gods have given us a much simpler system. The absolute golden rule of Ukrainian syntax is that a **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase) is always set off by commas. This punctuation process is officially called **відокремлення** (setting off with commas).

Unlike a participle phrase, which serves as an **означення** (attribute — syntactic role of дієприкметниковий зворот) and only takes commas under specific positional conditions, the gerund phrase operates independently. It modifies the main verb, meaning it functions as an **обставина** (adverbial modifier — syntactic role). Because of this strong independent action, it demands a pause in speech and commas in writing, regardless of whether it opens the sentence, interrupts the middle, or trails at the end.

Головне правило пунктуації для дієприслівникових зворотів є простим і логічним. Дієприслівниковий зворот завжди виділяється комами, незалежно від того, яку позицію він займає щодо головного дієслова. Це правило діє на початку, в середині та в кінці речення, що робить його набагато легшим для запам'ятовування.

Let's look at how this rigid consistency plays out in practice. Notice how the commas stubbornly frame the phrase no matter where we move it:

**Прочитавши листа, Оленка задумалася.** — *Having read the letter, Olenka fell to thinking.*
**Оленка, прочитавши листа, задумалася.** — *Olenka, having read the letter, fell to thinking.*
**Оленка задумалася, прочитавши листа.** — *Olenka fell to thinking, having read the letter.*

What happens when an author uses more than one gerund phrase to describe a complex sequence of background events? If the phrases describe separate, sequential actions without any connecting conjunctions, they are treated as a standard list. You must place a comma after each distinct phrase to separate them from one another, just as you would with a list of nouns or regular verbs.

**Дізнавшись правду, зрозумівши все, він почав діяти.** — *Having learned the truth, having understood everything, he began to act.*

However, Ukrainian syntax has a specific rule for homogeneous members joined by the conjunction "і" (and) or "та" (and). If two gerund phrases are connected by a single, non-repeating conjunction, they fuse together into a single logical block. Because they act as a unified, coordinated pair modifying the same verb, you do not put a comma between them. You only place commas at the outer edges of the entire joined block.

Коли два дієприслівникові звороти з'єднані сполучником «і», вони утворюють одну велику ритмічну групу. У такому випадку ми ставимо коми лише на початку та в кінці цієї спільної конструкції, але ніколи не розриваємо її всередині. Це допомагає зберегти безперервність опису кількох одночасних дій.

**Відчинивши двері і ввімкнувши світло, він оглянув кімнату.** — *Having opened the door and turned on the light, he inspected the room.*

:::note
**Quick tip** — When you see the conjunction *і* between two gerunds, treat the entire sequence as one giant phrase. The comma only goes where the gerund action stops and the main verb action begins.
:::

Every robust grammar rule has its exceptions, and the Ukrainian gerund is no different. The first major exception involves a **фразеологічний зворот** (phraseological expression — exception to comma rule). When a gerund phrase has been used together for centuries, it eventually loses its literal, verb-like action and fossilizes into a fixed idiom. Because these idioms function entirely like simple adverbs of manner (meaning "diligently," "fast," or "silently"), the comma disappears completely.

You will frequently encounter these in both classic literature and daily speech. For example, the phrase **не покладаючи рук** (without rest — phraseological, no commas) literally means "not putting down one's hands," but it simply translates to working tirelessly. Similarly, **затамувавши подих** (holding one's breath — phraseological, no commas) means to listen or watch with intense anticipation.

**Майстри працювали не покладаючи рук, щоб встигнути до свята.** — *The craftsmen worked tirelessly to finish before the holiday.*
**Діти слухали казку затамувавши подих.** — *The children listened to the fairy tale with bated breath.*
**Хлопець біг додому не чуючи ніг.** — *The boy ran home very fast (literally: not feeling his legs).*

Українська мова має багатий арсенал ідіом, які будуються за допомогою незмінних дієслівних форм. Оскільки ці стійкі вирази сприймаються носіями мови як одне ціле, вони ніколи не відокремлюються розділовими знаками на письмі. Ви можете читати такі речення на одному диханні, без жодних зупинок чи інтонаційних пауз.

The second major exception relies on the subtle difference between conjunctions and particles. Sometimes, a gerund phrase is preceded by the letter *і* or *й*. While this usually acts as the conjunction "and", it can also function as an intensifying particle meaning "even" or "also". When *і* or *й* acts as this intensifier, it bonds the gerund phrase directly to the main clause, completely erasing the need for a comma.

This particle pulls the gerund so close to the main verb that separating them with punctuation would disrupt the intended emphasis. It is a nuanced stylistic choice often found in journalistic or persuasive writing.

**Це завдання можна виконати й не знаючи всіх тонкощів правил.** — *This task can be completed even without knowing all the intricacies of the rules.*

Дуже важливо розрізняти звичайний сполучник, який з'єднує частини речення, та підсилювальну частку. Якщо слово «і» або «й» можна легко замінити словом «навіть», це вірна ознака того, що перед нами саме частка. Вона настільки міцно прив'язує зворот до головного дієслова, що кома між ними стає граматично зайвою.

:::tip
**Did you know?** — The intensifying particle *й* is often used to show that an action is possible despite a specific limitation. If you can translate it as "even without doing X", you should drop the comma.
:::

The third and final exception involves a fascinating grammatical process called adverbialization. Sometimes, a single gerund stands directly after the main verb. Over time, because it sits so close to the verb and answers the question "how?" rather than "what doing?", it fundamentally transforms from a verb form into a pure adverb of manner. When this happens, it is no longer treated as a separate action, and therefore, it is not set off by commas.

**Старий чоловік стояв похилившись.** — *The old man stood leaning.*
**Студент сидів задумавшись.** — *The student sat lost in thought.*
**Лікарі кажуть, що шкідливо читати лежачи.** — *Doctors say that it is harmful to read lying down.*

It is absolutely critical to remember that this exception ONLY applies to bare, single gerunds standing alone after the verb. The magic is instantly broken the moment you add even one dependent word. If the gerund regains its modifiers, it reverts back into a full gerund phrase, and the standard golden rule of commas applies once again!

Якщо ви додасте хоча б одне залежне слово до такого дієприслівника, він одразу перетвориться на повноцінний зворот. У цей момент граматична магія зникає, і вам знову доведеться ставити кому згідно з основним правилом.

Compare these two nearly identical sentences to see how fragile this exception is:

**Він читав лежачи.** — *He read lying down.* (No comma: single gerund acting as an adverb).
**Він читав, лежачи на старому дивані.** — *He read, lying on the old sofa.* (Comma required: gerund phrase with dependent words).

Ultimately, punctuation in Ukrainian is deeply connected to the natural rhythm of speech. Commas are not just arbitrary marks on a page; they are visual representations of logical, rhythmic pauses. When you set off a phrase with commas, you are physically pausing your breath to give that secondary action its own independent weight in the narrative.

Розділові знаки слугують своєрідною музичною партитурою тексту. Коли ви розмовляєте українською, ви природно робите короткі зупинки саме там, де потрібні коми. І навпаки, коли ви вимовляєте сталий вираз, ваш голос тече плавно, що ідеально відображається відсутністю ком на папері.

> *Punctuation marks serve as a kind of musical score for the text. When you speak Ukrainian, you naturally make short stops exactly where commas are needed. Conversely, when you pronounce a fixed expression, your voice flows smoothly, which is perfectly reflected by the absence of commas on paper.*

To master these nuances, rely on the rhythm of your reading. Let's summarize the scenarios for quick reference:
- Standard gerund phrase? **Comma.**
- Single gerund acting as a separate action? **Comma.**
- Fixed idiom (*склавши руки*)? **No comma.**
- Phrase introduced by "even" (*й не знаючи*)? **No comma.**
- Single gerund meaning "how" (*стояв похилившись*)? **No comma.**

## Порівняння зворотів: дієприкметниковий vs дієприслівниковий (~715 words)

When analyzing complex Ukrainian sentences, learners often confuse the participial phrase and the gerund phrase. Both constructions look somewhat similar on the surface because they are both built around special verb forms, and they both pack a lot of descriptive information into a single sentence. However, their fundamental purpose in the sentence is completely different. The participial phrase acts as an **означення** (attribute). Its sole job is to describe a specific object or person, answering the question "which one?" or "what kind?". On the other hand, the gerund phrase functions as an **обставина** (adverbial modifier). It does not describe an object at all; instead, it describes the circumstances of the main action, answering questions like "how?", "when?", or "why?". To see this difference clearly, we need to look at how these phrases interact with the rest of the sentence.

Дієприкметниковий зворот завжди описує предмет і відповідає на питання «який?». Натомість дієприслівниковий зворот завжди описує дію і відповідає на питання «коли?» або «за яких умов?». Ви можете легко побачити цю різницю, якщо порівняєте дві схожі ситуації.

> *A participial phrase always describes an object and answers the question "which one?". In contrast, a gerund phrase always describes an action and answers the question "when?" or "under what conditions?". You can easily see this difference if you compare two similar situations.*

**Студент, прочитавши книгу, написав есе.** — *The student, having read the book, wrote an essay.* (When did he write it? This is a gerund phrase).

**Книга, прочитана студентом, лежала на столі.** — *The book read by the student lay on the table.* (Which book lay there? This is a participial phrase).

This difference in targets fundamentally changes the logic of the sentence. When you use a participial phrase, your mental focus is on a noun. You are painting a more detailed picture of a thing. When you use a gerund phrase, your mental focus shifts to the verb. You are explaining the background events that surround the main action. It is crucial to identify what exactly the phrase is attached to.

Кожен зворот має своє головне слово, до якого він кріпиться в реченні. Дієприкметниковий зворот не може існувати без іменника або займенника, який він характеризує. Дієприслівниковий зворот ніколи не описує предмети, тому його єдиним господарем є головне дієслово-присудок.

> *Every phrase has its main word to which it is attached in the sentence. A participial phrase cannot exist without a noun or pronoun that it characterizes. A gerund phrase never describes objects, so its only master is the main verb-predicate.*

Let's examine how this target shift changes the entire logic of a statement using the concept of a letter and a friend.

**Лист, написаний другом, нарешті прийшов поштою.** — *The letter written by a friend finally arrived in the mail.* (The target is the noun "лист". The phrase describes the letter).

**Написавши листа другу, він пішов на прогулянку.** — *Having written a letter to a friend, he went for a walk.* (The target is the verb "пішов". The phrase describes the circumstances of going for a walk).

Another critical area where these two grammatical structures diverge is punctuation. Specifically, we are talking about **відокремлення** (setting off with commas). You might remember from our previous modules that the participial phrase follows a very strict "Position Rule". If the participial phrase comes after the noun it modifies, you must use commas. However, if you place it before the noun, the commas completely disappear. The gerund phrase operates under a much simpler "Absolute Rule". Because it describes the verb, its physical position in the sentence does not change its punctuation. A **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase) is always set off by commas, no matter where you put it, unless it has fossilized into a **фразеологічний зворот** (phraseological expression) like **не покладаючи рук** (without rest) or **затамувавши подих** (holding one's breath).

Правила пунктуації для цих двох конструкцій кардинально відрізняються, що часто плутає студентів. Якщо дієприкметник стоїть перед своїм словом, ми читаємо речення без пауз і не ставимо жодних ком. Але дієприслівник вимагає відокремлення завжди, навіть якщо він стоїть на самому початку речення.

> *The punctuation rules for these two constructions differ radically, which often confuses students. If a participle stands before its word, we read the sentence without pauses and do not place any commas. But a gerund always requires setting off, even if it stands at the very beginning of the sentence.*

Let's look at the same visual sequence producing two different punctuation outcomes:

**Написана другом книга стала бестселером.** — *The book written by a friend became a bestseller.* (No comma: the participial phrase is before the noun "книга").

**Написавши книгу, друг вирішив відпочити.** — *Having written the book, the friend decided to rest.* (Comma required: the gerund phrase is always set off).

:::info
**Quick tip** — If you are unsure whether to use a comma at the beginning of a sentence, check the last word of the introductory phrase. If it is a noun, you are likely dealing with a participial phrase without commas. If it is a gerund, you absolutely need a comma!
:::

One of the most reliable tests you can use to distinguish between these two phrases is the logical transformation test. Because they serve different functions, they map onto completely different types of subordinate clauses. A participial phrase is essentially a shortened version of an adjective clause. Therefore, you can always expand it back into a full clause using the relative pronoun «який» (which/that). Conversely, a gerund phrase is a compressed time or condition clause. You can unpack it by using conjunctions like «коли» (when) or «після того як» (after).

Щоб перевірити себе, спробуйте перетворити зворот на звичайне підрядне речення. Це найкращий спосіб зрозуміти справжню логіку вашого висловлювання. Якщо ви можете підставити слово «який», це точно означення. Якщо ж туди ідеально пасує слово «коли», перед вами обставина.

> *To check yourself, try transforming the phrase into a regular subordinate clause. This is the best way to understand the true logic of your statement. If you can substitute the word "which", it is definitely an attribute. But if the word "when" fits perfectly, you are looking at a modifier.*

Transformation of a participial phrase:
**Книга, написана студентом...** transforms into **Книга, яку написав студент...** — *The book that the student wrote...*

Transformation of a gerund phrase:
**Написавши книгу, студент...** transforms into **Коли студент написав книгу, він...** — *When the student wrote the book, he...*

To consolidate your understanding, let's look at a summary comparison of these two grammatical powerhouses. This table highlights all the key differences you need to keep in mind when choosing how to structure your advanced Ukrainian sentences. We will use the examples of closed doors to illustrate the final points.

| Feature | Дієприкметниковий зворот | Дієприслівниковий зворот |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Question** | Який? Яка? Яке? Які? | Як? Коли? Чому? |
| **Modifies** | Іменник або займенник | Дієслово-присудок |
| **Syntactic Role** | **Означення** (attribute) | **Обставина** (modifier) |
| **Punctuation** | Depends on position | Always set off with commas |
| **Clause Equivalent**| *який*-clause | *коли*-clause |
| **Example** | **Зачинені вчора двері** (No comma) | **Зачинивши двері, я пішов** (Comma) |

Mastering the difference between these two structures will dramatically improve both your reading comprehension and your writing style. You will know exactly when to describe a noun and when to add depth to an action.

## Помилки та стилістика (~715 words)

The most notorious mistake learners make when using a **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase) is creating what grammarians call a "dangling gerund". Because this structure functions as an **обставина** (adverbial modifier) that describes the main verb, the action it depicts must absolutely be performed by the subject of the main sentence. If the subject of the main clause is not the entity performing the action in the gerund phrase, the entire sentence breaks down logically. This logical disconnect often happens when learners translate directly from English or when they rely on impersonal constructions where the true "doer" of the action is hidden in the dative case.

**Перейшовши дорогу, мені стало легше.** — *Incorrect: Who crossed? The feeling of relief did not cross the street.*
**Перейшовши дорогу, я відчув полегшення.** — *Correct: Having crossed the road, I felt relief.*

Even when your sentence features a clear, unambiguous nominative subject, you must remain vigilant about subtle semantic mismatches. Sometimes, a learner correctly attaches a gerund phrase to the main clause, but the subject of that clause is completely incapable of performing the gerund's action in reality. This creates unintentional comedy and confuses native speakers. To resolve this issue, you cannot simply move the phrase around; you must unpack the gerund phrase back into a full, independent subordinate clause using time conjunctions like «коли» (when) or «поки» (while).

**Готуючи вечерю, кіт швидко забіг на кухню.** — *Incorrect: The fluffy cat did not cook dinner!*
**Коли я готував вечерю, кіт швидко забіг на кухню.** — *Correct: When I was preparing dinner, the cat quickly ran into the kitchen.*

:::info
**Grammar box** — Always double-check the subject of your main clause. Ask yourself: "Is the noun performing the main verb ALSO performing the action in the gerund phrase?" If the answer is no, the sentence is grammatically invalid.
:::

Beyond logical errors, we must consider stylistic weight. Overusing gerund phrases is a hallmark of "канцелярит" (bureaucratese) — an overly formal, heavy way of writing. While a well-placed **означення** (attribute — syntactic role of дієприкметниковий зворот) adds elegance, and proper **відокремлення** (setting off with commas) ensures clarity, stringing too many modifiers together suffocates your prose. Ukrainian literary tradition values rhythm and dynamic verbs.

Прокинувшись рано вранці, швидко вмившись, нашвидкуруч поснідавши, зібравши свої речі та вийшовши з дому, я пішов на роботу. Таке речення граматично правильне і розділові знаки стоять на своїх місцях, але воно звучить важко. Українська мова не любить такого нагромадження. Набагато краще використати кілька простих речень. Природний варіант: я прокинувся рано вранці, швидко вмився і поснідав. Потім я зібрав свої речі та вийшов на роботу.

> *Having woken up early in the morning, having quickly washed, having hastily eaten breakfast, having gathered my things and having left the house, I went to work. Such a sentence is grammatically correct and the punctuation marks are in their places, but it sounds extremely heavy. The Ukrainian language does not like such accumulation. It is much better to use a few simple sentences. The natural version: I woke up early in the morning, quickly washed, and had breakfast. Then I gathered my things and left for work.*

Register awareness is crucial when deciding whether to deploy these advanced grammatical structures. Gerund phrases belong primarily to the literary, scientific, and journalistic styles of communication. In written texts, they are incredibly powerful tools; they allow writers to compress background information gracefully, depict simultaneous actions without being repetitive, and establish a highly sophisticated tone. However, in everyday colloquial speech, Ukrainians rarely speak in such complex, layered sentences. Instead, they strongly prefer straightforward coordination and simple sentence structures. You might occasionally encounter gerunds in daily life, but usually only as a fixed **фразеологічний зворот** (phraseological expression — exception to comma rule). In these idiomatic cases, people might work **не покладаючи рук** (without rest — phraseological, no commas) to finish a project, or listen to an exciting story **затамувавши подих** (holding one's breath — phraseological, no commas). Because these are set phrases, they feel natural even in casual conversations.

Finally, we must address a crucial decolonization note regarding sentence structure. A legacy of Russian linguistic influence is the pervasive substitution of coordinate verbs with gerunds. In authentic Ukrainian, however, the sequence of two separate verbs is often far more natural.

Коли ви хочете описати дві дії, які відбуваються одночасно, не бійтеся використовувати звичайні дієслова. Замість російської кальки «я сидів на дивані, читаючи книгу», набагато краще сказати: «я сидів на дивані і читав книгу». Ця проста конструкція з двома присудками є дуже характерною для української мови. Вона звучить мелодійно і не перевантажує ваше мовлення.

> *When you want to describe two actions happening simultaneously, do not be afraid to use regular verbs. Instead of the Russian calque "I sat on the sofa, reading a book", it is much better to say: "I sat on the sofa and read a book". This simple structure with two predicates is very characteristic of the Ukrainian language. It sounds melodious and does not overload your speech.*

## Iнтегрована практика (~770 words)

Сонце повільно сходило над містом, яскраво освітлюючи порожні вулиці, старовинні площі та високі будинки. Зібравшись із силами, молодий марафонець вийшов на старт. Він стояв на лінії, глибоко дихаючи та подумки повторюючи стратегію забігу. Глядачі, зібрані навколо траси, з нетерпінням чекали початку змагань. Почувши гучний стартовий сигнал, сотні спортсменів рвонули вперед. Подолавши половину дистанції, хлопець відчув втому, але продовжував бігти. Випередивши головного суперника на останньому кілометрі, він гордо перетнув фінішну лінію. Марафон завершився, залишивши учасникам незабутні емоції. Хлопець щиро усміхався, приймаючи вітання від тренерів, родичів і друзів.

> *The sun was slowly rising over the city, brightly illuminating the empty streets, ancient squares, and tall buildings. Having gathered his strength, the young marathon runner stepped up to the start. He stood on the line, breathing deeply and mentally repeating the race strategy. The spectators, gathered around the track, eagerly awaited the start of the competition. Having heard the loud starting signal, hundreds of athletes dashed forward. Having overcome half the distance, the guy felt tired, but continued to run. Having overtaken his main rival in the last kilometer, he proudly crossed the finish line. The marathon ended, leaving the participants with unforgettable emotions. The guy smiled sincerely, accepting congratulations from coaches, relatives, and friends.*

Let's analyze the text above. It contains a rich mix of advanced grammatical structures that elevate the narrative. How do we distinguish between them in practice? Remember that a **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase) always answers the questions *how?*, *when?*, or *under what condition?*. In the sentence, it plays the syntactic role of an **обставина** (adverbial modifier — syntactic role), providing the background circumstances of the main action. For example, «Зібравшись із силами» explains exactly *when* the runner stepped up to the start. «Освітлюючи порожні вулиці» describes the manner in which the sun was rising. These phrases give the verbs additional depth and context.

In contrast, a participle phrase plays the syntactic role of an **означення** (attribute — syntactic role of дієприкметниковий зворот). It answers the question *which?* or *what kind of?* and strictly describes a noun. In the text, «Глядачі, зібрані навколо траси» uses a participle phrase to specify exactly *which* spectators were waiting. Understanding this fundamental difference in syntactic roles will prevent you from mixing up their distinct punctuation rules.

Now it is time to practice creating these structures yourself. The ability to combine simple, choppy sentences into flowing narrative arcs is a vital skill for B1 learners. It demonstrates that you can think in longer, more complex Ukrainian thoughts. Look at the following five pairs of sentences. Your task is to combine each pair into a single, elegant sentence by turning one of the verbs into a **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase). Pay close attention to the **відокремлення** (setting off with commas) rules as you write.

1. Хлопець уважно прочитав детальну інструкцію. Він одразу почав збирати нові меблі.
2. Втомлені туристи повільно йшли темним лісом. Вони тихо співали українські народні пісні.
3. Олена радісно повернулася додому після роботи. Вона швидко зателефонувала своїй мамі.
4. Журналіст дуже уважно слухав відомого політика. Він робив детальні нотатки у своєму блокноті.
5. Водій раптом побачив червоне світло світлофора. Він різко зупинив свій автомобіль перед пішохідним переходом.

:::tip
**Transformation Check** — Did you ensure that the action in your newly created gerund phrase is performed by the exact same subject as the main verb of the sentence? If the subjects do not match perfectly, you have accidentally created a dangling gerund!
:::

To further clarify the difference between the two types of phrases, let's practice transforming a relative clause into an attribute. Take the complex sentence: «Ми із задоволенням прочитали статтю, яку написав відомий київський журналіст.» To make this sound more formal and academic, we can change the «який» clause into a passive participle phrase: «Ми із задоволенням прочитали статтю, написану відомим київським журналістом.»

Пунктуація може здаватися складною, коли в одному реченні зустрічаються різноманітні сталі вирази або кілька відокремлених зворотів поспіль. Однак правила насправді дуже логічні. Запам'ятайте головний виняток: якщо **фразеологічний зворот** (phraseological expression — exception to comma rule) має значення обставини способу дії, він ніколи не вимагає відокремлення комами. Наприклад, досвідчені будівельники працювали **не покладаючи рук** (without rest — phraseological, no commas), щоб вчасно закінчити новий об'єкт. Захоплені глядачі дивилися неймовірну театральну виставу **затамувавши подих** (holding one's breath — phraseological, no commas). Ці вирази сприймаються як одне неподільне ціле, тому коми зруйнували б їхню цілісність.

> *Punctuation can seem extremely complicated when various fixed expressions or several isolated phrases in a row appear in one sentence. However, the rules are actually very logical. Remember the main exception: if a phraseological expression has the meaning of an adverb of manner, it never requires setting off with commas. For example, experienced builders worked without rest to finish the new object on time. The enthralled audience watched the incredible theatrical play holding their breath. These expressions are perceived as one indivisible whole, so commas would destroy their integrity.*

What happens when we use the coordinating conjunction «і» (and) with these structures? If two gerund phrases refer to the exact same main verb and are connected by a single «і», we treat them like any other homogeneous sentence members. We do not put a comma between them. Consider this sentence: «Він ішов вулицею, щиро усміхаючись і тримаючи в руках яскраві квіти.» The comma comes before the first gerund to separate the whole block from the main clause, but absolute rules dictate that no comma breaks the «і» joining the two modifiers.

Your final, culminating challenge for this module is to write a short, cohesive narrative of six to eight sentences. Imagine you have just returned from a wonderful weekend trip to a historically rich Ukrainian city, such as Lviv, Chernivtsi, Ivano-Frankivsk, or Odesa. You are writing a travel journal entry or a public blog post to share your vivid experiences with your friends.

In your text, you must deliberately incorporate exactly three gerund phrases to describe your sequence of actions. For example, you might describe what you observed while strolling through the narrow cobblestone streets, or what you felt after finally arriving at the central train station. Additionally, you must include exactly two participle phrases to describe the static environment around you. Use them to detail the majestic architecture of the old buildings or the cozy, welcoming atmosphere of the local coffee shops.

:::info
**Stylistic Balance** — Do not overload your text with heavy grammar! Use gerund phrases strategically to compress background actions and avoid repetitive verbs, but balance them with simple, dynamic sentences to keep your Ukrainian prose natural, modern, and highly readable.
:::

When drafting your narrative, pay special, meticulous attention to the punctuation rules we have studied. Ensure that every gerund phrase is properly isolated with commas, unless it happens to be a set idiom acting as an adverb of manner. Verify that every participle phrase is punctuated correctly based solely on whether it stands before or after the specific noun it modifies. Most importantly, when you finish writing, read your entire text aloud. Does it sound like a natural, flowing Ukrainian narrative, or does it feel artificially heavy and bureaucratic? Use the rules of **відокремлення** (setting off with commas) to guide the natural rhythm and intonation of your reading.

## Підсумок та перехід до M64 (~440 words)

Let's review everything we have learned in this module. A **дієприслівниковий зворот** (gerund phrase) is simply a gerund combined with any dependent words that belong to it. Syntactically, its main function in a sentence is to act as an **обставина** (adverbial modifier — syntactic role), answering questions like "how?", "when?", or "why?" the main action occurred. Contrast this with the participial phrase, whose role is always an **означення** (attribute — syntactic role of дієприкметниковий зворот). The primary takeaway for punctuation is beautifully simple. You must almost always use commas to set these adverbial modifiers off from the rest of the sentence, regardless of their position.

When editing your own Ukrainian writing, you must always be aware of the specific exceptions to the punctuation rules. You can use a quick mental checklist to verify your punctuation around gerunds.

:::info
**Exception Quick-Check List**
- Is it a **фразеологічний зворот** (phraseological expression — exception to comma rule)? Examples include idioms like **не покладаючи рук** (without rest — phraseological, no commas) or **затамувавши подих** (holding one's breath — phraseological, no commas). If yes, no comma is needed.
- Is there an intensifying particle «і» або «й» standing immediately before the phrase to emphasize it? If yes, do not separate the particle from the phrase with a comma.
- Is it a single, standalone gerund placed immediately after the main verb that answers the question "how?" and acts just like a regular adverb? If yes, leave the comma out.
- If the answer to all three of these questions is no, then you are looking at a regular gerund phrase. In that case, the rule is absolute: you must always use commas.
:::

Чи пам’ятаю я про небезпечний «звисаючий» дієприслівник і правило спільного підмета? Чи вмію я за потреби замінити складний зворот звичайним підрядним реченням, щоб зробити текст легшим для читання? Якщо ви впевнено відповіли «так» на всі ці запитання, ви чудово засвоїли матеріал.

Do I remember the dangerous "dangling" gerund and the shared subject rule? Do I know how to replace a complex phrase with a regular subordinate clause when necessary to make the text easier to read? If you confidently answered "yes" to all these questions, you have mastered the material perfectly.*
