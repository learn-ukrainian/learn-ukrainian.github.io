## Пане, пані: формальне звертання (Formal Address) (~580 words total)
- P1 (~80 words): [Intro: Reconnecting with the basic Vocative from A1 (Оксано, Іване). Introduce the shift to professional and formal registers in A2. Explain that in modern Ukraine, addressing someone by "Pani" or "Pane" is the standard for polite, respectful interaction.]
- P2 (~110 words): [The "Pane + Profession" pattern for men. Explain that both "пане" and the title must be in the vocative case. Examples: "пане директоре" (director), "пане професоре" (professor), "пане міністре" (minister). Contrast with the incorrect nominative "пане директор".]
- P3 (~110 words): [The "Pani + Profession" pattern for women. Explain that "пані" is invariable, but the title changes to its vocative form (usually ending in -о). Examples: "пані вчителько" (teacher), "пані лікарко" (doctor), "пані директорко" (director). Note the natural use of feminitives in modern Ukrainian.]
- P4 (~120 words): [Addressing people by surname. For men: "Пане" + surname in vocative (пане Ковальчуку, пане Шевченку). Note that masculine surnames decline like nouns. For women: "Пані" + surname. Note that feminine surnames ending in a consonant (Ковальчук) or -о (Шевченко) do not decline in the vocative: "пані Ковальчук", "пані Шевченко".]
- P5 (~100 words): [Cultural context: The decolonial choice of "Пан/Пані". Explain how these terms were suppressed during the Soviet era in favor of "товариш" (comrade) and why their return signifies European identity and linguistic dignity. Mention the literary/diaspora alternatives "добродію" and "добродійко" as polite forms of "sir/madam".]
- Dialogue (~60 words): [A formal encounter at a clinic. Patient: "Добрий день, пане лікарю!" Doctor: "Добрий день, пане Ковальчуку. Проходьте, сідайте."]

## Професійні звертання (Professional Vocative) (~510 words total)
- P1 (~100 words): [Masculine profession endings in the Vocative. Detail the soft vs. hard stem distinction for nouns in the 2nd declension. Soft stems (and those ending in -р with soft endings) take -у/-ю: лікарю (doctor), вчителю (teacher), водію (driver), секретарю (secretary). Hard stems take -е: професоре (professor), інженере (engineer).]
- P2 (~90 words): [Feminine profession endings. Most feminine titles end in -а, which changes to -о in the Vocative. Examples: лікарка → лікарко, вчителька → вчителько, журналістка → журналістко. This is a consistent and predictable pattern.]
- P3 (~80 words): [Special cases and common gender. Explain that "колега" (colleague) ends in -о for both genders: "колего". Mention "суддя" (judge), which takes the ending -ю: "пане/пані суддю". Mention "майстер" → "майстре" as a common professional address.]

## Друже мій, люба моя: емоційний кличний (Emotional Vocative) (~560 words total)
- P1 (~100 words): [Affectionate address between friends and partners. Explain the forms "друже" (friend, masc) and "подруго" (friend, fem). Introduce "любий" (dear, masc) and "люба" (dear, fem), and "коханий/кохана" (beloved).]
- P2 (~120 words): [The magic of Ukrainian diminutives in the Vocative. Explain how suffixes like -енько, -ечко, -онько create warmth. Examples: "серденько" (sweetheart), "сонечко" (sunshine), "зіронько" (little star), "донечко" (daughter), "синку" (son). Emphasize that this is not "baby talk" but a standard way to express affection in Ukrainian culture.]
- P3 (~110 words): [Word order with possessive pronouns. This is a critical rule: in the Vocative, the possessive pronoun (мій, моя) MUST follow the noun for a natural, emotional effect. Example: "друже мій" (not *мій друже), "мамо моя" (not *моя мамо), "люба моя" (not *моя люба). This creates a poetic and intimate tone.]
- P4 (~80 words): [Religious and habitual exclamations. Explain "Боже мій!" (My God!) as an exclamation of surprise or distress. Mention "Мамо!" and "Господи!" as common emotional outbursts. Note that the plural Vocative for most nouns is the same as the Nominative: "Друзі!" (Friends!).]
