## –ѓ–Ї —Ж–µ –њ–Њ—П—Б–љ—О—О—В—М —Г —И–Ї–Њ–ї—Ц (How Schools Teach This)
The topic of mail and addresses is introduced early and revisited with increasing complexity. The focus is practical: how to correctly write an address to ensure delivery.

- **Primary School (2-4 –Ї–ї–∞—Б):** The initial introduction focuses on the concept of a letter (`–ї–Є—Б—В`) and its purpose. Students learn basic components like writing a letter to a friend or Santa Claus (–Ф—Ц–і –Ь–Њ—А–Њ–Ј / –°–≤—П—В–Є–є –Ь–Є–Ї–Њ–ї–∞–є) (Source 12, 26). The structure is simple: greeting, body, closing, and signature (Source 43). They learn what a post office (`–њ–Њ—И—В–∞`) and postbox (`–њ–Њ—И—В–Њ–≤–∞ —Б–Ї—А–Є–љ—М–Ї–∞`) are. Practical skills include understanding who the sender (`–≤—Ц–і–њ—А–∞–≤–љ–Є–Ї`) and receiver (`–Њ–і–µ—А–ґ—Г–≤–∞—З`) are and the importance of a clear address (Source 8, 27). The concept of an electronic letter (`–µ–ї–µ–Ї—В—А–Њ–љ–љ–Є–є –ї–Є—Б—В`) is also introduced as a modern equivalent (Source 8, 26).

- **Middle School (5-7 –Ї–ї–∞—Б):** The focus shifts to the official, standardized format of a postal address (`–њ–Њ—И—В–Њ–≤–∞ –∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞`) as required by Ukrposhta (`–£–Ї—А–њ–Њ—И—В–∞`). Textbooks provide explicit rules and templates for filling out envelopes and forms (Source 24, 20, 25). They define key terms like `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞—В` (recipient) and `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞–љ—В` (sender), and the distinction between `–∞–і—А–µ—Б` (a formal written greeting) and `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞` (a physical location) is made explicit (Source 24, 31, 45). The components of an address are broken down: street, building number, apartment number, city, postal code (`—Ц–љ–і–µ–Ї—Б`), etc. (Source 20). The grammar involves correct use of abbreviations (`–≤—Г–ї.`, `–Љ.`, `–Ї–≤.`) and the cases required for location.

- **High School (8-11 –Ї–ї–∞—Б):** The topic is integrated into business communication (`–і—Ц–ї–Њ–≤–µ –ї–Є—Б—В—Г–≤–∞–љ–љ—П`). Students learn to compose various types of formal and informal letters, including letters of invitation (`–ї–Є—Б—В-–Ј–∞–њ—А–Њ—И–µ–љ–љ—П`) and official notices (`–ї–Є—Б—В-–њ–Њ–≤—Ц–і–Њ–Љ–ї–µ–љ–љ—П`) (Source 18, 19, 32, 39). The emphasis is on using the correct style (officially-business), tone, and structure (Source 18). Writing an address for international mail is also covered (Source 20). The concept extends to electronic communication, detailing the structure of an email (`–µ–ї–µ–Ї—В—А–Њ–љ–љ–Є–є –ї–Є—Б—В`) and the importance of a clear subject line and professional tone (Source 29).

Throughout the curriculum, the practical goal remains the same: "–Т—Ц–і —В–Њ–≥–Њ, –љ–∞—Б–Ї—Ц–ї—М–Ї–Є –њ—А–∞–≤–Є–ї—М–љ–Њ –љ–∞–њ–Є—Б–∞–љ–∞ –∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞ –љ–∞ –њ–Њ—И—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Љ—Г –≤—Ц–і–њ—А–∞–≤–ї–µ–љ–љ—Ц, –Ј–∞–ї–µ–ґ–Є—В—М –≥–∞—А–∞–љ—В—Ц—П –є–Њ–≥–Њ —Б–≤–Њ—Ф—З–∞—Б–љ–Њ—Ч –і–Њ—Б—В–∞–≤–Ї–Є –Њ–і–µ—А–ґ—Г–≤–∞—З—Г" (The guarantee of its timely delivery to the recipient depends on how correctly the address is written on the postal item) (Source 8).

## –І–∞—Б—В–Њ—В–љ—Ц—Б—В—М —Ц –њ—А—Ц–Њ—А–Є—В–µ—В–Є (Frequency & Priorities)
For an A2 learner, the primary goal is functional communication. The highest priority items are the phrases needed to complete the two core tasks: sending and receiving.

**High Priority (Must Master):**
1.  **Stating Intent:** The formula `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г + Perfective Infinitive` is fundamental.
    *   `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г –Ј–∞–±—А–∞—В–Є –њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї—Г.` (I want to pick up a parcel.) (Source 1)
    *   `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г –≤—Ц–і–њ—А–∞–≤–Є—В–Є –њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї—Г.` (I want to send a parcel.) (Source 1)
2.  **Asking for Location/Possibility:** The construction `–Ф–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ + Perfective Infinitive` is equally crucial.
    *   `–Ф–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ –љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є –ї–Є—Б—В—Ц–≤–Ї—Г?` (Where can I send a postcard?) (Source 3)
    *   `–Ф–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ –Ј–љ–∞–є—В–Є —Б—В–∞—А–Є–є —Д–Њ—В–Њ–∞–њ–∞—А–∞—В?` (Where can I find an old camera?) (Source 3)
3.  **Core Vocabulary:** `–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї–∞`, `–ї–Є—Б—В`, `–њ–Њ—И—В–∞`, `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞`, `–љ–Њ–Љ–µ—А —В–µ–ї–µ—Д–Њ–љ—Г`.
4.  **Providing Information:** Being able to state a name, city, and phone number is essential for sending items.
    *   `–Ъ–Њ–Љ—Г –≤—Ц–і–њ—А–∞–≤–ї—П—Ф–Љ–Њ?` -> `–Ю–≥–Њ–є–Ї–Њ –Р–љ–љ–∞ –Т–∞—Б–Є–ї—Ц–≤–љ–∞, –Љ—Ц—Б—В–Њ –Я–Њ–ї–Њ–љ–љ–µ...` (Source 1)

**Medium Priority (Should Know):**
1.  **Understanding Clerk's Instructions:**
    *   `–Ґ—А–Є–Љ–∞–є—В–µ –≤–∞—И –њ–∞–Ї—Г–љ–Њ–Ї.` (Here is your package.) (Source 1)
    *   `–Я–Њ—Б—В–∞–≤—В–µ —В—Г—В —Б–≤—Ц–є –њ—Ц–і–њ–Є—Б.` (Put your signature here.) (Source 1)
2.  **Key Details:**
    *   `–Ю—Б—В–∞–љ–љ—Ц —З–Њ—В–Є—А–Є —Ж–Є—Д—А–Є –љ–∞–Ї–ї–∞–і–љ–Њ—Ч...` (The last four digits of the consignment note...) (Source 1)
    *   `–Ю–≥–Њ–ї–Њ—И–µ–љ–∞ –≤–∞—А—В—Ц—Б—В—М.` (Declared value.) (Source 1)
3.  **Distinguishing Aspect:** Understanding the difference between a process (`—П –Ј–∞–≤–ґ–і–Є –Ї—Г–њ—Г—О`) and a one-time action (`–і–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ –Ї—Г–њ–Є—В–Є`). (Source 3)

**Low Priority (Can Wait):**
1.  Formal business letter terminology (`–ї–Є—Б—В-–Ј–∞–њ—А–Њ—И–µ–љ–љ—П`, `—А–µ–Ї–≤—Ц–Ј–Є—В–Є`, etc.) (Source 18, 19).
2.  The subtle difference between `–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї–∞` and `–њ–∞–Ї—Г–љ–Њ–Ї`. For A2, they are largely interchangeable.
3.  Writing a full, grammatically perfect letter in Ukrainian. The focus is on the transaction at the post office itself.

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)
The language of mail and communication contains several key distinctions from Russian that are critical for decolonized pedagogy. Presenting Ukrainian on its own terms, without reference to Russian as a default, is essential.

1.  **Verb `–Э–∞–і—Б–Є–ї–∞—В–Є` vs. `–Я–Њ—Б–Є–ї–∞—В–Є`**: The primary Ukrainian verb for sending mail (letters, emails, postcards) is `–љ–∞–і—Б–Є–ї–∞—В–Є / –љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є`. The Russian equivalent is `–њ–Њ—Б—Л–ї–∞—В—М / –њ–Њ—Б–ї–∞—В—М`. While Ukrainian has the verb `–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–∞—В–Є`, it's more often used in the sense of "to send a person somewhere" or in abstract/figurative senses, not typically for mail. Using `–љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є` for letters is a clear marker of authentic Ukrainian.
    *   Example: `–Ф–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ **–љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є** –ї–Є—Б—В—Ц–≤–Ї—Г?` (Source 3) is correct Ukrainian. A Russian speaker might incorrectly construct `*–њ–Њ—Б–ї–∞—В–Є –ї–Є—Б—В—Ц–≤–Ї—Г`.

2.  **Prepositional Phrases (`–њ–Њ—И—В–Њ—О`, `–Ј–∞ –∞–і—А–µ—Б–Њ—О`)**: As noted in the errors section, Ukrainian uses the Instrumental case (`–њ–Њ—И—В–Њ—О`) and the preposition `–Ј–∞` with the Instrumental (`–Ј–∞ –∞–і—А–µ—Б–Њ—О`). Russian uses different constructions: "–њ–Њ –њ–Њ—З—В–µ" and "–њ–Њ –∞–і—А–µ—Б—Г". Teaching the Ukrainian forms directly, without comparison, establishes them as the norm.
    *   Correct Ukrainian: `–Э–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є **–њ–Њ—И—В–Њ—О**.` (Source 40)
    *   Correct Ukrainian: `–Ґ–µ–Ј–Є –і–Њ–њ–Њ–≤—Ц–і–µ–є –њ—А–Њ—Б–Є–Љ–Њ –љ–∞–і—Б–Є–ї–∞—В–Є **–Ј–∞ –∞–і—А–µ—Б–Њ—О**.` (Source 14)

3.  **Vocabulary (`–њ–∞–Ї—Г–љ–Њ–Ї`)**: Ukrainian frequently uses `–њ–∞–Ї—Г–љ–Њ–Ї` (package) alongside `–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї–∞` (parcel). While Russian has `–њ–∞–Ї–µ—В`, `–њ–∞–Ї—Г–љ–Њ–Ї` is distinctly common in this context in Ukrainian.
    *   Example: `—В—А–Є–Љ–∞–є—В–µ –≤–∞—И **–њ–∞–Ї—Г–љ–Њ–Ї**` (Source 1)

4.  **False Friends (`–∞–і—А–µ—Б` / `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞`)**: The distinction between `–∞–і—А–µ—Б` (formal greeting) and `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞` (location) is a crucial point of Ukrainian usage (Source 24, 31). In Russian, `–∞–і—А–µ—Б` covers both meanings, with the "formal greeting" sense being somewhat archaic. Emphasizing the `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞` form for location is key.

5.  **Pedagogical Approach**: Never present Ukrainian forms as "different from Russian." Frame them as the standard. For example, state "The verb `–і—П–Ї—Г–≤–∞—В–Є` takes the Dative case" (Source 40), not "Unlike in Russian, `–і—П–Ї—Г–≤–∞—В–Є` takes the Dative." The Ukrainian system is the reference point.

## –†–µ–Ї–Њ–Љ–µ–љ–і–∞—Ж—Ц—Ч –і–ї—П –≤–њ—А–∞–≤ (Activity Concepts)

**Phase 1: Core Vocabulary & Concepts**
*   **Matching:** Provide images of a letter, parcel, post office, stamp, envelope, and postbox. Students match them with the words: `–ї–Є—Б—В`, `–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї–∞`, `–њ–Њ—И—В–∞`, `–Љ–∞—А–Ї–∞`, `–Ї–Њ–љ–≤–µ—А—В`, `–њ–Њ—И—В–Њ–≤–∞ —Б–Ї—А–Є–љ—М–Ї–∞`.
*   **Categorization:** Give students a list of terms: `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞—В`, `–≤—Ц–і–њ—А–∞–≤–љ–Є–Ї`, `–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї–∞`, `–ї–Є—Б—В`, `–Њ–і–µ—А–ґ—Г–≤–∞—З`, `–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞–љ—В`. They must group them into pairs of synonyms (`–Њ–і–µ—А–ґ—Г–≤–∞—З/–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞—В`, `–≤—Ц–і–њ—А–∞–≤–љ–Є–Ї/–∞–і—А–µ—Б–∞–љ—В`) and objects (`–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї–∞`, `–ї–Є—Б—В`).

**Phase 2: Building Basic Functional Phrases**
*   **Sentence Scramble:** Give students scrambled words to form the core questions and statements. Ex: `–њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї—Г / —Е–Њ—З—Г / –ѓ / –Ј–∞–±—А–∞—В–Є` -> `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г –Ј–∞–±—А–∞—В–Є –њ–Њ—Б–Є–ї–Ї—Г.`
*   **Fill-in-the-Blank (Aspect):** Create sentences where students must choose between the imperfective and perfective verb.
    *   `–Ч–∞–Ј–≤–Є—З–∞–є —П ___ (–љ–∞–і—Б–Є–ї–∞—О/–љ–∞–і—Ц—И–ї—О) –ї–Є—Б—В–Є –≤—А–∞–љ—Ж—Ц.` -> `–љ–∞–і—Б–Є–ї–∞—О`
    *   `–°—М–Њ–≥–Њ–і–љ—Ц —П —Е–Њ—З—Г ___ (–љ–∞–і—Б–Є–ї–∞—О/–љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є) —Ж—О –ї–Є—Б—В—Ц–≤–Ї—Г.` -> `–љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є`
    *   This directly reinforces the concept from Source 3.

**Phase 3: Dialogue and Simulation**
*   **Role-Play:** One student is the postal clerk (`–Њ–њ–µ—А–∞—В–Њ—А`), the other is the customer (`–Ї–ї—Ц—Ф–љ—В`). Provide a script skeleton based on the dialogue in Source 1. The customer must state they want to send a parcel, and the clerk must ask for the recipient's details (name, city, phone number).
*   **Form Filling:** Create a simplified version of a postal form or envelope template (like in Source 24, 25). Give students a scenario (e.g., "You are sending a birthday card to your friend Ivan Petrenko in Kyiv"). They must correctly fill in the `–Т—Ц–і–њ—А–∞–≤–љ–Є–Ї` (Sender) and `–Ю–і–µ—А–ґ—Г–≤–∞—З` (Recipient) fields with the correct address structure.

## –Ч–≤'—П–Ј–Ї–Є –Ј —Ц–љ—И–Є–Љ–Є —В–µ–Љ–∞–Љ–Є (Connections)

*   **Prerequisites:**

    *   **Dative Case:** Needed for indicating the indirect object, i.e., to whom something is given or sent (`–љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є **–≤–∞–Љ** SMS`, `–і—П–Ї—Г—О **–≤–∞–Љ**`).

    *   **Verbal Aspect (–Ф–Њ–Ї–Њ–љ–∞–љ–Є–є/–Э–µ–і–Њ–Ї–Њ–љ–∞–љ–Є–є –≤–Є–і):** Crucial for distinguishing between a one-time action (`–љ–∞–і—Ц—Б–ї–∞—В–Є`) and a repeated action or process (`–љ–∞–і—Б–Є–ї–∞–ї–∞`). This is explicitly taught in Source 3.

# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: Checkpoint Communication



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

Checkpoint communication modules serve as cumulative reviews where learners integrate previously learned grammar and vocabulary into practical, goal-oriented dialogues. The pedagogical approach, as seen in Ukrainian educational materials, is heavily context-driven and functional. Dialogues are not abstract grammar drills; they model real-life situations like making plans, inviting friends, and arranging meetings (Source 1, 2, 10).

The core method is built around listening to or reading a model dialogue, then breaking it down into functional chunks. For instance, the Ukrainian Lessons Podcast (ULP) consistently presents a full dialogue first, asks comprehension questions, and only then dissects the grammar and vocabulary (Source 1, 10, 18). This "whole-to-part" approach mirrors natural language acquisition.

Ukrainian textbooks for native speakers also emphasize dialogue as a central activity. They use dialogues to introduce new concepts and then immediately ask students to analyze, transform, or role-play them (Source 7, 12, 31). The goal is active use, not passive knowledge. Exercises often involve transforming a dialogue into a monologue, which forces the learner to process and re-synthesize the information, confirming comprehension (Source 6, 12).

Greetings and politeness are foundational. Materials for both native speakers and L2 learners introduce the distinction between formal and informal address early on, using vocative case for names (`–Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В, –Ю–ї—О`) and polite forms for strangers or elders (`–®–∞–љ–Њ–≤–љ–Є–є –°–µ—А–≥—Ц—О –Т–∞—Б–Є–ї—М–Њ–≤–Є—З—Г`) (Source 3, 7, 27). This isn't just a grammar point; it's a core element of communicative competence in Ukrainian culture.

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

A logical sequence for an A1 learner to acquire checkpoint communication skills should build from simple exchanges to more complex negotiations.

1.  **Step 1: Basic Greetings, Farewells, and Politeness Formulas.** This is the absolute foundation.
    *   **Informal:** `–Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В!` (Hello!), `–Я–∞-–њ–∞!` (Bye-bye!) (Source 1).
    *   **Formal/Neutral:** `–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М!` (Good day!), `–Ф–Њ –њ–Њ–±–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П!` (Goodbye!) (Source 19, 27).
    *   **Core Politeness:** `–Ф—П–Ї—Г—О` (Thank you), `–С—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞` (Please/You're welcome) (Source 1, 19).

2.  **Step 2: Core Status Check Questions.** The most common conversational openers.
    *   `–ѓ–Ї —Б–њ—А–∞–≤–Є?` (How are things?), `–ѓ–Ї —В–Є?` (How are you?) (Source 1, 2).
    *   Common responses: `–Т—Б–µ –і–Њ–±—А–µ`, `–Э–Њ—А–Љ–∞–ї—М–љ–Њ`, `–І—Г–і–Њ–≤–Њ` (Source 1, 2, 8).

3.  **Step 3: Making Simple, Direct Invitations.** The focus is on a clear proposal.
    *   Use of imperative or suggestive forms: `–•–Њ–і—Ц–Љ–Њ –≤ –Ї—Ц–љ–Њ!` (Let's go to the cinema!) (Source 20).
    *   Direct question form: `–•–Њ—З–µ—И –њ—Ц—В–Є –Ј—Ц –Љ–љ–Њ—О?` (Do you want to go with me?) (Source 2).
    *   Initial responses: `–Ґ–∞–Ї, –і–∞–≤–∞–є` (Yes, let's), `–Ф–Њ–±—А–µ` (Okay) (Source 2).

4.  **Step 4: Negotiating Time and Place.** This is the "checkpoint" skill, requiring specific grammar.
    *   Asking about time: `–Ю –Ї–Њ—В—А—Ц–є –≥–Њ–і–Є–љ—Ц?` (At what time?) or `–Э–∞ –Ї–Њ—В—А—Г –≥–Њ–і–Є–љ—Г?` (For what time?) (Source 1, 10).
    *   Telling time using `–Њ` + ordinal number in the Locative case: `–Њ —В—А–µ—В—Ц–є` (at three), `–Њ —Б—М–Њ–Љ—Ц–є` (at seven) (Source 1, 10).
    *   Asking about place: `–Ф–µ –Ј—Г—Б—В—А—Ц–љ–µ–Љ–Њ—Б—М?` (Where will we meet?).
    *   Responding with `–≤/—Г` or `–љ–∞` + Locative case: `—Г –њ–∞—А–Ї—Г`, `–љ–∞ —Д–µ—Б—В–Є–≤–∞–ї—Ц` (Source 18).

5.  **Step 5: Simple Apologies and Explanations for Refusal.**
    *   Politely declining: `–Э–∞ –ґ–∞–ї—М, –љ–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г` (Unfortunately, I can't) (Source 20).
    *   Giving a simple reason: `–Я–Њ–≥–∞–љ–Њ –њ–Њ—З—Г–≤–∞—О—Б—П` (I feel unwell), `–ѓ –Ј–∞–є–љ—П—В–∞` (I'm busy) (Source 2, 20).
    *   Simple apology: `–Т–Є–±–∞—З` (Sorry - informal), `–Я—А–Њ–±–∞—З` (Forgive me) (Source 7).

6.  **Step 6: Formal vs. Informal Register.** Introduce the concept of `—В–Є` (informal 'you') vs. `–≤–Є` (formal/plural 'you').
    *   Contrast informal `–Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В, –Ґ–∞—А–∞—Б–µ` with formal `–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М, –°–µ—А–≥—Ц—О –Т–∞—Б–Є–ї—М–Њ–≤–Є—З—Г` (Source 1, 3).
    *   Explain that `–≤–Є` is used with strangers, elders, and in professional contexts. Show how verb endings and pronouns change: `–•–Њ—З–µ—И?` (—В–Є) vs. `–•–Њ—З–µ—В–µ?` (–≤–Є).

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

Teaching Ukrainian communication requires a deliberate de-linking from Russian linguistic habits, which are often mistakenly presented as a "shortcut" to learners.

1.  **The Vocative Case is Non-Negotiable and a Key Differentiator:** Russian has lost the vocative case in everyday speech, retaining it only in archaic or religious contexts. Ukrainian, however, uses it constantly and actively (`–і—А—Г–ґ–µ`, `–Ю–ї–µ–љ–Њ`, `–њ–∞–љ–µ`). Emphasize that this is a core, living feature of Ukrainian grammar, not an optional flourish. Its absence in modern Russian is a point of divergence, not a model to follow (Source 5).
2.  **Teach `–Я–∞–љ–µ / –Я–∞–љ—Ц` as the Default Formal Address:** The use of `–Я–∞–љ–µ` (Sir/Mr.) and `–Я–∞–љ—Ц` (Madam/Mrs./Ms.) followed by a first name or title is the standard, polite, European way of formal address in Ukrainian (Source 3). Avoid introducing the Russian-style `–Ж–Љ'—П + –њ–Њ-–±–∞—В—М–Ї–Њ–≤—Ц` (Name + Patronymic) as the primary model. While patronymics are used (`–°–µ—А–≥—Ц—О –Т–∞—Б–Є–ї—М–Њ–≤–Є—З—Г`), they often appear in more official or older-generation contexts. The `–Я–∞–љ/–Я–∞–љ—Ц` model is more versatile, modern, and aligns Ukrainian with its Central European cultural context.
3.  **Avoid Russian Phonetic Analogies:** Do not teach Ukrainian sounds by comparing them to Russian ones (e.g., "Ukrainian `–Є` is like Russian `—Л`"). This creates phonetic interference and hinders the development of an authentic Ukrainian accent. Teach each sound on its own terms, using minimal pair drills based on Ukrainian words.
4.  **Calques and Surzhyk:** Actively teach against common Russian calques in conversational phrases. A prime example is the incorrect use of `*–Ф–∞–≤–∞–є!` for "bye" (a Russianism) instead of the proper `–Я–∞-–њ–∞` or `–Ф–Њ –њ–Њ–±–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П`. The brief should explicitly list and forbid such constructions.

    *   **Prompt:** *–Ґ—А–∞–љ—Б—Д–Њ—А–Љ—Г–є—В–µ –і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥ —Г –Љ–Њ–љ–Њ–ї–Њ–≥ —Ц –њ–µ—А–µ–Ї–∞–ґ—Ц—В—М –є–Њ–≥–Њ –≤—Ц–і —Ц–Љ–µ–љ—Ц: –Р. –Ф–∞–≤–Є–і–∞ (–≤—Ц–і –њ–µ—А—И–Њ—Ч –Њ—Б–Њ–±–Є); –С. –Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–Є (–≤—Ц–і —В—А–µ—В—М–Њ—Ч –Њ—Б–Њ–±–Є).* (Transform the dialogue into a monologue and retell it from the perspective of: A.
