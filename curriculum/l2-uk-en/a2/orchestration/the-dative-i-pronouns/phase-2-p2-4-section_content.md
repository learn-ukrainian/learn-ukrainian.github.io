## Dialogues / Діалоги

Now that you know how to ask about someone's state, let's see how these "check-ins" sound in a natural conversation. Notice how often Ukrainians switch to the Dative case to talk about comfort, feelings, and needs rather than just actions.

### Coffee Break / Перерва на каву

Imagine you are taking a break with a colleague. You want to ask about their drink and how they are feeling in the air-conditioned office. Watch how the focus shifts from "I" to "to me".

– Олю, як **тобі** [tɔˈbi] ця кава?  
(Olya, how is this coffee [to] you?)

– **Мені** [mɛˈnʲi] дуже смакує! А **тобі**?  
([To] me [it] tastes very good! And [to] you?)

– **Мені** [mɛˈnʲi] не дуже. Вона холодна.  
([To] me not very [much]. It is cold.)

– Тобі принести цукор?  
((Should I) bring sugar [to] you?)

– Ні, дякую. **Мені** [mɛˈnʲi] треба бігти.  
(No, thank you. [To] me [it is necessary] to run.)

> [!observe]
> **The "Me" vs. "To Me" Switch**
> Notice the last line: «**Мені** треба бігти». In English, you say "I need to run." In Ukrainian, the necessity presses *on* you. Literally: "To me (it is) necessary to run." This is the Dative mindset: feelings and needs happen *to* you.
