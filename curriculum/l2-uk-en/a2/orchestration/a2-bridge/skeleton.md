## Пригадуємо відмінки (Reviewing Cases) (~660 words total)

- Dialogue (~100 words): Opening scene — Новий студент (Майкл з Канади) arrives at a Kyiv language school on day one of A2. Natural, multi-turn exchange with Викладач that surfaces all four A1 cases organically: "Звідки ви?" → "Я **з Канади**." (genitive preview) / "Скільки вам років?" → "**Мені** 25 років." (dative preview) / "Що ви вивчаєте?" → "Я вивчаю **українську мову**." (accusative) / "Де ви живете?" → "Я живу **в Києві**." (locative) / Викладач closes: "Дуже приємно, **Майкле**!" (vocative). Do not explain cases yet — let the dialogue stand naturally.

- P1 (~70 words): Post-dialogue reflection — the paragraph names every case just used in the dialogue, asks the learner: "Помітили? Ви вже знаєте чотири відмінки." Frames the section as an audit of what the learner already owns, not new learning. Introduces the case-question method as the diagnostic tool: the question a word answers reveals its case.

- P2 (~100 words): Nominative case (Називний — Хто? Що?) — the dictionary form; subject and predicate noun. Adjective agreement in all three genders: новий студент, нова книга, нове місто. Three example sentences: "Це цікавий підручник." / "Марія — студентка." / "Київ — велике місто." Contrast: Nominative is the form you look up in the dictionary — all other cases are *derived* from it.

- P3 (~110 words): Accusative case (Знахідний — Кого? Що?) — the direct-object case. Three sub-patterns: (a) Feminine nouns: книга→книгу, кава→каву, земля→землю; (b) Masculine inanimate: підручник→підручник (unchanged, like nominative); (c) Masculine animate: студент→студента, учитель→учителя (like genitive — a key rule). Five natural verb + noun pairs: читати книгу, пити каву, знати відповідь, любити Україну, бачити студента.

- P4 (~100 words): Locative case (Місцевий — Де? На чому? В чому?) — location only, always with a preposition (в/у, на, при, по). Rule: never used without a preposition. City examples showing the -і/-і ending and softening: у Києві, у Харкові, у Львові, на уроці, в університеті, при школі, по дорозі. Soft-stem note: місто→у місті, море→на морі. Two sentences from the dialogue reused as anchor examples.

- P5 (~80 words): Vocative case (Кличний — звертання) — the case of direct address, unique to Ukrainian (absent in Russian). Endings: -е for hard-stem masculines (Тарасе, друже, хлопче), -є/-ю for soft-stem masculines (Сергію, Василю), -о for feminines on -а (мамо, Ганно, Маріє — note: Марія→Маріє for soft stems). Fixed forms: тату, Господи. One sentence reusing the dialogue: "Дуже приємно, Майкле!"

- Exercise — Quiz: Case Identification Drill (8 items). Each item = one sentence with one noun underlined; learner selects the case from four options (Н, З, М, К). Sentences drawn from the dialogue and P2–P5 examples: "Я живу **в Києві**." / "Вона читає **книгу**." / "Привіт, **Сергію**!" / "Це **цікавий підручник**." etc. Tests identification, not production.

- P6 (~100 words): Full seven-case map — present as a two-column reference: case name + question + one-line function + one example. Four A1 cases (Н, З, М, К) marked ✅. Three A2 cases introduced by name only with their question and core function: Родовий (Кого? Чого?) — possession and negation; Давальний (Кому? Чому?) — recipient and age; Орудний (Ким? Чим?) — means and company. Close with: "Три нові відмінки — це три нові способи виражати думки. Почнемо вже в наступному модулі."

## Магія української фонології (The Magic of Ukrainian Phonology) (~770 words total)

- P1 (~90 words): Framing paragraph — Ukrainian phonological alternations are not exceptions; they are a system. Two categories: голосні чергування (vowel alternations) and приголосні чергування (consonant alternations). Both are predictable once the learner knows two triggers: (1) whether a syllable is *closed* or *open*, and (2) which consonant precedes a front vowel. Promise: after this section, learners will *understand* why стіл becomes стола — not just memorize it.

- P2 (~150 words): О/І vowel alternation — the closed-syllable rule. When a syllable is *closed* (ends in a consonant), historical Proto-Slavic ŏ/ĕ became Ukrainian і. When the syllable *opens* (an ending adds a vowel), і reverts to о/е. Present as a rule-pair with four anchor pairs: стіл (closed, Nominative) / стола (open, Genitive); кіт / кота; ніч / ночі; піч / печі (і→е in open syllable). Construct a mini-table: Nominative (closed) → і | Genitive (open) → о or е. Add one sentence showing both forms: "На столі стоїть стіл." (locative стол-і vs nominative стіл).

- P3 (~150 words): Е/І alternation and more о/і pairs — extend with: корінь/кореня, камінь/каменя (і→е in genitive), осінь/осені. Explain that the same closed-syllable rule governs both о→і and е→і — they are one phenomenon, two historical source vowels. Acknowledge: learners will not need to *produce* these from scratch — they will encounter them in case endings and need to *recognize* them. Practical tip: when a genitive or dative form looks strange, check if it restores о/е from а nominative і.

- P4 (~150 words): Consonant alternations — the three-way palatalization pattern г/к/х → ж/ч/ш (diminutives and some case forms) → з/ц/с (locative singular). Full pattern table: г→ж/з (нога: ніжка / нозі), к→ч/ц (рука: ручка / руці), х→ш/с (вухо: вушко / у вусі). In verbs: пекти→печу (к→ч), бігти→біжу (г→ж), їхати→їду (х drops). Two sentences: "Я пишу рукою — але я тримаю ручку." / "Де моя рука? На руці — браслет." This directly previews the locative endings learners will build in A2 modules 3–5.

- P5 (~110 words): Stress as a meaning-differentiator — Ukrainian stress is *free* (not fixed to a syllable position) and *mobile* (can shift within a paradigm). Minimal pairs where stress changes meaning: зáмок (castle) / замóк (lock); аtлас (book of maps, Áтлас) / атлáс (satin fabric). Paradigm stress shift: рукá (Nominative) / рýки (Genitive singular) / рукú (Genitive plural); сестрá / сéстри / сестéр. Practical advice: dictionaries mark stress — always check goroh.pp.ua for a new word. Carry the stress with the word in memory, not just the letters.

- P6 (~120 words): Verb consonant alternations preview — the same г/к/х→ж/ч/ш pattern appears in the 1st person singular present tense, making this phonology directly actionable: писати→пишу (с→ш), казати→кажу (з→ж), їздити→їжджу (зд→жж). Add: просити→прошу (с→ш), водити→воджу (д→дж). Frame as a "bonus payoff" — learners who understand the consonant pattern already know the irregularity rule before seeing verb conjugation tables. One sentence: "Я кажу вам: ця закономірність поясненню піддається."

- Exercise — Fill-in: Phonological Alternation Pairs (8 items). Format: learner is given one form and must supply the alternating form, with the case or context labeled. Examples: стіл → _____ (Genitive); рука → _____ (Locative, "на"); пишу → _____ (infinitive); ніч → _____ (Genitive). Covers both vowel and consonant alternations, 4 items each type.

## Милозвучність мови: евфонія (The Melody of Language: Euphony) (~440 words total)

- P1 (~80 words): Introduction — Ukrainian actively avoids dissonant sound clusters through alternating variant pairs. This is not optional style; it is the norm. Термін: милозвучність (милозвучна мова = melodious language). Three alternating pairs to master: у/в, і/й, з/зі/із. One broken sentence as a negative example (from the Litvinova textbook grepping): "В ваших родинах" — show why this grates and how the rule fixes it: "У ваших родинах."

- P2 (~120 words): У/В alternation — the core rule: **after a vowel → в**; **after a consonant (or at sentence start before a consonant) → у**. Four contrasting pairs with identical meaning: "Я **в** університеті" (я ends in vowel) vs. "Він **у** університеті" (він ends in consonant); "Ми **в** Харкові" vs. "студент **у** Харкові." Rule for sentence start: "**У** Києві є метро" (before consonant К) vs. "**В** Одесі є море" (before vowel О). Common learner error to name and fix: "**В** вторник" → correct: "**У** вівторок."

- P3 (~90 words): І/Й alternation — the same phonetic logic: **after a vowel → й**; **after a consonant (or sentence start) → і**. Four pairs: "мати **і** батько" (consonant before і) vs. "батько **й** мати" (vowel before й); "Марія **й** Олег" vs. "Олег **і** Марія." Note: the sполучник і/й obeys the same rule as the preposition. Contrast with Russian и — which has no alternation. This is a purely Ukrainian feature.

- P4 (~80 words): З/Зі/Із alternation — з before most words starting with a single consonant or vowel: з Канади, з Києва, з тобою; зі/із before consonant clusters (especially зд-, зб-, лв-, шк-): зі Львова (Лв-), зі школи (шк-), зі зламаним (зл-); із before words starting with з or с: із Запоріжжя, із сестрою. Rule of thumb: whichever variant flows most smoothly is almost always correct.

- Exercise — Match-up: Euphony Choice Exercise (8 items). Each item = a sentence fragment with a blank and two options in brackets; learner selects the correct variant: "Він живе _____ [у/в] Польщі." / "Марія _____ [і/й] Іван." / "Я приїхав _____ [з/зі] Львова." etc. 4 items for у/в, 2 for і/й, 2 for з/зі/із.

- P5 (~70 words): Closing note — милозвучність becomes automatic with exposure. Native speakers do not consciously apply the rules; they *hear* what sounds natural. For learners: read Ukrainian text aloud, paying attention to these transitions. When something sounds awkward, that is the ear signaling a euphony error. By B1, these choices will feel natural rather than calculated.

## Що нас чекає на рівні А2? (What Awaits Us in A2?) (~330 words total)

- P1 (~100 words): The three new cases — each unlocks a communicative layer the learner currently cannot express. Родовий (Genitive): possession ("книга **брата**"), negation ("нема **часу**"), quantity ("багато **студентів**"). Давальний (Dative): recipient ("дати **другові**"), age ("**мені** 25 років" — revisit from dialogue), obligation ("**студентам** треба вчитися"). Орудний (Instrumental): means or tool ("писати **ручкою**"), company ("іти **з другом**"), profession ("він є **лікарем**"). Concrete one-sentence preview for each, showing a real communicative gap that will close.

- P2 (~80 words): Verbal aspect — the A2 conceptual challenge. Ukrainian verbs come in aspect pairs: imperfective (ongoing, repeated) / perfective (completed, single event). Pairs: читати/прочитати, писати/написати, говорити/сказати, робити/зробити. Two sentences contrasting the same situation: "Я **читав** цю книгу щодня." (imperfective, habit) vs. "Я **прочитав** цю книгу вчора." (perfective, completed). Aspect will be the subject of dedicated modules starting in A2.2.

- P3 (~80 words): Verbs of motion — a dedicated A2 sub-track. Two core pairs: іти (single direction on foot) / ходити (habitual or multi-directional); їхати (single direction by vehicle) / їздити (habitual). Preview of prefix extensions that transform meaning: при-їхати (arrive), ви-їхати (depart), пере-їхати (cross). Two dialogue sentences: "Я **іду** зараз на урок." vs. "Я **ходжу** на уроки щодня." Learners who master motion verbs unlock travel, daily routine, and narrative.

- P4 (~70 words): Closing encouragement — A2 is the inflection point. At A1 the learner described simple facts; at A2 they begin to *explain*, *compare*, and *narrate*. Return to the opening dialogue: Майкл started the lesson saying "Я з Канади" and "Я живу в Києві." By the end of A2, he will say "Книга, яку я купив **у центрі міста**, написана **відомим українським автором**." The path starts here.
