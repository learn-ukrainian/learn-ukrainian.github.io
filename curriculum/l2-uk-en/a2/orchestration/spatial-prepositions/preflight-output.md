## Вступ (Introduction)

Віта́ю! (Greetings!) I am so glad to see you again. Today, you are not just a student; you are a professional **дизайне́р інтер'є́ру** (interior designer). Your mission is special. You have been invited to help organize a traditional Ukrainian **ха́та** (house). In Ukraine, the way a home is organized is not just about style; it is about logic, culture, and hospitality. To succeed in your mission, you must master the art of precise spatial logic.

Have you ever wondered why we say «I am at the office» but «I am in the car»? In Ukrainian, choosing the right word for "where" depends on the type of space and, most importantly, your intention. Are you staying still, or are you moving? 

According to the Ukrainian State Standard §4.2.2.4.2 and §4.2.2.6, mastering spatial language requires us to understand two fundamental questions:
1. **Де?** (Where?) — This asks about a static position or location.
2. **Куди́?** (Whither / Where to?) — This asks about the destination or motion.

This distinction is the fundamental decision point in Ukrainian grammar. When you describe where something is located (**де?**), you will often use the Locative or Instrumental cases. But when you move something to a new spot (**куди́?**), you will switch to the Accusative case. Don't worry if this sounds complex right now. We will take it step by step, using the traditional house as our guide. 

By the end of this module, you will be able to arrange a room, give clear directions, and understand how Ukrainians perceive the space around them. Ви гото́ві? (Are you ready?) Let's start our design journey!

> [!note] **Quick Win**
> You already know the words **в** (in) and **на** (on). Just by adding the right ending to a noun, you can tell someone if you are already inside a building or just arriving there. That is the power of cases!

## Презентація: В, На та Орудний відмінок (Presentation: V, Na and Instrumental)

As a designer, your first tool is understanding the difference between **в/у** and **на**. This is a common area where learners feel a bit lost, but the logic is actually very consistent.

### В vs. На: Enclosures and Surfaces

We use **в/у** (in/into) for enclosed spaces, like a room or a box. We use **на** (on/onto) for surfaces, open spaces, and events.

*   **В ха́ті** (In the house) — Enclosed space.
*   **На ву́лиці** (On the street) — Open space.
*   **На конце́рті** (At the concert) — An event.
*   **На столі́** (On the table) — A surface.

When we talk about location (**де?**), these prepositions require the Locative case. But look what happens when we talk about motion (**куди́?**):

*   **Де?** Ми в ха́ті. (We are in the house.)
*   **Куди́?** Ми йдемо́ в ха́ту. (We are going into the house.)

> [!tip] **Euphony Rule**
> Use **у** when the previous word ends in a consonant and the next starts with a consonant: «Він у ха́ті». Use **в** after a vowel: «Вона́ в ха́ті». This makes the language sound smooth and melodic.

### The Stove: The Heart of the House

In a traditional Ukrainian home, the **піч** (stove) was the cultural anchor. It was used for cooking, heating the house, and even as a place to sleep! Because the stove is the center of the room, we use it to learn the Instrumental prepositions: **над** (above), **під** (under), **пе́ред** (in front of), **за** (behind/at), and **між** (between).

When describing where things are (**де?**), these prepositions take the **Ору́дний відмі́нок** (Instrumental case).

*   **Під столо́м** (Under the table) — Де сиди́ть кіт? Кіт під столо́м.
*   **Над голово́ю** (Above the head) — Ла́мпа над столо́м.
*   **За пі́ччю** (Behind the stove) — Дрова́ лежать за пі́ччю.
*   **Пе́ред вхо́дом** (In front of the entrance) — Кві́ти пе́ред ха́тою.
*   **Між ві́кнами** (Between the windows) — Стіл стої́ть між ві́кнами.

### The Sacred Corner and Motion

Opposite the stove, you will find the **поку́ття** (sacred corner). This is the most honorable place in the house, where icons are kept. 

To describe this area, we use Genitive prepositions: **бі́ля** (near), **навпро́ти** (opposite), and **до** (to/towards).

*   **Навпро́ти пе́чі** (Opposite the stove) — Поку́ття знахо́диться навпро́ти пе́чі.
*   **Бі́ля вікна́** (Near the window) — Стіл стої́ть бі́ля вікна́.
*   **Додо́му** (To home / Homeward) — Ми йдемо́ додо́му.

> [!culture] **Ukrainian Hospitality**
> In Ukraine, guests are the most important people. When a guest arrives, they are seated **на поку́тті** (at the sacred corner) or **за столо́м** (at the table). These are the most honorable places. Sitting **за столо́м** doesn't just mean being "behind" it; it means participating in the shared meal and conversation.

## Практика: Відмінки та Рух (Practice: Cases and Motion)

Now it is time for your design drills! Remember our core rule: **Де?** (Location) vs. **Куди́?** (Motion). This is the most common error for learners, so let's fix it early.

### Location vs. Destination

When you use **в/у** or **на**, the case changes based on the question.

*   **Location (Де? + Locative):** Я у шко́лі. (I am in school.)
*   **Motion (Куди́? + Accusative):** Я йду в шко́лу. (I am going to school.)

Incorrect: «Я йду в шко́лі» (This sounds like you are walking circles inside the school, not going to it).
Correct: **Я йду в шко́лу.**

### Designer Commands

As the lead designer, you need to give orders. Notice how the case changes when you tell someone to put something *onto* a surface (**куди́?**) versus describing where it stays (**де?**).

*   **Покладі́ть кни́гу на стіл.** (Put the book onto the table. — Accusative)
*   **Кни́га лежи́ть на столі́.** (The book is lying on the table. — Locative)
*   **Поста́вте ла́мпу над столо́м.** (Place the lamp above the table. — Instrumental)
*   **Пові́сьте рушни́к під ві́кно.** (Hang the towel under the window. — Accusative)

> [!practice] **Case Check**
> When using **під** (under) and **за** (behind), use the Instrumental case for location, but the Accusative case for the destination of the movement.
> 
> *   **Де?** Кіт під столо́м. (Instrumental)
> *   **Куди́?** Кіт заліз під стіл. (Accusative)

### Genitive Governance

Prepositions like **бі́ля** (near), **навпро́ти** (opposite), and **до** (to) always require the Genitive case. A common mistake is using the base form of the noun.

*   Incorrect: «бі́ля магази́н»
*   Correct: **бі́ля магази́ну** (near the shop)
*   Correct: **навпро́ти теа́тру** (opposite the theatre)
*   Correct: **до ха́ти** (to the house)
