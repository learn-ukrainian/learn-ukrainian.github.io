## Кличний + наказовий + орудний: Побажання (Vocative + Imperative + Instrumental: Wishes) (~600 words)

Ukrainians have a deep cultural tradition of expressing heartfelt wishes, blessings, and toasts for almost every occasion. Whether gathering for a holiday, celebrating a birthday, or simply saying goodbye to a family member, the act of giving a meaningful **побажання** (wish, blessing) is an essential part of daily life. To create these beautiful expressions, the Ukrainian language uses a powerful, elegant tripartite grammatical formula. This three-part structure consists of the Vocative case to address the person directly, the **наказовий спосіб** (imperative mood) of a verb to give the gentle command or wish, and the Instrumental case to describe what state or quality they should achieve. A perfect example of this refined formula is «Оленко, будь щасливою!» (Olenko, be happy!). Mastering this pattern allows you to participate in a living Ukrainian tradition.

Let's break down the first two components of this formula. The sentence always starts by calling out to the person using the **кличний відмінок** (Vocative case). As a brief review, remember how common forms of address change: a name like Олена becomes Оленко, мама changes to мамо, and друг becomes друже. Plural forms like діти remain діти. After establishing who you are talking to, you need the core verb. For these wishes, we almost always use the imperative mood of the verb "to be". The standard forms are **будь** (be) when speaking to one person informally, and **будьте** (be plural) when addressing multiple people or speaking formally.

The third and final component is the Instrumental case, which completes the thought by stating the desired quality. In Ukrainian, when you tell someone to "be" something, that following adjective naturally takes the Instrumental case because it describes a state of becoming or existing. For a feminine singular subject, the adjective ending is usually -ою: the word **щасливий** (happy) becomes **щасливою**. You can also use the word **спокійний** (calm), which becomes **спокійною**.

For plural subjects, you use the -ими ending. For example, the word **здоровий** (healthy) becomes **здоровими**, and **уважний** (attentive) becomes **уважними**. Let's look at full sentences using all three parts. You can say «Мамо, будь спокійною!» (Mom, be calm!) or «Діти, будьте уважними!» (Children, be attentive!). A very common toast is «Друзі, будьте здоровими!» (Friends, be healthy!).

У щоденних розмовах ви часто можете почути короткі побажання у називному відмінку. Люди кажуть «Будь щаслива!» або «Будьте здорові!» замість орудного відмінка. Це нормально для неформального спілкування з друзями чи рідними. Проте використання орудного відмінка робить вашу мову набагато багатшою та елегантнішою. Формула «будь щасливою» — це високий літературний стандарт. Такі фрази краще передають ідею тривалого стану або процесу.

> *In daily conversations, you can often hear short wishes in the Nominative case. People say "Будь щаслива!" or "Будьте здорові!" instead of the Instrumental case. This is normal for informal communication with friends or relatives. However, using the Instrumental case makes your language much richer and more elegant. The formula "будь щасливою" is a high literary standard. Such phrases better convey the idea of a continuous state or process.*

:::info
**Grammar box**
When you use the verb «бути» in the imperative (будь, будьте), the following adjective should ideally be in the Instrumental case. This rule applies to describing a temporary or desired state of being.
:::

You can also create beautiful, extended wishes without using the verb "to be" by combining the **хай** (let) or **нехай** (let) particles with third-person verbs. When you address someone in the Vocative case and use this construction, you are asking the universe, fate, or circumstances to act in their favor. A very common everyday wish is «Хай тобі щастить!» (May you have luck!). For more formal or poetic occasions involving a **мрія** (dream), you might say «Нехай здійсняться всі мрії!» (May all dreams come true!). These phrases show how the third-person imperative easily transforms from an indirect command into a warm blessing.
