## Хай і нехай: Наказ для третіх осіб (3rd Person Imperatives) (~600 words)
- P1 (~100 words): [Dialogue] Ukrainian cooking class setting. The chef gives commands to students using a mix of imperatives. "Наріжте цибулю! Хай вода закипить! Помішаймо разом! Нехай соус настоїться!" Students confirm they are following the instructions.
- P2 (~100 words): Introduce the concept of the 3rd person imperative, used for giving indirect commands or expressing wishes for people/things not directly addressed. Explain the core formation: the particles `хай` or `нехай` followed by the 3rd person present (or future perfective) verb. Provide clear examples: Хай він читає (Let him read). Нехай вона прочитає (Let her read it through).
- P3 (~100 words): Discuss the difference between the two particles. Explain that `нехай` is slightly more formal and literary, while `хай` is commonly used in everyday speech. Emphasize that they are completely interchangeable in meaning. Show parallel examples: Хай іде! vs. Нехай іде!
- P4 (~200 words): Detail the three primary uses of this construction. First, indirect commands (Нехай вони подзвонять — Have them call). Second, granting permission (Хай іде, я не проти — Let him go, I don't mind). Third, expressing general wishes (Хай щастить! — Good luck!). Emphasize that the verb must still agree with the subject in person and number, providing examples with plural subjects: Хай вони прийдуть, Нехай діти граються.
- P5 (~100 words): Briefly introduce the role of aspect in 3rd person commands. Contrast an ongoing process using the imperfective (Хай пише — let him write / be writing) with a completed result using the perfective (Хай напише — let him finish writing it).

## Читаймо! Ходімо! Перша особа множини (1st Person Plural Imperatives) (~500 words)
- P1 (~125 words): Introduce the 1st person plural imperative, the Ukrainian equivalent of "Let's...". Explain the formation rule: take the 2nd person singular imperative base and add the suffix `-мо`. Provide clear, straightforward transformations: читай → читаймо (let's read), ходи → ходімо (let's go).
- P2 (~125 words): Explain the formation for verbs whose imperative base ends in a consonant, noting that `-і-` or `-ь-` appears before the suffix. Demonstrate with transformations: зроби → зробімо (let's do it), поїдь → поїдьмо (let's go by vehicle).
- P3 (~150 words): Address the alternative "давайте" construction. Explain that `давайте читати` or `давайте поїдемо` is understood and often sounds softer or more suggestive. However, strictly warn against the common Russian calque `Давай(те) + 1st person plural verb`. Explicitly show that `Давайте поговоримо` is incorrect (❌), and `Поговорімо` is the correct, elegant Ukrainian form (✅).
- P4 (~100 words): Provide a practical list of highly common everyday `-мо` forms used in daily life: Ходімо! (Let's go!), Починаймо! (Let's start!), Зробімо це! (Let's do it!), Поговорімо (Let's talk). Briefly touch upon the aspect nuance: Ходімо (perfective nuance, one trip) vs. Читаймо (imperfective, habitual).

## Кличний + наказовий + орудний: Побажання (Vocative + Imperative + Instrumental: Wishes) (~600 words)
- P1 (~125 words): Introduce the deep cultural tradition of expressing wishes, blessings, and toasts in Ukrainian. Explain the powerful, elegant tripartite grammatical formula used to create them: Vocative case (who is addressed) + Imperative verb (the command/wish) + Instrumental case (what they should be/become). Example: Оленко, будь щасливою! (Olenko, be happy!).
- P2 (~125 words): Break down the first two components of the formula. Briefly review the Vocative case for common forms of address: Олена → Оленко, мама → мамо, друг → друже, діти → діти. Show the core imperative forms of the verb "to be": `будь` (singular) and `будьте` (plural).
- P3 (~150 words): Detail the third component: the Instrumental case endings required for the adjectives in this construction. Show examples for feminine singular (щасливою, спокійною) and plural (здоровими, уважними). Provide full sentence examples: Мамо, будь спокійною! (Mom, be calm!), Діти, будьте уважними! (Children, be attentive!), Друзі, будьте здоровими! (Friends, be healthy!).
- P4 (~100 words): Acknowledge that while short forms using the Nominative case (like "Будь щаслива!") are heard in casual, everyday speech, the Instrumental case is the refined, literary standard for describing "becoming" or "being" a certain state.
- P5 (~100 words): Show how to create extended, beautiful wishes by combining the `хай` / `нехай` particles with 3rd person verbs, addressed to someone using the Vocative case. Examples: Хай тобі щастить! (May you have luck!), Нехай здійсняться всі мрії! (May all dreams come true!).

## Вид дієслова в наказовому способі (Aspect in Imperatives) (~500 words)
- P1 (~125 words): Dive into how aspect choice drastically changes the tone and meaning of a command. Explain that the imperfective imperative is used for general instructions, repeated actions (habits), or polite invitations. Provide examples: Читай більше! (Read more — general advice), Пишіть щодня! (Write every day — habitual), Сідайте, будь ласка (Please sit down — polite invitation).
- P2 (~125 words): Explain the perfective imperative. Detail that it is used for specific, one-time commands where a concrete result is expected right away. Provide examples: Прочитай цю статтю! (Read this article — and finish it), Напиши мені! (Write to me — do it), Закрий двері! (Close the door — now).
- P3 (~150 words): Detail the strict rule for negative imperatives: negative commands almost exclusively use the imperfective aspect. Explain that telling someone NOT to do something implies an ongoing prohibition. Provide examples: Не читай це! (Don't read this), Не відкривайте вікно! (Don't open the window).
- P4 (~100 words): Explain the sole exception: using the perfective aspect in a negative command creates a harsh warning or expresses fear of an accidental, sudden result. Provide the classic example: Не впади! (Don't fall! — careful!). Conclude with a brief summary: general advice/polite/negative = imperfective; specific task/urgent warning = perfective.
