## Хай і нехай: Наказ для третіх осіб (3rd Person Imperatives)

<div class="dialogue">


**Олена:** Де Максим? *(Where is Maksym?)*


**Андрій:** Він ще працює. *(He is still working.)*


**Олена:** **Хай** (let) він закінчить цей звіт завтра. Вже пізно. *(Let him finish this report tomorrow. It is already late.)*


**Андрій:** Добре. А що робити з новими клієнтами? *(Okay. And what to do with the new clients?)*


**Олена:** **Нехай** (let) вони подзвонять у понеділок. *(Have them call on Monday.)*


</div>



Imagine you are a manager organizing a team, or a parent coordinating your children. Often, you need to give a command or grant permission regarding someone who is not in the room with you. In English, you use phrases like "let him" or "have them". In Ukrainian, this function is performed by the **наказовий спосіб** (imperative mood) for the third person. 

To form this structure, you do not need to memorize any new verb endings. You simply use the particle **хай** or its formal variant **нехай**, followed by the normal third-person present tense verb (if the verb is imperfective) or the perfective future tense verb. The structure looks exactly like a normal declarative sentence, just with the particle placed at the beginning.

- **Хай він читає.** (Let him read.) — Here we use the imperfective verb, focusing on the ongoing process.
- **Нехай вона прочитає.** (Let her read it through.) — Here we use the perfective verb, focusing on the completed result.

There are three primary situations where Ukrainians use this third-person imperative structure in daily life.

1. Permission. You are officially allowing someone to do something, or stating that you have no objections to their actions.
- **Хай іде, я не проти.** (Let him go, I don't mind.)
- **Хай діти гуляють у дворі.** (Let the children walk in the yard.)

2. Indirect Command. You are giving an instruction that someone else needs to pass along to a third party.
- **Нехай вони подзвонять.** (Have them call.)
- **Хай вона купить молоко.** (Tell her to buy milk.)

3. Wishes and Blessings. This grammatical structure is also the foundation for expressing a **побажання** (wish), especially towards abstract concepts or people in general. 
- **Хай щастить!** (Good luck! / May luck be with you!)
- **Нехай буде мир.** (Let there be peace.)

You might wonder about the difference between **хай** and **нехай**. Grammatically and semantically, there is absolutely no difference. They mean exactly the same thing. The distinction is purely about style and register. **Нехай** is slightly more formal and literary; you will encounter it frequently in official documents, literature, and news broadcasts. **Хай** is the standard for everyday spoken speech and casual conversation. You can interchange them freely based on how formal you want to sound.

When you use this structure with plural subjects, the rule of agreement remains exactly the same as in any normal sentence. The verb must agree with the subject in person and number.
- **Хай вони прийдуть.** (Let them come.)
- **Нехай діти граються.** (Let the children play.)

Even in the third person, your choice of aspect remains crucial. If you choose an imperfective verb, you are directing someone to engage in a continuous process. 
- **Хай пише.** (Let him write. — Let him be engaged in the activity of writing.)

If you choose a perfective verb, you are commanding a specific, completed result.
- **Хай напише.** (Let him write it. — Let him finish writing the specific thing we need.)

## Читаймо! Ходімо! Перша особа множини (1st Person Plural Imperatives)

<div class="dialogue">


**Тарас:** Я не хочу сидіти вдома. *(I don't want to sit at home.)*


**Ігор:** Тоді **ходімо** (let's go) до парку! *(Then let's go to the park!)*


**Тарас:** А потім **давайте** (let's) поїдемо в центр. *(And then let's go to the center.)*


**Ігор:** Чудова ідея. Зробімо це! *(Great idea. Let's do it!)*


</div>



When you are with a group of friends or colleagues and you want to propose a shared activity, you need the first-person plural imperative. This is the equivalent of the English "let's". Ukrainian offers two distinct ways to build these suggestions.

The first and most authentic method is using the suffix **-мо**. To form this, you start with the second-person singular command (the "you" command) and simply attach **-мо** to the end. The verb stem dictates the form.
- Ти читай (You read) → Ми читаймо (Let's read)
- Ти ходи (You go) → Ми ходімо (Let's go)
- Ти зроби (You do) → Ми зробімо (Let's do it)
- Ти поїдь (You drive) → Ми поїдьмо (Let's go by vehicle)

This **-мо** construction is highly dynamic, energetic, and culturally preferred in modern Ukrainian. It sounds like a true, active call to action. You will hear these common **-мо** forms constantly in daily life:
- **Ходімо!** (Let's go! — specifically on foot)
- **Починаймо!** (Let's start!)
- **Зробімо це!** (Let's do it!)
- **Поїдьмо!** (Let's drive! / Let's ride!)
- **Поговорімо!** (Let's talk!)

The alternative way to make a suggestion is using the particle **давайте** followed by an infinitive (for imperfective actions) or a future tense verb (for perfective actions).
- **Давайте читати.** (Let's read.)
- **Давайте поїдемо.** (Let's go.)

The word **давайте** acts as a softer, more tentative suggestion. While **ходімо** sounds like you are already putting on your shoes, **давайте підемо** sounds like you are politely putting the idea on the table for discussion. Both forms are grammatically correct, but using the **-мо** suffix demonstrates a deeper mastery of natural Ukrainian rhythm.

Just as with all commands, the aspect of the verb matters significantly when making group suggestions. If you are proposing a single, completed action, you use a perfective verb.
- **Ходімо до парку.** (Let's go to the park. — A one-time trip. The perfective nuance via **підімо** is also possible and frequently used).

If you are proposing a regular habit or a continuous process, you must use an imperfective verb.
- **Читаймо щодня.** (Let's read every day. — A habitual action).

## Кличний + наказовий + орудний: Побажання (Vocative + Imperative + Instrumental: Wishes)

<div class="dialogue">


**Марія:** Оленко, **будь** (be) **щасливою** (happy)! *(Olenko, be happy!)*


**Іван:** І будь **здоровою** (healthy)! Нехай **здійсняться** (to come true) всі **мрії** (dreams)! *(And be healthy! May all dreams come true!)*


**Оленка:** Дякую, друзі! А ви **будьте** (be) успішними! *(Thank you, friends! And you be successful!)*


</div>



When you attend a Ukrainian birthday celebration, a holiday dinner, or a wedding, you will hear many heartfelt speeches. A central element of Ukrainian culture is the formal **побажання** (wish or blessing). To craft an elegant and grammatically flawless wish, you need to combine three specific elements into a powerful formula.

The formula consists of:
1. The **кличний відмінок** (Vocative case) to directly address the person.
2. The imperative form of the verb "to be": **будь** for a single person you address informally, or **будьте** for a group or a formal relationship.
3. The adjective describing your wish, which must be in the Instrumental case.

Let's analyze Mariia's classic birthday wish: **Оленко, будь щасливою!** (Olenko, be happy!)
- **Оленко** is the Vocative case form of the name Олена.
- **будь** is the singular imperative "be".
- **щасливою** is the Instrumental feminine case of the adjective **щасливий** (happy).

This exact pattern scales to any situation or group of people. Here are more examples showing how the formula adapts to different subjects:
- **Друзі, будьте здоровими!** (Friends, be healthy!) — We use the Vocative plural **Друзі**, the plural imperative **будьте**, and the plural Instrumental adjective **здоровими** (healthy).
- **Мамо, будь спокійною!** (Mom, be calm!) — We use the Vocative **Мамо**, the singular imperative **будь**, and the feminine Instrumental **спокійною** (calm).
- **Діти, будьте уважними!** (Children, be attentive!) — We use the Vocative **Діти**, the plural imperative **будьте**, and the plural Instrumental **уважними** (attentive).

These phrases represent a living Ukrainian tradition. They are not merely grammatical exercises; they are the actual words people use to express care and love. You will often hear the shorter form **Будьте здорові!** (Be healthy!) as a toast or a response to a sneeze. While this everyday phrasing is extremely common, the full Instrumental pattern (**будьте здоровими**) is the gold standard for formal wishes and toasts. Another beautiful imperative is **живи** (live), often used in phrases like **Живи довго!** (Live long!).

To master this construction, a quick review of the Vocative case is essential. Remember that names and family terms change when you address someone directly:
- Олена → **Оленко**
- мама → **мамо**
- друг → **друже**
- друзі → **друзі** (For most plural nouns, the Nominative and Vocative cases share the exact same form).
- дитина → **дитино**

You can also create beautiful extended wishes by combining the Vocative case address with the third-person **хай** imperatives we learned earlier.
- **Хай тобі щастить!** (May you have luck!)
- **Нехай здійсняться всі мрії!** (May all dreams come true!) — In this poetic phrase, the verb **здійсняться** is in the third-person plural future tense, matching the subject **мрії** (dreams).

## Вид дієслова в наказовому способі (Aspect in Imperatives)

<div class="dialogue">


**Мама:** Читай більше книжок! *(Read more books!)*


**Син:** Добре, мамо. *(Okay, Mom.)*


**Мама:** А зараз прочитай цю статтю. *(And now read this article.)*


**Син:** Я не хочу. *(I don't want to.)*


**Мама:** Не читай її всю, але прочитай перший абзац. *(Don't read all of it, but read the first paragraph.)*


</div>



The most challenging aspect of mastering Ukrainian commands is choosing the correct verbal aspect. The choice between an imperfective and perfective verb completely changes the tone, urgency, and meaning of your command.

We use the imperfective imperative when we want to express general advice, encourage a repeated action, or make a polite invitation. 
- **Читай більше!** (Read more!) — This is general advice for life, not a demand for right now.
- **Пишіть щодня!** (Write every day!) — This establishes a habitual, repeated action.
- **Сідайте, будь ласка.** (Please sit down.) — Imperfective verbs are inherently more polite for invitations because they invite the person to begin a process comfortably, rather than demanding a completed result.

We use the perfective imperative when we need a specific, one-time action completed with a clear result. It is direct and expects immediate compliance.
- **Прочитай цю статтю!** (Read this article!) — The speaker expects the listener to read the article from beginning to end right now.
- **Напиши мені!** (Write to me!) — The speaker wants a specific message sent.
- **Закрий двері!** (Close the door!) — A single, immediate physical action.

The rules shift significantly when we form negative imperatives (telling someone not to do something). Usually, negative commands require an imperfective verb. This makes logical sense: you are telling the person to never begin the process, or to stop a process that is already happening.
- **Не читай це!** (Don't read this!)
- **Не відкривайте вікно!** (Don't open the window!)

However, if you use the negative word **не** with a perfective imperative, the meaning transforms from a simple prohibition into an urgent warning. It implies that the person might accidentally do something dangerous or undesirable.
- **Не впади!** (Don't fall!) — You are not forbidding them from falling; you are warning them to be careful.
- **Не забудь ключі!** (Don't forget the keys!) — A warning to be mindful.

**Summary table of aspect rules:**
- General advice / Habitual action → Imperfective (**Читай!**)
- Specific one-time task → Perfective (**Прочитай!**)
- Polite invitation → Imperfective (**Сідайте!**)
- Negative prohibition ("Don't do it") → Usually Imperfective (**Не читай!**)
- Urgent warning ("Be careful not to") → Perfective (**Не впади!**)
