## Тема і рема: що відоме, що нове? (Theme and Rheme) (~600 words total)
- P1 (~100 words): Introduce the concept of "free" word order in Ukrainian. Explain that while grammar doesn't strictly dictate word placement, order is far from random. It is driven by the flow of information: what is already known (тема - theme) and what is the new, important information (рема - rheme).
- P2 (~120 words): Detail the fundamental rule of Ukrainian information structure: in a neutral context, the known information (тема) comes first, and the new information (рема) comes at the very end. The "news" is always delivered last, carrying the logical stress (логічний наголос). Use the example: "Тарас (theme) купив нову книгу (rheme)."
- P3 (~130 words): Demonstrate how to test for theme and rheme by answering questions. If the question is "Хто купив книгу?", the book is known, and the person is new: "Книгу купив Тарас" (Тарас = rheme, placed last). If the question is "Що купив Тарас?", Taras is known: "Тарас купив книгу" (книгу = rheme, placed last).
- P4 (~150 words): Contrast this directly with English. English relies on a rigid Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order to indicate who is doing what, often using vocal intonation to shift emphasis. Ukrainian, however, uses case endings (like accusative "доньку" vs nominative "мама") to show grammatical roles. This frees up the word order to handle meaning and focus. "Мама любить доньку" and "Доньку любить мама" mean the same thing grammatically but have entirely different focuses.
- P5 (~100 words): Summarize that mastering Ukrainian word order means learning to place the communicative focus at the end of the sentence naturally, rather than relying solely on vocal stress like in English.

## Прямий порядок слів (Neutral Word Order) (~550 words total)
- P1 (~120 words): Define direct word order (прямий порядок слів) as the default, emotionally neutral arrangement used when reporting facts, writing essays, or starting a story. The core structure is Subject + Predicate + Object (SVO). Provide examples: "Студент читає книгу" or "Молоде покоління прагне свободи."
- P2 (~130 words): Explain the neutral placement of modifiers. Adjectives naturally go before the noun they modify ("нова книга", "тихий світанок"). Adverbs of manner are quite flexible but typically sit close to the verb, either before or after ("добре працює" or "працює добре"), with minimal shift in meaning.
- P3 (~100 words): Highlight a critical L2 error regarding adjectives and predicates. Explain that swapping the adjective and noun changes the sentence structure entirely. "Тихий світанок" is a noun phrase ("A quiet dawn"), whereas "Світанок тихий" is a full sentence with a nominal predicate ("The dawn is quiet"). 
- P4 (~100 words): Detail the placement of time and place expressions (обставини). They usually sit at the beginning to set the scene ("Вчора ми ходили в кіно") or at the end if they are the neutral focus of the sentence ("Ми ходили в кіно вчора").
- P5 (~100 words): Conclude that while direct order is the baseline, sticking to it 100% of the time in conversation will sound slightly robotic or overly formal to native speakers.

## Інверсія для контрасту (Fronting for Contrast) (~650 words total)
- P1 (~100 words): Introduce indirect word order, or inversion (інверсія). Explain that we break the neutral SVO pattern to emphasize a specific word, create contrast, or add emotional weight by "fronting" an element.
- P2 (~100 words): Dialogue: Friends discussing a movie. "Цей фільм зняв Сенцов. — Ні, цей фільм зняв не Сенцов, а Лозниця. — А от «Номери» — це точно Сенцов зняв."
- P3 (~130 words): Analyze the dialogue to explain fronting the object (OVS). "Цей фільм зняв Сенцов." The object (фільм) is fronted to become the theme ("As for this movie..."), pushing the subject (Сенцов) to the end as the rheme. Contrast this with the neutral "Сенцов зняв фільм."
- P4 (~120 words): Explain fronting the verb for strong assertion or emotion (V-S). "Прочитав я цю книгу!" (I DID read this book!) sounds much more defensive or expressive than the neutral "Я прочитав цю книгу." Mention that V-S is also a classic narrative opener: "Прийшла весна."
- P5 (~100 words): Detail corrective contrast using "не... а". When correcting someone, the corrected element is fronted: "Не Тарас це зробив, а Олег." (Not Taras did this, but Oleh).
- P6 (~100 words): Introduce cleft-like patterns using "Це". To explicitly identify the rheme at the beginning of a sentence, use "Це + noun": "Це Тарас допоміг мені" (It was Taras who helped me).

## Порядок слів у реальному мовленні (Word Order in Real Speech) (~400 words total)
- P1 (~100 words): Dialogue: Roommates dividing chores. "Хто помив посуд? — Посуд помив Тарас. А підлогу? — Підлогу ще ніхто не помив. Добре, підлогу помию я."
- P2 (~120 words): Analyze how word order shifts organically in the dialogue based on the flow of conversation. "Посуд" is introduced in the question, so in the answer, it becomes the known theme and moves to the front ("Посуд помив Тарас"). The same happens with "Підлогу."
- P3 (~100 words): Discuss how answering questions naturally dictates word order. If asked "Що ти купив?", the neutral SVO order works perfectly: "Я купив каву" (coffee is new). But if asked "Хто це купив?", the object is known, so it fronts: "Це купив я" (OVS).
- P4 (~80 words): Caution against mechanical translation. Warn learners not to use a marked order unintentionally. Answering "What did you buy?" with "Каву купив я" sounds bizarre, as if you are declaring "It was I who bought the coffee."
