## Сценарій 1: Плануємо вечірку (Scenario 1: Planning a Party)

Welcome to A2! In this phase, we move beyond simply describing things and start coordinating plans, solving problems, and telling stories. Today, **Олена** *(Olena)* and **Марк** *(Mark)* are planning a housewarming party — **новосілля** *(housewarming)*. Organizing an event requires specific language skills. We need to discuss future plans, count guests, identify what is missing, and confirm what is already done. We will achieve this by combining our knowledge of the Genitive case with a new superpower: verb aspect. Let's see how they start their planning process for the upcoming celebration.

> — **Олена:** Марку, ми **будемо святкувати** *(will celebrate)* новосілля в суботу.
> — **Марк:** Чудова ідея! А скільки гостей **прийде** *(will come)*?
> — **Олена:** Думаю, десять людей. Нам треба багато місця.
> — **Марк:** Це проблема. У нас **немає великого стола** *(don't have a large table)*.
> — **Олена:** Але у нас **є багато стільців** *(have many chairs)*. Ми зробимо фуршет.
> — **Марк:** Добре. Що ми **будемо купувати** *(will be buying)*?
> — **Олена:** Нам потрібні **напої** *(drinks)*, **серветки** *(napkins)* і **торт** *(cake)*.
> — **Марк:** Я **буду готувати** *(will be preparing)* піцу і салати.
> — **Олена:** А я **буду прибирати** *(will be cleaning)* квартиру.

When planning an event, we often focus on the process of preparation, the routine, or extended activities. In Ukrainian, we use the imperfective future tense to describe these ongoing or repeated future actions. You form this tense using the auxiliary verb **бути** *(to be)* conjugated for the person, combined with the infinitive of the main verb. It is a very common and easy way to talk about future plans.

*   **Я буду готувати піцу ввечері.** *(I will be preparing pizza in the evening.)*
*   **Ми будемо купувати напої завтра.** *(We will be buying drinks tomorrow.)*
*   **Вони будуть прибирати кімнату довго.** *(They will be cleaning the room for a long time.)*
*   **Ти будеш слухати музику чи працювати?** *(Will you be listening to music or working?)*
*   **Він буде чекати гостей біля метро.** *(He will be waiting for guests near the subway.)*

This structure highlights the action itself as a process. When Mark says «Я буду готувати», he is talking about the time he will spend making food in the kitchen, not just the final result on the table.

Planning also involves managing resources. What do we have, and what is missing? To express absence, we use the word **немає** *(there is no / does not have)* followed by the Genitive case.

*   **У нас немає великого стола.** *(We don't have a large table.)*
*   **У магазині немає торта.** *(The store has no cake.)*
*   **Тут немає кави.** *(There is no coffee here.)*

When organizing a party, you also deal with quantities. Words like **багато** *(many/much)* and **мало** *(few/little)* also require the Genitive case. For plural nouns, we use the Genitive plural.

*   **Нам треба купити багато фруктів.** *(We need to buy many fruits.)*
*   **У нас мало серветок.** *(We have few napkins.)*
*   **Там було багато людей.** *(There were many people there.)*
*   **Я бачу мало тарілок.** *(I see few plates.)*

### Читаємо українською (Reading Practice)

Олена і Марк дуже люблять приймати гостей. Вони хочуть зробити ідеальне новосілля. Олена складає довгий список продуктів, тому що їм треба багато їжі. Марк перевіряє шафи. Він бачить, що вдома немає соку, але є багато води. Також у них мало чистих склянок. Вони вирішують піти в супермаркет разом. Там вони будуть вибирати найкращі фрукти і смачні десерти.

> — **Олена:** Марку, вечірка вже сьогодні! Ти **купив** *(bought)* торт?
> — **Марк:** Так, я вже **купив** два торти.
> — **Олена:** Чудово. А ти **приготував** *(prepared)* салат?
> — **Марк:** Так, я **приготував** великий салат і **замовив** *(ordered)* піцу.
> — **Олена:** Молодець! А де напої?
> — **Марк:** Я **поставив** *(put)* їх у холодильник.
> — **Олена:** Добре. Я вже **прибрала** *(cleaned)* всю квартиру.
> — **Марк:** Гості скоро прийдуть. Ми готові!

Notice the significant shift in verbs on the day of the party. The focus is no longer on the process of preparation or what they were doing. The focus is strictly on the finished results and achievements. We use perfective verbs to confirm that tasks are completely finished. Compare the imperfective plan, the perfective intention, and the perfective result:

*   Imperfective Plan: **Я буду готувати вечерю.** *(I will be cooking dinner - process.)*
*   Perfective Intention: **Я приготую вечерю.** *(I will cook dinner - focusing on getting it done.)*
*   Perfective Result: **Я приготувала вечерю.** *(I cooked dinner - it is finished and ready.)*
*   Imperfective Plan: **Ми будемо купувати торт.** *(We will be shopping for a cake.)*
*   Perfective Result: **Ми купили торт.** *(We bought a cake - here it is.)*

You use perfective verbs when the action is completely done, closed, and the result is what matters right now for the current situation.

## Сценарій 2: На ринку (Scenario 2: At the Market)

Going to a **ринок** *(market)* in Ukraine is a lively cultural experience. Unlike a quiet supermarket, a Ukrainian market is full of conversation and fresh, seasonal products. Here, you can taste the food before you buy it and chat with the farmers. You will find fresh **мед** *(honey)*, homemade **сир** *(cottage cheese)*, seasonal **ягоди** *(berries)*, and rich **сметана** *(sour cream)*. When you walk between the stalls, you need to know how to ask for prices and specify exact quantities. Etiquette dictates a friendly but direct approach. You always greet the vendor, state exactly what you want using the correct grammar, and ask how much it costs. 

Let's look at a typical conversation at a honey stall. Notice how the shopper asks for the price of a specific weight. When you specify a quantity like a kilogram, the item itself must be in the Genitive case.

> — **Покупець:** Добрий день! *(Good day!)*
> — **Продавець:** Доброго дня! Що бажаєте? *(Good day! What would you like?)*
> — **Покупець:** Скажіть, будь ласка, скільки коштує кілограм меду? *(Tell me, please, how much does a kilogram of honey cost?)*
> — **Продавець:** Цей лісовий мед коштує сто гривень за кілограм. *(This forest honey costs one hundred hryvnias per kilogram.)*
> — **Покупець:** Він дуже смачний. Дайте, будь ласка, два кілограми. *(It is very tasty. Give me, please, two kilograms.)*
> — **Продавець:** Звісно. Щось ще? *(Of course. Anything else?)*
> — **Покупець:** Ні, дякую. Це все. *(No, thank you. That is all.)*

When shopping, you constantly use numbers. In Ukrainian, numbers change the grammatical case of the noun that follows them. This is often called the "1, 2-4, 5+ Rule."

*   **Number 1 (and numbers ending in 1, except 11):** Use the Nominative Singular case.
    *   **Один кілограм** *(one kilogram)*
    *   **Одне яблуко** *(one apple)*
*   **Numbers 2, 3, 4 (and numbers ending in them, except 12-14):** Use the Genitive Singular case.
    *   **Два кілограми** *(two kilograms)*
    *   **Три яблука** *(three apples)*
*   **Numbers 5 and above (including 5-20):** Use the Genitive Plural case.
    *   **П'ять кілограмів** *(five kilograms)*
    *   **Десять яблук** *(ten apples)*

Forming the Genitive Plural might seem tricky, but there are clear patterns for common market items. For masculine nouns ending in a hard consonant, we usually add the ending **-ів**.

*   **Один помідор** $\rightarrow$ **п'ять помідорів** *(five tomatoes)*
*   **Один лимон** $\rightarrow$ **десять лимонів** *(ten lemons)*
*   **Один кілограм** $\rightarrow$ **шість кілограмів** *(six kilograms)*

For most feminine nouns ending in **-а** or **-я**, and neuter nouns ending in **-о**, we use a "zero ending" where we drop the final vowel.

*   **Одна диня** $\rightarrow$ **п'ять динь** *(five melons)*
*   **Одна ягода** $\rightarrow$ **багато ягід** *(many berries)*
*   **Одне яблуко** $\rightarrow$ **сім яблук** *(seven apples)*

Sometimes the vendor does not have what you need. When expressing absence or negating a direct object, Ukrainian uses the Genitive case. This is called the Genitive of Negation.

> — **Покупець:** Вибачте, у вас є свіжі лимони? *(Excuse me, do you have fresh lemons?)*
> — **Продавець:** Вибачте, у мене немає лимонів. *(Excuse me, I do not have lemons.)*
> — **Покупець:** Шкода. А ви бачите помідори? *(A pity. And do you see tomatoes?)*
> — **Продавець:** Ні, я не бачу помідорів. Сьогодні їх немає. *(No, I do not see tomatoes. Today they are not here.)*
> — **Покупець:** Добре, я не купую овочі сьогодні. *(Good, I am not buying vegetables today.)*

After negative verbs like **не бачу** *(I do not see)* or **не купую** *(I do not buy)*, the object often switches from the Accusative case to the Genitive case. To finish your purchase politely, you need a few standard phrases. When the vendor asks if you want anything else, you can say **Це все** *(That is all)*. Then, you ask for the total price using **Скільки з мене?** *(How much do I owe?)*. When you hand over the cash, you can say **Тримайте решту** *(Keep the change)* if you want to leave a tip. Always remember your politeness markers: **Будь ласка** *(Please)* and **Дякую** *(Thank you)*.

### Читаємо українською (Reading Practice)
Сьогодні субота. Марк і Олена йдуть на ринок. Вони хочуть купити свіжі продукти. На ринку дуже багато людей. Марк купує овочі. Він бере один кілограм картоплі. Також він бере три помідори. Олена шукає фрукти. Вона дуже любить солодкі ягоди.

> — **Олена:** Дайте, будь ласка, кілограм полуниці. *(Give me, please, a kilogram of strawberries.)*
> — **Продавець:** Звісно. Тримайте. Це дуже смачна полуниця. *(Of course. Here you go. This is a very tasty strawberry.)*
> — **Олена:** Скільки з мене? *(How much do I owe?)*
> — **Продавець:** Вісімдесят гривень. *(Eighty hryvnias.)*
> — **Олена:** Дякую. А у вас є сметана? *(Thank you. And do you have sour cream?)*
> — **Продавець:** Ні, вибачте, у мене немає сметани. *(No, excuse me, I do not have sour cream.)*

## Сценарій 3: Як пройшов твій день? (Scenario 3: How Was Your Day?)

Now that you can plan your future and manage daily transactions, it is time to look back. Reporting on past events is one of the most common conversational tasks. When you talk with friends or colleagues, they will inevitably ask you about your day. Moving from describing what you usually do to detailing exactly what you did yesterday requires a new tool. This brings us to the concept of the "Daily Narrative Arc." You are no longer just stating facts; you are telling a story with a beginning, middle, and end. To do this effectively in Ukrainian, you need to master how to describe both the flow of time and the completion of specific milestones.

Let us look at a typical story about a workday. Pay attention to how the verbs change depending on whether the action is ongoing or finished.

### Читаємо українською (Reading Practice)
**Мій продуктивний вівторок** *(My productive Tuesday)*
> Учора був дуже звичайний день. *(Yesterday was a very ordinary day.)*
> Вранці я довго **снідав** і **читав** новини. *(In the morning I ate breakfast for a long time and read the news.)*
> Я **пив** гарячу каву на кухні. *(I drank hot coffee in the kitchen.)*
> Потім я **пішов** на роботу. *(Then I went to work.)*
> В офісі я багато **працював**. *(In the office I worked a lot.)*
> Спочатку я **написав** три листи. *(First I wrote three letters.)*
> Потім я **зателефонував** босу і ми **обговорили** новий проєкт. *(Then I called the boss and we discussed a new project.)*
> Вдень я **гуляв** у парку. *(During the day I walked in the park.)*
> Увечері я нарешті **прочитав** ту цікаву статтю. *(In the evening I finally read through that interesting article.)*
> Після цього я **приготував** вечерю і ліг спати. *(After that I prepared dinner and went to sleep.)*

When you tell a story, you need to establish the background. In Ukrainian, we use Imperfective verbs for this. Think of an Imperfective verb as a "movie camera." It records the process, the duration, or the habit, without focusing on the end result. In the text above, verbs like **снідав** *(ate breakfast)*, **читав** *(read)*, **пив** *(drank)*, and **гуляв** *(walked)* are Imperfective. They show that an action was happening over a period of time. You often use these verbs with time words that emphasize duration, such as **довго** *(for a long time)*, **часто** *(often)*, or **цілий день** *(all day)*. The focus is entirely on the activity itself, not on finishing it.
* **Я довго читав книгу.** *(I read the book for a long time. / I was reading the book.)*
* **Вона цілий вечір дивилася телевізор.** *(She watched TV all evening. / She was watching TV.)*
* **Ми часто гуляли в цьому парку.** *(We often walked in this park.)*

If Imperfective verbs are movie cameras, Perfective verbs are "photo cameras." They take a snapshot of a completed action. A Perfective verb shows a result, a milestone, or a single finished event. In our story, **пішов** *(went)*, **написав** *(wrote)*, **зателефонував** *(called)*, **прочитав** *(read completely)*, and **приготував** *(prepared)* are Perfective. You use Perfective verbs to move the plot forward. They create a chain of events: first A happened, then B, then C. You cannot use Perfective verbs to describe habits or ongoing processes.
* **Я прочитав книгу.** *(I read the book to the end. / I have finished the book.)*
* **Вона подивилася фільм.** *(She watched the movie to the end.)*
* **Спочатку він пообідав, а потім зателефонував другові.** *(First he had lunch, and then he called a friend.)*

The real magic of the Ukrainian past tense happens when you combine both aspects. You can use an Imperfective verb to set a scene, and a Perfective verb to introduce a sudden, completed event that interrupts it. The word **коли** *(when)* is your best friend for these contrasts. You visualize the timeline: a long continuous line (Imperfective) is suddenly crossed by a sharp point (Perfective).
* **Я писав листа, коли ти подзвонив.** *(I was writing a letter when you called.)*
* **Ми вечеряли, коли почався дощ.** *(We were having dinner when the rain started.)*
* **Олена читала новини, коли побачила це фото.** *(Olena was reading the news when she saw this photo.)*
If two actions happen at the exact same time as continuous processes, both are Imperfective:
* **Я готував вечерю, а вона читала.** *(I was preparing dinner, and she was reading.)*

Because of aspect, even simple questions in Ukrainian carry deeper meanings. When you ask someone about their day, you have to choose your focus. If you ask **«Що ти робив?»** *(What were you doing? / How did you spend your time?)*, you are using the Imperfective. You expect a list of activities: "I worked, I read, I walked." You are interested in the process. However, if you ask **«Що ти зробив?»** *(What did you achieve? / What did you get done?)*, you are using the Perfective. You expect a list of results: "I finished the report, I bought milk."
> — **Тарас:** Що ти вчора **робив**? *(What were you doing yesterday?)*
> — **Іван:** Я **читав** книгу і **писав** статтю. *(I was reading a book and writing an article.)*
> — **Тарас:** А що ти **зробив**? *(And what did you achieve?)*
> — **Іван:** Я **прочитав** п'ятдесят сторінок і **написав** два абзаци! *(I read fifty pages and wrote two paragraphs!)*

To report on your day accurately, you need to know both sides of the coin for common actions. Here is a table of essential aspect pairs. The first verb is Imperfective (process), and the second is Perfective (result).

| Недоконаний вид (Imperfective) | Доконаний вид (Perfective) | Переклад (Translation) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **робити** | **зробити** | *to do / to make* |
| **писати** | **написати** | *to write* |
| **читати** | **прочитати** | *to read* |
| **снідати** | **поснідати** | *to have breakfast* |
| **обідати** | **пообідати** | *to have lunch* |
| **вечеряти** | **повечеряти** | *to have dinner* |
| **купувати** | **купити** | *to buy* |
| **готувати** | **приготувати** | *to prepare / to cook* |

Let us practice these pairs in a short dialogue about a weekend trip.

> — **Анна:** Що ви **робили** в суботу? *(What were you doing on Saturday?)*
> — **Марк:** Ми **гуляли** містом. *(We were walking around the city.)*
> — **Анна:** Ви щось **купили**? *(Did you buy anything?)*
> — **Марк:** Так, ми **купили** сувеніри. *(Yes, we bought souvenirs.)*
> — **Анна:** А де ви **обідали**? *(And where did you have lunch?)*
> — **Марк:** Ми **пообідали** в новому кафе. *(We had lunch at a new cafe.)*

The A2 level is all about adding rich details, correct endings, and clear intentions to the strong foundation you already built in A1.

Take a moment for a quick self-check before moving on. Can you confidently say **«Я буду готувати смачну вечерю»** *(I will be preparing a tasty dinner)* to discuss your future plans? Do you remember the essential rule for counting items at the market: **один лимон** *(one lemon)*, **два лимони** *(two lemons)*, but **п'ять лимонів** *(five lemons)*? Most importantly, can you clearly feel the difference between the ongoing process in **«Я писав довгого листа»** *(I was writing a long letter)* and the finalized result in **«Я написав листа»** *(I wrote a letter)*? 

If you understand these concepts, you are perfectly prepared for the next challenges. **Пам'ятайте, що щоденна практика дає найкращий результат!** *(Remember that daily practice gives the best result!)* Keep using these new grammatical structures in your thoughts, and soon they will become second nature. **Ви чудово попрацювали сьогодні. До зустрічі в наступному уроці!** *(You worked wonderfully today. See you in the next lesson!)*
