**Alignment finding**: Temporal conjunctions «коли» and «поки» are **not explicitly listed** in §4.4.2. The plan's reference to "§1.4.1.1" is a communicative activity standard (narrative monologue sequencing), not a syntax standard — this is architecturally sound, as communicative needs can justify grammar beyond the explicit §4.4 list. Grade 9 textbooks (Avramenko, Voron tier 2) treat temporal subordinate clauses as core Gr. 9 content in Ukrainian schools. The A2 module is introducing this topic earlier for L2 communicative purposes, grounded in the narrative production requirement. Content writer should frame this as a communicative-first presentation, not a grammar taxonomy exercise.

## Cultural Hooks

1. **«Поки не зійде перша зірка»** — Святий Вечір (Christmas Eve) tradition: Ukrainian families traditionally wait until the first star appears in the sky before beginning Свята Вечеря. Date: 24 December (Gregorian/new Julian calendar) or 6 January (Julian calendar). Source: Wikipedia — Святий Вечір (uk.wikipedia.org). This is a live, natural use of the «поки не» structure. Украіна officially celebrates Christmas on 25 December (law from 2023), so both dates now coexist culturally.

2. **«Не кажи «гоп», поки не перескочиш»** — Ukrainian proverb (confirmed in Avramenko Grade 9 textbook). Pedagogically ideal: native speakers know this proverb, it demonstrates поки не naturally, and it can serve as a memorable anchor for the double-negative rule. Source: Textbook Grade 9, Avramenko (tier 2).

## Common Learner Errors

1. **The "when/if" confusion**: Using «коли» for conditional meaning → *Коли я матиму гроші, куплю машину* (correct: «якщо» for hypothetical / «коли» for predicted certain future). Happens because English "when" covers both temporal and conditional meaning; Ukrainian separates them.

2. **Missing the compound linker**: «Перед тим як» reduced to «перед» + bare infinitive or pronoun → *Перед я пішов* instead of «Перед тим як я пішов». Happens because learners notice «перед» is a preposition and apply prepositional phrase rules instead of subordinating clause rules.

3. **The «поки не» double-negative trap**: Learners produce «Я чекаю, поки ти прийдеш» (missing не) instead of «Я чекаю, поки ти не прийдеш» (= until you come). In Ukrainian, «поки не» requires не in the subordinate clause even though the meaning is not negative — the не is a logical boundary marker, not semantic negation. This pattern has no direct parallel in English.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **Immersion band**: Module 38 falls in the "Applied grammar" band (M21-50), target 55-75% Ukrainian. English permitted for abstract grammar explanations only; never mixed within a sentence.
- **Sentence length cap**: Max 15 words per Ukrainian sentence, max 2 clauses — this means dialogue and example sentences demonstrating time clauses must be kept tight. A time clause + main clause easily fills 15 words; avoid compound structures on top of that.
- **Pointer words (вказівні слова)**: Ukrainian grammar uses тоді as pointer for коли, доти for поки. Introducing these in a simple two-column chart will help learners recognize the full construction: *Тоді починай, коли будеш готовий.*
- **Aspect in time clauses**: The plan lists "aspect in time clauses" as a grammar point. At A2 the rule is simple: perfective in «після того як» and «як тільки» (completed action triggers new action); imperfective in «поки» (ongoing process). Keep this light — one rule, two examples.
- **Punctuation**: All Ukrainian time clauses require comma before the conjunction (or after the temporal clause if it leads). This is mechanical but must be modeled consistently in examples.
- **Proverbs as anchors**: Both «Не кажи «гоп», поки не перескочиш» and «Всьому свій час» (plan's framing proverb) are VESUM-clean, culturally authentic, and pedagogically useful. Use them as recurring reference points, not just one-time decorations.
- **Decolonized framing**: Time clause connectives in Ukrainian developed within the Slavic grammatical tradition independently. Frame «поки» as a uniquely Ukrainian conjunction with its double-negative boundary logic — describe it positively as Ukrainian expressing logical precision, not as something unusual or difficult.
