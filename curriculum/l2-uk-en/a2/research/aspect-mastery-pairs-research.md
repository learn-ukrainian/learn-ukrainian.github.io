## Cultural Hooks

1. **Борщ (UNESCO intangible heritage, 2022)**: Ukrainian borscht received UNESCO inscription in December 2022. A perfective-string cooking narrative (купив овочі → помив → приготував → подав на стіл) is an authentic, emotionally resonant cultural anchor for sequential perfective usage. Over 100 regional variations exist.
2. **Толока (communal labor tradition)**: Neighbors gather to build or harvest together — a pre-industrial mutual aid practice. Building a хата-мазанка involves both ongoing process (будувати = imperfective, days of work) and completion milestone (побудували = perfective). The tradition is still referenced in modern Ukrainian speech and carries positive connotations of community solidarity.
3. **Contextual trigger words in everyday Ukrainian**: Temporal adverbs like раптом, вже, нарешті naturally collocate with perfective verbs in Ukrainian daily speech — listening to Ukrainian TV or market conversations quickly reveals this pattern. Likewise, щодня, завжди, часто trigger imperfective. These are learnable cues, not abstract rules.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Prefix overgeneralization to з-/с-**: Learners treat з- as the universal perfective prefix → *змити (correct: помити), *зробити квиток (correct: купити квиток). Fix: Teach the top prefix-per-verb-family pattern; з-/с- only applies to specific semantic clusters.
2. **Perfective for durations**: Using perfective with time-span expressions → *Я купив молоко дві години (correct: Я купував молоко дві години). Durative adverbials (дві години, довго, весь день) block perfective aspect.
3. **Present-perfective trap**: Attempting to form a perfective present → *Я напишу зараз meaning "I am writing now." Perfective present forms are FUTURE in meaning (напишу = I will write). This is unique to Ukrainian/Slavic and requires explicit attention.
4. **Imperfective for single completed transactions**: Using imperfective when a one-time result is meant → Я купував квиток (implies repeated/habitual) vs. Я купив квиток (I bought the ticket — done). Very common in transactional contexts (café, market, ticket booth).

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise**: Present two WhatsApp-style message bubbles side by side:
  > A: "Що ти робиш?" / B: "Готую борщ."
  > A: "Ти вже приготував борщ?" / B: "Так, щойно приготував!"
  Ask learner: *What's different about the two verb forms? Which describes the process? Which describes the result?* Let them notice before naming the rule.

- **Best context**: Market/shop transactions (купувати vs купити), phone calls (дзвонити vs подзвонити), and household tasks (прибирати vs прибрати). These are high-frequency, low-stakes contexts where the process/result distinction is immediately felt.

- **Contrastive pairs for disambiguation**:
  - Я платив довго (I was paying for a long time — process, unusual) vs. Я заплатив (I paid — done)
  - Вони будували дім рік (They were building a house for a year) vs. Вони побудували дім (They built/completed the house)
  - Я забував це слово щодня (I kept forgetting the word every day) vs. Я забув слово (I forgot the word — this moment)

- **Textbook model**: While the excerpts provided are table-of-contents pages only, standard Grade 7 Авраменко exercises use fill-in-the-blank with underlined infinitives and contextual cues in the sentence — this is the exact structure suitable for the Практика section drills.

## Notes for Content Writing

- Immersion band: 45–65% Ukrainian (M01-20 Core grammar band per A2 quick-ref). Grammar theory may be in English; Ukrainian examples are the primary vehicle.
- Max 15 words per Ukrainian sentence; max 2 clauses. No participles.
- Borscht narrative in Практика section should use perfective string explicitly labeled — learner must see the chain: купив → помив → почистив → приготував → подав.
- The Toloka dialogue in Діалоги should contrast imperfective process lines (будували разом, допомагали сусідам) with the climactic perfective (нарешті побудували!).
- Do NOT use брати/взяти as a primary teaching example — suppletive pairs are confusing at this stage; reserve for a footnote or sidebar.
- Stress marks (´) required on all Ukrainian words. No Latin transliteration.
