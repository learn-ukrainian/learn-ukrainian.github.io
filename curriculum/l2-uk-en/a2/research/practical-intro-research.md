## Cultural Hooks

1. **Суржик as a transitional stage** — Wikipedia confirms: *"Суржик — елементи двох або кількох мов, об'єднані штучно, без дотримання норм літературної мови."* For speakers switching to Ukrainian, surzhyk is a documented transitional phenomenon — not a failure but a linguistic bridge. Framing grammar errors as a normal step in language acquisition (not as "bad Ukrainian") is pedagogically powerful and aligns with decolonized approaches that treat Ukrainian as a self-sufficient standard, not defined against Russian.

2. **Кличний відмінок as a Ukrainian cultural marker** — Wikipedia: *"Кличний відмінок посилює стилістичні функції звертань."* The Vocative is structurally distinct in Ukrainian (unlike many European languages). Real examples from the State Standard: *"Пане Юрію, повторіть, будь ласка"* (formal), *"Бабусю, сідайте"* (warm familial). This case encodes social register and respect directly into grammar — a cultural hook with high engagement value for L2 learners navigating Ukrainian social contexts.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Genitive vs. Accusative after negation** → *Я не бачу книгу* (incorrect, Acc) → *Я не бачу книги* (correct, Gen) — Why: English has no case alternation under negation; learners default to the direct object form they already know (Accusative).

2. **The Perfective Present trap** → *"я напишу"* is FUTURE, not present — Why: Perfective verbs cannot form a true present tense in Ukrainian; their conjugated forms (напишу, напишеш…) always refer to future completion. Learners who conjugate a perfective verb expecting a present meaning produce future meaning unintentionally. Grade 7 Avramenko (tier 1) states explicitly: *"Усі дієслова у формі теперішнього часу завжди недоконаного виду."*

3. **Vocative avoided entirely** → learners use Nominative for direct address (*Юра, підійди!* instead of *Юрію, підійди!*; *Олена, допоможи!* instead of *Оленко, допоможи!*) — Why: The Vocative has no equivalent in English; learners find the paradigm irregular and default to the dictionary form. Social consequence is real: using Nominative for direct address sounds unnatural or cold to native speakers.

4. **Motion verb йти/їхати confusion** → using *йти* for vehicle travel (*Я йду до Варшави автобусом* → *Я їду до Варшави автобусом*) — Why: English "go" covers both foot and vehicle movement; Ukrainian distinguishes them lexically.

## Notes for Content Writing

- The **Genitive-Accusative negation drill** must use concrete, everyday objects (not abstract nouns — A2 constraint). Examples: книга, кава, друг, час, гроші.
- The **Perfective Present trap** is the single most important aspect insight at this level. The minimal pair table (5 pairs, as the plan specifies) should contrast *пишу / напишу*, *читаю / прочитаю*, *говорю / скажу*, *іду / прийду*, *роблю / зроблю*.
- The **Vocative section** should include a social register mini-table: *Юра → Юрію* (formal), *Олена → Оленко* (warm), *пан Ігор → пане Ігорю* (official). The State Standard example sentences are directly usable.
- Complex sentence conjunctions §4.4.2 specifies exactly тому що / бо (reason) and щоб (purpose). The plan also includes якщо (condition) — this is present in the Standard implicitly via conditional subordinate clauses at A2. Use it.
- **Immersion target for Module 61 (Consolidation band):** 70–90% Ukrainian. English permitted only in vocabulary tables and one-line grammar notes.
- **Decolonized framing reminder:** The Vocative case exists in Ukrainian, Polish, and many other languages — always present it as a Ukrainian feature, not as "something Russian doesn't have." Frame Surzhyk through the lens of language resilience and the Ukrainian norm as the positive destination, not a correction of Russian.
- Word budget check: 300 + 525 + 425 + 425 + 325 = 2000 words. Sections are balanced; no section is under-allocated. Meet target by ensuring each point is developed with concrete examples (the aspect section especially needs 5 minimal pairs and motion verb scenarios — these take word count).
