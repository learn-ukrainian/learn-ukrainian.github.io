# Дослідження: Plural Adjective Declension

## State Standard Reference

Alignment: This module delivers the plural half of the full A2 adjective paradigm. The singular half is covered in the prerequisite A2 singular adjective module. Together they fulfill §4.2.1.2 completely.

## Cultural Hooks
1. **Ukrainian city descriptions**: Kyiv's districts are commonly described with plural adjective phrases — старі вулиці Подолу, нові квартали Оболоні, великі парки Голосієво. City-guide Ukrainian is rich in Locative and Genitive plural adjective phrases; learners encounter this immediately in travel contexts.
2. **Market/bazaar language**: At any Ukrainian ринок, vendors use plural adjectives to describe goods: свіжі овочі, великі яблука, маленькі помідори, різні фрукти. This grounds Nominative and Accusative plural in a real, highly memorable context. Textbook exercises (Grade 3, kravtsova p. 71) use именники + прикметники in plural matching tasks — the same marketplace vocabulary works at A2.
3. **Folk plurals in proverbs**: Ukrainian proverbs frequently use plural noun-adjective agreement: *Великі річки з малих струмків починаються* ("Great rivers begin from small streams") — a natural mnemonic for великі/малих contrast in Nom/Gen plural.

## Common Learner Errors
1. **Singular genitive bleed** → using великого/великої where великих is required (*немає великого студентів* → *немає великих студентів*) — caused by over-applying singular paradigm learned first.
2. **Russian Nom plural interference** → *великые/великие* → Ukrainian uses unified **-і** for both hard and soft groups (*великі, сині*); the Russian -ые/-ие split does not apply. Textbook (Grade 4, zaharijchuk p. 66) targets this explicitly with a gender-collapse table.
3. **Acc plural animate/inanimate confusion** → treating animate Acc like Nominative (*бачу нові студенти* → *бачу нових студентів*); the Acc-anim = Gen rule is A1 for nouns but transfers to adjectives too — learners need explicit reminding.
4. **Genitive vs Locative conflation** → both share -их/-іх ending; learners drop prepositions or add them incorrectly (*у великих* vs *великих*). Preposition presence/absence is the only distinguishing signal.

## Teaching Strategy
- **Discovery exercise**: Show a photo of a Ukrainian city square (or market). Write two columns: *один будинок / багато будинків* alongside *великий будинок / великі будинки*. Ask: "What happens to the adjective when the noun goes plural? Does гарний/гарна/гарне stay different in plural?" Learners notice: the three gender forms collapse into one — *великі*. Then show Gen plural: *немає великого студента / немає великих студентів*. Model: Grade 4 zaharijchuk p. 66 — the table showing gender disappears in plural is the exact discovery moment to replicate.
- **Best context**: City and group descriptions (Ukrainian cities, student groups, market goods) — learner immediately needs "нові студенти", "старі будинки", "різні країни."
- **Contrastive pairs**: (1) *великий будинок* → *великі будинки* (Nom sg → Nom pl); (2) *великого будинку* → *великих будинків* (Gen sg → Gen pl showing gender collapse); (3) *бачу великий будинок* vs *бачу великих студентів* (Acc inanimate = Nom vs Acc animate = Gen).

## Notes for Content Writing
- The simplification message (plural collapses gender) is motivating and should be front-loaded — learners who struggled with 3-gender singular find plural relief.
- Soft group (-іх, -ім, -іми) is identical in structure to hard group (-их, -им, -ими) with і substituted for и — present this as a simple vowel swap, not a new paradigm.
- Gen and Loc plural share the same ending (-их/-іх): always pair them with the preposition/no-preposition contrast in every example sentence.
- A2 band 1 (M01-20): immersion target is 45-65% Ukrainian. Grammar theory (case names, rule statements) may be in English; all example sentences and drills must be 100% Ukrainian.
- Max 15 words per Ukrainian sentence; max 2 clauses.
