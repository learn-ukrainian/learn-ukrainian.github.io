## Cultural Hooks

1. **Червона калина as national symbol** (Source: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Калина_звичайна): Калина звичайна (*Viburnum opulus*) — a high, branching shrub 2–4 m tall with grey bark. In Ukrainian folk tradition, the red berries of the viburnum appear in embroidery, songs, and oral medicine. Viburnum tea (чай з калиною) with honey is a traditional treatment for fever and cold symptoms — the berries contain ascorbic acid and have antipyretic properties. The plant is so deeply embedded in Ukrainian identity that "Червона калина" became one of the emblematic patriotic songs during the full-scale war (2022–). **Pedagogical use**: connects language learning to living Ukrainian culture; avoids borrowing from any Soviet/Russian framing (kalyna is distinctly Ukrainian, not shared culturally with Russian folk medicine to the same degree).

2. **Будьмо! — the Ukrainian health toast** (Source: lexical/etymological; no Wikipedia article found under this title): *Будьмо!* is the 1st-person plural hortative of *бути* (to be) — literally "Let us be!" The full form is often *Будьмо здорові!* (Let us be healthy!). This toast encapsulates the Ukrainian value placed on health (*здоров'я*) as the primary social wish. It differs from the calqued *На здоров'я* (borrowable from many Slavic languages) in being a uniquely Ukrainian imperative construction. **Pedagogical use**: natural entry point to discuss щоб (purpose clauses) via "Ми кажемо 'Будьмо!', щоб побажати здоров'я."

## Common Learner Errors

1. **щоб vs. що б** — The conjunction *щоб* (in order to / so that) is written as one word: *Я прийшов, щоб поговорити.* Learners confuse it with *що б* (what would / whatever), written as two words: *Що б ти хотів з'їсти?* The error is triggered by phonetic identity and L1 (English) lack of a corresponding formal distinction. Drill with contrastive minimal pairs.

2. **Literal "take medicine" — брати → приймати/пити** — English speakers translate "take medicine" as *\*брати ліки* (lit. to take/grab), which is non-idiomatic. Correct forms: *приймати ліки* (formal, written instructions, pharmacy speech) and *пити ліки* (colloquial, spoken). *Брати* is used for physically picking objects up; it does not collocate with ліки in standard usage.

3. **який agreement errors** — Relative pronoun *який* must agree in gender and number with its antecedent (the noun it modifies), NOT with the subject of the relative clause. Learners often default to masculine singular regardless: *\*лікар, який вона бачила* (wrong gender: should be *яку* for feminine antecedent if noun is feminine). Concrete drill: give antecedent noun → produce correct *який* form.

4. **тому що vs. бо register** — Both mean "because," but *тому що* belongs to written/formal register; *бо* is natural in spoken Ukrainian. Overuse of *тому що* in casual dialogue sounds stilted. State Standard §4.4.2 introduces both; the checkpoint should reinforce register choice, not just grammatical correctness.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **Word target**: 2000 words. Sections are tightly allocated (267 + 500 + 500 + 467 + 266). The two 500-word sections (Syntax of Logic; Description & Sequence) must carry the grammatical content density — plan ~6–8 Ukrainian example sentences per section.
- **Immersion band**: A2.2 applied grammar = 55–75% Ukrainian. English used only for grammar metalanguage (explaining щоб distinction, explaining який agreement rule). All dialogues and example sentences must be 100% Ukrainian.
- **Max sentence length**: 15 words, max 2 clauses per sentence — enforce strictly in all Ukrainian examples.
- **No participles**: the plan mentions "Preparing for B1 syntax: conditional якби sentences" — frame this as forward-pointing motivation only; do NOT introduce якби as an active grammar structure.
- **Register drill for бо/тому що**: the State Standard introduces both; the checkpoint is the right place to consolidate the spoken/written register distinction explicitly.
- **приймати vs. пити vs. брати**: this is a high-value correction point. Include a clear correction box in the pharmacy/medicine section.
- **Калина cultural hook**: factually grounded — ascorbic acid content, antipyretic properties, national symbol significance. Safe to use as the anchor for the folk medicine section.
- **Будьмо toast**: use as the closing cultural note — connects grammar (imperative/hortative щоб-like structure) to lived social practice. Avoid claiming it is the "only" Ukrainian toast; frame as one meaningful example.
