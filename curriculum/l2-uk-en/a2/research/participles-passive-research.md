# Дослідження: Passive Participles

## State Standard Reference
§4.2.1.2: "Прикметник: прикметники чоловічого роду із закінченням -ий... прикметники у формі множини"
Alignment: The State Standard introduces participles formally at the B1 level (§4.2.3.1). However, for A2, this module strictly aligns with §4.2.1.2 (Adjective Declension) by scaffolding passive participles (-ний, -тий) as lexicalized adjectives. This fulfills practical communicative needs (e.g., understanding signs and states) without overwhelming the learner with B1 morphological generation.

## Cultural Hooks
1. **Shop Signs & Daily Life**: Printed signs like «Відчинено» / «Зачинено» (adverbs) and «Відкритий» / «Закритий» (adjectives/participles) are ubiquitous on Ukrainian storefronts, serving as the most common first exposure to these grammar forms in real life.
2. **Proverbs**: «Дарованому коню в зуби не заглядають» (Don't look a gift horse in the mouth) — a classic folk saying that perfectly demonstrates a passive participle ("дарований") declined in the dative case.

## Common Learner Errors
1. **Agreement Mismatch**: *Двері відкритий* → *Двері відкриті*. Learners often forget that participles must agree in gender, number, and case, instead treating them as invariable verb forms.
2. **The Active Participle Calque**: *Читаючий хлопець* → *Хлопець, який читає*. Learners often calque English (-ing) or Russian (-ущий/-ащий) active participles, which are strictly restricted or unnatural in standard Ukrainian.

## Notes for Content Writing
- **Decolonized Framing**: When explaining the restriction on active participles (-ючий/-ачий), frame it positively as a feature of Ukrainian syntax. Ukrainian strongly prefers dynamic relative clauses («який робить») over static adjectival forms, making the language more active and verb-driven. Do not use Russian as the baseline for this comparison.
- **Lexical Focus**: Prioritize recognition of daily-use adjectives over complex morphological derivation. The focus is on *using* them as adjectives (e.g., agreeing with nouns).
- **Immersion**: At A2 sequence 73 (Band 3), maintain 75-90% Ukrainian immersion. Explanations should be primarily in Ukrainian, with English restricted to brief clarifications for the most abstract concepts.
