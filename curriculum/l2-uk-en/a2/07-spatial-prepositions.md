## Warm-up

Prepositions are small but mighty words that connect nouns to the rest of the sentence. In English, prepositions like "in," "on," "to," and "from" are straightforward. But in Ukrainian, prepositions work hand-in-hand with grammatical cases â€” and some prepositions even change meaning depending on which case follows them!

This module is your comprehensive guide to Ukrainian prepositions. By the end, you'll understand how to combine prepositions with the correct cases to express location, motion, time, and relationships accurately.

**Why this matters:** Incorrect preposition-case combinations are one of the most common errors learners make. Mastering these patterns will immediately make your Ukrainian sound more natural and precise.

## Presentation

## The Preposition-Case Connection

In Ukrainian, every preposition requires a specific grammatical case â€” or sometimes two different cases depending on whether you're describing motion (going somewhere) or location (being somewhere). This is fundamentally different from English, where the same preposition works for both.

For example, the preposition **Ğ²/Ñƒ** can take either accusative or locative case:

- **Ğ’ ÑˆĞºĞ¾Ğ»Ñƒ** (accusative) = into the school (motion, going there)
- **Ğ’ ÑˆĞºĞ¾Ğ»Ñ–** (locative) = in the school (location, already there)

This motion-vs-location distinction applies to several Ukrainian prepositions and is crucial to understand.

## Motion vs. Location: The Core Distinction

Many prepositions have two uses. When you're describing WHERE something goes (motion), use accusative. When describing WHERE something is (location), use the locative or instrumental case.

| -------- | ---------- | ----------------- | ---------------- |
| Motion   | Accusative | ĞšÑƒĞ´Ğ¸? (Where to?) | Ğ™Ñ‚Ğ¸ **Ğ² ÑˆĞºĞ¾Ğ»Ñƒ**  |
| Location | Locative   | Ğ”Ğµ? (Where?)      | Ğ‘ÑƒÑ‚Ğ¸ **Ğ² ÑˆĞºĞ¾Ğ»Ñ–** |

This pattern applies to these prepositions: **Ğ²/Ñƒ**, **Ğ½Ğ°**, **Ğ¿Ñ–Ğ´**, **Ğ·Ğ°**.

## The Ğ²/Ñƒ Alternation

Ukrainian has two forms of "in/into": **Ğ²** and **Ñƒ**. They mean exactly the same thing, but you choose between them based on surrounding sounds to make speech flow smoothly.

**Use Ñƒ when:**

- The previous word ends in a consonant
- The next word starts with a consonant cluster

**Use Ğ² when:**

- The previous word ends in a vowel
- The next word starts with a vowel

Examples:

- Ğ’Ñ–Ğ½ **Ñƒ** ÑˆĞºĞ¾Ğ»Ñ–. (He is in school.) â€” "Ğ’Ñ–Ğ½" ends in consonant
- Ğ’Ğ¾Ğ½Ğ° **Ğ²** ÑƒĞ½Ñ–Ğ²ĞµÑ€ÑĞ¸Ñ‚ĞµÑ‚Ñ–. (She is at university.) â€” "Ğ’Ğ¾Ğ½Ğ°" ends in vowel

> ğŸŒ **Real World**
>
> Native speakers switch between Ğ² and Ñƒ naturally for euphony (pleasant sound). As a learner, don't stress too much about this â€” both are correct! The important thing is getting the case right.
