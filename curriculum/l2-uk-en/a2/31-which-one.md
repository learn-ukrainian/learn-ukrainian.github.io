## Warm-up

How do you describe things?
- The car **red**. (Adjective)
- The car **of my father**. (Genitive)
- The car **that I bought**. (Relative Clause)

## 2. The Case Detective

This is the tricky part, but it's also what makes Ukrainian relative clauses so precise and elegant.

Inside the relative clause, **ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹** acts like a noun or adjective. Its case depends on its **role within the clause**, not on the main sentence.

**Understanding the Logic:**

When you use a relative clause, you're essentially embedding a mini-sentence inside your main sentence. The word **ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹** plays a role in that mini-sentence (subject, object, etc.), and its case reflects that role.

**Scenario A: Subject (Nominative)**
- *Ğ¯ Ğ·Ğ½Ğ°Ñ Ñ…Ğ»Ğ¾Ğ¿Ñ†Ñ, **ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹** Ğ»ÑĞ±Ğ¸Ñ‚ÑŒ Ğ¼ĞµĞ½Ğµ.* (I know a boy **who** loves me).
- Analysis: In the relative clause "ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹ Ğ»ÑĞ±Ğ¸Ñ‚ÑŒ Ğ¼ĞµĞ½Ğµ," the word "ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹" is the subject (he loves). Therefore, it's in the Nominative case.

**Scenario B: Object (Accusative)**
- *Ğ¯ Ğ·Ğ½Ğ°Ñ Ñ…Ğ»Ğ¾Ğ¿Ñ†Ñ, **ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ³Ğ¾** Ğ»ÑĞ±Ğ»Ñ Ñ.* (I know a boy **whom** I love).
- Analysis: In the relative clause "ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ³Ğ¾ Ğ»ÑĞ±Ğ»Ñ Ñ," the word "ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ³Ğ¾" is the direct object (I love whom?). Therefore, it's in the Accusative case. Note that for animate masculine nouns, Accusative = Genitive form.

**Scenario C: Prepositional (Locative)**
- *Ğ¦Ğµ Ğ±ÑƒĞ´Ğ¸Ğ½Ğ¾Ğº, **Ñƒ ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ¼Ñƒ** Ñ Ğ¶Ğ¸Ğ²Ñƒ.* (This is the house **in which** I live).
- Analysis: The preposition "Ñƒ" (in) requires the Locative case when indicating location. Therefore, "ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ¼Ñƒ" is in the Locative case.

**Step-by-Step Strategy:**

To determine the correct case of **ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹**, follow these steps:

1. **Isolate the relative clause**: Look ONLY at the part after the comma.
2. **Identify the role**: What role does "ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹" play in that clause? (subject, object, with a preposition, etc.)
3. **Apply case rules**: Use the same case rules you already know for nouns.
4. **Check agreement**: Make sure "ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹" agrees in gender and number with the antecedent (the word before the comma).

**Example Walkthrough:**

Sentence: *Ğ¦Ğµ Ğ±ÑƒĞ´Ğ¸Ğ½Ğ¾Ğº, Ñƒ ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ¼Ñƒ Ñ Ğ¶Ğ¸Ğ²Ñƒ.*

1. Isolate: "Ñƒ ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ¼Ñƒ Ñ Ğ¶Ğ¸Ğ²Ñƒ" (in which I live)
2. Role: "ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ¼Ñƒ" follows the preposition "Ñƒ" indicating location
3. Case: "Ñƒ" + location = Locative case
4. Agreement: "Ğ±ÑƒĞ´Ğ¸Ğ½Ğ¾Ğº" is masculine, so "ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹" â†’ "ÑĞºĞ¾Ğ¼Ñƒ" (masculine Locative)

## Deep Dive: "Who" vs "Which"

In English:
- People = Who (*The man **who** came*)
- Things = Which/That (*The car **which** stopped*)

## Cultural Insight

> [!cultural] The Art of Description
> Ukrainians love detailed descriptions. Unlike English, where you might say "The tall guy", a Ukrainian might say "Ğ¢Ğ¾Ğ¹ Ñ…Ğ»Ğ¾Ğ¿ĞµÑ†ÑŒ, ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹ Ğ´ÑƒĞ¶Ğµ Ğ²Ğ¸ÑĞ¾ĞºĞ¸Ğ¹" (That guy, who is very tall).
> Using relative clauses makes you sound more precise and deliberate. It is considered elegant to unfold your thought using **ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹**, rather than piling up adjectives before a noun.
>
> *English style:* "Accurate, detailed, beautiful map."
> *Ukrainian style:* "Map, which is accurate, which is detailed, and which is beautiful."

# Summary

1.  **Agreement:** *Ğ¯ĞºĞ¸Ğ¹* matches the gender/number of the word it describes (Antecedent).
2.  **Case:** *Ğ¯ĞºĞ¸Ğ¹* changes case based on its function inside the relative clause.
3.  **Punctuation:** Always use a comma before *ÑĞºĞ¸Ğ¹*.
4.  **Usage:** Used for both people and things.
5.  **Complexity:** Look at the clause as a mini-sentence to find the right case.
