## Три па́ри дієслі́в ру́ху (Three Pairs of Motion Verbs)

<div class="dialogue">


**Олег:** Приві́т, Аню! Ти куди́ за́раз іде́ш? *(Hi, Anya! Where are you walking right now?)*


**Аня:** Я іду́ до шко́ли. А ти? *(I am walking to school. And you?)*


**Олег:** Я теж! Але́ зазвича́й я не ходжу́ до школи пі́шки. *(Me too! But usually I don't walk to school on foot.)*


**Аня:** А як ти ї́здиш? *(And how do you ride?)*


**Олег:** Я ї́жджу на авто́бусі. Але сього́дні авто́бус не приї́хав! *(I take the bus. But today the bus didn't arrive!)*


</div>

Украї́нські міста́ ство́рені для пішохо́дів і ма́ють чудо́вий громадський транспорт. Тому́ украї́нці за́вжди́ ду́же то́чно опи́сують, як са́ме вони́ пересу́ваються. *(Ukrainian cities are built for pedestrians and have excellent public transport. Therefore, Ukrainians always describe very precisely exactly how they move around.)*

The English language uses the verb "to go" for almost everything. You go to work, you go by car, you go to another country. It is a highly versatile, one-size-fits-all word. Ukrainian grammar is much more demanding, but also much more precise. Before you form a sentence about motion, you must analyze your movement and ask two crucial questions. 

First, what is your mode of transport? Are you using your own two feet, are you riding inside a vehicle like a bus or a train, or are you flying in the air?

Second, what is the nature of this journey? Is it an **однонапрямний** (unidirectional) movement happening right now, moving steadily toward a specific goal? Or is it a **різнонапрямний** (multidirectional) movement that you do regularly, repeatedly, or back and forth?

These two questions give us three main pairs of motion verbs. It is vital to remember that this choice has absolutely nothing to do with speed or distance. You can "walk" for twenty kilometers across the mountains, and you can "ride" for two meters to park a car. The choice of verb is entirely about the vehicle and the frequency of the **рух** (movement, motion).

### Пішки (On Foot)

The first pair is **іти** and **ходити**. Both mean "to walk" or "to go on foot".

When you use **іти**, you are making a unidirectional trip. You are currently on your way, moving from point A to point B. It is happening now, and you have a clear destination in mind.
*   **Я іду до магази́ну.** (I am walking to the store right now.)
*   **Він іде́ на робо́ту.** (He is walking to work right now.)
*   **Ми ідемо́ в парк.** (We are walking to the park right now.)
*   **Куди ти ідеш?** (Where are you walking to right now?)

The second verb in the pair is **ходити**. This verb is multidirectional. You use it to describe habits, regular round-trips where you go and come back, or simply walking aimlessly without a specific endpoint. It is also used to describe the physical ability to walk.
*   **Я ходжу до магазину щодня́.** (I go to the store every day.)
*   **Він хо́дить на роботу пішки.** (He walks to work regularly.)
*   **Ми лю́бимо ходити по мі́сту.** (We love to walk around the city.)
*   **Моя мале́нька дити́на вже ходить!** (My little child already walks!)

### Транспортом (By Vehicle)

The second pair is **їхати** and **їздити**. You must use these verbs whenever you are not on your own two feet. If you are in a car, bus, train, tram, or on a bicycle, you are "riding". 

The unidirectional verb is **їхати**. Use this when you are currently in transit, moving in one direction toward your goal.
*   **Ми ї́демо до Льво́ва.** (We are driving/riding to Lviv right now.)
*   **Вона ї́де в автобусі.** (She is riding in the bus right now.)
*   **Таксі їде дуже пові́льно.** (The taxi is driving very slowly right now.)
*   **Чому ти ї́деш так шви́дко?** (Why are you driving so fast right now?)

The multidirectional verb is **їздити**. Use this for your regular commute, your annual vacations, or your general ability to drive.
*   **Ми ї́здимо до Львова щолі́та.** (We go to Lviv every summer.)
*   **Вони ча́сто ї́здять у го́ри.** (They often drive to the mountains.)
*   **Я не люблю́ їздити в метро́.** (I don't like riding in the subway.)
*   **Ти вмі́єш їздити на велосипе́ді?** (Do you know how to ride a bicycle?)

### Пові́трям (By Air)

The third pair is **летіти** and **літати**. Use these for airplanes, helicopters, birds, and insects.

The unidirectional verb is **летіти**. Use it when a flight is currently in progress toward a destination.
*   **Літак лети́ть до Ки́єва.** (The plane is flying to Kyiv right now.)
*   **Птах летить ви́со́ко.** (The bird is flying high right now.)
*   **Ми летимо́ у відпу́стку.** (We are flying on vacation right now.)
*   **Куди летить цей вертолі́т?** (Where is this helicopter flying right now?)

The multidirectional verb is **літати**. Use it for regular flights, return trips, or general facts about flying.
*   **Він часто літа́є до Києва.** (He often flies to Kyiv.)
*   **Ці птахи́ літа́ють швидко.** (These birds fly fast.)
*   **Пілоти бага́то літають.** (Pilots fly a lot.)
*   **Моя ма́ма бої́ться літати.** (My mom is afraid to fly.)

## Дієвідмі́на та доко́наний вид (Conjugation and Perfective Forms)

<div class="dialogue">


**Максим:** Куди ти так поспіша́єш? *(Where are you hurrying to so much?)*


**Олена:** Я лечу́ в аеропо́рт! Мій брат полеті́в у Пари́ж! *(I am flying to the airport! My brother took off for Paris!)*


**Максим:** А чому ти не їдеш на таксі? *(And why aren't you taking a taxi?)*


**Олена:** Тому що таксі їде повільно. Я іду на метро! *(Because a taxi drives slowly. I am walking to the subway!)*


</div>

Ко́жне дієсло́во ма́є свою́ фо́рму для рі́зних осі́б. Це дуже важли́во запам'ята́ти і практикува́ти. *(Every verb has its form for different persons. This is very important to remember and practice.)*

Now that you know the difference between the two types of motion verbs, let's learn how to conjugate them in the present tense. These verbs have some specific spelling rules and alternations that you need to memorize. 

### Дієвідміна: іти / ходити (Conjugating "On Foot")

The unidirectional verb **іти** is very straightforward and regular. The multidirectional verb **ходити** features a consonant change in the "я" form where the letter «д» becomes «дж».
*   я **іду** / **ходжу**
*   ти **ідеш** / **ходиш**
*   він, вона, воно́ **іде** / **ходить**
*   ми **ідемо** / **ходимо**
*   ви **ідете** / **ходите**
*   вони **ідуть** / **ходять**

Examples in context to see the difference:
*   **Ти ідеш додо́му?** (Are you walking home right now?)
*   **Ви часто ходите в теа́тр?** (Do you often go to the theater?)
*   **Він завжди ходить дуже швидко.** (He always walks very fast.)

### Дієвідміна: їхати / їздити (Conjugating "By Vehicle")

Pay attention to the spelling here. The letter «ї» stays throughout the conjugation for both verbs. The verb **їздити** has a tricky consonant cluster for the "я" form where «зд» becomes the complex sound «ждж».
*   я **їду** / **їжджу**
*   ти **їдеш** / **їздиш**
*   він, вона, воно **їде** / **їздить**
*   ми **їдемо** / **їздимо**
*   ви **їдете** / **їздите**
*   вони **їдуть** / **їздять**

Examples in context:
*   **Куди ви їдете?** (Where are you riding right now?)
*   **Ми їздимо на да́чу на вихідни́х.** (We drive to the country house on weekends.)
*   **Я ніколи не їжджу на мотоци́клі.** (I never ride a motorcycle.)

### Дієвідміна: летіти / літати (Conjugating "By Air")

The verb **летіти** changes its consonant specifically for the "я" form from «т» to «ч». The verb **літати** follows a very regular, simple pattern without any complex changes.
*   я **лечу** / **літаю**
*   ти **летиш** / **літаєш**
*   він, вона, воно **летить** / **літає**
*   ми **летимо** / **літаємо**
*   ви **летите** / **літаєте**
*   вони **летять** / **літають**

Examples in context:
*   **Я лечу до Парижа за́втра.** (I am flying to Paris tomorrow.)
*   **Ти літаєш літако́м чи їдеш по́їздом?** (Do you fly by plane or ride by train?)
*   **Птахи летять на пі́вдень.** (The birds are flying south right now.)

### Доконаний вид з префіксом "по-" (The Perfective Prefix "по-")

In Ukrainian, adding a prefix to a verb often changes its core meaning or makes it perfective, which indicates a completed action. When you add the prefix **по-** to the unidirectional verbs, you create a new perfective verb that means "to set off", "to depart", or "to start a journey".

:::tip
The prefix **по-** is the most common way to turn a motion verb into a completed action. It emphasizes the START of the journey.
:::

This is how we create the perfective partners:
*   **іти** → **піти** (to leave on foot, to set off)
*   **їхати** → **поїхати** (to leave by vehicle, to drive off)
*   **летіти** → **полетіти** (to take off, to fly away)

Notice that for pronunciation reasons, adding "по-" to "іти" merges the vowels to create **піти**. These perfective verbs are extremely common in the past tense to show that the journey has successfully begun or the person has departed.
*   **Він пішо́в додому.** (He left for home on foot.)
*   **Вона пої́хала на роботу.** (She left for work by vehicle.)
*   **Літак полетів.** (The plane took off.)
*   **Ми пої́хали у відпустку.** (We set off on vacation.)

These prefixed forms are used in daily conversation constantly. If someone asks where your colleague is, and they have already left the building, you simply say: «Він пішов» (He left).

## Моде́лі дієвідмі́нювання: каза́ти, пи́ти, боро́тися (Conjugation Models)

<div class="dialogue">


**Оленка:** Що ти ро́биш? *(What are you doing?)*


**Тарас:** Я п'ю ка́ву і борю́ся зі сном! *(I am drinking coffee and struggling with sleep!)*


**Оленка:** Я тобі́ кажу́, тре́ба бі́льше спа́ти! *(I am telling you, you need to sleep more!)*


</div>

Украї́нська грама́тика має чіткі́ правила і ло́гіку. Якщо́ ви зна́єте одну́ моде́ль, ви мо́жете зміни́ти багато і́нших дієслів. *(Ukrainian grammar has clear rules and logic. If you know one model, you can conjugate many other verbs.)*

Ukrainian conjugation is built on predictable patterns. While some verbs seem completely irregular at first glance, they actually belong to specific models. If you learn one model, you unlock dozens of other verbs that behave exactly the same way. Let's examine three important conjugation models that you will use every day.

### Модель 1: Змі́на при́голосного (Stem Consonant Change)

Many Ukrainian verbs feature **чергування** (alternation) of consonants in the present tense. The verb **казати** (to say / to tell) is a perfect example of this rule. The consonant **з** in the infinitive changes to **ж** in the present tense stem.

*   я **кажу** (I say)
*   ти **кажеш** (you say)
*   він, вона, воно **каже** (he/she/it says)
*   ми **кажемо** (we say)
*   ви **кажете** (you say)
*   вони **кажуть** (they say)

Notice that this **чергування** affects the ENTIRE present tense paradigm. It is not just for the "я" form like we saw earlier with some motion verbs. Once you know this rule, you can confidently conjugate other verbs with similar patterns. For example, the verb **писати** (to write) changes the consonant **с** to **ш**: я пишу́, ти пи́шеш, він пи́ше. The perfective verb **сказати** (to tell) changes exactly like **казати**: я скажу́, ти ска́жеш. This predictability makes Ukrainian grammar much easier once you see the hidden architecture.
*   **Що ти кажеш?** (What are you saying?)
*   **Він завжди каже пра́вду.** (He always tells the truth.)
*   **Я кажу, що це дуже ціка́во.** (I say that this is very interesting.)

### Модель 2: Коро́тка фо́рма з апо́стро́фом (Irregular Contraction)

The verb **пити** (to drink) looks very short, and its conjugation might seem strange. The stem reduces drastically and takes contracted endings with an apostrophe to separate the hard consonant from the soft vowel. 

*   я **п'ю** (I drink)
*   ти **п'єш** (you drink)
*   він, вона, воно **п'є** (he/she/it drinks)
*   ми **п'ємо** (we drink)
*   ви **п'єте** (you drink)
*   вони **п'ють** (they drink)

This model applies to several other common one-syllable verbs ending in "-ити". For example, the verb **бити** (to hit / to beat) becomes я б'ю, ти б'єш, він б'є. The verb **лити** (to pour) becomes я ллю, ти ллєш.
*   **Я п'ю гаря́чий чай.** (I am drinking hot tea.)
*   **Чому ти не п'єш воду́?** (Why aren't you drinking water?)
*   **Ми п'ємо каву щора́нку.** (We drink coffee every morning.)

### Модель 3: Зворо́тні дієслова́ на "-отися" (The Reflexive "-отися")

The verb **боротися** (to fight / to struggle) is a reflexive verb, meaning it ends in **-ся**. When you conjugate reflexive verbs, the endings change normally, and the **-ся** particle (or **-сь** after a vowel) stays attached at the very end. However, this specific verb also has a unique stem change from **от** to **ор**.

*   я **борюся** (I struggle)
*   ти **борешся** (you struggle)
*   він, вона, воно **бореться** (he/she/it struggles)
*   ми **боремося** (we struggle)
*   ви **боретеся** (you struggle)
*   вони **борються** (they struggle)

Pay special attention to the soft sign in **бореться** and **борються**. This model shows you how reflexive endings wrap around the conjugated verb perfectly. You will see this pattern in other reflexive verbs too.
*   **Я борюся зі стра́хо́м.** (I am fighting with fear.)
*   **Вони борються за свобо́ду.** (They fight for freedom.)
*   **Ми боремося з пробле́мами щодня.** (We are struggling with problems every day.)

## Рух + прийме́нники + відмі́нки (Motion + Prepositions + Cases)

<div class="dialogue">


**Майя:** Зві́дки ти йдеш? *(Where are you walking from?)*


**Денис:** Я йду зі школи. А куди ти їдеш? *(I am walking from school. And where are you riding to?)*


**Майя:** Я їду на вокза́л. *(I am riding to the train station.)*


</div>

Ко́ли ми гово́римо про рух, ми завжди ма́ємо на́прямок. Для цього́ нам потрі́бні правильні прийменники. *(When we talk about motion, we always have a direction. For this, we need the correct prepositions.)*

When you talk about a **напрямок** (direction), you need to use the correct prepositions paired with specific grammatical cases. Motion verbs typically pair with prepositions that indicate where you are going TO, where you are coming FROM, or what you are moving THROUGH. Let's look at the most common combinations.

### Напрямок до мети́ (Direction TO)

To answer the question **Куди?** (Where to?), you will use one of three prepositions depending on the destination.

1. **до** + Genitive case
Use this for people, places, and most buildings. This is the most common way to say "to".
*   **Я іду до лі́каря.** (I am walking to the doctor.)
*   **Ми їдемо до Києва.** (We are riding to Kyiv.)
*   **Він іде до дру́га.** (He is walking to a friend.)

2. **на** + Accusative case
Use this for events, open spaces, and some specific locations like the post office or work.
*   **Я їду на роботу.** (I am riding to work.)
*   **Вони ідуть на конце́рт.** (They are walking to the concert.)
*   **Вона летить на о́стрів.** (She is flying to the island.)

3. **в / у** + Accusative case
Use this for countries, cities, and enclosed spaces when you are going physically inside them.
*   **Ми летимо в Украї́ну.** (We are flying to Ukraine.)
*   **Я іду у вели́кий магази́н.** (I am walking into a large store.)
*   **Він їде в центр міста.** (He is driving to the city center.)

### Напрямок від мети (Direction FROM)

To answer the question **Звідки?** (From where?), you must use the Genitive case with specific prepositions.

1. **з / зі** + Genitive case
This is the direct opposite of "в" and "на". If you went "на роботу", you return "з робо́ти".
*   **Я йду з роботи.** (I am walking from work.)
*   **Він їде зі школи.** (He is riding from school.)
*   **Літак летить з Аме́рики.** (The plane is flying from America.)

2. **від** + Genitive case
This is the direct opposite of "до". It is used mostly when returning from people.
*   **Я йду від друга.** (I am walking from a friend.)
*   **Ми їдемо від лікаря.** (We are driving from the doctor.)
*   **Вона йде від батьків.** (She is walking from her parents.)

### Рух че́рез про́стір (Through or Along)

If your motion involves passing through a place or moving along a surface, use these patterns:

1. **через** + Accusative case
Use this when you cross something or go through an obstacle.
*   **Ми йдемо́ через парк.** (We are walking through the park.)
*   **Вони їдуть через міст.** (They are driving across the bridge.)
*   **Він летить через океа́н.** (He is flying across the ocean.)

2. **по** + Locative case
Use this when you are moving along the surface of something.
*   **Я йду по ву́лиці.** (I am walking along the street.)
*   **Машина їде по доро́зі.** (The car is driving on the road.)
*   **Ми гуля́ємо по місту.** (We are walking around the city.)

Using these combinations correctly will make your sentences sound incredibly natural and precise. Just remember to always match your preposition to your intention!

## Підсумок

Українська мо́ва вимага́є ува́ги до дета́лей, але ці дета́лі ро́блять її дуже логі́чною. *(The Ukrainian language requires attention to detail, but these details make it very logical.)*

You have now learned the foundational system of Ukrainian motion verbs. Whenever you talk about movement, remember to ask yourself: am I on foot, in a vehicle, or in the air? And is this trip an **однонапрямний** action happening right now, or a **різнонапрямний** habit?

By mastering the pairs **іти** and **ходити**, **їхати** and **їздити**, and **летіти** and **літати**, you can describe almost any journey accurately. Adding the perfective prefix **по-** allows you to describe specific departures. Furthermore, understanding conjugation models like **казати**, **пити**, and **боротися** unlocks your ability to use dozens of other verbs correctly without simply memorizing them blindly. Finally, pairing these verbs with the correct prepositions of direction ensures that your Ukrainian sentences sound natural and authoritative.
