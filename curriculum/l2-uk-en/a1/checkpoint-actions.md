## Що ми зна́ємо? (What Do We Know?)

The ability to talk about actions—what you do, what you want to do, and what you must do—is the engine of any language. Over the past six modules, you transitioned from expressing simple preferences to detailing complex daily routines. You learned how to formulate detailed questions and express your desires and obligations. This checkpoint consolidates your knowledge before introducing new variables.

Start by testing your recall of the recent modules. Can you read and understand these statements without checking the English translations?

*   Can you say what you like? (Module 15)
    *   **Мені подо́бається ка́ва.** (I like coffee.)
    *   **Я люблю́ чита́ти.** (I love to read.)
*   Can you conjugate Group I verbs? (Module 16)
    *   **Я зна́ю.** (I know.)
    *   **Ти зна́єш.** (You know.)
*   Can you conjugate Group II verbs? (Module 17)
    *   **Я говорю́.** (I speak.)
    *   **Ти гово́риш.** (You speak.)
*   Can you say what you want, can, and must do? (Module 18)
    *   **Я хо́чу ї́сти.** (I want to eat.)
    *   **Я му́шу працюва́ти.** (I must work.)
*   Can you ask questions? (Module 19)
    *   **Хто це?** (Who is this?)
    *   **Що ти ро́биш?** (What are you doing?)
*   Can you describe your morning? (Module 20)
    *   **Я прокида́юся.** (I wake up.)
    *   **Я вмива́юся.** (I wash myself.)

If you process these Ukrainian examples smoothly, you have built a solid foundation. You started by identifying basic objects and describing their traits. Now, you can describe a full sequence of events. You can ask for information, express your boundaries, and state your needs. This is the transition from passive vocabulary recognition to active sentence construction. 

Verbs give your sentences movement and purpose. Categorizing verbs builds essential mental muscle memory, so we begin by sorting the primary action words you have encountered.

## Чита́ння (Reading Practice)

Reading connected text tests your ability to process vocabulary and grammar simultaneously. When you read a paragraph, you track a narrative rather than decoding isolated words. You must recognize verb endings in real time to understand who is performing the action. 

Read the following text aloud. It uses only the vocabulary and grammar patterns you have learned in the A1.3 phase. Focus on the rhythm of the sentences and visualize the routine being described.

> **Я прокидаюся о сьо́мій.** (I wake up at seven.)
> **Потім вмиваюся і сні́даю.** (Then I wash myself and eat breakfast.)
> **Я п'ю ка́ву.** (I drink coffee.)
> **Мені подобається ра́нок.** (I like the morning.)
> **Я працю́ю в о́фісі.** (I work in the office.)
> **Я бага́то працюю.** (I work a lot.)
> **Увечері я поверта́юся.** (In the evening I return.)
> **Я хочу відпочива́ти.** (I want to rest.)
> **Я люблю читати кни́гу.** (I love to read a book.)
> **Увечері я дивлю́ся фільм.** (In the evening I watch a movie.)

Observe how sequence words organize the text. The word **потім** (then) moves the story forward sequentially from one action to the next. The word **увечері** (in the evening) establishes a clear time frame for the final actions. These structural words prevent your sentences from sounding like a disconnected list. 

Additionally, observe the reflexive verbs like **прокидаюся** (I wake up) and **вмиваюся** (I wash myself). The **-ся** ending confirms the action is directed back at the speaker. 

When reading, pay attention to the subject of each sentence. Because the verb endings change depending on the person, Ukrainian speakers often drop the pronoun **я** (I) in casual speech, as seen in the second sentence. However, in these foundational texts, we keep the pronouns visible to help you anchor the verb conjugations. Notice that every single verb describing the routine ends in **-ю** or **-я**, signaling the first-person perspective.

## Грама́тика (Grammar Summary)

Grammar provides the framework that holds your vocabulary together. The core mechanisms of Ukrainian verbs dictate how actions relate to the speaker. In the A1.3 phase, we covered the essential conjugation patterns and functional words. The essential patterns of Ukrainian verbs operate on strict rules.

**1. The Infinitive**
The dictionary form of a verb is the infinitive. You cannot use this form directly with a subject like **я** or **ти**. It must be conjugated. Almost all infinitives end in **-ти**.
*   **читати** (to read)
*   **говорити** (to speak)
*   **хотіти** (to want)

**2. Group I Verbs**
Group I verbs typically have an **-ати** or **-яти** ending in the infinitive. Their endings feature the vowels **-є-** or **-е-**.
*   **я** + **-ю** (я чита́ю)
*   **ти** + **-єш** (ти чита́єш)
*   **він/вона́/воно** + **-є** (він чита́є)
*   **ми** + **-ємо** (ми чита́ємо)
*   **ви** + **-єте** (ви чита́єте)
*   **вони** + **-ють** (вони чита́ють)

**3. Group II Verbs**
Group II verbs typically have an **-ити** or **-іти** ending in the infinitive. Their endings feature the vowels **-и-** or **-і-**.
*   **я** + **-ю** / **-у** (я говорю)
*   **ти** + **-иш** (ти говориш)
*   **він/вона/воно** + **-ить** (вона гово́рить)
*   **ми** + **-имо** (ми гово́римо)
*   **ви** + **-ите** (ви гово́рите)
*   **вони** + **-ять** / **-ать** (вони гово́рять)

**4. Modal Verbs**
Modal verbs express desire, ability, or obligation. They act as the primary conjugated verb in the sentence and are always followed immediately by an infinitive.
*   **Я хочу гуляти.** (I want to walk.)
*   **Я мо́жу працювати.** (I can work.)
*   **Я мушу йти.** (I must go.)

**5. Question Words**
Asking questions is crucial for active communication. These seven words allow you to extract specific information from a conversation partner.
*   **Хто** (Who)
*   **Що** (What)
*   **Де** (Where - location)
*   **Куди** (Where to - direction)
*   **Коли** (When)
*   **Чому** (Why)
*   **Як** (How)

**6. Negation**
To make a sentence negative, place the particle **не** directly before the verb. Ukrainian also uses double negation, which means words like **ніхто** (nobody) still require **не** before the verb.
*   **Я не знаю.** (I do not know.)
*   **Ніхто не зна́є.** (Nobody knows.)

**7. Reflexive Verbs**
Reflexive verbs show that the subject is performing the action on themselves. They add the particle **-ся** to the end of the conjugated verb.
*   **Я прокидаюся.** (I wake up.)
*   **Він вмива́ється.** (He washes himself.)

## Діало́г (Connected Dialogue)

Real conversations require you to ask questions, respond with statements, use modals to suggest plans, and negate verbs when necessary. A natural exchange rarely relies on a single grammar point. You must switch between different verb groups and perspectives seamlessly. 

Read the following dialogue. It represents a typical interaction where two friends meet and try to coordinate their schedules. Observe the mechanics driving this exchange.

<div class="dialogue">


**Максим:** Приві́т! Що ти робиш? *(Hi! What are you doing?)*


**Олена:** Привіт! Я читаю книгу. А ти? *(Hi! I am reading a book. And you?)*


**Максим:** Я хочу гуляти. Можеш піти зі мно́ю? *(I want to walk. Can you go with me?)*


**Олена:** Не можу, мушу працювати. *(I can't, I must work.)*


**Максим:** Коли ти повертаєшся? *(When do you return?)*


**Олена:** О шо́стій. *(At six.)*


**Максим:** До́бре, тоді́ гуля́ємо вве́чері! *(Good, then we walk in the evening!)*


</div>

First, Максим initiates the conversation with a clear question using the word **що** (what). He applies the **ти** (you) form of the Group II verb **робити** (to do). Олена answers using the **я** (I) form of the Group I verb **читати** (to read). 

The conversation then shifts to making plans. Максим uses the modal verb **хотіти** (to want) followed by an infinitive to state his desire. He immediately asks about her ability using another modal verb, **могти** (can, to be able to). 

Олена declines the invitation. She applies the negative particle **не** (not) directly before the modal verb. She justifies her refusal using a third modal verb, **мусити** (must), indicating an obligation. 

Максим extracts a specific detail about her schedule using the question word **коли** (when) and a reflexive verb. Once they establish a time, they confirm their new plans. 

This short exchange utilizes both verb conjugation groups, three distinct modal verbs, interrogative words, negation, and reflexive mechanics. Reconstruct this exchange by supplying the missing functional words.

## Підсумок — Summary

The A1.3 phase marks a critical shift in your Ukrainian learning. You have learned the precise mechanics required to operate verbs in real-world contexts. You can now talk about actions with accuracy. You understand that verbs function as the core of the sentence, and you know how to alter their endings to match the subject. By mastering the two primary conjugation groups, you have unlocked the ability to use hundreds of regular verbs.

Beyond simple actions, you can express your internal state. Using modal verbs allows you to negotiate plans, ask for permission, and define your obligations. You have also acquired the tools for inquiry. The seven essential question words allow you to actively participate in conversations, ask for critical information, and clarify details. 

Furthermore, you understand how to use negation to decline invitations or express inability, adhering to the Ukrainian rule of double negation. Finally, you can narrate a daily routine using reflexive verbs and sequence words. These are highly practical skills that form the basis of daily communication.
