## –Ф—Ц–∞–ї–ЊћБ–≥–Є вАФ Dialogues

Every morning has a distinct rhythm. We use specific verbs to describe daily routines, and in Ukrainian, many of these share a special feature because they describe actions performed on ourselves. Consider a typical morning scenario. Two roommates, **–Ы—ЦћБ–љ–∞** (Lina) and **–Э–∞ћБ—Б—В—П** (Nastia), are in their shared kitchen on a Tuesday morning. The time is early, and the sun is just coming up. Lina is an early bird; she is already sitting at the table and drinking her morning coffee. Nastia, however, is just starting her daily routine and walks into the kitchen looking a bit sleepy. Lina asks Nastia about her timing, wanting to know exactly when her day begins. Nastia explains her sequence clearly.

> **–Ы—Ц–љ–∞:** –Ъ–ЊћБ–ї–Є —В–Є –њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞ћБ—Ф—И—Б—П? *(When do you wake up?)*
> **–Э–∞—Б—В—П:** –ѓ –њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞ћБ—О—Б—П –Њ —Б—М–ЊћБ–Љ—Ц–є. *(I wake up at seven.)*
> **–Ы—Ц–љ–∞:** –©–Њ —В–Є —А–ЊћБ–±–Є—И –њ–ЊћБ—В—Ц–Љ? *(What do you do then?)*
> **–Э–∞—Б—В—П:** –Т–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—О—Б—П, –Њ–і—П–≥–∞ћБ—О—Б—П —Ц —Б–љ—ЦћБ–і–∞—О. *(I wash up, get dressed, and have breakfast.)*
> **–Ы—Ц–љ–∞:** –Р –Ї–Њ–ї–Є —В–Є –є–і–µ—И –љ–∞ —А–Њ–±–ЊћБ—В—Г? *(And when do you go to work?)*
> **–Э–∞—Б—В—П:** –Ю –≤–ЊћБ—Б—М–Љ—Ц–є. *(At eight.)*

Notice the specific verbs Nastia uses to describe her routine. She says **–њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—О—Б—П** (I wake up), **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞—О—Б—П** (I wash up), and **–Њ–і—П–≥–∞—О—Б—П** (I get dressed). These verbs all end in the same distinct suffix. However, when she mentions eating, she simply says **—Б–љ—Ц–і–∞—О** (I have breakfast) without that special ending. The first three verbs reflect actions Nastia is doing to her own body, while having breakfast is an external action.

Their conversation continues as they compare this busy weekday routine to their typical weekend habits. The pace of a weekend morning is entirely different.

> **–Э–∞—Б—В—П:** –£ —Б—Г–±–ЊћБ—В—Г —В–Є —В–µ–ґ –њ–Њ—Б–њ—Ц—И–∞ћБ—Ф—И? *(Do you also hurry on Saturday?)*
> **–Ы—Ц–љ–∞:** –£ —Б—Г–±–Њ—В—Г —П –љ–µ –њ–Њ—Б–њ—Ц—И–∞ћБ—О. –Я—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—О—Б—П –њ—ЦћБ–Ј–љ–Њ, –ї–µ–ґ—ГћБ, —З–Є—В–∞ћБ—О –љ–Њ–≤–ЄћБ–љ–Є. *(On Saturday I do not hurry. I wake up late, lie down, read the news.)*
> **–Э–∞—Б—В—П:** –Р —П –љ–∞–≤—З–∞ћБ—О—Б—П –≤—А–∞ћБ–љ—Ж—Ц. –Я–Њ—В—Ц–Љ –≥—Г–ї—ПћБ—О. *(And I study in the morning. Then I walk.)*

In this weekend contrast, Lina uses the verb **–њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—О—Б—П** (I wake up) again, but follows it with regular, non-reflexive verbs like **–ї–µ–ґ—Г** (I lie down) and **—З–Є—В–∞—О** (I read). Nastia mentions another verb with that special ending: **–љ–∞–≤—З–∞—О—Б—П** (I study). Mixing these different verb types is how you naturally describe your day in Ukrainian.

## –Ф—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–∞ћБ –љ–∞ -—Б—П вАФ Reflexive Verbs

The special verbs you saw in the dialogue are called reflexive verbs. As explained in the textbook *–Ъ–∞—А–∞–Љ–∞ћБ–љ Grade 10 (p. 176)*, the core rule is straightforward: ¬Ђ–Ф—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–∞ —Ц–Ј —Б—ГћБ—Д—Ц–Ї—Б–Њ–Љ -—Б—П(-—Б—М) –Њ–Ј–љ–∞—З–∞ћБ—О—В—М –і—ЦћБ—О, —Б–њ—А—П–Љ–ЊћБ–≤–∞–љ—Г –љ–∞ —Б–µ–±–µћБ¬ї. This means verbs with the suffix **-—Б—П** (or **-—Б—М**) indicate an action directed at oneself. Think of this suffix as the English equivalent of the words "oneself" or "myself." If you take a standard verb and attach this suffix, you instantly change the target of the action from an external object back to the speaker.

*   **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—В–Є** (to wash someone) вЖТ **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—В–Є—Б—П** (to wash oneself)
*   **–Њ–і—П–≥–∞ћБ—В–Є** (to dress someone) вЖТ **–Њ–і—П–≥–∞ћБ—В–Є—Б—П** (to dress oneself)

Conjugating these verbs is simple because the core mechanics do not change. You take the standard verb endings you already know from Group I and simply attach **-—Б—П** to the very end. The reflexive suffix acts like a fixed addition hooked to the end of the conjugated form. The conjugation paradigm for the present tense demonstrates this consistent pattern.

| –Ч–∞–є–Љ–µћБ–љ–љ–Є–Ї | –Ф—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–ЊћБ–≤–Њ | –Я–µ—А–µ–Ї–ї–∞–і |
| --- | --- | --- |
| –ѓ | **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—О-—Б—П** | I wash myself |
| –Ґ–Є | **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—Ф—И-—Б—П** | You wash yourself |
| –Т—Ц–љ / –Т–Њ–љ–∞ћБ | **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—Ф-—В—М—Б—П** | He / She washes himself/herself |

Notice that the core endings (**-—О**, **-—Ф—И**, **-—Ф**) remain perfectly regular. You just add the **-—Б—П** (or **-—В—М—Б—П** for "–≤—Ц–љ / –≤–Њ–љ–∞") suffix to the end.

While the spelling is perfectly regular, the pronunciation holds a secret. According to the textbook *–Ъ—А–∞–≤—Ж–ЊћБ–≤–∞ Grade 4 (p. 113)*, there is a crucial pronunciation note you must memorize to sound natural. The letters combine to create completely new sounds, hiding the original consonants.

:::caution
*   The combination **-—И—Б—П** sounds like a long, soft **[—Б':–∞]**: **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—Ф—И—Б—П** is pronounced **[–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞—Ф—Б':–∞]**.
*   The combination **-—В—М—Б—П** sounds like a long, soft **[—Ж':–∞]**: **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞ћБ—Ф—В—М—Б—П** is pronounced **[–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞—Ф—Ж':–∞]**.
:::

The spelling and pronunciation differ significantly here. You must write the distinct letters precisely, but you must pronounce the merged, soft sounds. This rule is fundamental to fluent Ukrainian speech, and native children practice it extensively in primary school.

It is important to know exactly when to use these reflexive forms. A reflexive verb is always intransitive, meaning it cannot take a direct object. You cannot use a reflexive verb to act upon something else. If you are getting a child ready for school, you use the non-reflexive form **–Њ–і—П–≥–∞—В–Є –і–Є—В–ЄћБ–љ—Г** (to dress a child). But if you are getting dressed yourself, you must use the reflexive form **–Њ–і—П–≥–∞—В–Є—Б—П** (to get dressed). Understanding this distinction between acting on others and acting on yourself is the key to mastering Ukrainian morning routines.

## –Ь—Ц–є —А–∞ћБ–љ–Њ–Ї вАФ My Morning

With an understanding of how reflexive verbs work, we can build a complete morning timeline. The vocabulary for your morning routine relies heavily on these reflexive verbs, but it also includes some standard verbs for contrast.

*   **–њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—В–Є—Б—П** (to wake up)
*   **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞—В–Є—Б—П** (to wash face/hands)
*   **–Њ–і—П–≥–∞—В–Є—Б—П** (to get dressed)
*   **–Ј–±–Є—А–∞ћБ—В–Є—Б—П** (to get ready)
*   **–њ–Њ–≤–µ—А—В–∞ћБ—В–Є—Б—П** (to return home)

There is a conceptual difference between the first actions of the day. The verb **–њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—В–Є—Б—П** means to wake up, specifically the moment you open your eyes and become conscious. A different verb, **–≤—Б—В–∞–≤–∞ћБ—В–Є**, means to physically get out of bed. Another essential daily action is **—З–ЄћБ—Б—В–Є—В–Є –Ј—ГћБ–±–Є** (to brush teeth), which is a common non-reflexive routine phrase. We also use non-reflexive verbs for consuming our morning food and drinks: **—Б–љ—ЦћБ–і–∞—В–Є** (to have breakfast) and **–њ–ЄћБ—В–Є –Ї–∞ћБ–≤—Г** (to drink coffee).

Once you are ready, you have to leave the house. To describe this, we use the verb **–є—В–Є** (to go). This is an irregular verb, meaning it does not perfectly follow the standard Group I or Group II patterns. You simply have to memorize its forms.

| –Ч–∞–є–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є–Ї | –Ф—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–Њ | –Я–µ—А–µ–Ї–ї–∞–і |
| --- | --- | --- |
| –ѓ | **–є–і—Г** | I go |
| –Ґ–Є | **–є–і–µ—И** | You go |
| –Т—Ц–љ / –Т–Њ–љ–∞ | **–є–і–µ** | He / She goes |
| –Ь–Є | **–є–і–µ–Љ–ЊћБ** | We go |
| –Т–Є | **–є–і–µ—В–µћБ** | You (pl/formal) go |
| –Т–Њ–љ–ЄћБ | **–є–і—Г—В—М** | They go |

This verb is used constantly when talking about daily schedules, specifically for leaving the house to go to work or study.

When you tell someone about your day, you do not just recite a random list of verbs. You connect your actions into a logical sequence. We use specific sequence words for telling a story smoothly.

*   **—Б–њ–Њ—З–∞ћБ—В–Ї—Г** (first)
*   **–њ–Њ—В—Ц–Љ** (then)
*   **–њ—ЦћБ—Б–ї—П —Ж—М–Њ–≥–ЊћБ** (after this)
*   **–љ–∞—А–µћБ—И—В—Ц** (finally)

These sequence words act as the structural glue holding your narrative together. They turn a disconnected list of verbs into a coherent description of your morning. A complete paragraph combines reflexive verbs, regular verbs, and sequence words naturally. Here is a model narrative showing a realistic timeline:

*   **–°–њ–Њ—З–∞—В–Ї—Г —П –њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—О—Б—П.** (First, I wake up.)
*   **–Я–Њ—В—Ц–Љ –≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞—О—Б—П —Ц –Њ–і—П–≥–∞—О—Б—П.** (Then I wash up and get dressed.)
*   **–Я—Ц—Б–ї—П —Ж—М–Њ–≥–Њ —Б–љ—Ц–і–∞—О.** (After this, I have breakfast.)
*   **–Э–∞—А–µ—И—В—Ц –є–і—Г –љ–∞ —А–Њ–±–Њ—В—Г.** (Finally, I go to work.)

This simple structure is exactly how a native speaker outlines their basic routine. Practice sequencing your own actions using these identical words.

## –Я—Ц–і—Б—Г–Љ–Њ–Ї вАФ Summary

The core concepts of this module follow a clear pattern. The fundamental formula for creating reflexive verbs is simple: regular verb plus **—Б—П** at the end. These verbs are essential because they describe actions directed at the speaker, which is exactly what a morning routine involves. The conjugation follows the standard Group I pattern with the suffix attached securely to the end.

*   **—П** ending: **-—О—Б—П**
*   **—В–Є** ending: **-—Ф—И—Б—П**
*   **–≤—Ц–љ / –≤–Њ–љ–∞** ending: **-—Ф—В—М—Б—П**

The sequence of a standard morning routine flows logically through these verbs: **–њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—В–Є—Б—П** вЖТ **–≤–Љ–Є–≤–∞—В–Є—Б—П** вЖТ **–Њ–і—П–≥–∞—В–Є—Б—П** вЖТ **—Б–љ—Ц–і–∞—В–Є** вЖТ **–є—В–Є**. To connect these actions into a fluent story, you must use the sequencing adverbs: **—Б–њ–Њ—З–∞—В–Ї—Г**, **–њ–Њ—В—Ц–Љ**, **–њ—Ц—Б–ї—П —Ж—М–Њ–≥–Њ**, and **–љ–∞—А–µ—И—В—Ц**.

:::tip
Phonetics play a massive role in making your speech sound natural. The written letters and the spoken sounds are entirely different for the **—В–Є** and **–≤—Ц–љ/–≤–Њ–љ–∞** forms of reflexive verbs. The ending **-—И—Б—П** must always be pronounced as a long, soft **[—Б':–∞]**. The ending **-—Ф—В—М—Б—П** must always be pronounced as a long, soft **[—Ж':–∞]**. Focus heavily on these soft, merged sounds to develop an authentic Ukrainian accent.
:::

To ensure you have mastered this material, perform a quick self-check. Try to answer the following questions mentally or aloud. Use full sentences and incorporate the sequencing words where possible.

*   **–Ю –Ї–Њ—В—А—ЦћБ–є –≥–Њ–і–ЄћБ–љ—Ц —В–Є –њ—А–Њ–Ї–Є–і–∞—Ф—И—Б—П?** (At what time do you wake up?)
*   **–©–Њ —В–Є —А–Њ–±–Є—И —Б–њ–Њ—З–∞—В–Ї—Г?** (What do you do first?)
*   **–Ґ–Є –њ'—Ф—И –Ї–∞–≤—Г —З–Є —З–∞–є –≤—А–∞–љ—Ж—Ц?** (Do you drink coffee or tea in the morning?)
*   **–Ъ–Њ–ї–Є —В–Є –є–і–µ—И –љ–∞ —А–Њ–±–Њ—В—Г –∞–±–ЊћБ –љ–∞–≤—З–∞ћБ–љ–љ—П?** (When do you go to work or study?)
*   **–Ґ–Є –Њ–і—П–≥–∞ћБ—Ф—И—Б—П —И–≤–ЄћБ–і–Ї–Њ —З–Є –њ–Њ–≤—ЦћБ–ї—М–љ–Њ?** (Do you get dressed quickly or slowly?)

Describe your morning in four to five sentences using the sequence words. If you can confidently answer these questions and build that short narrative, you are successfully mastering the mechanics of Ukrainian reflexive verbs.
