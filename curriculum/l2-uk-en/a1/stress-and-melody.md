## На́голос (Stress)

Every Ukrainian word has a heartbeat. Say the word **наголос** (stress) out loud — one of its three syllables is louder and longer than the others. That louder syllable is the stressed one. A Grade 1 textbook puts it simply: "Вимов сло́во на|го|лос. Оди́н зі складів у сло́ві ти вимовля́єш сильні́ше." Ukrainian stress is **ві́льний** (free) — it can land on any syllable in a word. This is unlike French, where stress always falls on the last syllable, or Czech, where it always falls on the first. In Ukrainian, you have to learn each word's stress individually. Textbooks and dictionaries mark stress with the symbol ´ over the vowel.

Here is what makes Ukrainian stress especially tricky: it is **рухо́мий** (mobile). Stress moves between different forms of the same word. The word **рука́** (hand) stresses the last syllable — but its plural **ру́ки** stresses the first. **Вода́** (water) stresses the end, but **во́ди** (waters) stresses the beginning. **Кни́га** (book) stresses the first syllable, but **кни́жка** (little book) shifts to the second. This happens constantly in Ukrainian. A Grade 2 textbook offers practical advice: "Щоб запам'ята́ти наголос у слові, пра́вильно проговори́ йо́го кі́лька разі́в" — to remember stress, say the word correctly several times. Repetition is your best tool.

Stress does not just affect pronunciation — it changes meaning entirely. Consider **замок**. With stress on the first syllable, **замок** means "castle." With stress on the second syllable, **замок** means "lock." Two completely different things. The same happens with **мука**: stressed on the first syllable, **мука** means "torment" or "suffering"; stressed on the last syllable, **мука** means "flour." And **атлас**: first syllable gives you **атлас** (atlas, a book of maps), while second syllable gives you **атлас** (satin fabric). A Grade 1 textbook page illustrates this beautifully — the same word **замок** appears twice, once beside a castle and once beside a lock. Wrong stress does not just sound odd. It produces a different word. This is exactly why stress marks in dictionaries matter for learners.

In everyday Ukrainian writing — texts, emails, signs — nobody writes stress marks. You will only see them in textbooks, dictionaries, and learning materials. As a learner, when you encounter a new word and are unsure where the stress falls, check **goroh.pp.ua** — a free online dictionary that shows stress for over 500,000 Ukrainian words. In this curriculum, stress marks appear on new vocabulary to guide your pronunciation.

Now, some patterns to get you started with common A1 words. Many familiar words stress the first syllable: **ма́ма** (mother), **та́то** (father), **ра́нок** (morning), **ка́ва** (coffee), **книга** (book). Others stress the last syllable: **вода** (water), **зима́** (winter), **рука** (hand), **метро́** (metro), **кафе́** (café). Some stress the middle: **столи́ця** (capital city), **Украї́на** (Ukraine), **апте́ка** (pharmacy). There is no universal shortcut — you must learn each word's stress individually. But you will notice patterns: many two-syllable words for family and food stress the first syllable, while many nature and place words stress the last.

## Інтона́ція (Intonation)

Picture yourself in a Kyiv café. Someone at the next table says: «Це кава.» ↘ — the voice falls at the end. They are stating a fact: this is coffee. Now they turn to the waiter: «Це кава?» ↗ — the voice rises on the stressed syllable. Same words, but now it is a question. Then they take a sip: «Як га́рно!» ↘↘ — the voice drops sharply, expressing delight. Three sentences, two words each, but the melody — the **інтонація** (intonation) — tells the listener whether you are telling, asking, or feeling.

Yes/no questions always rise ↗ on the last stressed syllable. «Це метро?» ↗ «Це аптека?» ↗ «Він студе́нт?» ↗ The voice lifts because there is no question word — intonation alone signals that you are asking. But when a question word does the work — **хто** (who), **що** (what), **де** (where), **коли́** (when), **як** (how) — the intonation falls ↘. «Що це?» ↘ «Де метро?» ↘ «Хто це?» ↘ A common mistake for learners is rising on every question. Remember: only rise when there is no question word.

Ukrainian grammar classifies sentences by purpose — **за мето́ю висло́влювання**. There are three types: **розповідні́** (declarative — you are telling something → ends with **кра́пка** .), **пита́льні** (interrogative — you are asking → ends with **знак пита́ння** ?), and **спонука́льні** (imperative — you are requesting or commanding → ends with **знак о́клику** !). Any of these can also be **окли́чне** (exclamatory) — that is a separate layer of emotion. «Це Ки́їв.» is a calm statement. «Це Київ!» is an excited statement. Both are розповідні, but the second adds emotional force. For now, remember three punctuation marks: **.** for telling, **?** for asking, **!** for strong feeling or commands.

A Grade 2 textbook defines it clearly: "Речення, у яко́му про щось розповіда́ється або́ повідомля́ється, назива́ється розповідни́м." Think of it like texting a friend. You are telling them something: «Я в метро.» — розповідне́, period, voice falls. You are asking: «Ти вдо́ма?» — пита́льне, question mark, voice rises. You are requesting: «Напиши́ мені́!» — спонука́льне, exclamation mark, voice falls with urgency. Each sentence type has its own punctuation AND its own melody. Try saying all three aloud right now — feel how your voice changes direction.

## Чита́ємо вго́лос (Reading Aloud)

Long Ukrainian words can look intimidating, but there is a simple method: break the word into syllables, find the stressed syllable, then say the whole word at natural speed. Try it: **украї́нська** (Ukrainian) → у-кра-їн-ська — stress on **ї**. **Столиця** (capital) → сто-ли-ця — stress on **и**. **Відпочи́нок** (rest, vacation) → від-по-чи-нок — stress on **и**. **Фотогра́фія** (photograph) → фо-то-гра-фі-я — stress on the second **а**. Do not read syllable-by-syllable forever — that sounds choppy and unnatural. Syllable-splitting is training wheels. The goal is smooth, natural pronunciation at normal speed.

Now read this short text aloud. First, find the stress on each word silently. Then read slowly with correct stress. Finally, read again at natural speed, using falling intonation ↘ for each statement:

> «Це Київ. Київ — столиця Украї́ни. Тут є метро, апте́ки, кафе. Це га́рне мі́сто.»

Every sentence here is a statement — your voice should fall at the end of each one. Now try turning some into questions: «Це Київ?» ↗ «Тут є метро?» ↗ «Де аптека?» ↘ Notice how your melody shifts? The statements felt settled and final. The yes/no questions lifted with curiosity. The **де** question fell because the question word carries the asking.

Now put everything together with a dialogue. Read aloud, paying attention to where the voice rises ↗ and falls ↘:

> — Приві́т! ↘
> — Привіт! Як спра́ви? ↗
> — До́бре! А у тебе́? ↗
> — Теж добре! ↘
> — Де ти? ↘
> — Я в кафе. ↘
> — О, кава! Як гарно! ↘↘

Notice the pattern: «Як справи?» rises because it expects a yes/no-style status answer — is everything fine? «Де ти?» falls because **де** is a question word that does the questioning work by itself. The greetings and statements all fall naturally. This dialogue uses vocabulary from Module 1 — you already know these words. Now you are adding music to them.

Ukrainian speech has a natural rhythm where stressed syllables are slightly longer than unstressed ones. Do not give every syllable equal weight — that sounds robotic, like a computer reading text. Listen to any Ukrainian speaker and you will hear it: the stressed syllable stands out, while unstressed syllables pass by quicker and lighter. Practice with longer words: **Україна**, **столиця**, **фотографія**. Exaggerate the stressed syllable at first — make it noticeably louder and longer. Then gradually smooth it out until it sounds natural. That flow is the music of Ukrainian.

## Підсумок — Summary

Three big ideas from this module. First: **наголос** is free and mobile — it can fall on any syllable in a Ukrainian word, and it moves between different forms of the same word (**вода** → **води**, **рука** → **руки**). There is no fixed rule like "always stress the first syllable." Each word must be learned individually. Second: wrong stress can change meaning entirely. **Замок** (castle) and **замок** (lock) are different words distinguished only by stress. Third: **інтонація** distinguishes statements ↘, yes/no questions ↗, and exclamations ↘↘ — but questions with question words (**що**, **де**, **хто**) take falling intonation because the word itself already signals a question.

:::tip
**Self-check — Переві́р себе́:**
- Що таке́ наголос? — One syllable in a word is louder and longer.
- Чи мо́же наголос зміни́ти зна́чення слова́? — Yes: замок/замок, мука/мука.
- Яка інтонація у пита́нні «Це кава?»? — Rising ↗.
- А у питанні «Що це?»? — Falling ↘, because «що» is a question word.
- Read aloud: «Це аптека? Так, це аптека. Як гарно!» — did your melody change for each sentence?
:::

What comes next? You now know Ukrainian sounds (Module 2), special signs (Module 3), and stress and intonation (this module). In Module 5, «Who Am I?», you will use all of this to introduce yourself and talk about identity — real sentences about real life. Before you move on, try this daily practice: pick five words from this module, check their stress on **goroh.pp.ua**, and say them aloud three times each. When you hear Ukrainian — in podcasts, music, or conversations — listen for the melody. Where does the voice rise? Where does it fall? Stress and intonation are the music of the Ukrainian language. Once you hear that music, you cannot unhear it. **Натхне́ння й наполе́гливість** — inspiration and persistence.
