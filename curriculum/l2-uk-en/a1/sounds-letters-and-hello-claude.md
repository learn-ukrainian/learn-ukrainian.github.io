## Зву́ки і лі́тери (Sounds and Letters)

«Ми чу́ємо і вимовля́ємо звуки, а ба́чимо і пи́шемо літери.»

This sentence from a Ukrainian first-grade textbook captures something every learner needs to understand before reading a single word. Ukrainian has two separate systems working together: **звуки** — sounds — are what you hear and say. **Літери** — letters — are what you see and write. They are not the same thing, and their numbers don't match: Ukrainian has 33 letters but 38 sounds.

Why the mismatch? Some letters represent more than one sound depending on their position in a word. Others combine to create sounds that have no single letter of their own. You'll discover these patterns naturally as you learn — for now, the key principle is this: a letter is a symbol on paper, a sound is what your mouth produces.

Ukrainian sounds divide into two families. **Голосні́** — vowels — are made with your voice alone. Air flows freely through your mouth with no obstruction from your lips, teeth, or tongue. A Ukrainian children's poem puts it simply: «Голосні почу́єш в пісні́» — "You'll hear vowels in a song." There are 6 vowel sounds in Ukrainian, but 10 vowel letters: А, О, У, Е, И, І, Я, Ю, Є, Ї. Four of those letters (Я, Ю, Є, Ї) do special work — you'll explore them fully in the next module.

**При́голосні** — consonants — are different. They're made when your lips, teeth, or tongue create an obstruction, producing noise alongside your voice (or sometimes noise alone). Another classroom poem: «Приголосні деренча́ть, і тихе́нько шелестя́ть» — "Consonants rattle and quietly rustle." Ukrainian has 32 consonant sounds — far more than the 6 vowels. That imbalance shapes the entire sound system of the language.

One more letter to know: **Ь** (the soft sign). It has no sound of its own — it modifies the consonant before it, making it soft. You'll learn how softness works in Module 2.

## Пе́рші слова́ (First Words)

The Ukrainian alphabet uses Cyrillic script. Some letters will look familiar — but looks can deceive. Let's sort every letter into one of three groups.

### Familiar friends

These letters look similar to their Latin counterparts and sound roughly the same: **А**, **О**, **К**, **М**, **Т**. With just these five, you can already read real Ukrainian words:

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **ма́ма** | mother |
| **та́то** | father |
| **мак** | poppy |
| **о́ко** | eye |
| **а́том** | atom |
| **ко́ма** | comma |

Read them aloud now. Sound out each letter, then blend: М-А-М-А → **мама**. Two small notes: Ukrainian **Т** is pronounced with the tongue touching the upper teeth (not the gum ridge as in English), and **К** and **Т** have no puff of air after them. These differences are subtle — close enough for now.

### False friends — the biggest trap

These letters look like Latin letters but make completely different sounds. This is where English speakers stumble most:

| Letter | Looks like | Actually sounds like |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **В** | B | "v" as in "van" |
| **Н** | H | "n" as in "no" |
| **Р** | P | a rolled "r" (tongue trills) |
| **С** | C | "s" as in "sun" |
| **У** | Y | "oo" as in "moon" |
| **Х** | X | like "ch" in Scottish "loch" |

Practice reading these words, fighting the English reflex:

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **вода́** | water |
| **рука́** | hand |
| **сон** | dream |
| **ніс** | nose |
| **ха́та** | house |

When you see **вода**, your brain will want to read "BODA." Override it: В = "v," so it's "voda." This reflex takes a few days to retrain — that's normal.

### New shapes

These letters have no Latin lookalike — they're unique to Cyrillic: **Б**, **Г**, **Ґ**, **Д**, **Ж**, **З**, **И**, **Й**, **Л**, **П**, **Ф**, **Ц**, **Ч**, **Ш**, **Щ**. Don't try to memorize them all at once. Meet them through words:

| Word | Meaning | New letter |
|------|---------|------------|
| **банк** | bank | Б |
| **дім** | house, home | Д |
| **зима́** | winter | З |
| **кни́га** | book | Г |
| **шко́ла** | school | Ш |

Two letters deserve special attention. **Щ** always makes two sounds together — "sh" immediately followed by "ch," blended as one. And **Ї** is unique to Ukrainian among all Slavic languages — no other Slavic alphabet has it. It always contains two sounds. You'll explore both fully in Module 2; for now, just recognize their shapes.

## Приві́т! (Hello!)

Time for your first Ukrainian conversation. You already have enough letters to read these words — sound them out:

> — **Привіт!**
> — **Привіт! Як спра́ви?**
> — **До́бре. А у тебе́?**
> — **Чудо́во!**

**Привіт** means "hi" — informal, for friends, family, and peers. Use it with anyone you'd address by first name. Let's break it down letter by letter: П (new shape) — р (false friend — rolled "r," not "p") — и (new vowel) — в (false friend — "v," not "b") — і (vowel) — т (familiar). Six letters, six sounds, one complete greeting.

**Як справи?** means "How are you?" — literally "how are things?" Common answers:

| Response | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **Добре.** | Fine. Good. |
| **Чудово!** | Great! Wonderful! |
| **Норма́льно.** | Okay. Not bad. |

After answering, you bounce the question back: **А у тебе?** — "And you?"

:::tip
Ukrainian has gendered forms. A woman says **ра́да тебе ба́чити** (glad to see you), a man says **ра́дий тебе бачити**. The ending changes: **-а** for feminine, **-ий** for masculine. This is your first glimpse of grammatical gender — it will become a major topic starting in Module 8.
:::

Two more essential phrases while we're here: **дякую** means "thank you" and **будь ла́ска** means "please" or "you're welcome." Treat these as fixed chunks for now — memorize them whole without analyzing the grammar.

## Чита́ємо (Reading Practice)

Walk down any street in Ukraine and you'll see Cyrillic everywhere — on storefronts, buses, street signs. Let's read what you'd actually see.

### Signs around town

Sound out each word letter by letter. Every letter here is one you've already met:

| Sign | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Апте́ка** | Pharmacy |
| **Банк** | Bank |
| **Кафе́** | Cafe |
| **Метро́** | Metro |
| **По́шта** | Post office |
| **Школа** | School |
| **Зупи́нка** | Bus stop |

Notice how some words look close to their English equivalents — **банк**, **метро**, **кафе** — while others are completely different. **Зупинка** (bus stop) shares no resemblance with English at all. That's fine. Sound it out: З-У-П-И-Н-К-А.

### Ukrainian cities

Now try city names. These are real places on the map:

> **Ки́їв. Львів. Оде́са. Харків. Дніпро́. Полта́ва.**

**Київ** — the capital. Five letters: К-И-Ї-В. Notice **Ї** in the middle — that special Ukrainian letter.

### First sentences

With one small word — **це** (this is) — you can make complete Ukrainian sentences:

> **Це мама.** — This is mom.
> **Це банк.** — This is a bank.
> **Це Київ.** — This is Kyiv.
> **Це школа.** — This is a school.
> **Це вода.** — This is water.

And two question words let you ask about anything:

> **Що це?** — What is this?
> **Хто це?** — Who is this?

**Що** asks about things, **хто** asks about people. The answers use the same pattern:

> — **Що це?** — **Це книга.**
> — **Хто це?** — **Це мама.**

## Підсумок — Summary

Here is what you now know:

**Sounds vs. letters:** Ukrainian has 33 **літери** (letters) and 38 **зву́ків** (sounds). Letters are written symbols. Sounds are what you hear and pronounce. The numbers differ because some letters represent more than one sound.

**Two sound families:** **Голосні** (vowels) — 6 sounds, 10 letters. Made with voice alone, no obstruction. **Приголосні** (consonants) — 32 sounds. Made with obstruction from lips, teeth, or tongue.

**Three letter groups:** Familiar friends (А, О, К, М, Т) sound like you'd expect. False friends (В, Н, Р, С, У, Х) look like English letters but sound completely different. New shapes (Б, Д, Ж, Ш, and others) are unique to Cyrillic.

**Your first phrases:** **Привіт!** (hi), **Як справи?** (how are you?), **Добре** / **Чудово** / **Нормально** (fine / great / okay), **Дякую** (thank you), **Будь ласка** (please).

**Your first sentence pattern:** **Це** + noun. **Це мама. Це школа. Це Київ.**

**Your first questions:** **Що це?** (what is this?) and **Хто це?** (who is this?).
