## Що ми зна́ємо? (What Do We Know?)

Learning a language is like constructing a house, and you have just finished pouring the solid foundation. You have reached the first major milestone in your Ukrainian journey. Stop for a moment and look back at what you have accomplished. You are no longer looking at random shapes on a screen; you are reading the Cyrillic alphabet. You have transformed unfamiliar symbols into meaningful sounds.

Let us review the core skills you have acquired. First, consider reading. Can you read any Ukrainian word you see? You know the friendly letters that look and sound like English, the false friends that look like English but represent entirely different sounds, and the unique Ukrainian letters that exist nowhere else. You also understand the mechanics of the Ukrainian sound system. You know exactly what the **м'яки́й знак** (soft sign) does. It has no sound of its own, but it softens the consonant that comes right before it, changing the entire meaning of a word. Conversely, you know the crucial job of the **апо́стро́ф** (apostrophe). It acts as a hard phonetic barrier, keeping the preceding consonant hard and separating it from the following iotated vowel.

You also understand the critical role of **на́голос** (stress). Hitting the right syllable with your voice changes everything. It provides the rhythm and melody of the language.

Beyond sounds and letters, you have started communicating. You know how to greet people appropriately, whether they are close friends or strangers on the street. You can confidently introduce yourself, share your name, and ask for someone else's name. You can also talk about your closest circle: your family. You know the words for parents, siblings, and grandparents. This is the absolute bedrock of human connection.

Let us test this foundational knowledge with a comprehensive review.

## Чита́ння (Reading Practice)

Now it is time to put your reading skills to the test with connected text. We are going to look at a short Ukrainian paragraph. This text contains absolutely no new words. Every single word here is something you have already encountered in the previous modules. Your goal is to read it aloud. Do not just translate it in your head; let the Ukrainian sounds flow naturally from your mouth.

Imagine you are reading a message from a new friend. She is introducing herself and telling you about her life.

«Привіт! Ме́не звати А́нна. Моє́ **прі́звище** (surname) — Кова́ль. Я з Украї́ни. Я — студе́нтка. Це моя́ сім'я́. Це мій тато. Мій тато — лі́кар. Це моя мама. Моя мама — вчи́телька. У мене є брат. Йо́го звати Анто́н. Він — студе́нт. Це мій дідусь і моя бабуся. Це моя вели́ка сім'я.»

Let us break down exactly what is happening in this text, sentence by sentence, so you can see how much you already understand without relying on direct English translation.

The text opens with a warm, informal greeting: «Привіт!» (Hi!). This sets a friendly, approachable tone. Next, she introduces herself using the standard chunk we use for names: «Мене звати Анна» (My name is Anna). She then provides her last name. The word **прізвище** means "surname" or "last name." Notice how she uses the neuter possessive pronoun **моє** (my) because the word **прізвище** ends in an "е" and is grammatically neuter.

Then, she shares her origin: «Я з України» (I am from Ukraine). She states her profession directly: «Я — студентка» (I am a student). Remember that Ukrainian does not use the verb "to be" in the present tense, so a simple dash often replaces the word "am" in writing.

She shifts the focus to her relatives: «Це моя сім'я» (This is my family). She introduces her father: «Це мій тато» (This is my dad), and states his profession: «Мій тато — лікар» (My dad is a doctor). She does the same for her mother: «Це моя мама» (This is my mom) and «Моя мама — вчителька» (My mom is a teacher).

Next, she talks about her sibling using the fundamental possession structure: «У мене є брат» (I have a brother). She tells us his name: «Його звати Антон» (His name is Anton), and his occupation: «Він — студент» (He is a student). Finally, she mentions her grandparents: «Це мій дідусь і моя бабуся» (This is my grandpa and my grandma). She concludes with a summarizing statement: «Це моя велика сім'я» (This is my big family).

You have just read and understood a complete Ukrainian paragraph. This is a massive achievement. You are no longer just learning isolated words on flashcards; you are processing real, connected Ukrainian thoughts.

## Грама́тика (Grammar Summary)

To speak confidently, you need to recognize the structural patterns of the language. Grammar is not a dry set of rules to memorize; it is a collection of patterns that native speakers use automatically to organize their thoughts. Let us review the six fundamental patterns you have mastered so far in this level.

First is the basic identification pattern using the word **це** (this is). You use this powerful little word to point things out or introduce people. The word **це** never changes its shape, regardless of what you are pointing at or how many things there are.
*   «Це кіт.» (This is a cat.)
*   «Це дім.» (This is a house.)
*   «Це мій брат.» (This is my brother.)

Second is the "Subject — Noun" pattern. In English, you must say "I am a student" or "She is a doctor." In modern Ukrainian, the present tense of the verb "to be" has completely faded away because it does not convey active meaning. You simply place the subject and the noun together. In writing, we often use a dash to represent the slight pause in speech.
*   «Я — студент.» (I am a student.)
*   «Він — лікар.» (He is a doctor.)
*   «Вона́ — сестра.» (She is a sister.)

Third is the pattern for possession. Instead of using a verb that means "to own" or "to have," Ukrainian uses a spatial structure that literally translates to "by me there is." The phrase is **у мене є** (I have). You follow this phrase directly with the thing or person you possess. This trains you to think about possession as proximity rather than action.
*   «У мене є кіт.» (I have a cat.)
*   «У мене є брат.» (I have a brother.)
*   «У мене є телефо́н.» (I have a phone.)

Fourth is the crucial pattern for asking names. You know that Ukrainian requires you to choose between the informal and the formal register based on your relationship with the person you are speaking to.
*   «Як тебе звати?» (What is your name? — informal, used when speaking to a friend, family member, or a child.)
*   «Як вас звати?» (What is your name? — formal, used when speaking to an elder, a stranger, a professional, or a group of people.)

Fifth is the use of possessive pronouns. In Ukrainian, words that mean "my" must match the grammatical gender of the noun they describe. You have learned the masculine, feminine, and neuter forms.
*   **Мій** (my - masculine): «мій тато» (my dad), «мій кіт» (my cat).
*   **Моя** (my - feminine): «моя мама» (my mom), «моя сестра» (my sister).
*   **Моє** (my - neuter): «моє мі́сто» (my city), «моє ім'я́» (my first name). The word **ім'я** means "first name" and it is grammatically neuter.

Sixth is the pattern for stating your geographical origin. When someone asks «Звідки ти?» (Where are you from?), you respond with the phrase **я з** (I am from) followed immediately by the country or city.
*   «Я з України.» (I am from Ukraine.)
*   «Я з Кана́ди.» (I am from Canada.)
*   «Я з Аме́рики.» (I am from America.)

These six grammatical patterns form the powerful engine of your basic Ukrainian conversations. If you master them, you can build hundreds of different sentences without ever needing to translate word-for-word from English.

## Діало́г (Capstone Dialogue)

We have reviewed the individual pieces of the language; now let us put the puzzle together. The ultimate goal of learning these patterns is to interact with another human being in the real world. Imagine you are sitting at a coffee shop in Kyiv, and you sit down next to someone new.

This is the Capstone Dialogue. It combines every single skill you have learned in the first six modules: offering a greeting, exchanging names, discussing your origins, talking about your professions, and describing your family.

Read the following dialogue aloud. Try to hear the distinct voices of the two speakers.

:::dialogue
<div class="dialogue">


— Добрий день!


— Добрий день!


— Як вас звати?


— Мене звати Максим. А як вас звати?


— Мене звати Де́від.


— Ду́же приє́мно, Де́віде. Звідки ви?


— Я з Америки. А ви звідки?


— Я з України. Я — архіте́ктор. А хто ви?


— Я — програмі́ст.


— У вас є сім'я?


— Так, у мене є сім'я. Це моя дружи́на. Її звати Анна. Вона — лікар. А це мій син. Його звати Марк.


— Дуже га́рна сім'я. У мене є брат. Це мій брат. Він — студент.


— Дуже приємно. До поба́чення, Макси́ме!


— До побачення, Девіде!


</div>


:::

Let us analyze the flow of this interaction. It begins formally because these are two adults meeting for the very first time. They use the respectful daytime greeting «Добрий день!» (Good day!). When asking for names, they use the formal construction «Як вас звати?» (What is your name?). Once names are exchanged, they express politeness with the phrase «Дуже приємно» (Very pleasant / Nice to meet you).

The conversation then naturally moves to their origins. They ask «Звідки ви?» (Where are you from?), carefully maintaining the formal "you." They state their professions using the direct Subject-Noun pattern without any verb: «Я — архітектор» (I am an architect) and «Я — програміст» (I am a programmer).

Then, the topic shifts to family, which is the universal bridge between people in Ukrainian culture. The question «У вас є сім'я?» (Do you have a family?) uses the formal possession structure. David shows a photo on his phone and uses the fundamental identification pattern: «Це моя дружина» (This is my wife). He uses the feminine possessive pronoun **моя** because the word for wife is feminine. He then states her name and her profession. He does exactly the same for his son, switching to the masculine possessive pronoun **мій**: «Це мій син» (This is my son).

Maksym responds warmly and shares his own family details, again using the core possession pattern: «У мене є брат» (I have a brother). The interaction ends politely with the standard farewell «До побачення!» (Goodbye!).

This dialogue represents your graduation from the absolute beginner phase. If you can follow this exchange, understand the grammar structures supporting it, and imagine yourself confidently participating in it, you have successfully completed the A1.1 phase.

Now, it is your turn to speak. We are going to build your personal connected monologue, your self-introduction speech. This is the paragraph you can use whenever you meet a Ukrainian speaker. Think of it as your verbal business card.

Here is the master template. Your job is to fill in the blanks with your own reality.

«Привіт! Мене звати [your name].
Моє прізвище — [your surname].
Я з [your country/city].
Я — [your profession].
Це моя сім'я.
Моя мама — [profession].
Мій тато — [profession].
У мене є [family members].»

Practice speaking this connected monologue out loud. Do not just read it silently in your head; perform it. Imagine the actual person you are speaking to. Stand in front of a mirror and introduce yourself in Ukrainian. This is the magical moment where the language stops being a puzzle on a page and becomes a living tool for your personal expression.
