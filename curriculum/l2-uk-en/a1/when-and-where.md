## Діало́ги (Dialogues)

Communication is rarely just about stating simple facts. As you talk more with people, you will want to express your thoughts, share what you know, and ask about things you don't know. To do this, you need to connect your ideas. 

Let's look at how two friends plan a meeting. Pay close attention to the words connecting the two halves of their sentences.

<div class="dialogue">


**Максим:** Ти зна́єш, де нове́ кафе́? *(Do you know where the new cafe is?)*


**Анна:** Так, я зна́ю, де воно́. *(Yes, I know where it is.)*


**Максим:** Скажи́, коли́ ти ві́льний. *(Tell me when you are free.)*


**Анна:** Я ві́льна, коли закі́нчу́ робо́ту. *(I am free when I finish work.)*


**Максим:** До́бре. Я ду́маю, що о шо́стій бу́де добре. *(Good. I think that at six will be good.)*


**Анна:** Так, я теж думаю, що це га́рний час. *(Yes, I also think that this is a good time.)*


</div>


Notice the words **де** (where), **коли** (when), and **що** (that). They are acting as bridges, connecting one complete thought to another. Anna doesn't just say "It is at six." She says, "I think **that** this is a good time." 

Now let's look at another conversation, where two colleagues are catching up and asking about a mutual friend. 

<div class="dialogue">


**Тарас:** Ти знаєш, що Оле́на вже в Ки́єві? *(Do you know that Olena is already in Kyiv?)*


**Ірина:** Ні, я не зна́ла! А де вона́ живе́? *(No, I didn't know! And where does she live?)*


**Тарас:** Я не знаю, де саме. Але́ я знаю, що бі́ля це́нтру. *(I don't know exactly where. But I know that near the center.)*


**Ірина:** Скажи їй, коли поба́чиш, що я хо́чу зустрі́тися. *(Tell her when you see her that I want to meet.)*


**Тарас:** Добре, скажу́, коли поба́чу. *(Good, I will tell her when I see her.)*


</div>


In this dialogue, Taras and Iryna use verbs like **знати** (to know), **казати** (to tell/say), and **бачити** (to see) to frame their thoughts. The words **що**, **де**, and **коли** act as subordinating conjunctions. They introduce a dependent idea (like "that Olena is in Kyiv") and attach it to a main idea (like "Do you know"). 

This is how natural conversation flows. You don't just state isolated facts; you constantly frame them with your own knowledge, thoughts, and conditions.

## Складне речення (Complex Sentences)

In the previous module, you learned to connect EQUAL ideas using words like **і** (and), **а** (but/and), and **але** (but). For example, **"Я чита́ю, і він пи́ше"** (I read, and he writes). Both parts of the sentence are independent and equally important.

Now we are looking at a different structure. We are connecting a MAIN idea with a DEPENDENT idea. In Ukrainian grammar (specifically in Grade 5 textbooks), this is called a **складнопідрядне речення** (a complex sentence with a subordinate clause). 

This structure has a specific anatomy:
1.  **Го́ловне речення** (The main clause)
2.  A subordinating conjunction (**що**, **де**, **коли**)
3.  The dependent clause

Let's break down how this works in practice.

*   **Я знаю, що він тут.** (I know that he's here.)
    *   Main idea: **Я знаю** (I know)
    *   Connection: **що** (that)
    *   Dependent idea: **він тут** (he is here)

*   **Я не знаю, де він живе.** (I don't know where he lives.)
    *   Main idea: **Я не знаю** (I don't know)
    *   Connection: **де** (where)
    *   Dependent idea: **він живе** (he lives)

*   **Скажи мені́, коли ти прийдеш.** (Tell me when you'll come.)
    *   Main idea: **Скажи мені** (Tell me)
    *   Connection: **коли** (when)
    *   Dependent idea: **ти прийдеш** (you will come)

### The Golden Comma Rule

There is one rule you must never forget when building these sentences. **Always use a comma before що, де, and коли when they connect clauses.** 

This is very different from English. In English, you write "I think that this is right" without any punctuation. In Ukrainian, the comma is strictly required. It visually separates the **головне** (main) thought from the dependent one.

*   **Я думаю, що це пра́вильно.** (I think that this is right.) — Notice the comma before **що**.
*   **Він не зна́є, де магази́н.** (He doesn't know where the store is.) — Notice the comma before **де**.
*   **Зателефонуй, коли прийдеш.** (Call when you arrive.) — Notice the comma before **коли**.

If you write these sentences without a comma, it is considered a significant punctuation error. The comma acts as a breath, a slight pause that shows the structure of your thought.

## Що, де, коли — дво́є обли́ч (Two Faces)

You have seen the words **що**, **де**, and **коли** before. Early in your learning, you used them to ask questions. Now, you are using them to connect sentences. They have two completely different jobs, or "two faces."

**Job 1: Question Words**
When used to ask a direct question, these words usually appear at the very beginning of the sentence. The sentence always ends with a question mark.
*   **Що це?** (What is this?)
*   **Де ти?** (Where are you?)
*   **Коли ти прийдеш?** (When will you arrive?)

**Job 2: Subordinating Conjunctions**
When used as conjunctions, these words appear in the middle of a complex sentence, connecting two parts. They are always preceded by a comma.
*   **Я знаю, що це кни́жка.** (I know that this is a book.)
*   **Я знаю, де ти.** (I know where you are.)
*   **Скажи, коли прийдеш.** (Tell me when you will arrive.)

How do you tell the difference? Look at the position and the punctuation. If it's at the start and asks for information, it's a question word. If it's in the middle following a comma and connecting thoughts, it's a conjunction.

### Common Verb Patterns

To build these complex sentences smoothly, you need to rely on specific verbs in your main clause. These verbs represent thoughts, speech, or perception. 

Here are the most common patterns using verbs like **знати** (to know), **думати** (to think), **казати** (to say), and **розуміти** (to understand).

**Patterns with ЩО (that)**
*   **Я знаю, що...** (I know that...)
*   **Я не знаю, що...** (I don't know that...)
*   **Я думаю, що...** (I think that...)
*   **Він каже, що...** (He says that...)
*   **Вона розумі́є, що...** (She understands that...)
*   **Я ба́чу, що...** (I see that...)
*   **Я чу́ю, що...** (I hear that...)

**Patterns with ДЕ (where)**
*   **Я знаю, де...** (I know where...)
*   **Я не знаю, де...** (I don't know where...)
*   **Він каже, де...** (He says where...)

**Patterns with КОЛИ (when)**
*   **Скажи, коли...** (Tell me when...)
*   **Я не знаю, коли...** (I don't know when...)
*   **Коли я прийду́, ми погово́римо.** (When I arrive, we will talk.) — *Note: The conjunction can start the sentence if the dependent clause comes first. The comma still separates the two clauses!*

## Підсумок — Summary

You now have the tools to express complex thoughts, opinions, and conditions. Let's review the subordinating conjunctions you learned in this module:

| Conjunction | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **що** | that | **Я знаю, що він тут.** (I know that he's here.) |
| **де** | where | **Я не знаю, де кафе.** (I don't know where the cafe is.) |
| **коли** | when | **Скажи, коли прийдеш.** (Tell me when you will arrive.) |

Remember the most important rule: **Always place a comma before the conjunction** to separate the main clause from the dependent clause. 

You can even combine these new structures with the conjunctions you learned previously (**і**, **а**, **але**, **бо**) to build incredibly rich, natural-sounding sentences. 

*   **Я не йду, бо я не знаю, де це.** (I am not going, because I don't know where it is.) — Two conjunctions in one sentence!
*   **Він каже, що прийде, коли закі́нчи́ть.** (He says that he will come when he finishes.) — Two subordinate clauses!

To check your own progress, try to build three new sentences using these patterns:
1.  **Я думаю, що...** (I think that...)
2.  **Я не знаю, де...** (I don't know where...)
3.  **Скажи мені, коли...** (Tell me when...)

Once you can do this easily, you are ready to navigate more complex conversations in Ukrainian.
