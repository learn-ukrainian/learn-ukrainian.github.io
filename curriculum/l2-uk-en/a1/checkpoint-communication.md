## Що ми зна́ємо? (What Do We Know?)

Communication is more than just stating facts. It is about connecting with people, organizing plans, and sharing emotions. Over the past five modules, you have learned the essential tools to build relationships in Ukrainian. You have moved from simple descriptions to active participation.

Let us review what you have achieved in this phase:

- **Can you call someone by name using vocative?** When you want to get someone's attention, you do not just use their base name. You change it. Instead of saying **Олена** (Olena), you say **Олено**! This small change makes your speech sound authentic.
- **Can you ask someone to do something?** You have learned how to give instructions and make requests using the imperative mood. Whether you are telling a friend to read a text (**Чита́й!**) or asking a group to listen (**Слухайте!**), you are now directing action.
- **Can you connect ideas with і, а, але́, and бо?** Sentences feel choppy if they are too short. By using conjunctions, you can show addition, contrast, and cause.
- **Can you build sentences with що, де, and коли́?** You can now express your thoughts and ask complex questions. You know how to link a main idea to a dependent clause.
- **Can you name Ukrainian holidays and greet people?** You have explored the vocabulary of celebration. You can say **З Різдво́м!** (Merry Christmas!) and understand cultural terms like **кутя** (kutia, a traditional dish).

## Чита́ння (Reading Practice)

Read the following message. Olena is sending a text to her friend Taras to plan a holiday celebration. Notice how she combines all the communication tools from this phase into one natural, realistic message.

> Приві́т, Тара́се! *(Hi, Taras!)*
> Ти зна́єш, що ско́ро Різдво́. *(You know that Christmas is soon.)*
> Прийди́ в го́сті, бо ми святку́ємо! *(Come visit, because we are celebrating!)*
> Принеси́ кутю́, будь ла́ска! *(Bring kutia, please!)*
> Але я не зна́ю, коли ти ві́льний. *(But I don't know when you are free.)*
> Ми співа́ємо коля́дки. *(We are singing carols.)*
> Напиши́ повідо́млення! *(Write a message!)*
> З Різдвом! *(Merry Christmas!)*

In just a few short sentences, Olena uses multiple advanced communication strategies. Let us break down her message. 

First, she uses the vocative case to address her friend directly: **Тарасе!** (Taras!). This immediately establishes a warm, personal connection. 

Next, she uses a complex sentence with a subordinating conjunction: **Ти знаєш, що скоро Різдво** (You know that Christmas is soon). She connects her ideas logically using coordinating conjunctions: **бо ми святкуємо** (because we are celebrating) explains why he should come, and **але я не знаю** (but I do not know) adds a contrast. 

She also uses the imperative mood to make direct requests: **Прийди** (Come), **Принеси** (Bring), and **Напиши** (Write). These verbs direct the action and tell Taras exactly what she wants him to do. 

Finally, she includes rich holiday vocabulary like **Різдво** (Christmas), **кутя** (kutia), and **колядки** (carols). This is how native speakers talk. They do not use isolated grammar rules; they combine them to express clear, organized thoughts.

## Грама́тика (Grammar Summary)

Here are the key patterns from the Communication phase. Use these rules as a quick reference when building your own sentences.

### Кли́чний відмі́нок (Vocative Case)

The vocative case is used exclusively when addressing someone directly. It replaces the nominative case for names and titles, showing respect and engagement. The ending changes based on the last letter of the word.

- **Олена** → **Олено!** (Words ending in -а change to -о)
- **Тарас** → **Тарасе!** (Words ending in a hard consonant add -е)
- **Андрі́й** → **Андрію!** (Words ending in a soft consonant add -ю)
- **Ма́ма** → **Ма́мо!** (Mom!)
- **Брат** → **Брате!** (Brother!)
- **Друг** → **Друже!** (Friend!)

### Наказо́вий спо́сіб (Imperative Mood)

When you need to give an instruction or make a request, use the imperative mood. Ukrainian distinguishes between informal (singular) and formal/plural requests. Always add **будь ласка** (please) to be polite.

- **ти** (informal singular): **читай** (read), **дай** (give), **скажи** (tell), **слухай** (listen)
- **ви** (formal or plural): **читайте** (read), **дайте** (give), **скажіть** (tell), **слухайте** (listen)

### Сполу́чники суря́дності (Coordinating Conjunctions)

Conjunctions link your ideas together. Without them, communication is fragmented.

- **і** / **та** (and): used for simple addition. «Хліб і вода́» (Bread and water).
- **а** (and/but): used for a soft contrast or comparison. «Я чита́ю, а ти пи́шеш» (I read, and you write).
- **але** (but): used for a strong contrast. «Я хо́чу, але не мо́жу» (I want to, but I cannot).
- **бо** (because): used to provide a reason. «Я вдо́ма, бо хо́лодно» (I am home, because it is cold).

### Підря́дні сполучники (Subordinating Conjunctions)

These conjunctions link a main thought to a dependent thought. In Ukrainian, these clauses are ALWAYS separated by a comma. If you write **що** (that), you must place a comma before it.

- **що** (that): «Я знаю, що це пра́вда» (I know that this is true).
- **де** (where): «Скажи, де ти» (Tell [me] where you are).
- **коли** (when): «Я не знаю, коли він тут» (I do not know when he is here).

### Привіта́ння зі свя́та́ми (Holiday Greetings)

To congratulate someone on a holiday, use the preposition **з** (with) followed by the instrumental case. You are wishing them joy "with" the holiday.

- **З Різдвом!** (Merry Christmas!)
- **З Вели́коднем!** (Happy Easter!)
- **З Новим ро́ком!** (Happy New Year!)
- **З Днем наро́дження!** (Happy Birthday!)
- **Зі свя́том!** (Happy Holiday! — a universal greeting for any celebration)

## Діало́г (Connected Dialogue)

<div class="dialogue">


**Тарас:** Олено, привіт! Ти знаєш, що скоро Різдво? *(Olena, hi! Do you know that Christmas is soon?)*


**Олена:** Так, Тарасе! Я ду́маю, що ми мо́жемо святкува́ти ра́зом. *(Yes, Taras! I think that we can celebrate together.)*


**Тарас:** До́бре! Скажи, коли ти ві́льна, бо я хочу запроси́ти дру́зів. *(Good! Tell [me] when you are free, because I want to invite friends.)*


**Олена:** Я вільна два́дцять четве́ртого. Але я не знаю, де ми бу́демо. *(I am free on the twenty-fourth. But I don't know where we will be.)*


**Тарас:** Ході́мо до мене́! Принеси кутю, будь ласка. *(Let's go to my place! Bring kutia, please.)*


**Олена:** Добре, принесу́! І я знаю, де купи́ти га́рні свічки́. З Різдвом! *(Good, I will bring [it]! And I know where to buy beautiful candles. Merry Christmas!)*


</div>



Notice how every tool from the Communication phase appears naturally: vocative (**Олено**, **Тарасе**) for warm address, imperatives (**Скажи**, **Принеси**, **Ходімо**) for requests and invitations, coordinating conjunctions (**бо**, **але**, **і**) linking ideas with cause, contrast, and addition, subordinating conjunctions (**що**, **коли**, **де**) building complex thoughts, and the holiday greeting **З Різдвом!** to close.

## Підсумок — Summary

You have reached the end of the Communication phase. You can now address people properly in Ukrainian using the vocative case. You can ask people to do things, both politely and informally, using the imperative mood. You can connect your ideas into longer, natural sentences using conjunctions like **і**, **а**, **але**, and **бо**. You can build complex sentences with **що**, **де**, and **коли**. You can also talk about Ukrainian holidays and congratulate people accurately. 

You are no longer just making simple statements; you are participating in dynamic, two-way communication. Next is phase A1.8 — Past, Future, Graduation. In the final phase of this level, you will learn how to step out of the present tense and talk about events in the past and the future.
