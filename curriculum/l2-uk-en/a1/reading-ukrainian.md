## Склади — Syllables

Ukrainian reading starts not with individual letters, but with **склади** (syllables). Every Ukrainian first-grader learns one golden rule from the very first page of their буква́р. It comes from Большако́ва, Grade 1, page 25: «У сло́ві сті́льки складів, скі́льки голосни́х зву́ків.» In English: a word has as many syllables as it has vowel sounds. Count the vowels, count the syllables. This rule never breaks — not once, not ever.

Take the word **мама** (mother). Find the vowels: А, А — two vowels. That means two syllables: ма-ма. Now try **молоко** (milk). Find the vowels: О, О, О — three vowels, three syllables: мо-ло-ко. What about **банк** (bank)? One vowel: А. One syllable. That's the whole word in a single breath. Try a few more: **сон** (dream) — one vowel О, one syllable. **Оса** (wasp) — two vowels О, А, two syllables: о-са. **Ананас** (pineapple) — three vowels А, А, А, three syllables: а-на-нас. The rule works every single time.

So how do you actually read a new word you've never seen before? Follow the same method Ukrainian children learn — the звукови́й ана́ліз (sound analysis) from Большакова, page 29. Three steps. Step one: find the vowels — they are the cores of every syllable. Step two: split into syllables. In Ukrainian, consonants prefer to start new syllables rather than end old ones. This is called the open-syllable principle. So **молоко** splits as мо-ло-ко, not мол-ок-о. Step three: sound out each syllable, then blend them together at natural speed. Try it: **аптека** (pharmacy) → find А, Е, А → а-пте-ка. **Шоколад** (chocolate) → find О, О, А → шо-ко-лад.

<!-- INJECT_ACTIVITY: quiz-syllable-count -->

This method is exactly how Ukrainian first-graders learn to read. From here on, every time you meet a new Ukrainian word, the process is the same: find the vowels, split into syllables, read. Try the longest word yet: **університет** (university) → find У, І, Е, И, Е → у-ні-вер-си-тет. Five syllables. If you can split that, you can split anything.

## Голосні́ лі́тери — Vowel Letters

In the previous module, you learned that Ukrainian has 6 vowel sounds but 10 vowel letters. Now it's time to meet all 10 individually. First, the six simple vowels — each letter makes one consistent sound with no surprises: А, О, У, Е, И, І. Unlike English, where the letter "a" can sound completely different in "cat," "cake," and "father," each Ukrainian vowel letter always produces the same sound. А is always А. О is always О. Predictable, reliable, consistent.

Now for the four iotated vowels — letters that can represent TWO sounds. **Я** at the beginning of a word or after another vowel sounds like [й] + [а]. The word **яблуко** (apple) starts with Я, so you hear [йа]-блу-ко. In **моя** (my, feminine), Я comes after the vowel О, so again: мо-[йа]. But after a consonant, Я does something different — it softens that consonant and then sounds as [а]. In **пісня** (song), the Н before Я becomes soft. The same pattern applies to **Ю**: at a word's start, **юрта** (yurt) sounds like [йур]-та; after a consonant, Ю softens it plus [у]. **Є** works identically: **єнот** (raccoon) starts with [йе]. This pattern is consistent across words like **яблуко**, **маля**, **буряк**, and **юшка**.

Then there is **Ї** — a uniquely Ukrainian letter. No other Slavic language has it. Ї ALWAYS represents two sounds: [й] + [і]. It never softens a consonant. It only appears at the start of a word, after another vowel, or after an apostrophe. The rule is strict: бу́ква Ї за́вжди́ познача́є два зву́ки — [йі]. Examples: **їжак** (hedgehog), мої́ (my, plural), твої́ (your, plural), **лілії** (lilies).

<!-- INJECT_ACTIVITY: match-iotated-vowels -->

One more critical distinction: **И** versus **І**. These two sounds don't exist in English, and they are the number one challenge for learners. Mixing them up changes meanings completely. **Кит** (whale) versus **кіт** (cat). **Дим** (smoke) versus **дім** (house). **Лис** (fox) versus **ліс** (forest). **Бик** (bull) versus **бік** (side). Every pair is a completely different word. Listen carefully when you hear Ukrainian — your ear will adjust with practice, but awareness of the difference starts now.

## Чита́ння слів — Reading Words

When reading real Ukrainian words, the key strategy: don't read letter by letter. Read syllable by syllable. Start by finding the vowels, then build outward. Walk through an example: **книга** (book). Spot the vowels: И, А. Two vowels, two syllables: кни-га. Read it aloud. Another: **столиця** (capital city). Spot the vowels: О, И, Я. Three vowels, three syllables: сто-ли-ця. **Київ** — **столиця** України (Kyiv is the capital of Ukraine). With practice, you'll stop consciously splitting words — your eye will start grabbing whole syllables automatically, the way a fluent reader does.

Here are common word patterns for reading practice, organized from easiest to more challenging. Start with two-syllable words — these are your warm-up. Read each one aloud in under a second: **мама** (mother), **тато** (father), **каша** (porridge), **вода** (water), **рука** (hand), **хата** (house), **коза** (goat), **нога** (leg). Now three-syllable words: **школа** (school) — шко-ла (note how the ШК cluster stays together at the start of the syllable), **книга** (book) — кни-га, **парта** (desk) — пар-та. And one-syllable words: **дім** (house), **сон** (dream), **ліс** (forest), **дуб** (oak), **хліб** (bread), **банк** (bank). The more patterns your eye recognizes, the faster you read.

<!-- INJECT_ACTIVITY: quiz-read-choose-meaning -->

Three special letter combinations will appear as you read, and they're worth knowing now. **Щ** is always two sounds — [шч] — packed into one letter. Sound out **що** (what): [шчо]. Also: **ще** (still, yet), **щастя** (happiness). **Ь** (soft sign) has no sound of its own. It tells you to soften the consonant before it: **день** (day), **сіль** (salt), **кінь** (horse). And the apostrophe (') separates a consonant from an iotated vowel so the iotated vowel keeps its full two-sound value: **сім'я** (family) — the Я sounds as [йа], not as softening. Also: **м'ясо** (meat), **п'ять** (five). All three of these — Щ, Ь, and the apostrophe — will get their full spotlight in M03.

Now put it all together. Read these words using everything you've learned — find vowels, split into syllables, watch for special combinations. **Аптека** (pharmacy): а-пте-ка, 3 syllables. **Фотографія** (photography): фо-то-гра-фі-я, 5 syllables. **Бібліотека** (library): бі-блі-о-те-ка, 5 syllables. **Шоколад** (chocolate): шо-ко-лад, 3 syllables. **Університет** (university): у-ні-вер-си-тет, 5 syllables. If you can read **університет** syllable by syllable, you can read any Ukrainian word.

## Чита́ємо ра́зом — Reading Together

A progressive reading ladder — start simple, build confidence. Read each word aloud.

**Level 1** (2 syllables): **мама**, **тато**, **вода**, **рука**, **хата**, **каша**.

**Level 2** (3 syllables): **аптека**, **молоко**, **людина** (person), **вулиця** (street).

**Level 3** (4+ syllables): **університет**, **бібліотека**, **фотографія**.

Time yourself — can you read all 13 words in under 30 seconds? Speed comes from recognizing syllable patterns, not from rushing.

Now, your first Ukrainian text. Read it aloud, one sentence at a time. Every sentence uses **Це** (this is) plus a noun — no verbs, pure reading practice:

> Це Київ. Це столиця. Тут аптека і банк. Там школа. Що це? Це кафе́. А це по́шта.

Read it again, faster. You just read seven Ukrainian sentences. Every word follows the rules you learned: find vowels, split into syllables, read.

Challenge round — read these brand-new words you haven't seen before, using only the skills from this module. Don't look at the meanings yet: **зупинка**, **перехрестя**, **троянда**, **морозиво**, **черговий**. Apply the method: find vowels, count syllables, split, read aloud. **Зупинка** (bus stop): зу-пин-ка, 3 syllables. **Перехрестя** (crossroads): пе-ре-хре-стя, 4 syllables. **Троянда** (rose): тро-ян-да, 3 syllables. **Морозиво** (ice cream): мо-ро-зи-во, 4 syllables. **Черговий** (on duty): чер-го-вий, 3 syllables. Five brand-new words, read without any help.

## Підсумок — Summary

This module gave you three core skills for reading Ukrainian. First: the syllable rule — count the vowels to count the syllables. This is Большакова's golden rule, and it never fails. **Молоко** has three vowels, so three syllables. **Банк** has one vowel, so one syllable. Always. Second: the 10 vowel letters — six simple ones (А, О, У, Е, И, І) that each make one consistent sound, and four iotated ones (Я, Ю, Є, Ї) that can represent two sounds or soften a consonant. Remember that Ї is unique to Ukrainian and always sounds as [й] + [і]. Third: the reading strategy — find the vowels, split into syllables, read syllable by syllable, then blend into the full word at natural speed. These three skills let you read any Ukrainian word, no matter how long.

Self-check: How do you count syllables in a Ukrainian word? What are the 6 vowel sounds? Name the 4 iotated vowel letters. What does Ь do? What does the apostrophe do? Read this word: **бібліотека** — how many syllables? Five: бі-блі-о-те-ка. If you answered all of these, you're ready for M03: Special Signs, where Ь and the apostrophe get their full spotlight.
