## Діало́ги (Dialogues)

Imagine you are walking down the street in Kyiv on a busy Tuesday morning. You bump into a friend who is rushing in the opposite direction. In English, you would naturally ask, "Where are you going?" To ask this fundamental question in Ukrainian, you need a specific question word and a specific grammatical case to express motion toward a destination. 

Read the following conversation between two friends, Maksym and Anna, as they cross paths in the city center. Pay close attention to the endings of the places they mention.

<div class="dialogue">


**Макси́м:** Приві́т, А́нно! **Куди** ти йдеш? *(Hi, Anna! Where are you going?)*


**Анна:** Привіт! Я йду **в банк**. А ти? *(Hi! I am going to the bank. And you?)*


**Максим:** Я йду **на роботу**. *(I am going to work.)*


**Анна:** А по́тім? *(And then?)*


**Максим:** Потім іду́ **в магазин**. *(Then I am going to the store.)*


**Анна:** Зустрі́немося **в кафе**? *(Shall we meet in a cafe?)*


**Максим:** До́бре, до зу́стрічі! *(Good, see you!)*


</div>



In this short exchange, we see a crucial distinction in Ukrainian grammar. When Anna says she is heading toward her destination, she says **я йду в банк** (I am going to the bank). This indicates direction and movement. However, when she suggests a meeting spot, she says **зустрінемося в кафе** (shall we meet in a cafe). Here, **кафе** is a special word — it is indeclinable, meaning its form never changes regardless of the grammatical case. Whether you are going *to* a cafe or sitting *in* a cafe, the word **кафе** looks identical. For indeclinable nouns like this, only the context and the verb tell you whether the meaning is static or directional. 

Now, let us look at a second dialogue. This time, two colleagues are discussing their plans for the upcoming weekend. Notice how they talk about traveling to different cities.

<div class="dialogue">


**Олег:** **Куди** ти ї́деш у субо́ту? *(Where are you going on Saturday?)*


**Ірина:** Я ї́ду **у Львів**. *(I am traveling to Lviv.)*


**Олег:** А Оле́на? *(And Olena?)*


**Ірина:** Вона́ ї́де **в Одесу**. *(She is traveling to Odesa.)*


**Олег:** Га́рної доро́ги! *(Have a good trip!)*


</div>



When discussing travel to cities, the pattern remains exactly the same. You pair a verb of motion with a preposition and the destination city. Just as you go to a building, you travel to a city. The question word **куди** (where to) is the anchor for all these expressions of movement. It signals to the listener that you are asking about a destination, not a current location.

## Куди? Знахі́дний відмі́нок (Where To? Accusative)

In English, we change the preposition to show the difference between being somewhere and going somewhere. We say "I am *in* the bank" (location) but "I am going *to* the bank" (direction). 

Ukrainian approaches this differently. The prepositions often stay exactly the same—usually **в** or **у** (in/to) and sometimes **на** (on/to). Instead of changing the preposition, Ukrainian changes the ending of the noun. 

To express direction or motion toward a place, we use the Accusative case (**Знахідний відмінок**). 

In Ukrainian primary schools, fourth-grade students learn the cases using a special helper method. For the Accusative case, the helper word is **бачу** (I see), and the core questions are **кого?** (whom?) and **що?** (what?). When a word answers the question "what do I see?", it takes the Accusative form. 

By extension, when we move toward a destination, that destination is the "what" of our movement. **в/у** or **на** + Accusative case = WHERE TO (motion toward)

Compare this directly to what you already know about static locations. 
- **Де ти?** (Where are you?) — **Я в ба́нку.** (I am in the bank. -> Locative case, you ARE there, static position).
- **Куди ти йдеш?** (Where are you going?) — **Я йду в банк.** (I am going to the bank. -> Accusative case, you are GOING there, direction).

Forming the Accusative case for places follows a simple pattern. For many nouns, the word does not change at all from its dictionary form. Here are the rules by gender.

For masculine inanimate nouns (places, buildings, objects), the Accusative form is identical to the Nominative (dictionary) form. There is no change!
- **банк → у банк** (to the bank)
- **магазин → у магазин** (to the shop)
- **парк → у парк** (to the park)

For neuter nouns, the rule is the same. The Accusative form is identical to the Nominative form. No change is required.
- **кафе → у кафе** (to the cafe)
- **місто → у місто** (to the city)

The only time you need to change the ending for a destination is with feminine nouns ending in **-а** or **-я**. In the Accusative case, the **-а** changes to **-у**, and the **-я** changes to **-ю**. 
- **школа → у школу** (to school)
- **робота → на роботу** (to work)
- **бібліотека → у бібліотеку** (to the library)
- **Одеса → в Одесу** (to Odesa)

If you are heading to a restaurant for dinner, "restaurant" is masculine (**ресторан**), so it does not change: **ресторан → у ресторан** (to the restaurant). Remember: masculine and neuter destinations keep their dictionary form. Only feminine destinations shift their final vowel.

When the day is over and you are heading back, you use the verb **повертатися** (to return). If you are returning to your house or apartment, you use the special directional adverb **додому** (home / homeward).
- **Я поверта́юся додому.** (I am returning home.)

## Де чи куди? (Where or Where To?)

The absolute most important skill in this module is learning to distinguish between static position and directional movement. This distinction dictates everything else in the sentence. 

If someone calls you on the phone, they might ask about your current position or your destination. You must listen for the specific question word to understand what they are asking.

The key question pair is:
1. **Де ти?** (Where are you?) → This demands a location. You must use **в/у/на** + LOCATIVE case.
2. **Куди ти йдеш?** (Where are you going?) → This demands a direction. You must use **в/у/на** + ACCUSATIVE case.

Here is a paradigm table that clearly shows the difference between these two states for common places. Notice how the feminine nouns change in both columns, while the masculine nouns only change in the Locative column.

| Place (Nominative) | Де? (Locative - Static) | Куди? (Accusative - Motion) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **школа** | **у шко́лі** | **у школу** |
| **робота** | **на роботі** | **на роботу** |
| **банк** | **у банку** | **у банк** |
| **парк** | **у парку** | **у парк** |
| **Київ** | **у Ки́єві** | **у Київ** |
| **аптека** | **в аптеці** | **в аптеку** |

Now that we understand destinations, we must talk about the verbs that take us there. In English, the verb "to go" is universal. You go to the kitchen, you go to the store, you go to Paris. Ukrainian splits this concept into two distinct verbs based on your method of transportation. 

The two primary motion verbs are **йти** and **їхати**.

Use **йти** (to go) when the movement is on foot. This is for walking distances.
- **Я йду в магазин.** (I am going to the store [walking].)
- **Він іде в парк.** (He is going to the park [walking].)
- **Ми йдемо на роботу.** (We are going to work [walking].)

Use **їхати** (to go / to travel) when the movement involves any form of transportation—a car, a bus, a train, a bicycle, or a plane. This is for driving or riding distances.
- **Я їду на вокза́л.** (I am going to the train station [by transport].)
- **Вона їде в Київ.** (She is traveling to Kyiv [by transport].)
- **Вони ї́дуть в Одесу.** (They are traveling to Odesa [by transport].)

Both of these verbs express motion toward a goal, so both are always followed by **в/на** + Accusative case. The choice between them depends entirely on whether your feet are on the ground or you are sitting in a vehicle. Note: **йти** is the infinitive form of this verb. When conjugated, the stem changes: **я іду**, **він іде**, **ми йдемо**. You will see both **й-** and **і-** forms — they are the same verb in different grammatical forms.

## Підсумок — Summary

Mastering movement in Ukrainian is about pairing the right question with the right case. 

There are two fundamental questions that govern place and space:
- **Де?** (Where?) asks for a static location. It requires the Locative case (e.g., **в школі**, **на роботі**, **у банку**). 
- **Куди?** (Where to?) asks for a direction or destination. It requires the Accusative case (e.g., **у школу**, **на роботу**, **у банк**).

When answering **Куди?**, forming the Accusative case for destinations is straightforward. For masculine and neuter places, the Accusative is identical to the Nominative dictionary form; there is no change at all. For feminine places ending in **-а** or **-я**, the endings shift to **-у** and **-ю** respectively. Therefore, **школа** becomes **у школу**, and **бібліотека** becomes **у бібліотеку**.

Finally, always choose your motion verb based on your method of travel. Use **йти** when you are walking on foot, and use **їхати** when you are using any kind of transportation. Both verbs indicate movement, so both will always trigger the Accusative case for your destination. 

Self-check your understanding every time you form a sentence about a place: Are you asking where you are (**Де?**), or are you asking where you are going (**Куди?**)? Your answer to this simple question will guide you to the perfect Ukrainian sentence.
