## Зву́ки і лі́тери (Sounds and Letters)

Look at the text on this page. What you are seeing are letters. Now, say a word out loud. What you just produced is a sound. This distinction is the absolute foundation of the Ukrainian language. There is a golden rule taught to every Ukrainian student in the first grade, which we will adopt right now. The rule is: **Ми чу́ємо і вимовля́ємо звуки, а ба́чимо і пи́шемо літери**. This translates to: We hear and pronounce sounds, but we see and write letters. Letters are simply the physical or digital symbols that we use to represent the spoken language on a page. Sounds are the actual physical vibrations you make with your vocal cords, tongue, lips, and breath.

In the Ukrainian alphabet, there are thirty-three letters. However, the language actually has thirty-eight distinct sounds. Why is there a difference? Because sometimes one letter can represent two sounds, and sometimes a letter has no sound of its own at all, acting only as a modifier for the letters around it.

All of these sounds live in two large families. The first family is the vowels, known in Ukrainian as **голосні́**. The word **голосні** comes from the word for voice. These sounds are created using only your voice. Your mouth is open, and the air flows out freely without hitting any obstruction like your teeth or lips. There is a famous poem used in Ukrainian primary schools that goes: "Голосні почу́єш в пісні́." This means you will hear vowels in a song. They are the musical heart of the language. There are exactly six vowel sounds in Ukrainian, but we use ten different vowel letters to write them down: **А**, **О**, **У**, **Е**, **И**, **І**, **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, **Ї**.

The second family is the consonants, called **при́голосні**. These sounds are made with your voice combined with some kind of noise, or sometimes just noise alone. To make a consonant sound, you must create a physical obstruction in your mouth using your lips, your teeth, or your tongue. The air cannot just flow freely; it has to push past a barrier. There are thirty-two consonant sounds in total. As the school poem continues: "Приголосні деренча́ть, і тихе́нько шелестя́ть." Consonants rattle and quietly rustle. They give the language its structure and rhythm.

## Пе́рші слова́ (First Words)

Learning the Cyrillic alphabet can feel like a daunting task, but it is actually much more logical than English spelling. To make it easier, we break the alphabet down into manageable groups. We start with the friendliest letters: the ones that look exactly like the letters you already know and make the exact same sounds. 

These friendly letters are **А**, **О**, **К**, **М**, and **Т**. Because they are so familiar, you can start reading real Ukrainian words immediately. Look at the word **ма́ма**. It means mother, and you already know how to read it. Now look at **та́то**. It means father. You can also read **ко́ма** (comma), **а́том** (atom), **мак** (poppy), and **о́ко** (eye). There is one small detail to remember. The Ukrainian letter **Т** is dental. This means you must touch the tip of your tongue directly to the back of your upper front teeth when you say it, not to the gum ridge above them. Also, the letters **К** and **Т** are unaspirated, meaning you should not let out a puff of air when you say them. But if you use your English pronunciation for now, you will be understood.

Next, we encounter the false friends. This is the biggest trap for English speakers. These letters look like familiar Latin letters, but they make completely different sounds. You must retrain your brain not to trust your eyes. 
The letter **В** makes a "v" sound, not a "b". 
The letter **Н** makes an "n" sound, not an "h". 
The letter **Р** makes a rolled "r" sound, not a "p". 
The letter **С** makes an "s" sound, not a "c" or "k". 
The letter **У** makes an "oo" sound, not a "y". 
The letter **Х** makes a guttural sound from the back of your throat, similar to the Scottish word "loch". It does not sound like an "x".

Let us practice reading some words with these false friends. Read the word **вода́**. It means water. Now try the word **рука́**. It means hand. Notice how the first letter is a rolled sound. Look at **сон**, which means dream. Try reading **ніс**, meaning nose, and **ха́та**, meaning house. You have to actively suppress your English reading habits to get these right.

Finally, we have the new shapes. These are letters that do not look like anything in the Latin alphabet. They are **Б**, **Г**, **Ґ**, **Д**, **Ж**, **З**, **И**, **Й**, **Л**, **П**, **Ф**, **Ц**, **Ч**, **Ш**, **Щ**. You will learn these gradually, but let us look at a few examples right now. The word **банк** means bank. The word **дім** means home. The word **зима́** means winter. You can read the word **кни́га**, which means book, and **шко́ла**, which means school.

There are also a few special characters. The letter **Щ** always makes two distinct sounds fused together. The soft sign **Ь** is completely silent; it has no sound of its own. Its only job is to soften the consonant that comes right before it. The letter **Ї** is completely unique to the Ukrainian language. It always makes two sounds, and it never softens the consonant before it. The letters **Я**, **Ю**, and **Є** are flexible. They can either make two sounds, or they can soften a preceding consonant. We will explore these special rules in the next module, but for now, it is enough just to recognize their shapes.

## Приві́т! (Hello!)

Now that you understand the basic sounds and letters, it is time for your first actual conversation in Ukrainian. We will follow a simple exchange that you might hear anywhere in Ukraine. 

When you meet a friend, a family member, or a peer, the most common informal greeting is **Привіт!** This means "Hi!" It is warm, friendly, and casual. Do not use this with a police officer or a professor, but use it freely with people your own age or those you know well.

After you say hi, the natural next step is to ask how the person is doing. You say: **Як спра́ви?** This means "How are you?" or "How are things?" 
There are a few common ways to answer this question. If everything is fine, you say **До́бре**. This means "fine" or "good". If you are having a fantastic day, you can say **Чудо́во**, which means "great" or "wonderful". If things are just okay, neither exceptionally good nor bad, you can say **Норма́льно**. 

Once you have answered, it is polite to return the question. You simply say: **А у тебе́?** This means "And you?" or more literally, "And with you?"

Let us look at another phrase you might hear when you meet someone. If a woman is speaking to you, she might say **Ра́да тебе ба́чити!** This means "Glad to see you!" If a man is speaking to you, he will say **Ра́дий тебе бачити!** 
Take close note of this difference. Ukrainian has gendered forms for many words. The ending of the word changes depending on who is speaking. Women use the form **рада**, and men use the form **радий**. This is your very first encounter with grammatical gender in Ukrainian. It is a fundamental part of how the language works, and it will become a major topic of study starting in later modules. For now, just remember that the words you use can reflect who you are.

Let us practice your reading skills using your new vocabulary. Look at the word **привіт** again. Read each letter individually, then blend them into syllables: П-ри-віт! 
This single word is an excellent test because it uses letters from every group we discussed. 
The first letter **П** is a new shape. 
The second letter **р** is a dangerous false friend. You must roll it. 
The third letter **и** is a new shape representing a unique vowel sound. 
The fourth letter **в** is another false friend. 
The fifth letter **і** is a vowel. 
The final letter **т** is a familiar, friendly letter. 
Take your time sounding it out. Every word you conquer is a victory.

## Чита́ємо (Reading Practice)

The best way to solidify your knowledge of the Cyrillic alphabet is through environmental reading. Imagine you are walking down a street in Kyiv. You need to be able to navigate the city, find places to eat, and handle your daily life. You will see large signs everywhere, and many of these words are very similar to English words. You just need to decode the letters.

Let us practice with some common signs you will see in Ukraine. 
Look at the word **апте́ка**. Sound out each letter slowly. It means pharmacy. 
Here is another one: **банк**. This one is easy to guess. It means bank. 
You might want to stop for coffee at a **кафе́**. This means cafe. 
If you need to travel across the city, you will look for the **метро́**, which is the metro or subway system. 
To send a postcard, you must find the **по́шта**, the post office. 
You will see children walking to **школа**, the school. 
And if you are waiting for a bus, you will stand at a **зупи́нка**, which means bus stop.
The key to reading these words is not to rush. Sound out each letter one by one. Blend the letters into syllables, and then read the whole word smoothly.

We can also practice reading by looking at a map of Ukraine. The names of Ukrainian cities are excellent reading practice. 
Try to read the capital city: **Ки́їв**. 
Now read the name of the beautiful western city: **Львів**. 
Head south to the sea and read: **Оде́са**. 
Look to the east and read: **Харків**. 
Read the name of the city on the great river: **Дніпро́**. 
And finally, try reading: **Полта́ва**. 

Now that you can read individual words, let us build your very first sentences. In Ukrainian, you can say "this is" simply by using the word **Це**. You do not need a verb like "is" in these basic sentences.
Look at this sentence: **Це мама**. It means "This is mother."
Try this one: **Це банк**. It means "This is a bank."
And this one: **Це Київ**. It means "This is Kyiv."
We can also ask questions. To ask "What is this?", you say **Що це?** You use this for objects and places.
To ask "Who is this?", you say **Хто це?** You use this for people. 
These are your first steps into Ukrainian sentence structure. You are no longer just making sounds; you are communicating ideas.

## Підсумок — Summary

You have taken your first major steps into the Ukrainian language. Let us perform a quick self-check to make sure you have absorbed the core concepts of this module. 
Do you remember how many letters are in the Ukrainian alphabet? There are thirty-three. And how many sounds are there? There are thirty-eight. You should now understand why this difference exists: we hear and pronounce sounds, but we see and write letters.
Can you define what **голосні** are? They are vowel sounds, made with your voice flowing freely. What are **приголосні**? They are consonant sounds, made by creating an obstruction with your lips, teeth, or tongue.
Do you know what **Привіт** means? It is an informal way to say "Hi!" How do you respond if someone asks you **Як справи?** You can say **Добре**, **Чудово**, or **Нормально**. 
If you can answer these questions confidently, you are fully prepared to move forward. The Cyrillic alphabet will quickly become second nature to you as long as you keep practicing and reading environmental signs out loud.
