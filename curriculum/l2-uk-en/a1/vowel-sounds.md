## Вступ — Introduction

Welcome back! In Module 1, you mapped out the entire Ukrainian alphabet — all 33 letters — and practiced reading 10 of them. You also learned your first 20 Ukrainian words, from **ма́ма** to **кіно́**. That was your alphabet map. Today, you're going deeper into the engine room: the **vowel system**.

Why do vowels matter so much? Here's a simple rule that works every time: **every Ukrainian syllable has exactly one vowel**. Count the vowels in a word, and you know how many syllables it has. The word **мо-ло-ко́** has three vowels — three syllables. The word **кіт** has one vowel — one syllable. This rule never breaks.

Ukrainian has **10 vowel letters** that represent **6 vowel sounds**. By the end of this module, you'll know every one of them, and you'll understand why Ukrainian vowels behave so differently from English ones.

Let's start with the six base vowels — the foundation of every word you'll ever read.

## Шість основних голосних — Six Base Vowels

Ukrainian has six core vowel sounds: **А**, **О**, **У**, **Е**, **И**, **І**. Each one is clear, stable, and never blurs — no matter where it sits in a word. This is one of the best things about Ukrainian pronunciation: what you see is what you say.

<!-- adapted from: Bolshakova, Grade 1, p. 24 — vowel/consonant classification -->

### Літера А

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — А](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hvB3VpcR3ZE)

**А** sounds like the «a» in «father» — open and relaxed. It never changes its quality, no matter where it appears in a word.

You already know **ма́ма** (mom) and **са́ло** (lard) from Module 1. Now add a new word to your kitchen vocabulary: **ка́ша** (porridge) — a daily staple in Ukrainian homes. 

- **ма́ма** — mom *(M1 review)*
- **ка́ша** — porridge
- **са́ло** — lard *(M1 review)*
- **мак** — poppy *(M1 review)*

> [!tip] **Quick win!** You can already read four А-words. Say them out loud — your А should sound the same in every one.

### Літера О

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — О](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gJFxRIPRZbI)

**О** is rounded, like the «o» in «more». The key rule: О stays О even when it's not stressed. This is different from English, where unstressed vowels often turn into a lazy «uh» sound, and unlike Russian, where an unstressed О reduces to an «a» sound.

Here is your first big test: the word **село́** (village). The stress falls on the last syllable, but that first О-like letter — wait, that's an **Е**! And it stays a clear Е, not «uh». The same logic applies to every О in **молоко́** (milk) — all three О sounds are identical.

- **о́ко** — eye *(M1 review)*
- **молоко́** — milk *(M1 review)*
- **село́** — village

### Літера У

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — У](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VB1O6PmtYRU)

**У** sounds like «oo» in «moon». Round your lips firmly.

- **тут** — here *(M1 review)*
- **лук** — bow *(M1 review)*
- **ву́хо** — ear

Your body has two **ву́ха** (ears) — and now you have a word for them! Notice how the У is clear and round, just like the «oo» in «moon».

### Літера Е

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — Е](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KFlsroBW0dk)

**Е** sounds like the «e» in «set». Be careful — this is NOT the «ee» sound. Many English speakers read Ukrainian Е as «ee» by reflex. Fight that urge.

*(Note: You will see some new consonants below like Д, В, and Р. Don't worry about them yet — just focus on reading the vowel sounds.)*

- **не́бо** — sky
- **село́** — village
- **день** — day

Notice **день** (day) — the Е is short and clear, like in «set». You'll use this word every day of your Ukrainian journey.

> [!warning] **Common mistake:** Don't pronounce Ukrainian **Е** like English «ee». It's always the short «e» of «set».

### Літера И

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — И](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=W-1rCu0indE)

Now for something uniquely Ukrainian. **И** has no exact match in English. Your jaw should be relaxed, your tongue sitting lower than for І. Think of the «i» in «bit», but with a more open, relaxed mouth.

- **ри́ба** — fish
- **сир** — cheese
- **син** — son

The word **ри́ба** (fish) is your anchor word for И. Say it slowly — feel how your jaw drops slightly compared to the «ee» sound.

### Літера І

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — І](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z9TH0H4ShGo)

**І** is brighter and higher — like «ee» in «see». Your tongue rises up, your lips spread slightly.

- **ліс** — forest *(M1 review)*
- **кіт** — cat *(M1 review)*
- **сік** — juice *(M1 review)*

### И vs І — The Hardest Contrast

This is the single most important vowel distinction for English speakers learning Ukrainian. Both И and І might sound like «ee» to your ear at first. But they are different sounds, and Ukrainians hear the difference instantly.

Try this minimal pair:

- **кит** — whale (И: jaw relaxed, tongue lower)
- **кіт** — cat (І: tongue high, like «see»)

The consonants are the same. Only the vowel changes — and the meaning changes completely.

> [!practice] **Jaw test:** Put your hand under your chin. Say **кит** — your jaw drops slightly. Say **кіт** — your jaw stays higher. Feel that difference? That's И vs І.

## Наголос — Word Stress

Every Ukrainian word has **one stressed syllable**. The stressed vowel is a little louder and a little longer. But here's the golden rule — the most important pronunciation rule in this entire module:

> [!tip] **Golden Rule:** Ukrainian vowels stay pure in any position — stressed or unstressed. Never swallow them into «uh».

English speakers have a deeply ingrained habit: in English, unstressed vowels collapse into a vague «uh» sound (called schwa). Think of how you say «photograph» — the first «o» becomes «uh». Or «banana» — the first «a» becomes «uh».

Ukrainian does NOT do this. Ever.

Take **молоко́** (milk). The stress falls on the last syllable: мо-ло-**ко́**. But all three О sounds are exactly the same quality. You don't say «muh-luh-KO» — you say «mo-lo-KO», with three clean О sounds.

More examples:

- **ка́ша** — stress on first syllable: **КА́**-ша. Both vowels are pure.
- **село́** — stress on last syllable: се-**ЛО́**. The unstressed Е stays a clear Е.
- **я́блуко** — stress on first syllable: **Я́**-блу-ко. The unstressed О at the end stays О.

> [!warning] **Fight the schwa!** Every time you want to pronounce an unstressed vowel as «uh», stop and give it its full, pure sound. This takes practice, but it's the single biggest improvement you can make to your Ukrainian pronunciation.

## Йотовані голосні — Iotated Vowels

You've mastered the six base vowels. Now meet the four «double-duty» vowels: **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, **Ї**. These letters are special because they can represent **two sounds** packed into one letter.

The rule is simple: at the **start of a word** or **after another vowel**, these letters represent the sound **Й** plus a base vowel. Think of them as a consonant and a vowel working together.

<!-- adapted from: Kravtsova, Grade 2, p. 10 — iotated vowel discovery exercise -->

### Літера Я

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — Я](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yhSAf41LX8I)

**Я** = **Й + А** (when at word start or after a vowel).

- At word start: **я́блуко** (apple) — you hear «й-а» at the beginning
- After a vowel: **моя́** (my, feminine) — the Я comes after О, so it's two sounds: мо-**йа́**

The word **я́блуко** (apple) is your anchor word for Я. Picture a bright red apple — that's your Я.

After a consonant, Я works differently: it softens the consonant instead of adding a Й sound. Compare **ма́ти** (mother) with **м'я́ти** (to crumple) — the apostrophe in **м'я́ти** tells you the Й sound is preserved. But don't worry about this detail now — we'll cover it when we study consonants.

**Яйце́** (egg) is another everyday Я-word. You'll find **я́йця** (eggs) on every Ukrainian breakfast table next to the **ка́ша**.

### Літера Ю

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — Ю](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9JdIBYCTWGw)

**Ю** = **Й + У** (at word start or after a vowel).

- At word start: **юна́к** (young man) — you hear «й-у» at the beginning
- At word start: **ю́шка** (broth/soup) — a warm bowl of **ю́шка** on a cold day!

After a consonant, Ю softens it. In the word **лю́ди** (people), the Л becomes soft. You'll explore soft consonants in Module 3.

### Літера Є

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — Є](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O0bwRyyBQSc)

The letter **Є** brings together the sounds **Й** and **Е**. 

Look at the word **Євро́па** (Europe) — it starts with this clean combination. When it follows another vowel, like in the word **моє́** (my, neuter), it keeps those two sounds distinct: мо-**йе́**. This is a great word to practice because it directly contrasts with the feminine form **моя́** that you saw earlier.

### Літера Ї

📺 [Anna Ohoiko — Ukrainian Lessons — Ї](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UcjdjQXhAY8)

**Ї** is the most Ukrainian of all vowel letters. It **always** represents two sounds: **Й + І**. Unlike Я, Ю, and Є, it never softens a preceding consonant. It always keeps its double nature.

- **ї́жак** — hedgehog
- **ї́жа** — food
- **Украї́на** — Ukraine

> [!cultural] **The letter Ї** exists only in the Ukrainian alphabet — no other Slavic language has it. Some Ukrainians wear Ї on jewelry or tattoos as a symbol of their language and identity. When you write Ї, you're writing something uniquely Ukrainian.

### Літера Й

**Й** is the semi-vowel itself — the short «y» sound (like the «y» in «yes»). It never forms a syllable on its own. You'll find it at the end of words and between vowels.

- **край** — edge, land
- **йо́гурт** — yogurt
- **мій** — my (masculine)

The word **край** (land, edge) carries a poetic weight in Ukrainian — it can mean your homeland, your native land. And that Й at the end? Smooth and short, like a gentle closing.

> [!note] **Counting trick:** Й is not a full vowel, so it doesn't create its own syllable. The word **край** has one vowel (А) = one syllable, even though Й is there too.

## Голосні в словах — Vowels in Words

Time to put everything together! Let's practice reading words organized by their vowel focus. Don't worry about any unfamiliar consonants — just focus on the vowel sounds.

**А-words:** ма́ма, ка́ша, мак, са́ло

**О-words:** о́ко, молоко́, село́

**У-words:** тут, суп, ву́хо

**Е-words:** не́бо, день, село́

**И-words:** ри́ба, сир, син

**І-words:** ліс, кіт, сік

**Я-words:** я́блуко, яйце́, моя́

**Ю-words:** юна́к, ю́шка

**Є-words:** Євро́па, моє́

**Ї-words:** ї́жак, ї́жа, Украї́на

Now try reading these short sentences:

> Це я́блуко.

> Це моє́ село́.

> Ма́ма ка́же «так».

> Де мій кіт?

> Ї́жак тут.

> [!practice] **Count the vowels!** Remember — one vowel = one syllable.
- **мо-ло-ко́** → 3 vowels = 3 syllables
- **У-кра-ї́-на** → 4 vowels = 4 syllables (Ї counts as one syllable because it contains the vowel І)
- **кіт** → 1 vowel = 1 syllable
- **я́-блу-ко** → 3 vowels = 3 syllables
- **ї́-жак** → 2 vowels = 2 syllables

Here are a few micro-dialogues to practice. Read them aloud!

> — Це ка́ша?
> — Так, це ка́ша.

> — Де моя́ ри́ба?
> — Ри́ба тут.

> — Це моє́ яйце́?
> — Так, моє́.

> — Мій сир тут?
> — Ні, сир там.

## Підсумок — Summary

You've just learned the complete Ukrainian vowel system! Here's what you now know:

**10 vowel letters:** 6 base (**А О У Е И І**) + 4 iotated (**Я Ю Є Ї**) + the semi-vowel **Й**.

**The Golden Rule:** Ukrainian vowels stay pure — never swallow or reduce them, no matter where the stress falls. This is the single most important pronunciation habit to build.

**Self-check** — can you answer these?

1. Can you pronounce all 6 base vowels? Say them: А, О, У, Е, И, І.
2. What two sounds does **Я** make at the start of a word? *(Answer: Й + А)*
3. What is the difference between **И** and **І**? *(Answer: И has a lower, more relaxed jaw; І is higher, like «ee»)*
4. Which iotated vowel ALWAYS represents two sounds? *(Answer: Ї)*
5. How many syllables does **молоко́** have? *(Answer: 3 — count the vowels!)*

Great work! You've built a solid foundation. In Module 3, you'll master the consonant system — sonorant consonants like Л, М, Н, Р, В, voiced/voiceless pairs, and hard vs soft consonants that give Ukrainian its distinctive sound.
