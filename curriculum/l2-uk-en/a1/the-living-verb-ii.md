# The Living Verb II

## Warm-up

Welcome back, neighbor! Today is a beautiful day to learn something new. Today, we are opening a new door in our language journey together. So far, we have met many useful words. But today we focus on the engine of the sentence: the **–ī—Ė—Ē—Ā–Ľ–ĺ–≤–ĺ** (verb) [d ≤ijeňąsl…Ē č…Ē]. In our previous lessons, we looked at the first group of verbs, like **—á–ł—ā–į—ā–ł** (to read) [tÕ° É…™ňątat…™]. We call them **–ü–Ķ—Ä—ą–į –ī—Ė—Ē–≤—Ė–ī–ľ—Ė–Ĺ–į** (First Conjugation) [ňąp…õr É…ź d ≤ijeňą čidm ≤in…ź].

Now, it is time to meet their cousins in the **–Ē—Ä—É–≥–į –ī—Ė—Ē–≤—Ė–ī–ľ—Ė–Ĺ–į** (Second Conjugation) [ňądru…¶…ź d ≤ijeňą čidm ≤in…ź]. You can think of them as the "–ł-family". The letter **–ł** appears everywhere! These verbs are essential for your daily life. They help us describe how we **–≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł—ā–ł** (to speak) […¶…Ē č…Ēňąr…™t…™]. They help us describe what we **–Ī–į—á–ł—ā–ł** (to see) [ňąbatÕ° É…™t…™]. They even help us describe who we **–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł** (to love) [l ≤uňąb…™t…™]. **–¶–Ķ –ī—É–∂–Ķ –≤–į–∂–Ľ–ł–≤–į —ā–Ķ–ľ–į.** (This is a very important topic) [tÕ°s…õ ňądu í…õ  č…ź íňąl…™ č…ź ňąt…õm…ź].

### Aspect Overview: Ongoing Actions
Before we look at the patterns, let‚Äôs talk about how these verbs behave in time. In Ukrainian, every action has a specific aspect. The verbs we are studying today belong to the **–Ĺ–Ķ–ī–ĺ–ļ–ĺ–Ĺ–į–Ĺ–ł–Ļ –≤–ł–ī** (imperfective aspect) [n…õd…Ēňąk…Ēnan…™j  č…™d]. This is a very friendly aspect for beginners! This simply means the action is currently happening, is a habit, or is not finished yet.

When you speak in the present tense, you are using this aspect. Use it to tell your neighbor what you do every day or what you are doing right now.

_–ü—Ä–ł–ļ–Ľ–į–ī:_
**–ú–ł –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł–ľ–ĺ –Ņ–ĺ–≤—Ė–Ľ—Ć–Ĺ–ĺ.** [m…™ …¶…Ēňą č…Ēr…™m…Ē p…Ēňą čil ≤n…Ē] ‚ÄĒ We speak slowly.

## Presentation

How do you know the family of a verb? The secret is usually in the vowel of the endings. Class I verbs usually have the letter **–Ķ** or **—Ē**. Class II verbs prefer the letter **–ł**. Let‚Äôs look at a side-by-side comparison of **—á–ł—ā–į—ā–ł** [tÕ° É…™ňątat…™] and **–≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł—ā–ł** […¶…Ē č…Ēňąr…™t…™].

| Person | Class I (—á–ł—ā–į—ā–ł) | Class II (–≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł—ā–ł) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **–Į** (I) | —á–ł—ā–į**—é** | –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä**—é** |
| **–Ę–ł** (You) | —á–ł—ā–į**—Ē—ą** | –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä**–ł—ą** |
| **–í—Ė–Ĺ / –í–ĺ–Ĺ–į** (He / She) | —á–ł—ā–į**—Ē** | –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä**–ł—ā—Ć** |
| **–ú–ł** (We) | —á–ł—ā–į**—Ē–ľ–ĺ** | –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä**–ł–ľ–ĺ** |
| **–í–ł** (You pl.) | —á–ł—ā–į**—Ē—ā–Ķ** | –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä**–ł—ā–Ķ** |
| **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ł** (They) | —á–ł—ā–į**—é—ā—Ć** | –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä**—Ź—ā—Ć** |

Notice the clear difference. Class II keeps the **–ł** sound strong. This vowel harmony is a great guide for your ears. It helps you practice speaking.

> [!tip]
> Think of Class II as the "High Tongue" family. The vowel **–ł** requires you to lift your tongue high. Whenever you see a verb ending in **-–ł—ā–ł** in the dictionary, prepare your tongue!

### The Present Tense Paradigm
To build these verbs, we start with the **–Ü–Ĺ—Ą—Ė–Ĺ—Ė—ā–ł–≤** (infinitive) [inf ≤iniňąt…™ č]. This is the basic form ending in **-—ā–ł**. To conjugate it, we remove the **-—ā–ł** to find the root. Then, we add our new neighborly endings. Let's practice with the verb **–Ī–į—á–ł—ā–ł** (to see) [ňąbatÕ° É…™t…™].

- **–Į –Ī–į—á—É** [ňąbatÕ° Éu] ‚ÄĒ I see. (We use **-—É** here after the **—á**).
- **–Ę–ł –Ī–į—á–ł—ą** [ňąbatÕ° É…™ É] ‚ÄĒ You see.
- **–í—Ė–Ĺ / –í–ĺ–Ĺ–į –Ī–į—á–ł—ā—Ć** [ňąbatÕ° É…™t ≤] ‚ÄĒ He / She sees.
- **–ú–ł –Ī–į—á–ł–ľ–ĺ** [ňąbatÕ° É…™m…Ē] ‚ÄĒ We see.
- **–í–ł –Ī–į—á–ł—ā–Ķ** [ňąbatÕ° É…™t…õ] ‚ÄĒ You see.
- **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ł –Ī–į—á–į—ā—Ć** [ňąbatÕ° Éat ≤] ‚ÄĒ They see.

These endings (**-—É/—é, -–ł—ą, -–ł—ā—Ć, -–ł–ľ–ĺ, -–ł—ā–Ķ, -–į—ā—Ć/—Ź—ā—Ć**) are the keys to clear communication. They tell everyone who is doing the action.

_–ü—Ä–ł–ļ–Ľ–į–ī:_
**–Ę–ł –Ī–į—á–ł—ą –Ņ–į—Ä–ļ?** [t…™ ňąbatÕ° É…™ É park] ‚ÄĒ Do you see the park?

### Consonant Mutations: The 1st Person Shift
Sometimes, a verb changes its face a little bit when you talk about yourself! This is called **—á–Ķ—Ä–≥—É–≤–į–Ĺ–Ĺ—Ź –Ņ—Ä–ł–≥–ĺ–Ľ–ĺ—Ā–Ĺ–ł—Ö** (consonant mutation) [tÕ° É…õr…¶uňą čan ≤n ≤…ź ňąpr…™…¶…Ēl…Ēsn…™x]. In Class II, this only happens in the **–Į** form. For all other people, the verb is normal.

Here are common shifts:
- **–ī** becomes **–ī–∂** [dÕ° í]: **—Ā–ł–ī—Ė—ā–ł** (to sit) [s…™ňąd ≤it…™] ‚Üí **–Į —Ā–ł–ī–∂—É** [s…™ňądÕ° íu].
- **—ā** becomes **—á** [tÕ° É]: **–Ņ–Ľ–į—ā–ł—ā–ł** (to pay) [pl…źňąt…™t…™] ‚Üí **–Į –Ņ–Ľ–į—á—É** [pl…źňątÕ° Éu].
- **—Ā** becomes **—ą** [ É]: **–Ņ—Ä–ĺ—Ā–ł—ā–ł** (to ask) [pr…Ēňąs…™t…™] ‚Üí **–Į –Ņ—Ä–ĺ—ą—É** [ňąpr…Ē Éu].

These shifts help the words flow naturally. This small change helps you sound like a native speaker!

_–ü—Ä–ł–ļ–Ľ–į–ī:_
**–Į —Ā–ł–ī–∂—É —ā—É—ā.** [j…ź s…™ňądÕ° íu tut] ‚ÄĒ I am sitting here.

### The Labial L: A Helping Sound
When a verb root ends in a sound made with your lips, we add a letter **–Ľ** [l ≤] in the **–Į** form. We call these "labial" sounds. These consonants are **–Ī, –Ņ, –≤, –ľ, —Ą**. If your verb root ends in one of these, add the **–Ľ**!

Look at these very common verbs:
- **–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł** (to love) [l ≤uňąb…™t…™] ‚Üí **–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é** [l ≤uňąbl ≤u].
- **—Ä–ĺ–Ī–ł—ā–ł** (to do) [r…Ēňąb…™t…™] ‚Üí **–Į —Ä–ĺ–Ī–Ľ—é** [ňąr…Ēbl ≤u].

This extra letter only appears when you say "I". For everyone else, the **–Ľ** is not there.

> [!warning]
> Don't forget the **–Ľ**! A common mistake is to say **—Ź —Ä–ĺ–Ī—é**. Always remember to add that smooth **–Ľ** sound after the lip consonants in the **–Į** form.

### Error Prevention: The Conjugation Trap
The biggest trap is using the **-–į—ā–ł** endings for everything. We often learn Class I first. It feels like the rule. But remember, a verb like **–≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł—ā–ł** follows its own path!

If you say **—ā–ł –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–Ķ—ą** instead of **—ā–ł –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł—ą**, it sounds clumsy. Imagine putting a hat on your foot. Check the **–Ü–Ĺ—Ą—Ė–Ĺ—Ė—ā–ł–≤** in your mind. If it ends in **-–ł—ā–ł**, you are in the "–ł-family". You will be a pro in no time!

_–ü—Ä–ł–ļ–Ľ–į–ī:_
**–í–ł –≤—á–ł—ā–Ķ –ľ–ĺ–≤—É.** [ č…™ ňą čtÕ° É…™t…õ ňąm…Ē ču] ‚ÄĒ You are learning the language.

## Practice

Now you can tell the families apart. Let's see how these verbs behave in real life.

### Identifying the Conjugation Families
Think of verbs as neighbors. Some belong to the **–ü–Ķ—Ä—ą–į –ī—Ė—Ē–≤—Ė–ī–ľ—Ė–Ĺ–į** family. Others are part of the **–Ē—Ä—É–≥–į –ī—Ė—Ē–≤—Ė–ī–ľ—Ė–Ĺ–į**. To help them, look at the **—Ė–Ĺ—Ą—Ė–Ĺ—Ė—ā–ł–≤** ending. If a verb ends in **-–į—ā–ł**, it is group one. If it ends in **-–ł—ā–ł**, it is our family.

_–ü—Ä–ł–ļ–Ľ–į–ī–ł:_
**–Į —Ä–ĺ–Ī–Ľ—é.** [ja r…Ēňąbl ≤u] ‚ÄĒ I am doing.
**–Ę–ł –Ī–į—á–ł—ą.** [t…™ ňąbatÕ° É…™ É] ‚ÄĒ You see.
**–í—Ė–Ĺ —Ö–ĺ–ī–ł—ā—Ć.** [ čin ňąx…Ēd…™t ≤] ‚ÄĒ He walks.

[!observe]
Look at the verb **—Ä–ĺ–Ī–ł—ā–ł** (to do). Can you see the **-–ł-** in almost every form? This is the signature of our new family!

### High-Frequency Verb Pairings
In Ukrainian, some words are best friends. These are collocations. For example, you say **–≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł—ā–ł –Ņ–ĺ–≤—Ė–Ľ—Ć–Ĺ–ĺ** (to speak slowly). Another pair is **—Ä–ĺ–Ī–ł—ā–ł –Ņ–ĺ–ľ–ł–Ľ–ļ–ł** (to make mistakes). Don't worry, everyone makes them!

_–ü—Ä–ł–ļ–Ľ–į–ī–ł:_
**–ú–ł –≥–ĺ–≤–ĺ—Ä–ł–ľ–ĺ –Ī–į–≥–į—ā–ĺ.** [m…™ …¶…Ēňą č…Ēr…™m…Ē b…źňą…¶at…Ē] ‚ÄĒ We speak a lot.
**–Į —Ä–ĺ–Ī–Ľ—é —Ą–ĺ—ā–ĺ.** [ja r…Ēňąbl ≤u ňąf…Ēt…Ē] ‚ÄĒ I am taking a photo.
**–í–ĺ–Ĺ–į –Ņ–Ľ–į—ā–ł—ā—Ć –≥—Ä–ĺ—ą—Ė.** [ č…Ēňąna pl…źňąt…™t ≤ ňą…¶r…Ē Éi] ‚ÄĒ She is paying money.

## Production

The third person forms are he, she, it, and they. In the **–Ē—Ä—É–≥–į –ī—Ė—Ē–≤—Ė–ī–ľ—Ė–Ĺ–į**, the singular ending is usually **-–ł—ā—Ć**. When you move to the plural "they", it changes to **-—Ź—ā—Ć**. It is a small shift. It helps your listener.

| Singular (–í—Ė–Ĺ/–í–ĺ–Ĺ–į) | Plural (–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ł) | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **–í—Ė–Ĺ –Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā—Ć** [ čin ňąl ≤ub…™t ≤] | **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ł –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—Ź—ā—Ć** [ č…Ēňąn…™ ňąl ≤ubl ≤at ≤] | He loves / They love |
| **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–į –Ī–į—á–ł—ā—Ć** [ č…Ēňąna ňąbatÕ° É…™t ≤] | **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ł –Ī–į—á–į—ā—Ć** [ č…Ēňąn…™ ňąbatÕ° Éat ≤] | She sees / They see |
| **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ĺ —Ā—ā–ĺ—ó—ā—Ć** [ č…Ēňąn…Ē st…Ēňąjit ≤] | **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ł —Ā—ā–ĺ—Ź—ā—Ć** [ č…Ēňąn…™ st…Ēňąjat ≤] | It stands / They stand |

### Common Sentence Blueprints
Now, let‚Äôs put everything together. You can use simple blueprints. A popular one is **–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é...** followed by another verb. You can also describe what you see using **–Ę–ł –Ī–į—á–ł—ą...**. These patterns are like Lego blocks.

_–ü—Ä–ł–ļ–Ľ–į–ī–ł:_
**–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é –Ņ–ł—ā–ł –ļ–į–≤—É.** [ja l ≤uňąbl ≤u ňąp…™t…™ ňąka ču] ‚ÄĒ I love to drink coffee.
**–Ę–ł –Ī–į—á–ł—ą –Ņ–į—Ä–ļ.** [t…™ ňąbatÕ° É…™ É park] ‚ÄĒ You see the park.
**–ú–ł –Ī–į—á–ł–ľ–ĺ –ľ–ĺ—Ä–Ķ.** [m…™ ňąbatÕ° É…™m…Ē ňąm…Ēr…õ] ‚ÄĒ We see the sea.

### Special Guests: Eating and Drinking
We have two verbs that are vital: **—ó—Ā—ā–ł** (to eat) and **–Ņ–ł—ā–ł** (to drink). **–ü–ł—ā–ł** follows the rules of the First Conjugation. **–á—Ā—ā–ł** is an ancient bonus verb. It has its own unique rhythm and is very old. But you will learn it quickly!

**Conjugation of —ó—Ā—ā–ł (to eat):**
- **–Į —ó–ľ** [ja jim] ‚ÄĒ I eat
- **–Ę–ł —ó—Ā–ł** [t…™ jiňąs…™] ‚ÄĒ You eat
- **–í—Ė–Ĺ/–í–ĺ–Ĺ–į —ó—Ā—ā—Ć** [ čin ňąjist ≤] ‚ÄĒ He/She eats
- **–ú–ł —ó–ľ–ĺ** [m…™ jiňąm…Ē] ‚ÄĒ We eat
- **–í–ł —ó—Ā—ā–Ķ** [ č…™ jisňąt…õ] ‚ÄĒ You (plural) eat
- **–í–ĺ–Ĺ–ł —ó–ī—Ź—ā—Ć** [ č…Ēňąn…™ jiňąd ≤at ≤] ‚ÄĒ They eat

**Conjugation of –Ņ–ł—ā–ł (to drink):**
- **–Į –Ņ'—é** [ja pju] ‚ÄĒ I drink
- **–Ę–ł –Ņ'—Ē—ą** [t…™ pje É] ‚ÄĒ You drink
- **–í—Ė–Ĺ/–í–ĺ–Ĺ–į –Ņ'—Ē** [ čin pje] ‚ÄĒ He/She/It drinks

## Cultural Insight

These two verbs are essential for hospitality. Keep them close!

> [!cultural]
> In Ukrainian culture, offering food and drink is a duty. You will hear **‚Äú–ü—Ä–ĺ—ą—É –ī–ĺ —Ā—ā–ĺ–Ľ—É!‚ÄĚ** (Please come to the table!). Then you will hear **‚Äú–í–ł –Ī—É–ī–Ķ—ā–Ķ —ó—Ā—ā–ł?‚ÄĚ** (Will you eat?). Knowing how to answer with **‚Äú–Į —ó–ľ...‚ÄĚ** or **‚Äú–Į –Ņ‚Äô—é...‚ÄĚ** is the first step.

When you enjoy a meal with your Ukrainian friends, you join a tradition. The words you use ‚ÄĒ verbs ending in **-–ł—ā–ł** ‚ÄĒ are echoes of the ancient language of Kyivan Rus‚Äô. These structures have remained stable since the 10th century.

### Historical Stability
The **-–ł—ā–ł** ending is a descendant of the Proto-Slavic form. Other parts of the language have shifted. But this specific class of verbs remained a linguistic anchor. In the era of Kyivan Rus‚Äô, speakers used these same patterns. You find these familiar verb forms in ancient manuscripts.

For a beginner, this history is a comfort. The system is not a modern invention. This tried-and-tested engine has served millions of people for a millennium.

| Period | Form | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Proto-Slavic (Ancient) | `*lubiti` | to love |
| Kyivan Rus' (11th c.) | **–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł** ‚ÄĒ [l ≤uňąb…™t…™] | to love |
| Modern Ukrainian | **–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł** ‚ÄĒ [l ≤uňąb…™t…™] | to love |

### Etymology: Love and People
A beautiful connection is in the word **–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł** (to love). The root is connected with the word **–Ľ—é–ī–ł** (people). In the Slavic worldview, loving is connected with being among people. To love is to recognize humanity in others.

This connection reflects social traditions in Ukraine. Loving is expressed through community. When you tell someone **¬ę–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é —ā–Ķ–Ī–Ķ¬Ľ**, you use a word rooted in the idea of being human.

> [!fact]
> The verb **–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł** is used for people and things you enjoy. For deep romantic love, Ukrainians also use the verb **–ļ–ĺ—Ö–į—ā–ł**. As a beginner, **–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł** is your all-purpose word!
