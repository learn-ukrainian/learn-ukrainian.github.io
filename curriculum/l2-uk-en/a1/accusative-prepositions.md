# Accusative Prepositions

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> You already know how to use the Accusative case for direct objects. Now we will add prepositions to talk about where you are going, what you are thankful for, and what you are thinking about. This unlocks a whole new level of expressing direction and purpose in Ukrainian.

## Direction and Movement: Куди? (Where to?)

In the Ukrainian language, we use different prepositions depending on whether we are moving towards a destination or if we are already located there. When you are going somewhere, you use the question word **куди́**. This specific question requires the Accusative case to show dynamic motion and direction. You are not static; you are crossing a boundary to get to a new place.

Let us look at the two main prepositions for direction. The very first one is **в** or **у**. We use this preposition when we are going inside an enclosed space like a building, a defined area, or a container.

Я іду в магази́н.
Вона́ іде в па́рк.

Remember the important euphony rule we learned? We switch between **в** and **у** to make the sentence sound melodic and easy to pronounce. If the previous word ends in a consonant and the next word starts with a consonant, we use **у** to break up the consonant cluster. If the previous word ends in a vowel, we use **в**. This avoids harsh sounds and keeps the spoken language flowing smoothly without awkward pauses.

Вони́ йдуть у магази́н.
Студе́нти йдуть у па́рк.

The second essential preposition for direction is **на**. We use this for open spaces, surfaces, or public events. If you are going to a place that does not have a roof, or if you are attending an activity rather than just entering a structure, you use **на**. It emphasizes the surface or the occasion rather than the physical interior.

Я іду на по́шту.
Вона́ їде на конце́рт.
Ми ідемо́ на пло́щу.
Вони́ йдуть на стадіо́н.

Notice how the noun endings change according to the Accusative case rules you learned earlier. Feminine words ending in **-а** change to **-у**. Masculine inanimate words do not change their form and look exactly like the dictionary form.

Let us compare these two prepositions side by side to see how they function in different contexts.

| Phrase | Preposition | Reason |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **в магази́н** (to the shop) | **в** | enclosed space |
| **на по́шту** (to the post office) | **на** | open space / public service |
| **у па́рк** (to the park) | **у** | enclosed area / euphony |
| **на конце́рт** (to the concert) | **на** | event |
| **на стадіо́н** (to the stadium) | **на** | open space / event venue |
| **на пля́ж** (to the beach) | **на** | surface / open space |
| **на о́стрів** (to the island) | **на** | surface / open space |

[!tip]
> A good trick is to think about whether you can stand "on" the place or if you go "inside" it. Events always take **на**. The post office is a notable exception that takes **на**, treating it like a public service surface rather than just a regular building.

Куди́ іде брат?
Брат іде в магази́н.
Куди́ їде сестра́?
Сестра́ їде на о́стрів.

Choosing between these two prepositions takes practice. Sometimes the logic is different from the logic used in English. You just have to memorize that a stadium or a public square is an open space, so it requires the preposition for a surface. Always ask yourself if the destination is a container or a platform.

Студе́нт іде в університе́т.
Студе́нт іде на ле́кцію.

In the first sentence, the university is a physical building. In the second sentence, the lecture is an event that you attend.

## Exchange and Gratitude: За (For)

When you want to say "thank you for" something or "pay for" something, you must use the preposition **за** followed by the Accusative case. This represents a direct exchange of value between two people.

Дя́кую за допомо́гу.
Я плачу́ за квито́к.
Вона́ пла́тить за ка́ву.

This concept of exchange is deeply embedded in everyday life in Ukraine. When you hand money to a bus driver, you are literally exchanging your money for the ride. You are giving something of value to get something of value back. The same applies when you buy tickets at a cinema or a train station.

Гро́ші за прої́зд.
Дя́кую за сніда́нок.

[!warning]
> Visual trap and common mistake! English speakers often try to use the direct translation of "for" which is **для**. You must never say «Дякую для...». Always use **за** when expressing gratitude or paying for an item. The word **для** is used for the beneficiary of an action, not for an exchange.

The preposition **за** has two other important meanings. It can mean spatial direction, specifically going "behind" something. When you move to the back of an object, you use this preposition to describe your path.

Я іду за буди́нок.
Він іде за шко́лу.
Ми ідемо́ за маши́ну.

It also has a time meaning. We use **за** to express how long it takes to complete an action. It means "within" a certain timeframe. It highlights the duration of the effort required to finish the task.

Я зроблю́ це за годи́ну.
Брат чита́є текст за хвили́ну.

Let us review the three unique uses of **за**.

| Phrase | Meaning | Function |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **за допомо́гу** (for help) | exchange / gratitude | **за** |
| **за буди́нок** (behind the building) | direction | **за** |
| **за годи́ну** (within an hour) | time duration | **за** |

## Cause and Crossing: Через (Through/Because of)

The preposition **че́рез** is incredibly versatile and useful in daily conversation. Its physical meaning is spatial, expressing movement "through" or "across" an area. If you cross a space from one side to the other, you use this word.

Я іду че́рез па́рк.
Ми ідемо́ че́рез мі́ст.
Вона́ іде че́рез доро́гу.
Хло́пець іде че́рез ліс.

However, **че́рез** is also used to express cause or reason. When something happens "because of" a situation or an obstacle, we use this preposition followed by the Accusative case. This preposition is vital for explaining your actions and your route. If someone asks why you are late, you will almost certainly need to use this word to explain the obstacle in your path.

Я вдо́ма че́рез дощ.
Він не іде че́рез робо́ту.
Ми не їдемо че́рез сніг.

Finally, **че́рез** has a time meaning, but it is entirely different from the preposition **за**. While **за** means "within" a duration of effort, **че́рез** means "in" or "after" an amount of time from the present moment. It sets an appointment in the future.

Я бу́ду че́рез годи́ну.
Вона́ приї́де че́рез ти́ждень.

[!did-you-know]
> The word **че́рез** clearly divides cause and time. If you say you will arrive in exactly an hour, use **че́рез**. If you say it will take you a full hour of effort to finish the given task, use **за**.

Compare these two time expressions to see the difference clearly. One looks at the active process, and the other looks at the timeline.

За годи́ну.
Че́рез годи́ну.

## Topics and Thoughts: Про (About)

When you talk, read, or think about a specific topic, you use the preposition **про** followed by the Accusative case. This preposition points directly to the subject of your attention. Whether you are summarizing a news article, asking a friend about their day, or describing a movie plot, you need this preposition. It links your thoughts to the external world.

Я чита́ю про Украї́ну.
Він ду́має про ма́му.
Це кни́га про істо́рію.
Ми говоримо́ про фі́льм.

Many common communication and cognitive verbs pair naturally with this preposition. You will use these phrases constantly in conversation.

Говори́ти про.
Ду́мати про.
Чита́ти про.
Зна́ти про.

To ask a question about the topic of a conversation or a thought, you use the phrase "About what?" or "About whom?".

Про що?
Про кого?
Про що ви говорите́?
Про кого він ду́має?

[!culture]
> Ukrainians love to discuss history, culture, and literature around the dinner table. Knowing how to use **про** allows you to join these rich conversations and ask people what they are reading or thinking about.

## Summary & Practice

Let us see how all these prepositions work together in a realistic scenario. Since we do not use direct commands at this stage, we will use statements to describe what people are doing. Watch how the prepositions change the meaning of the motion or the topic.

— Ти ідеш на пло́щу?
— Ні, я іду в магази́н че́рез па́рк.
— Про що ти ду́маєш?
— Я ду́маю про конце́рт. Він бу́де че́рез годи́ну.
— Добре, дя́кую за допомо́гу.
— Будь ла́ска.

Here is a quick reference summary of the prepositions we learned today. Use this table as a study guide to review the fundamental concepts.

| Preposition | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **в / у** | to (inside) | **в магази́н** |
| **на** | to (surface/event) | **на конце́рт** |
| **за** | for (exchange) | **за допомо́гу** |
| **че́рез** | through / because of / in (time) | **че́рез па́рк** |
| **про** | about | **про істо́рію** |

Let us test your understanding with a few self-check questions. Try to answer them based on the rules we discussed above.

1. Which preposition do you use when going to a stadium or a music concert?
2. How do you say "thank you for the help" without using the word for "beneficiary"?
3. What is the precise difference between going **в па́рк** and **че́рез па́рк**?
4. If you are delayed because of the rain, which preposition explains the cause of the delay?

Успі́хів!

By mastering these small but powerful words, you instantly make your Ukrainian sound much more authentic and precise. You are no longer just naming objects; you are describing complex relationships in space and time. You now have the tools to navigate around the city, express your gratitude politely, and discuss your favorite topics. Keep practicing these prepositions until choosing the correct one becomes entirely natural.
