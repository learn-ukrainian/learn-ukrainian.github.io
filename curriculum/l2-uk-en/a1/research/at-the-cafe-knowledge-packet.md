# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: At The Cafe



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

The "At the Cafe" scenario is a cornerstone of A1 communicative learning. Ukrainian pedagogical materials approach this topic through highly contextualized, functional dialogues. The primary goal is to equip the learner with the essential phrases to successfully navigate a simple ordering and paying interaction.

The core method is built on a "scaffolding" of politeness and utility:
1.  **Politeness First:** All interactions are framed with core etiquette phrases like `–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М`, `–С—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞`, and `–Ф—П–Ї—Г—О`. These are treated as non-negotiable building blocks for any request (Source: `4-klas-ukrayinska-mova-varzatska-2021-1_s0004`, `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0018`).
2.  **Start with Invariables:** The initial vocabulary intentionally includes a high number of indeclinable, often international, nouns: `–Ї–∞—Д–µ`, `–Љ–µ–љ—О`, `–Ї–∞–Ї–∞–Њ`, `–Ї–∞–њ—Г—З–Є–љ–Њ`, `–µ—Б–Ї—Ц–Љ–Њ`, `–Љ–µ—В—А–Њ`, `—В–∞–Ї—Б—Ц` (Source: `6-klas-ukrmova-zabolotnyi-2020_s0116`, `6-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2023_s0178`). This allows the learner to form sentences without the immediate cognitive load of noun declension, focusing purely on the communicative act.
3.  **Model-Based Learning:** Learning happens through modeling and repetition of dialogue chunks. For example, the pattern `–Ь–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ –Љ–µ–љ—Ц, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞, [item]?` is introduced as a complete, polite formula for making a request (Source: `ext-ulp_youtube-125`). Similarly, role-playing simple situations, like ordering from a menu or asking for directions to a cafe, is a common classroom activity (Source: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0109`, `6-klas-ukrmova-zabolotnyi-2020_s0116`).
4.  **Practical Outcomes:** Exercises are designed around achieving a real-world goal: ordering food, asking for the bill, or paying. The language is a tool to complete the task (Source: `ext-ulp_youtube-116`, `ext-ulp_youtube-209`). The focus is not on grammatical analysis but on successful communication.

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

This sequence is designed to build communicative competence progressively, minimizing grammatical hurdles at the start.

- **Step 1: Foundational Politeness & Location.**
  - Introduce core greetings (`–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М`) and politeness markers (`–±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞`, `–і—П–Ї—Г—О`, `–≤–Є–±–∞—З—В–µ`).
  - Introduce the key location nouns: `–Ї–∞—Д–µ`, `—А–µ—Б—В–Њ—А–∞–љ`. Emphasize that `–Ї–∞—Д–µ` is indeclinable.
  - Practice with simple statements: `–¶–µ –Ї–∞—Д–µ.`

- **Step 2: The Core Request Formula.**
  - Introduce the most versatile A1 request phrase: `–Ь–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ –Љ–µ–љ—Ц, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞, ...?` (Can I please have...?) (Source: `ext-ulp_youtube-125`).
  - Pair this with basic, indeclinable, or high-frequency menu items: `–Ї–∞–≤—Г`, `—З–∞–є`, `–≤–Њ–і—Г`, `—Б—Ц–Ї`, `–Љ–µ–љ—О`.
  - Example drill: `–Ь–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ –Љ–µ–љ—Ц, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞, –Ї–∞–≤—Г?`, `–Ь–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ –Љ–µ–љ—Ц, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞, –Љ–µ–љ—О?`.

- **Step 3: Reading a Simple Menu & Making a Choice.**
  - Introduce more simple, often international, food words: `–њ—Ц—Ж–∞`, `—Б–∞–ї–∞—В`, `—Б—Г–њ`, `—В—Ц—Б—В–µ—З–Ї–Њ`, `–Љ–Њ—А–Њ–Ј–Є–≤–Њ`.
  - Introduce the phrase `–ѓ –±—Г–і—Г...` (I will have...) or `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г...` as a way to state a choice. Example: `–ѓ –±—Г–і—Г –њ—Ц—Ж—Г —Ц —Б—Ц–Ї.` (Source: `ext-ulp_youtube-83`, `ext-ulp_youtube-132`).
  - Introduce the question `–©–Њ –≤–Є –±—Г–і–µ—В–µ –Ј–∞–Љ–Њ–≤–ї—П—В–Є?` (What will you be ordering?) that the learner might hear.

- **Step 4: Asking for the Bill and Paying.**
  - Teach the crucial phrase: `–Ь–Њ–ґ–љ–∞ —А–∞—Е—Г–љ–Њ–Ї, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞?` (Can I have the bill, please?) (Source: `ext-ulp_youtube-116`).
  - Introduce the cashier's question: `–Ъ–∞—А—В–Ї–∞ —З–Є –≥–Њ—В—Ц–≤–Ї–∞?` (Card or cash?).
  - Provide the simple answers: `–Ъ–∞—А—В–Ї–∞.` or `–У–Њ—В—Ц–≤–Ї–∞.` (Source: `ext-ulp_youtube-125`).

- **Step 5: Differentiating "For Here" or "To Go".**
  - Introduce the common question from the barista/cashier: `–Т–∞–Љ —В—Г—В —З–Є –Ј —Б–Њ–±–Њ—О?` (For here or to go?).
  - Teach the two possible replies: `–Ґ—Г—В.` (Here) or `–Ч —Б–Њ–±–Њ—О.` (To go) (Source: `ext-ulp_youtube-125`). This is a high-frequency interaction in modern cafes.

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

This topic is a frontline for establishing correct, modern, Russian-free Ukrainian from day one.
1.  **The `–Ч–∞–Љ–Њ–≤–Є—В–Є` vs. `–Ч–∞–Ї–∞–Ј–∞—В–Є` Rule is Absolute:** The verb `–Ј–∞–Ї–∞–Ј–∞—В–Є` is one of the most common and persistent Russianisms. It must be actively flagged and corrected from the very first lesson. The only verb for ordering food, drinks, or services is **`–Ј–∞–Љ–Њ–≤–ї—П—В–Є` (imperfective) / `–Ј–∞–Љ–Њ–≤–Є—В–Є` (perfective)** (Source: `10-klas-ukrmova-glazova-2018_s0042`). Presenting `–Ј–∞–Ї–∞–Ј–∞—В–Є` as "colloquial" or "also heard" is unacceptable; it is incorrect in standard Ukrainian.
2.  **Internationalisms are not Russianisms:** Words like `–Ї–∞—Д–µ`, `–Љ–µ–љ—О`, `—А–µ—Б—В–Њ—А–∞–љ`, `–і–µ—Б–µ—А—В`, `–Њ–Љ–ї–µ—В`, `—Б–∞–ї–∞—В` entered Ukrainian primarily from French (Source: `10-klas-ukrmova-karaman-2018_s0176`). They should be presented as international vocabulary that Ukrainian shares with many European languages, not as words borrowed via Russian. This framing reinforces Ukrainian's place in a broader European context.
3.  **Teach Ukrainian Phonetics on Their Own Terms:** When teaching pronunciation of cafe-related words, avoid any comparison to Russian pronunciation (e.g., how 'e' or 'o' are pronounced). The learner must build a Ukrainian phonetic foundation from scratch, using Ukrainian audio models.
4.  **Politeness Formulas:** Emphasize uniquely Ukrainian or pan-European politeness formulas. For "you're welcome," teach `–Я—А–ЊћБ—И—Г` (with correct stress) and `–С—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞`. Avoid defaulting to patterns that might be more common in Russian conversational style.

# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: Around The City



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

The core methodological approach for teaching "Around The City" at the A1 level is communicative and situational. The goal is not to exhaustively list vocabulary but to equip the learner with functional chunks to solve a real-world problem: getting lost and asking for directions. The approach should mirror how a native speaker would help a foreigner, simplifying language into clear, actionable steps.

Instruction should be built around a core dialogue pattern, as demonstrated in Ukrainian Lessons Podcast episodes (Source 22, Source 23). This involves:
1.  **Gaining attention politely:** Starting with `–Т–Є–±–∞—З—В–µ, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞...` (Source 23).
2.  **Asking the core question:** Using the simple construction `–Ф–µ [–љ–∞–Ј–≤–∞ –Љ—Ц—Б—Ж—П]?` (e.g., `–Ф–µ –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї?`, `–Ф–µ —Ж–µ–љ—В—А?`) (Source 23).
3.  **Understanding a simple response:** Processing basic directional adverbs (`–њ—А—П–Љ–Њ`, `–њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З`, `–ї—Ц–≤–Њ—А—Г—З`) and verbs (`—Ц–і—Ц—В—М`, `–њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М`) (Source 22).
4.  **Clarifying transport:** Differentiating between types of transport like `–∞–≤—В–Њ–±—Г—Б` (bus) and `–њ–Њ—Ч–Ј–і` (train), which dictates whether one needs an `–∞–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` or `–Ј–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (Source 22).

Ukrainian elementary textbooks introduce related concepts through simple, repetitive structures. For example, exercises focus on using prepositions with locations (`–Я—Ц–і—Ц–є—И–ї–Є –і–Њ —А—Ц—З–Ї–Є`, `–°—Е–Њ–≤–∞–≤—Б—П –Ј–∞ –і–µ—А–µ–≤–Њ–Љ`) (Source 10) or listing related items to build semantic fields (`–ѓ–±–ї—Г–Ї–∞, –≥—А—Г—И—Ц, —Б–ї–Є–≤–Є... вАФ —Ж–µ —Д—А—Г–Ї—В–Є`) (Source 28). This method of grouping and association should be used for city vocabulary (e.g., `–Љ—Г–Ј–µ–є`, `—Ж–µ—А–Ї–≤–∞`, `–Љ–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ` are all places in a city).

The learning process should be scaffolded, starting with recognizing place names, then forming a question, and finally understanding a multi-step answer. Role-playing dialogues is a highly effective activity at this stage (Source 12, Source 20).

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

1.  **Core Question & Basic Locations:** Start with the most fundamental survival question: `–Ф–µ...?` (Where is?). Pair it with the most essential, high-frequency A1-level locations.
    *   `–Ф–µ —Ж–µ–љ—В—А?` (Where is the center?) (Source 23)
    *   `–Ф–µ –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї?` (Where is the station?) (Source 22)
    *   `–Ф–µ –Љ–µ—В—А–Њ?` (Where is the metro?) (Source 23)
    *   `–Ф–µ —В—Г–∞–ї–µ—В?` <!-- VERIFY -->
    This immediately gives the learner a functional tool.

2.  **Simple Positional Answers:** Introduce the simplest possible answers a person might point and give.
    *   `–Ґ—Г—В` (Here)
    *   `–Ґ–∞–Љ` (There)
    *   `–Ю–љ —В–∞–Љ` (Over there) (Source 23)

3.  **Essential Directional Commands:** Introduce the imperative verbs and adverbs for giving basic directions. Always teach the formal "–≤–Є" forms first (`-—Ц—В—М` ending) as they are safest for speaking to strangers.
    *   `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М –њ—А—П–Љ–Њ` (Go straight) (Source 22)
    *   `–Я–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` (Turn right) (Source 22)
    *   `–Я–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –ї—Ц–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` (Turn left) (Source 22)

4.  **Key Nouns for Navigation:** Introduce nouns that act as landmarks in directions.
    *   `–≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—П` (street) (Source 28)
    *   `–њ–µ—А–µ—Е—А–µ—Б—В—П` (intersection) (Source 22)
    *   `—Ж–µ—А–Ї–≤–∞` (church) (Source 2, Source 9)
    *   `–Љ–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ` (shop) (Source 28)

5.  **Combining into Short Instructions:** Practice combining the elements from steps 3 and 4.
    *   `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М –њ—А—П–Љ–Њ –њ–Њ –≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—Ц...` (Go straight on ... street) (Source 22).
    *   `–Э–∞ –њ–µ—А–µ—Е—А–µ—Б—В—Ц –њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` (At the intersection, turn right) (Source 22).

6.  **Transportation Vocabulary:** Introduce basic modes of transport and the places associated with them. It is crucial to distinguish between `–∞–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` and `–Ј–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї`.
    *   `–∞–≤—В–Њ–±—Г—Б` (bus) вЖТ `–∞–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (bus station) (Source 22)
    *   `–њ–Њ—Ч–Ј–і` (train) вЖТ `–Ј–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (railway station) (Source 22)
    *   `–Љ–µ—В—А–Њ` (metro/subway) (Source 23)

7.  **The Concept of "Needing to Take":** Introduce the impersonal construction `—В—А–µ–±–∞ —Ч—Е–∞—В–Є` (one needs to go/travel).
    *   `–Ґ—А–µ–±–∞ —Ч—Е–∞—В–Є –љ–∞ –Љ–µ—В—А–Њ.` (You need to go by metro.) (Source 23) This is a critical A1 structure that avoids complex verb conjugations.

## –Ґ–Є–њ–Њ–≤—Ц –њ–Њ–Љ–Є–ї–Ї–Є L2 (Common L2 Errors)

| вЭМ –Я–Њ–Љ–Є–ї–Ї–Њ–≤–Њ | вЬЕ –Я—А–∞–≤–Є–ї—М–љ–Њ | –І–Њ–Љ—Г |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `–Т–Є–±–∞—З–∞—О—Б—П` | `–Т–Є–±–∞—З—В–µ` | `–Т–Є–±–∞—З–∞—О—Б—П` is a reflexive verb meaning "I apologize myself," which is a calque from Russian and considered unnatural and slightly self-centered in modern Ukrainian. The correct form for apologizing or getting someone's attention is the imperative `–Т–Є–±–∞—З—В–µ` (Excuse me / Forgive me) (Source 26). |
| `–Ф–µ —Ф —Ж–µ–љ—В—А?` | `–Ф–µ —Ж–µ–љ—В—А?` | English speakers often try to insert the verb "to be" (`—Ф`) in simple "Where is X?" questions, directly translating from English. In Ukrainian, the verb "to be" is omitted in present tense location questions. The structure is simply `–Ф–µ + [ —Ц–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є–Ї ]?` (Source 23). |
| `–Ж—В–Є –і–Њ –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` | `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` or `–Я–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` | Learners may confuse adverbs of direction (`–њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` - to the right) with nouns of place, incorrectly adding a preposition like `–і–Њ` (to). The adverbs `–њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З`, `–ї—Ц–≤–Њ—А—Г—З`, and `–њ—А—П–Љ–Њ` do not require prepositions when used with verbs of motion (Source 22). |
| Asking for the "train station" and getting the "bus station" | `–Ч–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (for trains) vs. `–Р–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (for buses) | In English, "station" can be ambiguous. In Ukrainian, the distinction is critical. `–Т–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` by itself often implies the main railway station, but it's best to be specific. A speaker asking for a `–њ–Њ—Ч–Ј–і` (train) needs the `–Ј–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї`; someone asking for an `–∞–≤—В–Њ–±—Г—Б` (bus) needs the `–∞–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (Source 22). |
| Using informal `–Ж–і–Є` with a stranger | `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М` | Learners might encounter the informal `—В–Є` forms (`—Ц–і–Є`, `–њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ–Є`) first. It is crucial to emphasize that when asking for directions from a stranger, the formal `–≤–Є` form (`—Ц–і—Ц—В—М`, `–њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М`) is mandatory for politeness and respect (Source 22). |
| `–¶–µ –і–∞–ї–µ–Ї–Њ?` (with rising intonation) | `–¶–µ –і–∞–ї–µ–Ї–Њ?` | This is a positive interference. Unlike English, which often uses auxiliary verbs for questions ("**Is it** far?"), Ukrainian can often form a yes/no question simply by applying a rising intonation to a declarative sentence (Source 23). This is an easy win for learners. |

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

This topic is highly susceptible to colonial narratives, and it is imperative to address this from the beginning.

1.  **The "Empty Land" Myth:** The Russian imperial narrative often claims that southern and eastern Ukrainian cities were "founded" by Russian monarchs (like Catherine II) on empty, wild land. This is false. Content must explicitly state that these cities were often built upon or agglomerated from pre-existing Cossack settlements. The city of Dnipro, for instance, was established on the site of the Cossack sloboda `–Я–Њ–ї–Њ–≤–Є—Ж—П` (Source 9). The textbook should present this as the norm: a Ukrainian settlement was renamed and absorbed, its history erased.

2.  **Authentic Toponymy:** Ukrainian place names have deep historical and geographical roots. Village names often derive from geography (`–У—А—П–і–Є–љ–∞` - from garden beds, Source 2), local crafts (`–≥–Њ–љ—З–∞—А—Ц` - potters, Source 1), or ancient landmarks (`–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –Ф—Г–±` - a sacred oak, Source 2). Presenting vocabulary like `–Ї—Г—В–Њ–Ї` (a neighborhood or corner of a village) (Source 2) and `—Г—А–Њ—З–Є—Й–µ` (a distinct natural landmark) (Source 2) grounds the learner in an authentic Ukrainian perception of space, rather than a generic, universal one.

3.  **No Russian Analogies:** Do not teach Ukrainian directions or locations by comparing them to Russian. For example, never say "–≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—П is like Russian —Г–ї–Є—Ж–∞." Teach Ukrainian on its own terms. Phonetics, grammar, and vocabulary should be presented as a self-contained system. The presence of Cossack, Polish, and other historical layers (Source 3) should be highlighted to show Ukraine's history is European and distinct.

4.  **Transportation Hubs as Ukrainian Spaces:** While `–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` is an internationalism (from Vauxhall Gardens), its culture in Ukraine is distinctly Ukrainian. Train travel is a major part of Ukrainian life (Source 22). Frame `–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї–Є` not as generic transport hubs, but as vibrant centers of Ukrainian life, often with their own markets (`—А–Є–љ–Њ–Ї`) and social dynamics (Source 22).

## –Я—А–Є–Ї–ї–∞–і–Є –Ј –њ—Ц–і—А—Г—З–љ–Є–Ї—Ц–≤ (Textbook Examples)

**1. Role-Play Dialogue (Situational Practice)**
*   **Task:** Based on the model from Ukrainian Lessons Podcast (Source 20, Source 22), create a dialogue. One learner is lost and asks for directions to the museum. The other gives simple, two-step directions.
*   **Student A (Lost Tourist):** `–Т–Є–±–∞—З—В–µ, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞. –°–Ї–∞–ґ—Ц—В—М, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞, –і–µ –Љ—Г–Ј–µ–є?`
*   **Student B (Local):** `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М –њ—А—П–Љ–Њ –њ–Њ —Ж—Ц–є –≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—Ц, –∞ –њ–Њ—В—Ц–Љ –њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З. –Ь—Г–Ј–µ–є –±—Г–і–µ —В–∞–Љ.`
*   **Student A:** `–Ф—Г–ґ–µ –і—П–Ї—Г—О!`
*   **Student B:** `–С—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞.`

**2. Fill-in-the-Preposition (Grammar Focus)**
*   **Task:** Complete the sentences with the correct preposition from the box: `–і–Њ`, `–≤/—Г`, `–љ–∞`, `–Ј–∞`. (Inspired by Source 10).
    *   1. –Ь–Є –њ—Ц–і—Ц–є—И–ї–Є ____ –±—Г–і–Є–љ–Ї—Г. (We approached the house.)
    *   2. –У–Њ–ї—Г–±–Є –њ–Њ—В—А–∞–њ–Є–ї–Є ____ –њ–∞—Б—В–Ї—Г. (The doves got into the trap.) (Source 10)
    *   3. –Ь–Є —Б—Ц–ї–Є ____ –Љ–µ—В—А–Њ. (We got on the metro.)
    *   4. –®–Ї–Њ–ї–∞ –Ј–љ–∞—Е–Њ–і–Є—В—М—Б—П ____ —В–Є–Љ –њ–Њ–≤–Њ—А–Њ—В–Њ–Љ. (The school is behind that turn.)
*   **Answers:** 1. –і–Њ, 2. –≤, 3. –љ–∞, 4. –Ј–∞

**3. Location Identification on a Simple Map (Visual Comprehension)**
*   **Task:** Provide a simple, schematic map of a town center with 4-5 labeled buildings (e.g., `–®–Ї–Њ–ї–∞`, `–Т–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї`, `–¶–µ—А–Ї–≤–∞`, `–Ь–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ`). Ask the learner "Where is the X?" and have them respond using simple prepositions. (Adapted from the map task in Source 5).
*   **Question:** `–Ф–µ —И–Ї–Њ–ї–∞?`
*   **Possible Answer:** `–®–Ї–Њ–ї–∞ –±—Ц–ї—П —Ж–µ—А–Ї–≤–Є.` (The school is near the church.)
*   **Question:** `–Ф–µ –Љ–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ?`
*   **Possible Answer:** `–Ь–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ –љ–∞ –≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—Ц –®–µ–≤—З–µ–љ–Ї–∞.` (The shop is on Shevchenko street.)

**4. Building Sentences (Syntax Practice)**
*   **Task:** Give the learner scrambled words and have them form a correct sentence giving a direction.
    *   1. `–њ—А—П–Љ–Њ / –Ж–і—Ц—В—М / –≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—Ц / –њ–Њ` -> `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М –њ—А—П–Љ–Њ –њ–Њ –≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—Ц.` (Source 22)
    *   2. `–љ–∞–ї—Ц–≤–Њ / –Э–∞ / –њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М / –њ–µ—А–µ—Е—А–µ—Б—В—Ц` -> `–Э–∞ –њ–µ—А–µ—Е—А–µ—Б—В—Ц –њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –љ–∞–ї—Ц–≤–Њ.` (Source 22)
    *   3. `—В—А–µ–±–∞ / –љ–∞ / –З—Е–∞—В–Є / –Љ–µ—В—А–Њ` -> `–Ґ—А–µ–±–∞ —Ч—Е–∞—В–Є –љ–∞ –Љ–µ—В—А–Њ.` (Source 23)
