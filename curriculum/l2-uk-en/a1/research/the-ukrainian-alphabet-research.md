## Cultural Hooks

1. **Загадка (riddle) using decodable letters**: «Мала, мала, а всіх годує» → milk (молоко). This riddle uses only decodable vocabulary and demonstrates that even tiny words carry meaning — a hook for why phonetic reading unlocks real language.
2. **Origin story**: Cyrillic was created in the 9th century by disciples of Saints Cyril and Methodius (the "Solunski brothers"), adapting Greek uncial script for Slavic phonetics. Ukrainian Cyrillic diverged from Church Slavonic over centuries. This is NOT Russian script — Ukrainian has letters Russian lacks (І, Є, Ї, Ґ) and lacks letters Russian has (Ъ, Ы, Э). This distinction is culturally important and factually grounded.
3. **False friends for English speakers**: Cyrillic Н looks like English H but sounds like N; Р looks like English P but sounds like R; С looks like English C but sounds like S; У looks like English Y but sounds like oo. This "visual trap" can be introduced as a game: "Spot the impostor."

---

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Н → H confusion** → Correct: Н = /n/ as in "no" — happens because Н is visually identical to H; occurs in virtually every English-speaker's first encounter with Cyrillic (textbook kravtsova Grade 3 §Звуки і букви targets this explicitly)
2. **Р → P confusion** → Correct: Р = /r/ as in "run" — same visual-trap mechanism; learners often read «рак» as starting with P
3. **С → S/C confusion** → Correct: С = /s/ — looks like English C or S; learners may read «сік» as starting with C-sound
4. **Letter = Sound assumption** → Ukrainian has 33 letters but 38 phonemes; Я Ю Є Ї are iotated (two-sound letters at word start); Ь has no sound of its own. Learners expect 1:1 mapping after being told the system is "phonetic" — the exceptions need flagging early.

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise**: Show learners the word «МАМА» in large Cyrillic letters. Ask: "Does any part of this look familiar?" (М resembles M, А resembles A). Then show «МОЛОКО» — the О is recognizable. Then flip to «НІС» — "What sound does Н make?" (Most say "H" — wrong! That's the trap.) Reveal: Н = N. This surprise creates the memorable hook that Cyrillic is *almost* Latin but not quite.
- **Best context**: A Ukrainian child's first-reader page — photos of мама, тато, кіт, молоко with labels. The visual-to-word mapping grounds abstract letter learning in recognizable everyday objects. Textbook model: Bolshakova Grade 1 pp. 13–22 (object labeling pattern).
- **Contrastive pairs for false friends**: Н/H, Р/P, С/C displayed side by side. "In English, H says 'h'. In Ukrainian, Н says 'n'." Simple, memorable, prevents the most common errors.
- **Blending walkthrough**: М → МА → МАМА (demonstrate by adding one letter at a time). Then К → КІ → КІТ. This mirrors the kravtsova Grade 3 syllable-blending exercises (§Звуки і букви).

## Notes for Content Writing

- Decodable word list is fixed: мама, тато, кіт, молоко, масло, ліс, місто, око, так, ні, сон, сом, ніс, мак, сік, стіл, тут, там, лук, кіно — only these in reading drills
- Sight words (привіт, дякую, це) must be labeled explicitly as "sight words — learn as whole shapes"
- NO imperative verbs in Ukrainian anywhere — classroom instructions in English only ("Listen carefully", "Read aloud", "Repeat")
- Ukrainian section headers must be bilingual per A1.md rule: `## Голосні та приголосні — Vowels and Consonants`
- The «Це + noun» frame handles all in-module Ukrainian sentences — no verb needed
