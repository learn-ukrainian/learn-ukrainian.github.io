## Cultural Hooks

1. **Textbook discovery hook — рідний paradigm**: The Vashulenko Grade 3 textbook uses *«рідний край / рідна мова / рідне слово / рідні люди»* to introduce adjective gender agreement — one adjective, all four forms. This is a semantically rich anchor (homeland, language, native people) and works perfectly as a discovery warm-up. Students see the pattern before the rule is stated.

2. **Kyiv Metro lines**: «синя лінія» (Blue Line M1) and «червона лінія» (Red Line M2) — real, Google-verifiable transport data. This anchors soft-stem синій vs. hard-stem червоний in functional context. Learners can look it up on their phone.

3. **Mavka (Мавка) from Lesya Ukrainka's "Forest Song" (Лісова пісня)**: A молода, гарна, зелена forest spirit — all feminine forms. This gives the feminine paradigm a memorable cultural persona. Lesya Ukrainka is printed on the 200-hryvnia note, so it's also a practical touchpoint.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Gender mismatch — masculine dictionary form applied universally**: *\*новий машина* → correct: **нова машина**. English has no gender, so learners default to the dictionary form (-ий). Textbook Grade 3 Kravtsova explicitly targets this with fill-in exercises where gender must be determined from the noun.

2. **Hard/soft confusion — \*синий vs. синій**: English phonetics push learners toward -ий spelling since the word sounds similar to "siniy." The two-dot Ukrainian letter й after и must be drilled. Textbook Grade 4 Kravtsova shows color adjectives in context; use the Metro mnemonic to fix this.

3. **Plural form — gender-differentiated endings**: Learners try *\*гарна дні / гарне дівчата* — they carry singular gender logic into the plural. Ukrainian plural adjective form is always -і regardless of noun gender: **гарні дні, гарні дівчата, гарні вікна**.

4. **Adjective after noun in predicative position**: English "the house is big" maps naturally to Ukrainian «будинок великий» — acceptable but less natural than «великий будинок». At A1 this is secondary; priority is getting the ending correct. Note: verbs are forbidden at M11, so predicative sentences with є cannot be taught here.

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise** (from Vashulenko Grade 3 model): Present four noun phrases without explanation — *рідний край, рідна мова, рідне слово, рідні люди* — and ask: "What changes? What stays the same?" Learners notice the shared root (рідн-) and the changing endings. Then show the question patterns: «Який? → -ий / Яка? → -а / Яке? → -е / Які? → -і». Rule emerges from data.

- **Best context**: Real estate / apartment listing. "Це новий будинок. Це мала квартира. Це велике вікно." — all three genders appear naturally in describing one property. The plan's Real Estate Agent persona fits well here.

- **Contrastive pairs** for disambiguation:
  - Hard vs. soft: **новий** (hard, -ий) vs. **синій** (soft, -ій) — same question «Який?», different ending
  - Singular vs. plural: **гарний день** vs. **гарні дні** — plural always -і
  - Masc vs. Fem: **новий рік** (m) vs. **нова квартира** (f) — use color-coding or table

## Notes for Content Writing

- Module M11 is pre-verbal: NO conjugated verbs, NO imperatives. All Ukrainian sentences must be noun phrases or «Це + adj + noun» patterns. Classroom instructions stay in English.
- The plan's section 2 mentions predicative position with «бути» — this is **not allowed at M11** since бути is a verb. Reframe predicative as «Це + noun» only: «Це нова квартира» (not «Квартира є нова»).
- Bilingual section headers required (M1-14 style): e.g., *«## Тверда група — Hard Stem Adjectives»*
- Hard-stem paradigm table (4 forms) and soft-stem paradigm table (4 forms) should be visual and color-coded — this is how Ukrainian school textbooks Grade 3-4 present it.
- Do NOT use the word «прикметник» in Ukrainian without English gloss — learners haven't seen Ukrainian grammatical terminology yet.
- Grade 5 Avramenko excerpt shows that description/adjective vocabulary directly enables portrait-writing — useful framing for the Mavka practice section.
