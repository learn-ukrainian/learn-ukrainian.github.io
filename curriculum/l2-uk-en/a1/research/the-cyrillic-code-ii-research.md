## Cultural Hooks
1. **The Letter Ґ**: Banned by Soviet authorities from 1933 to 1990 in a deliberate policy to forcefully assimilate Ukrainian pronunciation into Russian and erase distinct linguistic features. Its restoration represents a reclaiming of linguistic sovereignty.
2. **The Letter Ї**: A unique symbol of modern Ukrainian resistance. In 2022, activists painted the letter Ї throughout occupied Mariupol and other cities to mark Ukrainian presence and defiance, showcasing its uniqueness to the Ukrainian alphabet.

## Common Learner Errors
1. **Г vs Ґ confusion** → Learners default to the English hard "g" for both letters. Correction: Г is a voiced "h" (head), while Ґ is the rare hard "g" (go).
2. **Ignoring the Soft Sign (Ь)** → Learners treat it as a silent letter that can be skipped. Correction: It is an active palatalization marker that changes the preceding consonant (e.g., pressing the tongue flat against the palate).
3. **И vs І blending** → English speakers struggle to hear the difference and merge both into an "ee" sound. Correction: Use the "Smile (І) vs Grin (И)" physical technique to force proper jaw and tongue placement.

## Notes for Content Writing
- Decolonized framing: Treat Ukrainian as an independent, complete linguistic system. Do not compare to Russian; compare sounds to English equivalents or use anatomical descriptions for pronunciation.
- Establish clear English scaffolding, as A1 requires heavy English bridging (10-50% immersion) to explain complex phonetics safely.
- Incorporate IPA annotations ONLY for the first occurrence of new vocabulary words to avoid visual clutter.
