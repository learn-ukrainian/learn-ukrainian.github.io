## Cultural Hooks

1. **Київське метро sign culture**: The Kyiv Metro uses standardized signage with characteristic Ukrainian phrasing — **Вхід**, **Вихід**, **Перехід** — distinct from Moscow Metro Russian equivalents. The metro announcement "Увага! Двері зачиняються!" is recognizable to any Kyiv resident and makes for an excellent authentic listening anchor.
2. **Не чіпай! / Не торкатися! contrast**: In Ukrainian museums and galleries, signs say **Не торкатися!** (infinitive, impersonal), but a parent to a child says **Не чіпай!** (imperative). This real-life contrast is observable at any Ukrainian museum — the distinction is taught through lived context, not abstract grammar.
3. **"Технічна перерва"** (Technical break): A characteristically post-Soviet institution-wide practice — shops, banks, government offices all post **Технічна перерва** signs. Learners instantly connect to the phenomenon; it is culturally resonant and pragmatically important.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Using imperative instead of infinitive on signs**: Writing *Не куріть!* instead of **Не курити!** — English speakers map "Don't smoke!" to the imperative they learned in Module 47, not to the infinitive register of public signage.
2. **Confusing вихідний (day off) with вихід (exit)**: The similarity in root (-хід-/-хідний) causes mixing. Learners may read "Вихідний — неділя" as something to do with an exit.
3. **Misreading з... до... as з (with)**: The preposition **з** in schedules means *from* (з 9:00 до 18:00 = from 9 to 18), not the familiar *with*. Learners used to з + instrumental will misparse. The textbook Grade 6 Avramenko exercise on negation pronouns models the "refusal" discourse pattern (exercise 7) — a useful parallel for how Ukrainians phrase avoidance/prohibition in natural register.
4. **Treating зачинено / відчинено as adjectives requiring agreement**: These are short-form predicates (not declined), always the same form regardless of noun gender — *Магазин зачинено*, *Крамниця зачинено*, *Кафе зачинено* all take the same form.

## Teaching Strategy
- **Discovery exercise**: Show learners three pairs of signs side-by-side: (1) a photo of a museum door reading *Не торкатися!* vs a parent saying *Не чіпай!*; (2) a shop door saying *Зачинено* vs a greeter saying *Ми зачинені*; (3) a schedule reading *Вихідний — субота* vs someone saying *Сьогодні — вихідний день*. Ask: "Which version appears on the sign? What's different about the grammar?" Let learners notice the infinitive / short-form predicate pattern BEFORE the rule is stated.
- **Best context**: Metro stations and shop fronts — both generate immediate practical need and provide visually memorable contexts for all target vocabulary. The з... до... schedule construction is most naturally encountered when reading a bank or pharmacy door.
- **Contrastive pairs**: Не курити! (sign, infinitive) vs Не кури! (personal command, imperative) | Зачинено (predicative, invariant) vs зачинений магазин (attributive adjective, declines) | вихідний (noun: day off) vs вихід (noun: exit)

## Notes for Content Writing
- The plan's `register: розмовний` is slightly misleading — public signs use **офіційно-діловий** register (infinitive prohibitions). Frame this as "official sign language" distinct from conversational register. Content writer should note this explicitly.
- Keep the з... до... schedule examples numerically grounded — use real Ukrainian business hours (9:00–18:00 with перерва 13:00–14:00) to make exercises feel authentic.
- Abbreviations on street signs (вул., пл., просп.) can be introduced as a brief recognition-only sidebar — don't require production.
- The plan's grammar tag "Sign language conventions" signals that learners should *recognize* these forms (reading goal), not produce them freely — keep activities at comprehension level, not generation.
