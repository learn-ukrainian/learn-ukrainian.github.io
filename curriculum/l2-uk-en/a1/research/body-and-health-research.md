## Cultural Hooks

1. **Proverb (школа/спорт)**: «У здоровому тілі — здоровий дух» — used in Ukrainian schools and sports contexts. Attributed to Roman satirist Juvenal but deeply embedded in Ukrainian school culture. Learnable as a whole chunk; introduces **здоровий** in a memorable frame.

2. **Folk remedy tradition (траволікування)**: Ukrainian home medicine for cold/flu — чай з малиною (raspberry tea), чай з калиною (viburnum), меду ложка. Pharmacies stock folk-remedy teas (трав'яні чаї). Real-world connection: when a Ukrainian says «Попий чаю з малиною», this IS the first-line treatment for a cold.

3. **Green cross sign (аптека)**: The green cross (зелений хрест) universally marks pharmacies in Ukraine. Distinction between «Аптека» (full pharmacy) and «Аптечний пункт» (dispensary kiosk). Learners visiting Ukraine can immediately apply this visual knowledge — high practical value.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **«Я болю»** → **«Мені болить»** — English "I hurt/I am in pain" maps onto 1st person, but Ukrainian constructs pain as impersonal: the body part hurts. The body part is the grammatical subject; the person is experiencer in dative/genitive construction.

2. **Agreement mismatch: «У мене болять голова»** → **«У мене болить голова»** — English "my eyes hurt" = plural verb, which learners over-apply. Rule: verb agrees with the body-part subject. **болить** (sg.) / **болять** (pl. — «болять очі, болять ноги»).

3. **«Я йду в лікар»** → **«Я йду до лікаря»** — Confusing the person **лікар** with the place **лікарня**. "Going to the doctor" = direction toward a person → genitive + **до**. "Going to hospital" = **до лікарні**. This error is in the plan's teaching points — prioritize.

4. **«Я погано»** → **«Мені погано»** — English "I feel bad" → direct translation produces non-sentence in Ukrainian. «Мені погано» is impersonal; «я» is not the subject. Teach as a fixed chunk before explaining the structure.

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise**: Show 3 sentences in English: "My head hurts." / "My legs hurt." / "I feel bad." Then show the Ukrainian: «Болить голова» / «Болять ноги» / «Мені погано». Ask: What is different about how Ukrainian expresses this compared to English? Who is the "main actor" in each sentence? Let the learner notice that the BODY PART is the subject, not the person.

- **Best context**: Doctor's waiting room or a phone call to a friend explaining why you can't come — the plan's dialogue structure (formal polyclinic vs. casual friend) provides both registers naturally.

- **Contrastive pairs for disambiguation**:
  - **лікар** (person) vs **лікарня** (place) — «до лікаря» vs «до лікарні»
  - **болить** (singular subject) vs **болять** (plural subject) — «болить голова» vs «болять ноги»
  - **хворий** (state adjective: він хворий today) vs **хворіти** (process verb: він часто хворіє)
  - **за рецептом** vs **без рецепту** — pharmacy interaction pair

## Notes for Content Writing

- **«Рука» and «нога» ambiguity**: Flag explicitly that рука = arm AND hand, нога = leg AND foot. English learners expect separate words. A quick visual table (рука → ✋ arm + hand) prevents confusion.
- **Plural irregulars**: Plan mentions **око/очі, вухо/вуха** — introduce as visual pairs with a note "Ukrainian has some special plural forms for paired body parts." Don't over-explain; show the pattern.
- **«Мені» chunk teaching**: Introduce «Мені погано» / «Мені добре» as frozen expressions BEFORE explaining they involve dative. Say: "In Ukrainian, you say 'to me it is bad' — use this phrase as a whole."
- **Imperative forms available** (M47 introduced them): «Дайте, будь ласка...», «Скажіть, де аптека?», «Відкрийте рот» — use imperatives freely in dialogue sections.
- **Register contrast**: The plan calls for two dialogues — casual (friend) vs. formal (полікліника). Keep Ukrainian sentences ≤10 words per A1 sentence constraint.
