## Cultural Hooks

1. **Їжачок folk character** — їжак (hedgehog) appears in Ukrainian folk riddles and children's verse. Classic загадка: *«Не собака, а кусає?»* — їжак. Perfect Ї hook with cultural warmth.
2. **Каша as national food identity** — каша (porridge) is the quintessential Ukrainian comfort food, referenced in proverbs (*«Щи та каша — їжа наша»* — though the proverb is shared, каша is a real daily staple). Grounds А in daily life.
3. **Україна as 5-vowel showcase** — У-К-Р-А-Ї-Н-А: a 4-syllable word that contains А, Ї, and У — the country name itself teaches vowel counting. Deeply motivating for learners to count the heartbeat of their target language's name.

---

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Vowel reduction on unstressed syllables** → Pronounce all О's in молокО as the same pure /o/, not /ə/. English speakers default to schwa on unstressed syllables ("muhluhKO"). Textbook exercises target this with multi-syllable repetition drills (cf. kravtsova grade 3: наголос section).
2. **И pronounced as "ee" (І)** → Correct form: jaw relaxed, tongue body lower than for І. Minimal pair: **сир** /sɪr/ (cheese) vs **сір** /sir/ (grey) — feeling the difference is key. English has no equivalent phoneme.
3. **Iotated vowels treated as single sounds at word start** → Learners read Я as just /a/ instead of /ja/. Contrast: **мати** (mother) vs **м'яти** (to crumple) — without the /j/ onset, meaning collapses.
4. **Ї conflated with Є or І** → Ї is ALWAYS two sounds /ji/ — it never softens a preceding consonant (unlike Я/Ю/Є). Drills using їжак, їжа, Україна reinforce the invariant two-sound rule.

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise**: Present three Ukrainian words side-by-side with English cognates or near-equivalents — **мама** / **oko** / **sin** — and ask: *"How many syllables?"* Then count by vowels. Learner discovers the rule (one vowel = one syllable) before it's stated. Follow with **молоко** — three О's, three syllables — and contrast with English "milk" (no reduction). The schwa-vs-pure contrast lands viscerally.
- **Best context**: Kitchen/food vocabulary grounds vowels in daily life — каша, сир, їжа, юшка, яблуко are all things learners will encounter in Ukrainian homes and markets. Every word teaches a different vowel.
- **Contrastive pairs**:
  - **И vs І**: сир (cheese) / сік (juice) — same consonant frame, different vowel
  - **Я start vs Я after consonant**: яблуко (2 sounds) / мати→м'яти (softening function)
  - **Ї vs Є**: їжак (always /ji/) / Є (softens, can be /je/ at start or softener after consonant)
  - **О stressed vs unstressed**: молоко — all three О's the same (contrast English "photograph")

- **Textbook model**: kravtsova grade 3 (p. 142) organizes phonetics as звуки і букви → наголос — same sequence this module follows. The наголос section there uses multi-syllable repetition as the primary drill type, which maps to the fill-in syllable-count activity in this plan.

## Notes for Content Writing

- Module is pre-verbal (M2): ALL instructions in English. Ukrainian appears only in word/example containers — tables, bullet pattern boxes, blockquote syllable drills.
- The И/І section is the pedagogical core — give it the most care. A native Ukrainian И sounds wrong when Russified (closer to Я/И in Russian); emphasize jaw position and the "no exact English equivalent" flag.
- Ї cultural note (Ukrainian identity symbol) is brief but motivating — include it, don't skip it.
- Minimal pair сир/сір is ideal for И vs І; сір (grey, adjective) is verified via context though it's an adjective form — use it as a listening discrimination example, not a vocabulary target.
- Stress marks (´) are the ONLY phonetic notation allowed at A1 — no IPA, no Latin transliteration.
- Word budget is 1200: 300 words on base vowels is the largest section — stay disciplined.
