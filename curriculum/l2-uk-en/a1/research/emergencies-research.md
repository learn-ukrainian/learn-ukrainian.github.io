## Cultural Hooks
1. **112 — єдиний номер**: Ukraine transitioned to the EU-standard single emergency number 112 (combining 101/fire, 102/police, 103/ambulance) as part of digital-reform and European integration — a concrete modern fact learners can anchor to their own 911 knowledge.
2. **"Дія" та "єДопомога"**: Ukraine's Дія app (launched 2019) lets citizens report incidents and access digital documents, used widely during wartime for emergency coordination — a real-world digital hook showing Ukrainian technological leadership.
3. **Textbook model (Grade 7, Avramenko, p.81)**: The Avramenko Grade 7 textbook contains a fire-safety instruction exercise ("Як поводитися під час пожежі") using imperative forms — directly parallel to this module's register. The exercise uses: "виходьте з класу", "захищайте органи дихання", "подавайте сигнали рятувальникам" — strong model for emergency imperative phrasing.

## Common Learner Errors
1. **"Визивати" → "викликати"** — Russian-calque *визивати* does not exist in Ukrainian; correct verb is **викликати** (швидку, поліцію). Happens because Russian *вызывать* sounds similar.
2. **"Дзвонити поліцію" → "викликати поліцію"** — English speakers map "call the police" → *дзвонити поліцію* (wrong accusative without preposition). Correct: *викликати поліцію* or *дзвонити до поліції*.
3. **Nominative in emergency address → Vocative** — "Лікар!" instead of "Лікарю!" English has no vocative; learners use nominative. Avramenko Grade 7 exercises model imperative imperatives but not explicit vocative drilling — this module must fill that gap.
4. **"Небезпечний" overextension** — learners try to form adjective collocations before establishing the noun *небезпека* securely; keep adjective use minimal and anchor vocabulary as nouns + fixed phrases at A1.

## Teaching Strategy
- **Discovery exercise**: Present 3 short dispatch-call dialogues (blockquote containers) without labels — one fire, one medical, one theft. Ask learner: "What's different about how the caller says the service name?" → They notice *Лікарю!* vs *Поліціє!* before the rule is named. This mirrors the Avramenko model of presenting real instructions first, then generalizing.
- **Best context**: The 112 dispatch call scenario is the natural frame — learner plays caller, module voice plays dispatcher. Sequence: emergency type → location → problem description → instruction response.
- **Contrastive pairs**: *визивати* ✗ vs *викликати* ✓; *Лікар!* ✗ vs *Лікарю!* ✓; *дзвонити поліцію* ✗ vs *викликати поліцію* ✓ / *дзвонити до поліції* ✓

## Notes for Content Writing
- Keep Ukrainian sentences to max 10 words (A1 constraint). Emergency phrases are naturally short — this is a strength.
- Vocative forms at A1: drill only *Лікарю!*, *Поліціє!*, *Рятувальники!* (limited set, fixed-phrase treatment is fine — no paradigm table needed).
- The "lost documents" scenario (scenario 2 in Practice) productively recycles *документи* + *заява* + *поліція* — ensure these connect explicitly back to the vocabulary introduced in Presentation.
- Avoid subordinate clauses (що, якщо, тому що) — A1 forbidden. Emergency phrases are inherently short commands and statements, so this constraint is easy to respect.
- "Дія" app reference should be brief (1–2 sentences) — cultural color, not a tech tutorial.
