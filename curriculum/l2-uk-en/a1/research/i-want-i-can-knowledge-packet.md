# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: I Want I Can



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

The ability to express wants, needs, and capabilities is a cornerstone of communicative competence for A1 learners. The Ukrainian pedagogical tradition introduces these concepts via the **—Б–Ї–ї–∞–і–µ–љ–Є–є –і—Ц—Ф—Б–ї—Ц–≤–љ–Є–є –њ—А–Є—Б—Г–і–Њ–Ї** (compound verbal predicate). This structure is fundamental and appears in textbooks for native speakers from an early stage (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `8-klas-ukrmova-zabolotnyi-2025_s0060`, `8-klas-ukrmova-avramenko-2025_s0048`).

The core structure consists of a conjugated auxiliary verb (a modal verb like **—Е–Њ—В—Ц—В–Є** or **–Љ–Њ–≥—В–Є**) followed by the main verb in its infinitive form (–љ–µ–Њ–Ј–љ–∞—З–µ–љ–∞ —Д–Њ—А–Љ–∞).

**–Ъ–ї—О—З–Њ–≤—Ц –і—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–∞ (Key Verbs):**
1.  **–•–Њ—В—Ц—В–Є** (to want): Expresses desire. It's highly frequent and versatile.
2.  **–Ь–Њ–≥—В–Є** (to be able to, can): Expresses ability or permission.
3.  **–Ґ—А–µ–±–∞** (to need to, must): An impersonal form used to express necessity, providing a simpler alternative to conjugating `–Љ—Г—Б–Є—В–Є` at the A1 level.

The native teaching approach, as seen in various textbooks, anchors this grammatical structure in practical, everyday situations. Learners are asked to talk about what they want to eat, what they want to do, or what they can help with (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0207`, `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0053`). This contextual learning makes the abstract grammar immediately useful. For example, exercises often revolve around food (`–Т–Є ... –Њ–±—Ц–і–∞—В–Є?` Source: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0207`) or daily activities (`–Я—Ц–і–µ—И —Б—М–Њ–≥–Њ–і–љ—Ц –Ј –љ–∞–Љ–Є –Ї–∞—В–∞—В–Є—Б—П...? вАФ –Э–∞ –ґ–∞–ї—М, —П –љ–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г!` Source: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0053`).

The progression is logical: start with the first-person singular ("I want..."), which is most relevant to the learner, and then expand to other persons and the negative form. The structure `[conjugated modal] + [infinitive]` is explicitly defined in grammar guides for native speakers (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `11-klas-ukrmova-zabolotnyi-2019_s0355`).

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

To build a solid foundation, introduce modal verb constructions in a phased, cumulative manner.

**Step 1: Expressing Desire with Nouns (`—Е–Њ—З—Г + —Ц–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є–Ї`)**
*   **Concept:** Introduce the first-person singular `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г...` followed by a simple noun in the accusative case.
*   **Rationale:** This is the most direct way to express a want. It's communicatively powerful and grammatically simple for inanimate masculine and feminine nouns at this stage.
*   **Examples:** `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г –±–Њ—А—Й.` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `ext-ulp_youtube-264`), `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г –Ї–∞–≤—Г.` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0022`), `–Т—Ц–љ —Е–Њ—З–µ –љ–Њ–≤–Є–є —В–µ–ї–µ—Д–Њ–љ.` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0207`).

**Step 2: Expressing Desire with Actions (`—Е–Њ—З—Г + –і—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–Њ`)**
*   **Concept:** Introduce the core compound verbal predicate: `—Е–Њ—В—Ц—В–Є` + infinitive.
*   **Rationale:** This expands the learner's expressive range from objects to actions, unlocking countless possibilities. Ukrainian textbooks for native speakers emphasize this structure heavily (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `8-klas-ukrmova-zabolotnyi-2025_s0060`, `8-klas-ukrmova-avramenko-2025_s0048`).
*   **Examples:** `–Ф–Њ–±—А–µ —В–Њ–≥–Њ –≤—З–Є—В–Є, —Е—В–Њ —Е–Њ—З–µ –≤—Б–µ –Ј–љ–∞—В–Є.` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `8-klas-ukrmova-avramenko-2025_s0060`), `–Ф—Ц—В–Є —Е–Њ—З—Г—В—М —Б–њ–∞—В–Є.` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0207`).

**Step 3: Expressing Ability (`–Љ–Њ–ґ—Г + –і—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–Њ`)**
*   **Concept:** Introduce `–Љ–Њ–≥—В–Є` + infinitive to talk about ability or permission.
*   **Rationale:** This structure mirrors the `—Е–Њ—З—Г + –і—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–Њ` pattern, reinforcing the grammatical concept while introducing a new, essential meaning.
*   **Examples:** `–°–ї–Њ–≤–Њ –Љ–Њ–ґ–µ –≤—А—П—В—Г–≤–∞—В–Є –ї—О–і–Є–љ—Г...` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `8-klas-ukrmova-avramenko-2025_s0060`), `–ѓ –љ–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г!` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0053`).

**Step 4: Full Conjugation and Negation**
*   **Concept:** Teach the full present-tense conjugation for `—Е–Њ—В—Ц—В–Є` and `–Љ–Њ–≥—В–Є`. Emphasize the consonant shifts (`—В вЖТ —З`, `–≥ вЖТ –ґ`) which are a key feature of Ukrainian verb conjugation (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0212`). Introduce negation by placing `–љ–µ` before the modal verb.
*   **Rationale:** This allows learners to ask questions and talk about others, moving beyond first-person statements.
*   **Examples:** `–©–Њ —В–Є —Е–Њ—З–µ—И?`, `–Т—Ц–љ –љ–µ —Е–Њ—З–µ.`, `–Т–Њ–љ–Є –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г—В—М.`, `–Т–Є –љ–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ–µ—В–µ.` (`–љ–µ —Е–Њ—З—Г` is seen in –Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `7-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2024_s0076`).

**Step 5: Expressing Necessity with `—В—А–µ–±–∞`**
*   **Concept:** Introduce the impersonal construction `[Dative pronoun] + —В—А–µ–±–∞ + infinitive`.
*   **Rationale:** This is an extremely common way to express need or obligation in Ukrainian. It provides a high-frequency alternative to `–Љ—Г—Б–Є—В–Є` (which can be introduced later). It's also grammatically simpler as `—В—А–µ–±–∞` does not conjugate.
*   **Examples:** `–Щ–Њ–Љ—Г —В—А–µ–±–∞ –є—В–Є.` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `5-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2022_s0205`), `...–Љ–µ–љ—Ц —В—А–µ–±–∞...` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `7-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2024_s0256`).

## –Ґ–Є–њ–Њ–≤—Ц –њ–Њ–Љ–Є–ї–Ї–Є L2 (Common L2 Errors)

Learners transitioning from English often make predictable errors. Prevention is key.

| вЭМ –Я–Њ–Љ–Є–ї–Ї–Њ–≤–Њ | вЬЕ –Я—А–∞–≤–Є–ї—М–љ–Њ | –І–Њ–Љ—Г |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **—Ч–Љ**.` | `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **—Ч—Б—В–Є**.` | **Double Conjugation:** English speakers may try to conjugate the second verb (`I want I eat`) instead of using the infinitive. The rule is firm: a conjugated modal verb is followed by an infinitive (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `8-klas-ukrmova-zabolotnyi-2025_s0060`). |
| `–ѓ –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г **–і–Њ** —З–Є—В–∞—В–Є.` | `–ѓ –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г —З–Є—В–∞—В–Є.` | **Phantom Preposition:** English "I can read" is structurally different from "I want **to** read". Learners may incorrectly try to insert a preposition analogous to "to" before the infinitive. In Ukrainian, no preposition is used here. |
| `–•–Њ—З—Г—В—М –ї—О–і–Є —Б–њ–∞—В–Є.` | `–Ы—О–і–Є —Е–Њ—З—Г—В—М —Б–њ–∞—В–Є.` | **Word Order (SVO):** While Ukrainian has flexible word order, learners should master the standard Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order first. Inverting the subject and verb can sound overly poetic or be confusing for beginners. |
| `–ѓ –љ–µ **—Е–Њ—З—Г —Ч—Б—В–Є**.` (stress on —Ч—Б—В–Є) | `–ѓ **–љ–µ** —Е–Њ—З—Г —Ч—Б—В–Є.` (stress on –љ–µ) | **Placement of `–љ–µ` and Negation:** Learners may try to negate the infinitive (`I want *not to eat*`). In Ukrainian, the particle `–љ–µ` negates the conjugated modal verb directly (`–љ–µ —Е–Њ—З—Г`, `–љ–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г`). (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `7-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2024_s0076`). |
| `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **–≤–Њ–і–∞**.` | `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **–≤–Њ–і—Г**.` | **Accusative Case:** When a modal verb is followed by a noun object, that noun must be in the accusative case. For feminine nouns ending in -–∞/—П, this means changing the ending to -—Г/—О. A1 lessons should start with masculine inanimate nouns where this change isn't visible, but this error must be addressed as feminine nouns are introduced. |

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

Teaching Ukrainian requires a conscious effort to de-link it from Russian in the learner's mind. The goal is to build a distinct Ukrainian phonological and grammatical system from scratch.

1.  **Independent Sound System:** The verb `—Е–Њ—В—Ц—В–Є` must be taught with the authentic Ukrainian pronunciation [x…ФЋИt ≤ite], not through the lens of Russian `—Е–Њ—В–µ—В—М` [x…РЋИt ≤et ≤]. The vowel sounds are different. Avoid comparisons like "it's like the Russian word but...".
2.  **Authentic Conjugation Patterns:** The consonant alternations in `—Е–Њ—В—Ц—В–Є` (`—В` вЖТ `—З`: `—Е–Њ—З—Г`, `—Е–Њ—З–µ—И`) and `–Љ–Њ–≥—В–Є` (`–≥` вЖТ `–ґ`: `–Љ–Њ–ґ—Г`, `–Љ–Њ–ґ–µ—И`) are ancient, integral features of the Ukrainian language. They should be presented as a core rule, not an exception. Textbooks for natives drill this extensively (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0212`).
3.  **Cultural Vocabulary is Ukrainian:** Food is a primary domain for A1 learners. Words like `–±–Њ—А—Й`, `–≤–∞—А–µ–љ–Є–Ї–Є`, `–Ї–∞—И–∞`, `—Б–∞–ї–Њ` are not just generic "Slavic" terms; they are pillars of Ukrainian culture and cuisine (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `ext-ulp_youtube-84`, `ext-ulp_youtube-264`). Emphasize that `–±–Њ—А—Й` is a Ukrainian national dish, now recognized by UNESCO, and its history is deeply intertwined with Ukraine (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `ext-ulp_youtube-84`).
4.  **Avoid Surzhyk:** Be aware of code-mixing (`—Б—Г—А–ґ–Є–Ї`). The podcast episode mentioning the experience of an American in Vinnytsia highlights how prevalent and confusing this can be (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `ext-ulp_youtube-154`). All teaching materials must use standard literary Ukrainian exclusively.

# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: I Eat I Drink



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

The core methodological principle for introducing "I eat, I drink" at the A1 level is to move from simple identification to active use through the introduction of the Accusative case. Ukrainian pedagogy emphasizes a structured, cyclical approach where vocabulary is introduced in thematic blocks and immediately put into grammatical practice.

1.  **Thematic Vocabulary Blocks:** Native-speaker textbooks introduce food and drink vocabulary in clear, logical groups. The writer should follow this model. For example, a Grade 5 textbook for Hungarian speakers groups words by question (`–©–Њ?`, `–©–Њ —А–Њ–±–Є—В–Є?`) and then by category: `–°—В—А–∞–≤–Є` (Dishes), `–Я—А–Њ–і—Г–Ї—В–Є` (Products), `–§—А—Г–Ї—В–Є` (Fruits), `–Ю–≤–Њ—З—Ц` (Vegetables), and `–°–Љ–∞–Ї` (Taste) (Source `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0022`). This method helps learners build a mental map of the vocabulary domain.

2.  **Verb-Noun Pairing:** The curriculum should immediately link a new noun to its relevant verb. For instance, when teaching `—Б–љ—Ц–і–∞–љ–Њ–Ї` (breakfast), also teach the verb `—Б–љ—Ц–і–∞—В–Є` (to have breakfast). Similarly, pair `–Њ–±—Ц–і` with `–Њ–±—Ц–і–∞—В–Є` and `–≤–µ—З–µ—А—П` with `–≤–µ—З–µ—А—П—В–Є` (Source `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0022`, Source `ext-ulp_youtube-255`). This reinforces the connection and moves the learner from passive vocabulary to active use.

3.  **Grammar in Context (The Accusative Case):** The concept of a direct object (`–Ј–љ–∞—Е—Ц–і–љ–Є–є –≤—Ц–і–Љ—Ц–љ–Њ–Ї`) is central to this topic. It should not be taught as a dry grammar table. Instead, introduce it through high-frequency sentence patterns like "–ѓ —Ч–Љ...", "–ѓ –њ'—О...", "–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г...". The learner first hears and mimics the pattern, for example, `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г –≤–Њ–і—Г` (Source `ext-ulp_youtube-119`). Only after they are comfortable with the pattern should the rule (feminine `-–∞` вЖТ `-—Г`) be explained. This "pattern before rule" approach is crucial for internalizing the case system naturally. Textbooks for native speakers demonstrate this by showing contrasting sentences like ¬Ђ–Э–µ—Б–µ –У–∞–ї—П –≤–Њ–і—Г¬ї where `–≤–Њ–і—Г` is the object (Source `5-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2022_s0219`).

4.  **Interactive Practice through Q&A:** Learning is solidified through simple, repetitive questions. For example: `–©–Њ —В–Є —Ч—Б–Є?` (What are you eating?), `–©–Њ —В–Є –њ'—Ф—И?` (What are you drinking?), `–ѓ–Ї—Г –≤–Њ–і—Г —В–Є –≤–ґ–Є–≤–∞—Ф—И?` (What water do you consume?) (Source `3-klas-ukrainska-mova-ponomarova-2020-1_s0008`). This encourages active recall and production from the very beginning.

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

The introduction must be carefully scaffolded to prevent cognitive overload. Follow this sequence strictly.

1.  **Step 1: Core Verbs & Pronouns:** Introduce the two most critical verbs: `—Ч—Б—В–Є` (to eat) and `–њ–Є—В–Є` (to drink). Teach only the 1st person singular forms: **`–ѓ —Ч–Љ`** (I eat) and **`–ѓ –њ'—О`** (I drink). (Source `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0206`, Source `ext-ulp_youtube-255`).

2.  **Step 2: Basic Nouns (Nominative Case):** Introduce 5-7 essential, high-frequency food and drink nouns in their dictionary (nominative) form. Focus on items that do not require complex explanations.
    *   `–≤–Њ–і–∞` (water), `—З–∞–є` (tea), `–Ї–∞–≤–∞` (coffee), `—Б—Ц–Ї` (juice)
    *   `—Е–ї—Ц–±` (bread), `—Б–Є—А` (cheese), `—Б—Г–њ` (soup)

3.  **Step 3: The `—Ж–µ` Construction:** Teach the first complete sentence structure using the verb "to be" (which is omitted in the present tense).
    *   `–¶–µ –≤–Њ–і–∞.` (This is water.)
    *   `–¶–µ —З–∞–є.` (This is tea.)

4.  **Step 4: Introducing the Accusative Case (Direct Object):** This is the most critical step. Use the high-frequency verb `—Е–Њ—В—Ц—В–Є` (to want) in the `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г` form.
    *   **Feminine nouns:** Explicitly show the change `–∞` вЖТ `—Г`. This is the first and most important case change for learners to master for this topic.
        *   `–≤–Њ–і–∞` вЖТ `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **–≤–Њ–і—Г**.`
        *   `–Ї–∞–≤–∞` вЖТ `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **–Ї–∞–≤—Г**.`
        (Source `ext-ulp_youtube-119`)
    *   **Masculine/Neuter nouns:** Explain that for inanimate objects, the form *does not change*. This is a point of relief for the learner.
        *   `—Б—Ц–Ї` вЖТ `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **—Б—Ц–Ї**.`
        *   `—З–∞–є` вЖТ `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **—З–∞–є**.`
        *   `–Љ–Њ–ї–Њ–Ї–Њ` вЖТ `–ѓ —Е–Њ—З—Г **–Љ–Њ–ї–Њ–Ї–Њ**.`
        (Source `ext-ulp_youtube-119`)

5.  **Step 5: Active Use with `—Ч–Љ` and `–њ'—О`:** Now, circle back to the first verbs and apply the new Accusative case knowledge.
    *   `–ѓ –њ'—О **–≤–Њ–і—Г**.` `–ѓ –њ'—О **–Ї–∞–≤—Г**.` `–ѓ –њ'—О **—Б—Ц–Ї**.`
    *   `–ѓ —Ч–Љ **—Е–ї—Ц–±**.` `–ѓ —Ч–Љ **—Б–Є—А**.` `–ѓ —Ч–Љ **—Б—Г–њ**.`

6.  **Step 6: Expanding Vocabulary:** Once the grammatical pattern is established, introduce more food and meal-related vocabulary, including meals of the day.
    *   Nouns: `—Б–љ—Ц–і–∞–љ–Њ–Ї` (breakfast), `–Њ–±—Ц–і` (lunch), `–≤–µ—З–µ—А—П` (dinner), `—А–Є–±–∞` (fish), `–Љ'—П—Б–Њ` (meat), `—Б–∞–ї–∞—В` (salad), `–±–Њ—А—Й` (borscht).
    *   Verbs: `—Б–љ—Ц–і–∞—В–Є` (to have breakfast), `–Њ–±—Ц–і–∞—В–Є` (to have lunch), `–≤–µ—З–µ—А—П—В–Є` (to have dinner).
    (Source `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0022`)

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

This section is non-negotiable. The curriculum must teach Ukrainian on its own terms, completely independent of Russian.

-   **No Russian Phonetic Analogues:** Never teach Ukrainian sounds by comparing them to Russian. For example, do not describe Ukrainian `–Є` as "like Russian —Л" or `—Ц` as "like Russian –Є". Learners must build a new, distinct phonetic system for Ukrainian from zero.
-   **Vocabulary Purity:** Strictly use Ukrainian vocabulary. The word for coffee is **`–Ї–∞–≤–∞`**. The word `–Ї–Њ—Д–µ` is a Russianism and must be actively corrected. The word for sugar is **`—Ж—Г–Ї–Њ—А`**. The word `—Б–∞—Е–∞—А` is a Russianism. Use the word lists from Ukrainian textbooks as the source of truth (e.g., Source `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0022`).
-   **`–У–Њ—А—Ц–ї–Ї–∞` vs. Vodka:** When introducing alcoholic beverages (if at all at A1), present `–≥–Њ—А—Ц–ї–Ї–∞` as a traditional Ukrainian national drink in its own right, not merely as the "Ukrainian word for vodka." (Source `ext-ulp_youtube-123`).
-   **Cultural Context:** Food idioms and proverbs are deeply tied to culture. While A1 learners won't master them, they should be taught that phrases like `–Ј–∞–≤–∞—А–Є—В–Є –Ї–∞—И—Г` (to start trouble) or `—Ч—Б—В–Є —З—Г–ґ–Є–є —Е–ї—Ц–±` (to be dependent on someone) are uniquely Ukrainian cultural expressions and not loan translations. (Source `5-klas-ukrmova-golub-2022_s0059`). The goal is to build respect for Ukrainian as a complete and independent linguistic and cultural system from day one.

## –Я—А–Є–Ї–ї–∞–і–Є –Ј –њ—Ц–і—А—Г—З–љ–Є–Ї—Ц–≤ (Textbook Examples)

1.  **Q&A Practice (Source `3-klas-ukrainska-mova-ponomarova-2020-1_s0008`):**
    *   **Prompt:** Ask and answer simple questions about what you drink.
    *   **Example:**
        *   `вАФ –ѓ–Ї—Г –≤–Њ–і—Г —В–Є –≤–ґ–Є–≤–∞—Ф—И?` (What water do you consume?)

        *   `вАФ –©–Њ —В–Є –њ'—Ф—И –љ–∞ —Б–љ—Ц–і–∞–љ–Њ–Ї?` (What do you drink for breakfast?)
        *   `вАФ –ѓ –њ'—О —З–∞–є –±–µ–Ј —Ж—Г–Ї—А—Г.` (I drink tea without sugar.) (Source `ext-ulp_youtube-255`)

2.  **Dialogue for Ordering (Source `ext-ulp_youtube-117`):**
    *   **Prompt:** Complete the dialogue to order food at a restaurant. Use the Accusative case.
    *   **Example:**
        *   `вАФ –Т–Є –≥–Њ—В–Њ–≤—Ц –Ј—А–Њ–±–Є—В–Є –Ј–∞–Љ–Њ–≤–ї–µ–љ–љ—П?` (Are you ready to make an order?)

3.  **Sentence Transformation (Implicit in Source `5-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2022_s0219`):**
    *   **Prompt:** Create a sentence using "–ѓ —Ч–Љ" or "–ѓ –њ'—О". Remember to change the noun if necessary.
    *   **Example:**
        *   Noun: `–Ї–∞–≤–∞` (f.) вЖТ Sentence: `–ѓ –њ'—О **–Ї–∞–≤—Г**.`
        *   Noun: `–±–Њ—А—Й` (m.) вЖТ Sentence: `–ѓ —Ч–Љ **–±–Њ—А—Й**.`
        *   Noun: `—А–Є–±–∞` (f.) вЖТ Sentence: `–ѓ —Ч–Љ **—А–Є–±—Г**.`

4.  **Forming Questions (Source `2-klas-ukrmova-bolshakova-2019-2_s0076`):**
    *   **Prompt:** Read the statement and write a question for it.
    *   **Statement:** `–С—Ц–ї–Є–є –≤–µ–і–Љ—Ц–і—М —Ч—Б—В—М —А–Є–±—Г.` (The polar bear eats fish.)
    *   **Possible Questions:**
        *   `**–•—В–Њ** —Ч—Б—В—М —А–Є–±—Г?` (Who eats fish?)
        *   `**–©–Њ** —Ч—Б—В—М –±—Ц–ї–Є–є –≤–µ–і–Љ—Ц–і—М?` (What does the polar bear eat?)
