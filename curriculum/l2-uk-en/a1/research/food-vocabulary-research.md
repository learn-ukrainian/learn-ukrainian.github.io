# Дослідження: Food and Drink

## State Standard Reference
**§3** (Thematic Catalogue, A1): "купівля", "ресторан", "побут" — all directly applicable to food vocabulary.
**§4.2.1.1**: Noun declension — gender of food nouns (хліб m., каша f., м'ясо n.) is core A1 morphology requirement.
**§4.2.1.2**: Adjective declension — gender agreement (свіжий хліб, гаряча каша) required at A1.
**§4.2.3.2**: Accusative case — direct object (Я хочу ка́ву, Я їм борщ) required from M25, available here at M39.
Alignment: This module satisfies §3 thematic requirement for "ресторан/купівля/побут" and exercises §4.2.1.1 + §4.2.1.2 (gender-agreement with food nouns) + §4.2.3.2 (Accusative for food verbs їсти/пити/хотіти).

## Cultural Hooks

1. **«Хліб — усьому голова»** (Bread is the head of everything) — proverb establishing хліб as the most important food; connects to хліб-сіль hospitality greeting where guests are welcomed with bread and salt. Concrete, teachable, culturally resonant.
2. **Паляниця as shibboleth** (2022) — this round wheat bread became a wartime identity marker because Russian speakers cannot easily reproduce Ukrainian-specific й sound in "па-ля-ни-ця." A real-world story that makes the vocabulary emotionally memorable.
3. **Компот** — Ukrainian (and wider Eastern European) tradition of boiling seasonal fruits (cherries, plums, apples) to make a sweet drink. Not a juice, not a tea — a uniquely Ukrainian daily drink. Textbook excerpts show it in domestic contexts. Great contrast with English-speaking students' assumption that drinks = water/juice/coffee/tea.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Gender with food adjectives** → Incorrect: \*гарячий кава, \*свіжа хліб — Correct: гаря́ча ка́ва (f.), сві́жий хліб (m.) — English has no gender so learners default to one form for all.
2. **їсти vs пити** — Incorrect: \*пити суп → Correct: їсти суп (Ukrainian treats soup as food, not drink). Textbook excerpt (Grade 6 Avramenko): "глянув на сир, на яєчню" — food items listed with їсти context.
3. **кушати instead of їсти** — Russian borrowing кушати is a Russianism; Ukrainian uses їсти exclusively. Must be flagged explicitly.
4. **Accusative form for drinks**: Incorrect: \*Я хочу кава → Correct: Я хо́чу ка́ву (Accusative feminine -у). Same for борщ → borshch stays borshch (masculine Accusative = Nominative for inanimate). The pattern shift to -у for feminine is a key target.

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise**: Show 6 pictures of Ukrainian foods with their Ukrainian labels. Ask: "Which ones do you already eat? How would you tell someone you don't eat meat?" Let learners construct Я не їм м'ясо before the rule is given. Then reveal: any food after їсти/пити stays in its base (Nominative) form after negation. Contrast: Я хочу борщ (want) → positive Accusative BUT Я не їм м'ясо → м'ясо unchanged (negation blocks Accusative shift for neuter nouns — they're identical anyway, so this is a low-stress introduction).
- **Best context**: Café/kitchen scene — "What does Olena order for breakfast?" mini-dialogue. Textbook Grade 6 Zabolotnyi excerpt provides a natural model: Яришка sets out молоко, яєчня, сир, хліб з маслом — exactly the kind of scene that anchors food vocabulary in domestic reality.
- **Contrastive pairs**: кава (f.) vs чай (m.) — so learners see gender difference in collocations: гаря́ча ка́ва / гаря́чий чай. Also їсти суп vs пити воду — verb choice determined by food/drink distinction.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **Avoid**: кушати anywhere (Russianism). Use їсти throughout.
- **Apostrophe in м'ясо**: Must be correctly rendered — this is a teaching moment about the apostrophe.
- **сир**: Explicitly note this means "cheese" — contrast with Russian сыр (same word, same meaning) and творог (cottage cheese = Ukrainian сир кисломолочний/кисляк at higher levels — don't open that at A1).
- **Паляниця section**: Keep it factual and brief (plan allocates 175 words). Don't dramatize — the cultural hook sells itself.
- **Accusative scope**: Я хочу + Accusative is safe (M25+). Мені подобається + Nominative (plan section 4) requires Dative мені — technically A2, but подобається + Dative subject is common enough at A1 that the plan explicitly includes it. Write it as a fixed phrase ("Мені подобається борщ" = "I like borscht") without explaining Dative mechanics — learners absorb it as a chunk.
- **Immersion pattern**: Paragraphs in English with Ukrainian bolded inline. Full Ukrainian sentences only in tables, blockquote dialogues, or pattern boxes. No same container type twice in a row.
