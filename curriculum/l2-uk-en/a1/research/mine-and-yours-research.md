# Дослідження: Mine and Yours

## State Standard Reference
§4.2.1.4 + §4.2.2: "Personal pronouns — basic declension" and "Pronoun gender and number forms"

## Cultural Hooks

1. **Перехід на "ти"** — The ritual of switching from formal **ваш** to informal **твій** address is a real social milestone in Ukrainian relationships. Acquaintances may use formal "ваш" for months before one person proposes "давайте на ти" — and that moment changes the pronoun system they use for each other's possessions permanently.
2. **Бюро знахідок** (Lost and Found) — A vivid real-world scenario found in Ukrainian train stations and airports. Asking "Чий це паспорт?" or "Це ваша сумка?" is authentic first-day-in-Ukraine language. The scenario naturally generates all possessive question forms without artificial drilling.
3. **Proverb: "Не займай чужого"** (Don't touch what belongs to others) — A common parental saying that encodes the чуже/своє distinction. Can anchor свій culturally as "what's one's own" vs чуже "what belongs to another."

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Gender mismatch on variable forms** → *мій мама* → **моя мама** — English has no gender agreement; learners default to мій for all nouns regardless of gender. The fix is pairing each possessive form explicitly with its gender trigger noun.
2. **Using "їх" as possessive** → *їх будинок* → **їхній будинок** — Calque from Russian "их дом." The Avramenko Grade 7 textbook explicitly flags this as a standard violation. Must be presented as a decolonization marker, not just a correction.
3. **його/її instead of свій** → *Він взяв його книгу* (ambiguous — "his" = someone else's) → **Він взяв свою книгу** ("his own") — English uses "his" for both meanings; Ukrainian enforces the distinction structurally. The textbook Grade 7 (Авраменко) has pronoun substitution exercises that target exactly this.
4. **Case freeze on possessed noun** → treating "мій" as fixed while forgetting the possessed noun must also decline. At A1 nominative is primary, but learners notice mismatch quickly when they hit "без мого телефону."

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise**: Show 3 items on a table — a phone, a bag, a book. Label each with a speech bubble: "Це мій телефон." / "Це моя сумка." / "Це моє взуття." Ask: "What changed? What stayed the same?" Learners notice мій/моя/моє before the rule is stated. Then introduce: "Це не мій телефон. Чий він?" → "Це його телефон. / Це її телефон." This makes invariable його/її immediately contrastive.
- **Best context**: Lost & Found desk (Бюро знахідок) — a clerk holding items, asking "Чий це телефон?" Passengers claiming: "Мій!" / "Не мій." / "Це його речі." Covers question forms, affirmation, denial, and third-person in one scene.
- **Contrastive pairs**: (1) мій/моя/моє vs його/її (variable vs invariable) — present side-by-side in a table. (2) їхній vs *їх* — mark *їх* as ❌ immediately. (3) його vs свій — "Він бачить його маму" (someone else's mother) vs "Він бачить свою маму" (his own mother) — one sentence, one swap, big meaning change.
- **Textbook model**: Avramenko Grade 7 занять with pronoun question tables (питання: чий? чия? чиє?) serves as the structural model for the chiy/chya/chye paradigm section.

## Notes for Content Writing

- The plan's immersion strategy uses English-paragraph + bolded Ukrainian inline, not full-Ukrainian prose. All grammar explanation stays in English.
- Present свій as a "bonus advanced point" — it's genuinely hard and A1 learners only need recognition-level understanding at this stage.
- Max 10 words per Ukrainian example sentence; single clause only.
- Do NOT use imperatives in instructions (see sequence constraints). Use "Notice that..." / "Look at the table..." in English.
- їхній section should include a brief decolonization note — "їх as possessive = Russianism" — framed as language quality, not political lecture.
