## Cultural Hooks
1. **Друг vs. знайомий**: In Ukrainian culture, *друг* implies a soul-level bond — someone you trust with your life. A mere acquaintance is *знайомий*. Saying "це мій друг" to introduce someone signals genuine closeness. This makes "я бачу мого друга" culturally loaded in a way English "I see my friend" is not.
2. **Diminutives as adult language**: Unlike English, where diminutives like "mommy" can sound childish from adults, Ukrainian adults routinely say *матуся*, *татусь*, *братик*, *сестричка* to express warmth — not infantilism. This normalizes these forms as natural vocabulary, not baby-talk.
3. **Naming pets in Ukrainian**: Ukrainians widely keep cats (*кіт*) and dogs (*пес*); grammatical animacy applies to all animals. The animal animate rule is a real-life hook: "Я маю кота" vs. the common learner error "Я маю кіт."

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Masculine animate trap** → "Я бачу брат" instead of "Я бачу брáта" — English has no case endings, so learners leave the nominative form unchanged. The textbook exercise (Grade 5 Litvinova, p. 214: "Катерина запросила подругу / Подруга запросила Катерину") models exactly this accusative transformation pattern.
2. **Feminine overthinking** → "Я бачу сестри" instead of "Я бачу сестрý" — learners hear "Acc=Gen for animates" and over-apply it to feminines. Feminines follow the same -у/-ю rule regardless of animacy.
3. **Animal animacy confusion** → "Я маю кіт" instead of "Я маю кота" — learners transfer L1 intuition (English treats animals as inanimate grammatically) and miss that Ukrainian grammar classifies pets as animate.
4. **Profession soft-group endings** → "Я знаю вчитель" instead of "Я знаю вчителя" — soft-group masculines (вчитель, лікар) end in -я, not -а, in the accusative. Learners who have only seen hard-group (-а) endings generalize incorrectly.

## Teaching Strategy
- **Discovery exercise**: Show a split screen of two sentences with the same verb: "Я бачу стіл." / "Я бачу брата." Ask the learner: "Same verb, same subject — what's different about the noun? Why does *брат* change but *стіл* stays the same?" Let the learner notice the pattern before naming it. This mirrors the Grade 5 Litvinova p. 214 approach of presenting minimal pairs (Катерина запросила подрýгу / Подрýга запросила Катерину) so the pattern emerges inductively.
- **Best context**: Family introductions and café/street encounters: "Ти знаєш мого брата?" / "Я чекаю маму." These are daily life triggers where animate direct objects arise naturally. The HR Recruiter persona (plan) also allows "Я знаю кандидата" framing.
- **Contrastive pairs** (animate vs. inanimate, same verb):
  - Я бачу стіл. → Я бачу брата. (masc. animate Acc=Gen)
  - Я маю книгу. → Я маю сестру. (fem. animate = standard Acc)
  - Я люблю місто. → Я люблю кота. (animate animal)

## Notes for Content Writing
- The "Heart Words vs. Stone Words" metaphor already baked into the plan's warm-up is strong — lean into it; use it as the recurring anchor throughout.
- Колéга is a useful vocab item but note: it declines like a feminine noun even when referring to a male colleague → Acc is *колегу* regardless. Flag this explicitly as a mini-trap.
- Пес → пса is a stem change (not just an ending change); mention it once with a note, don't make it a focal point at A1.
- Keep animal examples to кіт/пес only — don't introduce птах, риба, etc. (out of A1 scope).
- The checklist at the end (Acc=Gen or not?) should be a table with 3 rows: masc. animate / fem. animate / inanimate (any gender) — the clearest summary format.
- Subordinate clauses (який, що) are FORBIDDEN at A1 — all example sentences must be single-clause, max 10 words.
