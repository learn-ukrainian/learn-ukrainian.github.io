# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: People Around Me



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)
The core of teaching "People Around Me" at the A1 level is to build from the self outwards: me (`—П`), you (`—В–Є`), my family (`–Љ–Њ—П —Б—Ц–Љ'—П`), my friends (`–Љ–Њ—Ч –і—А—Г–Ј—Ц`), and then their roles (professions). The approach should be communicative and pattern-based, mirroring how Ukrainian children learn about their social circle.

1.  **Start with Identification, Not Deep Grammar:** The initial focus is on simple identification using `–¶–µ...` (This is...). Example: `–¶–µ –Љ–Њ—П –Љ–∞–Љ–∞. –¶–µ –Љ—Ц–є —В–∞—В–Њ.` (Source 37). This immediately and intuitively introduces noun gender through the possessive pronouns `–Љ—Ц–є/–Љ–Њ—П` without needing to explain the concept of gender itself.

2.  **Introduce Verbs through Natural Context:** Key verbs like `–њ—А–∞—Ж—О–≤–∞—В–Є` (to work) and `–Ј–≤–∞—В–Є` (to be called) are introduced in simple sentences. The present tense of `–±—Г—В–Є` (to be) is typically omitted, which is a key feature of Ukrainian. Instead of `–Ь–Њ—П –Љ–∞–Љ–∞ —Ф –ї—Ц–Ї–∞—А`, the natural phrasing is `–Ь–Њ—П –Љ–∞–Љ–∞ вАФ –ї—Ц–Ї–∞—А` (My mom is a doctor) (Source 18).

3.  **Use Dialogues for Social Context:** Learning about people is inherently social. Short, simple dialogues are the primary vehicle for teaching greetings, introductions, and asking about others. For example, `вАФ –•—В–Њ —Ж–µ? вАФ –¶–µ –Љ–Њ—П —Б–µ—Б—В—А–∞ –Ъ–∞—В—П.` (вАФ Who is this? вАФ This is my sister Katia.) (Source 18). This format also naturally introduces question words (`—Е—В–Њ?`, `–і–µ?`, `–Ї–Є–Љ?`).

4.  **Teach Cases Communicatively, Not Theoretically:** At A1, learners don't need to memorize declension tables. Instead, cases are taught through functional "chunks."
    *   **Nominative (–Э–∞–Ј–Є–≤–љ–Є–є):** For the subject. `–Ь–∞–Љ–∞ –њ—А–∞—Ж—О—Ф.` (Mom works.) (Source 37).
    *   **Accusative (–Ч–љ–∞—Е—Ц–і–љ–Є–є):** For the direct object. `–ѓ –±–∞—З—Г –Љ–∞–Љ—Г.` (I see Mom.) This is one of the first and most critical case distinctions for an L2 learner (Source 12, 20).
    *   **Vocative (–Ъ–ї–Є—З–љ–Є–є):** For direct address. `–Ь–∞–Љ–Њ, –њ—А–Є–≤—Ц—В!` (Mom, hello!). This case is vital for politeness and natural speech and should be introduced early (Source 13, 27). It's a major point of difference from Russian and a feature of authentic Ukrainian speech.

5.  **Focus on Real-World Application:** Learners should immediately be able to apply what they've learned. Exercises should involve describing a family photo, writing a simple shopping list (`—Б–њ–Є—Б–Њ–Ї –њ–Њ–Ї—Г–њ–Њ–Ї`) for family members, or creating a simple dialogue inviting a friend to a party (`–Ј–∞–њ—А–Њ—Б–Є—В–Є —В–µ–±–µ –≤ –≥–Њ—Б—В—Ц`) (Source 7, 5).

The goal is to build a functional scaffolding of language that allows learners to talk about their immediate social world. Abstract grammar rules come later; at A1, it's all about concrete, repeatable patterns (Source 2, 5).

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

**Step 1 вЖТ Core Vocabulary: Immediate Family & Friends**
*   Introduce the core nouns: `–Љ–∞–Љ–∞, —В–∞—В–Њ, –±—А–∞—В, —Б–µ—Б—В—А–∞, –і—А—Г–≥, –њ–Њ–і—А—Г–≥–∞, —Б—Ц–Љ'—П` (family). These are high-frequency and immediately useful (Source 37).
*   Pair them with possessive pronouns to implicitly teach gender: `–Љ—Ц–є —В–∞—В–Њ, –Љ—Ц–є –±—А–∞—В, –Љ—Ц–є –і—А—Г–≥` vs. `–Љ–Њ—П –Љ–∞–Љ–∞, –Љ–Њ—П —Б–µ—Б—В—А–∞, –Љ–Њ—П –њ–Њ–і—А—Г–≥–∞`.

**Step 2 вЖТ Simple Identification & Naming**
*   Use the structure `–¶–µ + [person]`. Example: `–¶–µ –Љ–Њ—П —Б—Ц–Љ'—П –љ–∞ —Д–Њ—В–Њ–≥—А–∞—Д—Ц—Ч.` (This is my family in the photo) (Source 18).
*   Introduce naming with `–Щ–Њ–≥–Њ/—Ч—Ч –Ј–≤–∞—В–Є...` (His/her name is...). Example: `–¶–µ –Љ—Ц–є –±—А–∞—В. –Щ–Њ–≥–Њ –Ј–≤–∞—В–Є –Ф–µ–љ–Є—Б.` (Source 18).

**Step 3 вЖТ Professions**
*   Introduce common professions using the structure `[Person] + [profession]`. Example: `–Ь–Њ—П –Љ–∞–Љ–∞ вАУ –ї—Ц–Ї–∞—А, –∞ —В–∞—В–Њ вАУ –≤–Є–Ї–ї–∞–і–∞—З.` (My mom is a doctor, and dad is a university teacher) (Source 18).
*   Introduce gendered pairs where they are common: `–≤—З–Є—В–µ–ї—М / –≤—З–Є—В–µ–ї—М–Ї–∞`, `—Б—В—Г–і–µ–љ—В / —Б—В—Г–і–µ–љ—В–Ї–∞` (Source 11).
*   Also, point out common professions that use the masculine form for both genders, like `–і–Є–Ј–∞–є–љ–µ—А`, `–њ—А–Њ–≥—А–∞–Љ—Ц—Б—В`, `–Љ–µ–љ–µ–і–ґ–µ—А` (Source 11, 18).

**Step 4 вЖТ Grammatical Roles: Subject vs. Object (Nominative vs. Accusative)**
*   Introduce the Nominative case (—Е—В–Њ? —Й–Њ?) as the "doer" of the action. The initial vocabulary is already in this form. `–Ь–∞–Љ–∞ —З–Є—В–∞—Ф.` (Mom is reading.) (Source 37, 40).
*   Introduce the Accusative case (–Ї–Њ–≥–Њ? —Й–Њ?) as the "receiver" of the action. Start with feminine nouns, as the change is obvious: `–ѓ –ї—О–±–ї—О –Љ–∞–Љ**—Г**.` vs. `–ѓ –ї—О–±–ї—О —Б–µ—Б—В—А**—Г**.` (Source 12, 20).
*   Then, introduce the Accusative for masculine animate nouns, which is a major learning point: `–ѓ –±–∞—З—Г –±—А–∞—В**–∞**.` (I see my brother.) Contrast this with inanimate nouns: `–ѓ –±–∞—З—Г —Б—В—Ц–ї.` (I see the table.) (Source 13, 21). This distinction is fundamental.

**Step 5 вЖТ Direct Address (Vocative Case)**
*   Introduce the Vocative case for addressing people directly, as it is essential for natural communication.
*   Start with simple, common forms: `–Ь–∞–Љ–Њ!`, `–Ґ–∞—В—Г!`, `–Ф—А—Г–ґ–µ!`, `–Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–Њ!` (Source 10, 13, 30).
*   Explain that this form is used instead of the Nominative when calling out to someone or getting their attention. `–Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–Ї–Њ, —Б–Ї–∞–ґ–Є –ї–∞–≥—Ц–і–љ–µ, –і–Њ–±—А–µ —Б–ї–Њ–≤–Њ.` (Oksanka, say a kind, good word) (Source 34).

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)
**This is a mandatory section for all pedagogical briefs.** The goal is to teach Ukrainian on its own terms, free from the historical dominance of Russian-centric linguistics.

1.  **The Vocative Case is a Ukrainian Feature:** Emphasize that the robust Vocative case (`–і—А—Г–ґ–µ`, `–њ–∞–љ–µ`, `–Ю–ї–µ–љ–Њ`) is a vibrant, living part of modern Ukrainian, distinguishing it clearly from modern Russian, where the vocative is archaic or stylistically limited. It is not an optional or poetic form; it is standard grammar for direct address. (Source 10, 14, 27).

2.  **Gendered Nouns (Feminitives) are Standard:** Forms like `–≤—З–Є—В–µ–ї—М–Ї–∞`, `–њ–Є—Б—М–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є—Ж—П`, `–і–Є–Ј–∞–є–љ–µ—А–Ї–∞`, `–Ј–∞—Б–љ–Њ–≤–љ–Є—Ж—П` are not recent inventions or slang; they are a standard and evolving part of the Ukrainian language. The writer should use them consistently and naturally. (Source 4, 11). The use of these forms reflects a conscious break from the Soviet-era practice of using masculine forms as a universal default. (Source 6).

3.  **Teach Ukrainian Phonetics Directly:** Never use Russian as a phonetic reference point (e.g., "Ukrainian `–Є` is like Russian `—Л`"). This builds an incorrect phonetic base. Ukrainian `–Є` and `—Ц` are distinct sounds that must be learned from native audio examples. The historical pressure to Russify Ukrainian, including language, was a deliberate imperial policy (`–Х–Љ—Б—М–Ї–Є–є —Г–Ї–∞–Ј`), and consciously choosing to be a "—Б–≤—Ц–і–Њ–Љ–∞ —Г–Ї—А–∞—Ч–љ–Ї–∞" (conscious Ukrainian) was an act of identity. (Source 6, 16).

4.  **Avoid "False Friends" with Russian:** The writer must be aware of words that exist in both languages but have different meanings or connotations. For example, `–і—А—Г–ґ–Є–љ–∞` in Ukrainian primarily means "wife," whereas in Russian, it means "squad" or "retinue." While not directly related to this topic, the principle applies. Vocabulary should be sourced from Ukrainian explanatory dictionaries and usage guides (`–°–£–Ь-11`, Source 29).

## –°–ї–Њ–≤–љ–Є–Ї–Њ–≤–Є–є –Љ—Ц–љ—Ц–Љ—Г–Љ (Vocabulary Boundaries)

#### –Ж–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є–Ї–Є (Nouns)
*   **Family & People (–°—Ц–Љ'—П —В–∞ –ї—О–і–Є):**
    *   вШЕвШЕвШЕ: `—Б—Ц–Љ'—П` (family), `–Љ–∞–Љ–∞` (mom), `—В–∞—В–Њ` (dad), `–±–∞—В—М–Ї–Є` (parents), `–±—А–∞—В` (brother), `—Б–µ—Б—В—А–∞` (sister), `–і—А—Г–≥` (male friend), `–њ–Њ–і—А—Г–≥–∞` (female friend), `–ї—О–і–Є` (people). (Source 37, 17, 26).
    *   вШЕвШЕ: `–і—Ц–і—Г—Б—М` (grandfather), `–±–∞–±—Г—Б—П` (grandmother), `—З–Њ–ї–Њ–≤—Ц–Ї` (husband), `–і—А—Г–ґ–Є–љ–∞` (wife), `—Б–Є–љ` (son), `–і–Њ–љ—М–Ї–∞` (daughter), `—Е–ї–Њ–њ–µ—Ж—М` (boy/boyfriend), `–і—Ц–≤—З–Є–љ–∞` (girl/girlfriend). (Source 37, 41).
    *   вШЕ: `—В—Ц—В–Ї–∞` (aunt), `–і—П–і—М–Ї–Њ` (uncle), `–Ї–Њ–ї–µ–≥–∞` (colleague), `—Б—Г—Б—Ц–і` (neighbor). (Source 41, 8).
*   **Professions (–Я—А–Њ—Д–µ—Б—Ц—Ч):**
    *   вШЕвШЕвШЕ: `–≤—З–Є—В–µ–ї—М / –≤—З–Є—В–µ–ї—М–Ї–∞` (teacher), `–ї—Ц–Ї–∞—А` (doctor), `—Б—В—Г–і–µ–љ—В / —Б—В—Г–і–µ–љ—В–Ї–∞` (student). (Source 11, 18).
    *   вШЕвШЕ: `–њ—А–Њ–≥—А–∞–Љ—Ц—Б—В` (programmer), `–і–Є–Ј–∞–є–љ–µ—А` (designer), `–Љ–µ–љ–µ–і–ґ–µ—А` (manager), `–њ—А–Њ–і–∞–≤–µ—Ж—М / –њ—А–Њ–і–∞–≤—З–Є–љ—П` (salesperson). (Source 11, 19).
    *   вШЕ: `–њ–Є—Б—М–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є–Ї / –њ–Є—Б—М–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є—Ж—П` (writer), `–Љ—Г–Ј–Є–Ї–∞–љ—В` (musician), `–≤–Њ–і—Ц–є` (driver), `–њ–µ–љ—Б—Ц–Њ–љ–µ—А / –њ–µ–љ—Б—Ц–Њ–љ–µ—А–Ї–∞` (pensioner). (Source 11, 18).

#### –Ч–∞–є–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є–Ї–Є (Pronouns)
*   **Personal (–Ю—Б–Њ–±–Њ–≤—Ц):**
    *   вШЕвШЕвШЕ: `—П, —В–Є, –≤—Ц–љ, –≤–Њ–љ–∞, –≤–Њ–љ–Њ, –Љ–Є, –≤–Є, –≤–Њ–љ–Є`.
    *   вШЕвШЕвШЕ (Accusative forms): `–Љ–µ–љ–µ, —В–µ–±–µ, –є–Њ–≥–Њ, —Ч—Ч, –љ–∞—Б, –≤–∞—Б, —Ч—Е`. (Source 7, 43).
*   **Possessive (–Я—А–Є—Б–≤—Ц–є–љ—Ц):**
    *   вШЕвШЕвШЕ: `–Љ—Ц–є, –Љ–Њ—П, –Љ–Њ—Ф, –Љ–Њ—Ч` (my); `—В–≤—Ц–є, —В–≤–Њ—П, —В–≤–Њ—Ф, —В–≤–Њ—Ч` (your). (Source 41).

#### –Ф—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–∞ (Verbs)
*   вШЕвШЕвШЕ: `–±—Г—В–Є` (to be, including its omission in the present), `–Ј–≤–∞—В–Є` (to be called), `–њ—А–∞—Ж—О–≤–∞—В–Є` (to work), `–ґ–Є—В–Є` (to live), `–Љ–∞—В–Є` (to have), `–±–∞—З–Є—В–Є` (to see), `–Ј–љ–∞—В–Є` (to know), `–ї—О–±–Є—В–Є` (to love).

## –Я—А–Є–Ї–ї–∞–і–Є –Ј –њ—Ц–і—А—Г—З–љ–Є–Ї—Ц–≤ (Textbook Examples)

**1. Activity: Matching Professions to Workplaces (Source 19)**
*   **Format:** A matching exercise. Two columns are presented, and the learner must draw a line connecting the person to their place of work.
*   **Prompt:** `–Ч–љ–∞–є–і—Ц—В—М –њ–∞—А–Є –Ј–∞ –Ј—А–∞–Ј–Ї–Њ–Љ.` (Find the pairs according to the example).
*   **Example:**
    | –•—В–Њ? (Who?) | –Ф–µ? (Where?) |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | –ї—Ц–Ї–∞—А | —И–Ї–Њ–ї–∞ |
    | –≤—З–Є—В–µ–ї—М | –ї—Ц–Ї–∞—А–љ—П |
    | –њ—А–Њ–і–∞–≤–µ—Ж—М | –±–∞–љ–Ї |
    | –µ–Ї–Њ–љ–Њ–Љ—Ц—Б—В | –Љ–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ |
*   **Goal:** Reinforces vocabulary for professions and places in a simple, visual way.

**2. Activity: Distinguishing Subject and Object (Nominative vs. Accusative) (Source 20)**
*   **Format:** Sentence comparison. The learner reads two very similar sentences where the subject and object are swapped.
*   **Prompt:** `–°–њ–Є—И—Ц—В—М —А–µ—З–µ–љ–љ—П. –Т–Є–Ј–љ–∞—З—В–µ, —П–Ї–Є–Љ–Є —З–ї–µ–љ–∞–Љ–Є —А–µ—З–µ–љ–љ—П —Ф –≤–Є–і—Ц–ї–µ–љ—Ц —Б–ї–Њ–≤–∞.` (Copy the sentences. Determine which part of the sentence the highlighted words are).
*   **Example:**
    *   `**–Ъ–∞—В–µ—А–Є–љ–∞** –Ј–∞–њ—А–Њ—Б–Є–ї–∞ **–њ–Њ–і—А—Г–≥—Г**.` (Kateryna invited her friend.) -> –Я—Ц–і–Љ–µ—В: –Ъ–∞—В–µ—А–Є–љ–∞ (–Э.–≤.), –Ф–Њ–і–∞—В–Њ–Ї: –њ–Њ–і—А—Г–≥—Г (–Ч–љ.–≤.)
    *   `**–Я–Њ–і—А—Г–≥–∞** –Ј–∞–њ—А–Њ—Б–Є–ї–∞ **–Ъ–∞—В–µ—А–Є–љ—Г**.` (The friend invited Kateryna.) -> –Я—Ц–і–Љ–µ—В: –Я–Њ–і—А—Г–≥–∞ (–Э.–≤.), –Ф–Њ–і–∞—В–Њ–Ї: –Ъ–∞—В–µ—А–Є–љ—Г (–Ч–љ.–≤.)
*   **Goal:** Forces the learner to focus on word endings to understand "who did what to whom," which is the core function of these cases.

**3. Activity: Forming the Vocative Case for Address (Source 14, 27)**
*   **Format:** A transformation drill. The learner is given a name or a title in the Nominative and must write it in the Vocative.
*   **Prompt:** `–£—В–≤–Њ—А—Ц—В—М –Ј–≤–µ—А—В–∞–љ–љ—П –Ј–∞ –і–Њ–њ–Њ–Љ–Њ–≥–Њ—О —Б–њ–Њ–ї—Г–Ї —Б–ї—Ц–≤ –Ј–∞ –Ј—А–∞–Ј–Ї–Њ–Љ.` (Form an address using the word combinations, following the example).
*   **Example:**
    *   *Input:* `–±—А–∞—В –°–µ—А–≥—Ц–є`, `–њ–∞–љ –њ—А–Њ—Д–µ—Б–Њ—А`, `–њ–Њ–і—А—Г–≥–∞ –У–∞–ї—П`
    *   *Output:* `–±—А–∞—В–µ –°–µ—А–≥—Ц—О`, `–њ–∞–љ–µ –њ—А–Њ—Д–µ—Б–Њ—А–µ`, `–њ–Њ–і—А—Г–≥–Њ –У–∞–ї—О`
*   **Goal:** To practice forming the vocative case, which is essential for polite and correct direct address.

**4. Activity: Fill-in-the-blanks with Personal Pronouns (Source 36)**
*   **Format:** A short paragraph with blanks where the learner must insert the correct form of a personal pronoun.
*   **Prompt:** `–°–њ–Є—И–Є, –≤—Б—В–∞–≤–ї—П—О—З–Є –Ј–∞–Љ—Ц—Б—В—М –Ї—А–∞–њ–Њ–Ї –Ј–∞–є–Љ–µ–љ–љ–Є–Ї **—В–Є** —Г –≤—Ц–і–њ–Њ–≤—Ц–і–љ–Є—Е –≤—Ц–і–Љ—Ц–љ–Ї–Њ–≤–Є—Е —Д–Њ—А–Љ–∞—Е.` (Copy, inserting the pronoun "you" in the correct case forms instead of the dots).
*   **Example:** "–І–Є —Ф (—Г –Ї–Њ–≥–Њ?) **–≤ —В–µ–±–µ** —Б–њ—А–∞–≤–ґ–љ—Ц–є –і—А—Г–≥? –Ґ–∞–Ї —А–∞–і—Ц—Б–љ–Њ –ґ–Є—В–Є, –Ї–Њ–ї–Є –њ–Њ—А—Г—З (–Ј –Ї–Є–Љ?) **–Ј —В–Њ–±–Њ—О** –і–Њ–±—А—Ц –ї—О–і–Є. –Т–Њ–љ–Є –Ј–∞–≤–ґ–і–Є –≥–Њ—В–Њ–≤—Ц –њ—А–Є–є—В–Є (–Ї–Њ–Љ—Г?) **—В–Њ–±—Ц** –љ–∞ –і–Њ–њ–Њ–Љ–Њ–≥—Г."
*   **Goal:** To drill the declension of personal pronouns in different cases within a meaningful context.

# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: Around The City



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

The core methodological approach for teaching "Around The City" at the A1 level is communicative and situational. The goal is not to exhaustively list vocabulary but to equip the learner with functional chunks to solve a real-world problem: getting lost and asking for directions. The approach should mirror how a native speaker would help a foreigner, simplifying language into clear, actionable steps.

Instruction should be built around a core dialogue pattern, as demonstrated in Ukrainian Lessons Podcast episodes (Source 22, Source 23). This involves:
1.  **Gaining attention politely:** Starting with `–Т–Є–±–∞—З—В–µ, –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞...` (Source 23).
2.  **Asking the core question:** Using the simple construction `–Ф–µ [–љ–∞–Ј–≤–∞ –Љ—Ц—Б—Ж—П]?` (e.g., `–Ф–µ –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї?`, `–Ф–µ —Ж–µ–љ—В—А?`) (Source 23).
3.  **Understanding a simple response:** Processing basic directional adverbs (`–њ—А—П–Љ–Њ`, `–њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З`, `–ї—Ц–≤–Њ—А—Г—З`) and verbs (`—Ц–і—Ц—В—М`, `–њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М`) (Source 22).
4.  **Clarifying transport:** Differentiating between types of transport like `–∞–≤—В–Њ–±—Г—Б` (bus) and `–њ–Њ—Ч–Ј–і` (train), which dictates whether one needs an `–∞–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` or `–Ј–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (Source 22).

Ukrainian elementary textbooks introduce related concepts through simple, repetitive structures. For example, exercises focus on using prepositions with locations (`–Я—Ц–і—Ц–є—И–ї–Є –і–Њ —А—Ц—З–Ї–Є`, `–°—Е–Њ–≤–∞–≤—Б—П –Ј–∞ –і–µ—А–µ–≤–Њ–Љ`) (Source 10) or listing related items to build semantic fields (`–ѓ–±–ї—Г–Ї–∞, –≥—А—Г—И—Ц, —Б–ї–Є–≤–Є... вАФ —Ж–µ —Д—А—Г–Ї—В–Є`) (Source 28). This method of grouping and association should be used for city vocabulary (e.g., `–Љ—Г–Ј–µ–є`, `—Ж–µ—А–Ї–≤–∞`, `–Љ–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ` are all places in a city).

The learning process should be scaffolded, starting with recognizing place names, then forming a question, and finally understanding a multi-step answer. Role-playing dialogues is a highly effective activity at this stage (Source 12, Source 20).

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

1.  **Core Question & Basic Locations:** Start with the most fundamental survival question: `–Ф–µ...?` (Where is?). Pair it with the most essential, high-frequency A1-level locations.
    *   `–Ф–µ —Ж–µ–љ—В—А?` (Where is the center?) (Source 23)
    *   `–Ф–µ –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї?` (Where is the station?) (Source 22)
    *   `–Ф–µ –Љ–µ—В—А–Њ?` (Where is the metro?) (Source 23)
    *   `–Ф–µ —В—Г–∞–ї–µ—В?` <!-- VERIFY -->
    This immediately gives the learner a functional tool.

2.  **Simple Positional Answers:** Introduce the simplest possible answers a person might point and give.
    *   `–Ґ—Г—В` (Here)
    *   `–Ґ–∞–Љ` (There)
    *   `–Ю–љ —В–∞–Љ` (Over there) (Source 23)

3.  **Essential Directional Commands:** Introduce the imperative verbs and adverbs for giving basic directions. Always teach the formal "–≤–Є" forms first (`-—Ц—В—М` ending) as they are safest for speaking to strangers.
    *   `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М –њ—А—П–Љ–Њ` (Go straight) (Source 22)
    *   `–Я–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` (Turn right) (Source 22)
    *   `–Я–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –ї—Ц–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` (Turn left) (Source 22)

4.  **Key Nouns for Navigation:** Introduce nouns that act as landmarks in directions.
    *   `–≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—П` (street) (Source 28)
    *   `–њ–µ—А–µ—Е—А–µ—Б—В—П` (intersection) (Source 22)
    *   `—Ж–µ—А–Ї–≤–∞` (church) (Source 2, Source 9)
    *   `–Љ–∞–≥–∞–Ј–Є–љ` (shop) (Source 28)

5.  **Combining into Short Instructions:** Practice combining the elements from steps 3 and 4.
    *   `–Ж–і—Ц—В—М –њ—А—П–Љ–Њ –њ–Њ –≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—Ц...` (Go straight on ... street) (Source 22).
    *   `–Э–∞ –њ–µ—А–µ—Е—А–µ—Б—В—Ц –њ–Њ–≤–µ—А–љ—Ц—В—М –њ—А–∞–≤–Њ—А—Г—З` (At the intersection, turn right) (Source 22).

6.  **Transportation Vocabulary:** Introduce basic modes of transport and the places associated with them. It is crucial to distinguish between `–∞–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` and `–Ј–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї`.
    *   `–∞–≤—В–Њ–±—Г—Б` (bus) вЖТ `–∞–≤—В–Њ–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (bus station) (Source 22)
    *   `–њ–Њ—Ч–Ј–і` (train) вЖТ `–Ј–∞–ї—Ц–Ј–љ–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` (railway station) (Source 22)
    *   `–Љ–µ—В—А–Њ` (metro/subway) (Source 23)

7.  **The Concept of "Needing to Take":** Introduce the impersonal construction `—В—А–µ–±–∞ —Ч—Е–∞—В–Є` (one needs to go/travel).
    *   `–Ґ—А–µ–±–∞ —Ч—Е–∞—В–Є –љ–∞ –Љ–µ—В—А–Њ.` (You need to go by metro.) (Source 23) This is a critical A1 structure that avoids complex verb conjugations.

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

This topic is highly susceptible to colonial narratives, and it is imperative to address this from the beginning.

1.  **The "Empty Land" Myth:** The Russian imperial narrative often claims that southern and eastern Ukrainian cities were "founded" by Russian monarchs (like Catherine II) on empty, wild land. This is false. Content must explicitly state that these cities were often built upon or agglomerated from pre-existing Cossack settlements. The city of Dnipro, for instance, was established on the site of the Cossack sloboda `–Я–Њ–ї–Њ–≤–Є—Ж—П` (Source 9). The textbook should present this as the norm: a Ukrainian settlement was renamed and absorbed, its history erased.

2.  **Authentic Toponymy:** Ukrainian place names have deep historical and geographical roots. Village names often derive from geography (`–У—А—П–і–Є–љ–∞` - from garden beds, Source 2), local crafts (`–≥–Њ–љ—З–∞—А—Ц` - potters, Source 1), or ancient landmarks (`–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –Ф—Г–±` - a sacred oak, Source 2). Presenting vocabulary like `–Ї—Г—В–Њ–Ї` (a neighborhood or corner of a village) (Source 2) and `—Г—А–Њ—З–Є—Й–µ` (a distinct natural landmark) (Source 2) grounds the learner in an authentic Ukrainian perception of space, rather than a generic, universal one.

3.  **No Russian Analogies:** Do not teach Ukrainian directions or locations by comparing them to Russian. For example, never say "–≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—П is like Russian —Г–ї–Є—Ж–∞." Teach Ukrainian on its own terms. Phonetics, grammar, and vocabulary should be presented as a self-contained system. The presence of Cossack, Polish, and other historical layers (Source 3) should be highlighted to show Ukraine's history is European and distinct.

4.  **Transportation Hubs as Ukrainian Spaces:** While `–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї` is an internationalism (from Vauxhall Gardens), its culture in Ukraine is distinctly Ukrainian. Train travel is a major part of Ukrainian life (Source 22). Frame `–≤–Њ–Ї–Ј–∞–ї–Є` not as generic transport hubs, but as vibrant centers of Ukrainian life, often with their own markets (`—А–Є–љ–Њ–Ї`) and social dynamics (Source 22).
