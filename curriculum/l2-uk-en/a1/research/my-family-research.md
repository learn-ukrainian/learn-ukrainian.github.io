## Cultural Hooks

1. **Прислів'я**: *"Без сім'ї нема щастя"* — proverb used by Zaболотний (Grade 5) illustrating how family is the unit of happiness in Ukrainian folk tradition. Natural opener for Why family vocab matters.
2. **Бабуся і дідусь as active caregivers**: In Ukrainian family culture, grandparents (бабусі та дідусі) frequently live with or near the family and are primary childcare providers. This contrasts with English-speaking norms and makes the vocabulary feel culturally real — the learner's "Ukrainian friend's grandma" scenario works perfectly.
3. **Клична форма in everyday speech**: Ukrainians naturally call out *"Мамо!"*, *"Тату!"* — not "Мама!" as in Russian. This vocative is one of the most immediately observable differences from Russian and a source of pride. Textbook exercise pattern from Zabolotnyi Grade 5 (p.194) explicitly drills the vocative-for-address transformation: *"Денис допомагає бабусі"* → *"Бабусю, допомагай!"*

## Common Learner Errors

1. **Possessive gender mismatch**: *мій сестра* → **моя сестра** — English has no gendered possessives, so learners default to a single form. The сестра/брат contrastive pair is the core drill here.
2. **Nominative for address**: *"Мама, іди сюди!"* → **"Мамо, іди сюди!"** — English speakers use the citation form for address; Ukrainian requires vocative. Zabolotnyi Grade 5 p.194 uses exactly this transformation exercise as a model.
3. **чоловік confusion**: Learners use чоловік to mean "man" in a sentence like *"Той чоловік — мій тато"* (correct) but then use it for husband without context, creating ambiguity. Contrastive pairs with дружина help anchor the husband meaning.
4. **Я маю vs. У мене є**: English speakers calque "I have a brother" → *Я маю брата* — grammatically not wrong but less natural than **У мене є брат** for A1 existential possession.

## Teaching Strategy

- **Discovery exercise**: Show a short family photo caption dialogue:
  > *— Хто це? — Це моя мама. — А це? — Це мій тато. — А це хто? — Це моя сестра і мій брат.*

  Before explaining possessives, ask: "Why does the speaker say *моя* before *мама* but *мій* before *тато*?" Learners notice the pattern before the rule arrives.

- **Best context**: Family photo album / family tree diagram. The "point and name" activity is universal and immediately transferable — learners can literally do this at home with their own family photos.

- **Contrastive pairs for key distinctions**:
  - `мій брат` vs. `моя сестра` (possessive gender)
  - `мама` (subject) vs. `мамо!` (address / vocative)
  - `сім'я` (nuclear, living together) vs. `родина` (extended, lineage)
  - `чоловік` as "man" (contextual) vs. `чоловік` as "husband" (possession context: *мій чоловік*)

- **Textbook model**: Zabolotnyi Grade 5 p.194 — the nominative→vocative transformation drill is directly applicable: learners rewrite family sentences replacing nominative with vocative address form.

## Notes for Content Writing

- The plan references dative for age (*"моєму братові…"*) — **flag**: dative is A2 scope per quick-ref. This should be omitted or replaced with a simpler age construction (*"Моєму братові 10 років"* can be simplified to avoid dative, or this point should be dropped entirely at A1).
- Сім'я requires the apostrophe (') not a regular quote — verify encoding in YAML and MDX output.
- Vocative section should come AFTER the learner knows the nominative forms — sequence: introduce names → possessives → then vocative as a "speaking to" layer.
- Keep adjective examples simple: добрий/добра, молодий/молода — avoid unfamiliar adjectives introduced here for the first time.
