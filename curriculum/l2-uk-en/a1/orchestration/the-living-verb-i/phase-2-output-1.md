# The Living Verb I

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Verbs are the driving force of any language. Mastering the most common conjugation pattern unlocks your ability to describe your daily life, actions, and habits.

## Вступ (Introduction)

Віта́ємо на ново́му ета́пі! (Welcome to a new stage!) 
Ми чита́ємо текст про сві́т. (We read a text about the world.)
Це стати́чні речі́. (These are static things.)
Тепе́р час для ді́ї. (Now it is time for action.)
Ми вивча́ємо дієсло́ва. (We study verbs.)
Дієсло́ва даю́ть життя́. (Verbs give life.)

This transition is a major milestone. You are now building the foundation for real conversational fluency. The ability to use verbs correctly will completely transform how you interact with native speakers. Verbs are the engine of any language. They give life and movement to your sentences. 

Сього́дні ми вивча́ємо нови́й матеріа́л. (Today we study new material.)
Це ду́же важли́ві слова́. (These are very important words.)
Ми вивча́ємо слова́ чита́ти, писа́ти і працюва́ти. (We study the words to read, to write, and to work.)
Це щоде́нне життя́. (This is daily life.)
Ви зна́єте про хо́бі. (You know about hobbies.)
Ви зна́єте про робо́ту. (You know about work.)

> [!culture]
> Кни́ги — це бага́та істо́рія. (Books are rich history.)
> Пе́рша кни́га — це «Апо́стол». (The first book is the "Apostol".)
> Кни́га ма́є старе́ похо́дження. (The book has an old origin.)
> Іва́н Фе́доров ма́є тала́нт. (Ivan Fedorov has talent.)
>
> The first known printed book in Ukraine is the «Апо́стол» (Apostol). It was printed by Ivan Fedorov in the city of Lviv in the year 1574. This historic event marks the beginning of a deep-rooted literacy tradition.

Because reading has such a rich history here, **чита́ти** (to read) is the perfect verb to start our journey into grammar. We will use it as our main example to show you how Ukrainian verbs work. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe exactly what you are doing. Let us dive into the wonderful world of the living verb.

## Презентація (Presentation)

Украї́нські дієсло́ва ма́ють нові́ закінчення. (Ukrainian verbs have new endings.)
Це зале́жить від осо́би. (This depends on the person.)
В англі́йській мо́ві це іна́кше. (In English, this is different.)
Тут ко́жна осо́ба ма́є своє́ закінчення. (Here every person has its own ending.)

Це пра́вило — дієвідмі́на. (This rule is conjugation.)
Це логі́чно і просто́. (This is logical and simple.)
Ми зна́ємо пе́ршу дієвідмі́ну. (We know the first conjugation.)
Це дієсло́ва на -ати. (These are -ати verbs.)
Сло́во «чита́ти» ма́є закінчення -ати. (The word "читати" has the -ати ending.)

This grammatical feature is called conjugation. This might seem complex at first, but it follows a very logical and predictable pattern. Let us look at the First Conjugation pattern. We often call these the "-ати" verbs because their basic dictionary form ends in those three letters. The basic dictionary form is called the infinitive. For example, the infinitive **чита́ти** means "to read".

Як знайти́ осно́ву сло́ва? (How to find the word stem?)
Ми не чита́ємо закінчення -ти. (We do not read the -ти ending.)
Осно́ва сло́ва «чита́ти» — «чита-». (The stem of the word "читати" is "чита-".)
До осно́ви ми ма́ємо нове́ закінчення. (To the stem we have a new ending.)

Think of the stem as the core identity of the verb, and the ending as the personal signature of the speaker. They work together perfectly to convey meaning. Once you have the stem, you add the correct personal ending based on who is performing the action.

Ось нові́ закінчення. (Here are the new endings.)
- Я (I) takes the ending **-ю**.
- Ти (You, informal) takes the ending **-єш**.
- Він, вона́, воно́ (He, she, it) takes the ending **-є**.
- Ми (We) takes the ending **-ємо**.
- Ви (You, formal/plural) takes the ending **-єте**.
- Вони́ (They) take the ending **-ють**.

Let us apply this rule to our main verb.

| Person | Pronoun | Stem + Ending | Result | Meaning |
|--------|---------|---------------|--------|---------|
| I | я | чита- + -ю | **я чита́ю** | I read / I am reading |
| You (informal) | ти | чита- + -єш | **ти чита́єш** | You read / You are reading |
| He/She/It | він/вона́/воно́ | чита- + -є | **він чита́є** | He reads / He is reading |
| We | ми | чита- + -ємо | **ми чита́ємо** | We read / We are reading |
| You (formal/plural) | ви | чита- + -єте | **ви чита́єте** | You read / You are reading |
| They | вони́ | чита- + -ють | **вони́ чита́ють** | They read / They are reading |

Це пра́вило ді́є для бага́тьох слів. (This rule works for many words.)
Ви зна́єте нове́ сло́во. (You know a new word.)
Воно́ ма́є закінчення -ати. (It has the -ати ending.)
Тепе́р ви зна́єте пра́вило. (Now you know the rule.)

> [!note]
> Notice that Ukrainian verbs in the present tense do two jobs at once. They describe both habitual actions and ongoing actions. The phrase **я чита́ю** means both "I read every day" and "I am reading right now". This is called the imperfective aspect. You do not need a completely separate tense for ongoing actions like you do in English.

Украї́нська мо́ва ма́є секре́т. (The Ukrainian language has a secret.)
Сло́во «я» мо́жна не писа́ти. (The word "я" can not be written / can be omitted.)
Закінчення — це осо́ба. (The ending is the person.)
Замі́сть «я чита́ю» ка́жемо «чита́ю». (Instead of "я читаю" we say "читаю".)
Це ду́же приро́дно. (This is very natural.)

Let us look at another very common verb: **зна́ти** (to know). 
Ми не чита́ємо -ти. (We do not read -ти.)
Осно́ва сло́ва — «зна-». (The word stem is "зна-".)

Я зна́ю все. (I know everything.)
Вона́ зна́є пра́вду. (She knows the truth.)
Ми не зна́ємо. (We do not know.)

## Практика (Practice)

Тепе́р ми працю́ємо. (Now we work.)
Ми не ро́бимо поми́лки. (We do not make mistakes.)
Ми зна́ємо пра́вила. (We know the rules.)

The first common mistake is "Infinitive Abuse". Because English verbs do not change much, learners often try to use the basic dictionary form in sentences. They try to translate "I read" word-for-word and say "Я читати." This is incorrect. It sounds like "I to read." You must always conjugate the verb to match the person.

Перша пробле́ма — це інфініти́в. (The first problem is the infinitive.)
В англі́йській мо́ві слова́ не змі́нюються. (In English, words do not change.)
Лю́ди ча́сто ка́жуть «я чита́ти». (People often say "I to read".)
Це поми́лка. (This is a mistake.)
Ми за́вжди змі́нюємо дієсло́во. (We always change the verb.)

Incorrect: Я розумі́ти все. (I to understand everything.)
Correct: Я розумі́ю все. (I understand everything.)

Incorrect: Ми чека́ти на авто́бус. (We to wait for the bus.)
Correct: Ми чека́ємо на авто́бус. (We are waiting for the bus.)

The second common mistake is "Pronoun Overuse". When you speak English, you constantly say "I do this, I do that." If you repeat the word **я** before every single verb in Ukrainian, you will sound a bit robotic and unnatural. Remember the pro-drop rule we discussed earlier. You can safely drop the pronoun if the context is clear.

Ми чита́ємо ці ре́чення. (We read these sentences.)
Ми не пи́шемо всі слова́. (We do not write all words.)
Ми говори́мо приро́дно. (We speak naturally.)
Ми про́симо вас говори́ти вго́лос. (We ask you to speak aloud.)
Це до́бре для вас. (This is good for you.)

Працю́ю вдо́ма. (I work at home.)
Працю́ю бага́то. (I work a lot.)
Працю́ю над проє́ктом. (I am working on a project.)
Слу́хаю му́зику. (I am listening to music.)
Слу́хаю ува́жно. (I listen carefully.)
Ду́маю про це. (I am thinking about it.)
Ду́маю бага́то. (I think a lot.)
Гра́ю в і́гри. (I play games.)
Гра́ю на гіта́рі. (I play the guitar.)

> [!tip]
> Це секре́т украї́нської мо́ви. (This is a secret of the Ukrainian language.)
> Без сло́ва «я» ре́чення ду́же га́рне. (Without the word "я" the sentence is very beautiful.)
> Лю́ди зна́ють це пра́вило. (People know this rule.)

We can also build sentences with objects. To avoid complex grammar rules right now, we will use inanimate objects. For these specific words, the form does not change when they become the target of your action. This lets you build sentences easily and confidently. Do not worry if you make mistakes at first. Language learning is a process of gradual refinement. The most important thing is that you are now constructing real, active sentences.

Чита́ю кни́гу. (I am reading a book.)
Чита́ю вго́лос. (I am reading aloud.)
Чита́ю нови́ни. (I am reading the news.)
Пишу́ листа́. (I am writing a letter.)
Пишу́ повідо́млення. (I am writing a message.)
Га́рно пишу́. (I write beautifully.)
Слу́хаю ра́діо. (I am listening to the radio.)
Пита́ю про це. (I am asking about it.)
Чека́ю на ві́дповідь. (I am waiting for an answer.)
Вивча́ю мо́ву. (I am studying the language.)
Вивча́ю слова́. (I am studying words.)

Ви зна́єте про свій день. (You know about your day.)
Ви зна́єте про відпочи́нок. (You know about rest.)
Це ду́же прості́ структу́ри. (These are very simple structures.)

Відпочива́ю вдо́ма. (I rest at home.)
Відпочива́ю влі́тку. (I rest in the summer.)
Не розумі́ю. (I do not understand.)

By combining correctly conjugated verbs with simple objects or adverbs, you create full, meaningful sentences. You are now communicating real actions.

## Підсумок та культурний аспект (Summary and Cultural Insight)

Ми зна́ємо бага́то но́вого. (We know a lot of new things.)
Ви зна́єте акти́вне життя́. (You know active life.)
Пе́рша дієвідмі́на — це ключ. (First conjugation is a key.)
Тепе́р ви зна́єте ці пра́вила. (Now you know these rules.)

We have covered a massive amount of ground today. You have successfully transitioned from merely naming objects to describing an active, moving life. The First Conjugation pattern is like a master key. By learning this single framework, you instantly unlock the ability to use hundreds of everyday Ukrainian verbs. Once you know how to conjugate **чита́ти**, you automatically know how to conjugate **писа́ти**, **працюва́ти**, and **слу́хати**. This is the beauty of a structured grammar system. Grammar is not just a set of rules; it is the underlying logic that holds the language together. Embracing this logic will make your learning journey incredibly rewarding.

> [!did-you-know]
> В Украї́ні є відо́ме прислі́в'я. (In Ukraine there is a famous proverb.)
> Пти́цю пізна́ти по пі́р'ю. (A bird is known by its feathers.)
> Люди́ну пізна́ти по мо́ві. (A person is known by their speech.)
> 
> This means that your words and how you use them reflect your character. Speaking accurately shows respect for the language and the culture.

Слова́ про регуля́рні ді́ї. (Words about regular actions.)
Слова́ про пото́чні ді́ї. (Words about current actions.)
Ви говори́те без всі́х слів. (You speak without all words.)
Ви говори́те про свій день. (You speak about your day.)

You learned that verbs in the present tense describe both habitual actions and ongoing actions. There is no need to worry about complex tense structures just yet. You also learned how to drop unnecessary pronouns to make your sentences flow naturally like a native speaker. Now, you can confidently describe your daily activities. You can say that you work, you read, you rest, and you understand. 

Test your knowledge with these quick questions. If you can answer them, you are perfectly ready for the next step.

1. Яке́ закінчення для «ми»? (What is the ending for "we"?) — Закі́нчення -ємо / The ending is -ємо.
2. Чи завжди́ тре́ба писа́ти «я»? (Is it always necessary to write "I"?) — Ні, закінчення — це осо́ба / No, the ending is the person.
3. Як знайти́ осно́ву сло́ва «чита́ти»? (How to find the stem of the word "читати"?) — Ми не пи́шемо -ти. Осно́ва — «чита-» / We do not write -ти. The stem is "чита-".
4. Як сказа́ти "I am reading"? (How to say "I am reading"?) — Чита́ю / I read.

Keep practicing these verbs in your daily life. Every time you perform an action, try to name it in Ukrainian. Your journey into the living language has officially begun.
