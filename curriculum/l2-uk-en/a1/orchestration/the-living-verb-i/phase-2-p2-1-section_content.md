## Презентація: Перша дієвідміна

### The Sleeping Verb

Imagine you buy a new mixer for your kitchen. It is shiny and beautiful. But if you do not plug it in, it does nothing. It just sits on the counter. In Ukrainian, verbs in the dictionary are like that unplugged mixer. We call this the **інфінітив** (infinitive).

You can easily spot these "sleeping verbs". They almost always end in **-ти**.
*   **читати** [tʃɪˈtɑtɪ] — to read
*   **знати** [znɑtɪ] — to know
*   **слухати** [ˈsluxɑtɪ] — to listen

These words name the action, but they do not *perform* it. If you say «Я читати» [jɑ tʃɪˈtɑtɪ], you are saying "I to read". It sounds like a robot or a caveman. To make the verb work, we must "plug it in". We must change the ending to match the person. This is what we learn today.

### The Master Key

Here is the good news. You do not need to memorize every verb individually. Ukrainian is very logical. There is a specific group of verbs that follow one simple pattern. We call them "Type 1" verbs, or the **-ати** group.

This is your "Master Key". If you learn this pattern for one verb, you instantly know how to use hundreds of others. The logic is simple:
1.  Take the infinitive: **читати** [tʃɪˈtɑtɪ].
2.  Remove the **-ти** to find the "stem": **чита-**.
3.  Add the specific ending for the person.

> [!tip]
> **The Golden Rule**
> For this group of verbs, the stem **чита-** stays strong. It does not change. You just glue the new endings onto it. It is like Lego bricks.

### Model Verb: Читати (To Read)

Let us see this pattern in action with our model verb **читати** [tʃɪˈtɑtɪ] (to read). Watch how the endings change to tell us *who* is reading.

| Person | Ukrainian | Pronunciation | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Я** (I) | **читаю** | [tʃɪˈtɑju] | I read |
| **Ти** (You, sing.) | **читаєш** | [tʃɪˈtɑjɛʃ] | You read |
| **Він / Вона** (He/She) | **читає** | [tʃɪˈtɑjɛ] | He/She reads |
| **Ми** (We) | **читаємо** | [tʃɪˈtɑjɛmɔ] | We read |
| **Ви** (You, pl./formal) | **читаєте** | [tʃɪˈtɑjɛtɛ] | You read |
| **Вони** (They) | **читають** | [tʃɪˈtɑjutʲ] | They read |

Notice the rhythm. **Я** (I) always loves the **-ю** ending. **Ти** (You) ends with a soft hiss **-ш**. **Вони** (They) has the long tail **-ють**.

*   **Я читаю журнал.** [jɑ tʃɪˈtɑju ʒurˈnɑl] — I read a magazine.
*   **Ми читаємо тут.** [mɪ tʃɪˈtɑjɛmɔ tut] — We read here.

### Where is "I"? (The Pro-Drop Concept)

In English, you must strictly say "I read" or "We read". If you just say "Read", it sounds like a command. But Ukrainian is different. Ukrainian verbs are very informative. The ending **-ю** in **читаю** *already* signals that "I" am doing it. The ending **-ємо** in **читаємо** screams "We"!

Because the verb is so strong, we often drop the pronoun completely. This is very natural. It makes your speech faster and more fluid.

*   **Знаю.** [znɑju] — (I) know.
*   **Слухаєш?** [ˈsluxɑjɛʃ] — (Do you) listen?
*   **Чекаємо.** [tʃɛˈkɑjɛmɔ] — (We) are waiting.

> [!context]
> **Modesty in Language**
> Ukrainians focus on the action, not the ego. Using "Я" (I) in every sentence can sound a bit self-centered or emphatic. If the context is clear, feel free to drop it. "Working" sounds better than "I am working".

### Expanding Your Toolkit

Now that you have the key, let us unlock more doors. All these essential verbs follow the exact same **читати** pattern. You just swap the stem.

*   **слухати** [ˈsluxɑtɪ] (to listen) → **Я слухаю** [jɑ ˈsluxɑju]
*   **питати** [pɪˈtɑtɪ] (to ask) → **Ми питаємо** [mɪ pɪˈtɑjɛmɔ]
*   **грати** [ˈɦrɑtɪ] (to play) → **Вони грають** [wɔˈnɪ ˈɦrɑjutʲ]
*   **знати** [znɑtɪ] (to know) → **Ти знаєш** [tɪ znɑjɛʃ]

Try to listen to the music of these words.
**Він слухає радіо.** [win ˈsluxɑjɛ ˈrɑdijo] — He listens to the radio.
**Ви знаєте все.** [wɪ ˈznɑjɛtɛ wsɛ] — You know everything.

### A Note on "Working"

There is one very common verb that fits this rhythm perfectly but looks slightly different in the dictionary: **працювати** [prɑtsʲuˈwɑtɪ] (to work).

When we conjugate it, we keep the clear, strong sound **працю-** [prɑtsʲu-].
*   **Я працюю.** [jɑ prɑˈtsʲuju] — I work.
*   **Ти працюєш.** [tɪ prɑˈtsʲujɛʃ] — You work.
*   **Вони працюють.** [wɔˈnɪ prɑˈtsʲujutʲ] — They work.

It sounds exactly like **читаю**, **читаєш**, **читають**. So do not be afraid of the longer dictionary form. The result is familiar and comfortable. You will use this word every day.
**Я працюю вдома.** [jɑ prɑˈtsʲuju ˈwdɔmɑ] — I work at home.

### Common Trap: The "Robot" Mistake

The biggest mistake beginners make is keeping the verb in the "freezer". They learn the word **знати** (to know) and say:
*   ❌ **Я знати.** (I to know.)

This is understandable, but it is wrong. It stops the flow of the sentence. Always ask yourself: "Who is doing the action?"
*   If it is me → **-ю**. (Я знаю)
*   If it is you → **-єш**. (Ти знаєш)
*   If it is us → **-ємо**. (Ми знаємо)

Make the verb live. Let it breathe and change.
**Ми знаємо місто.** [mɪ ˈznɑjɛmɔ ˈmistɔ] — We know the city.
