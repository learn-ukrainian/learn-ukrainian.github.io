## Діалоги (Dialogues)

One of the most common questions you will hear when meeting someone new in Ukraine is about your origins. Ukrainians are deeply connected to their hometowns and regions, and asking someone where they are from is a standard, polite icebreaker. 

Because of recent history, many people have moved between cities or countries, making this question even more frequent in daily conversation. We use the question word **звідки** (where from) to ask this. 

Listen to how two people meet and exchange information about their hometowns.

**Олег:** Я з України, з Києва. А ти? *(I am from Ukraine, from Kyiv. And you?)*


**Джон:** Я з Канади, із Торонто. *(I am from Canada, from Toronto.)*


**Олег:** Давно тут? *(Have you been here long?)*


**Джон:** Ні, я приїхав місяць тому. *(No, I arrived a month ago.)*


</div>


Notice that when Oleg specifies his location, he starts with the country and narrows it down to the city: **з України, з Києва** (from Ukraine, from Kyiv). This is the natural order in Ukrainian. John does the same with **з Канади, із Торонто**. You might also notice the phrase **я приїхав місяць тому** (I arrived a month ago). While we will study the past tense in a later module, this is a very common memorized phrase you can use right now if someone asks how long you have been in the country.

The word **звідки** is not only for asking about someone's permanent origin or hometown. We also use it to ask where someone is walking or driving from at this exact moment.

**Тарас:** Звідки ти йдеш? *(Where are you walking from?)*

## Звідки? (Where From?)

English uses the word "where" for almost everything related to location. "Where are you?", "Where are you going?", "Where are you from?" 

Ukrainian is much more precise. We split the concept of "where" into three completely different question words based on direction and movement. This is a fundamental concept that you must master to think in Ukrainian.

Here is the complete location trio:

*   **Де ти?** (Where are you?) — This asks about your current, static location. You answer using **в** or **на** plus the Locative case: **В Україні.** (In Ukraine.)
*   **Куди ти їдеш?** (Where are you going?) — This asks about your destination. You answer using **в** or **на** plus the Accusative case: **В Україну.** (To Ukraine.)
*   **Звідки ти?** (Where are you from?) — This asks about your origin or starting point. You answer using **з** plus the Genitive case: **З України.** (From Ukraine.)

At this stage in your learning, you do not need to memorize all the complex rules for forming the Genitive case. Instead, you should learn these common origins as fixed, memorized chunks. Just like you learned to say **в Києві** to mean "in Kyiv," you will now learn **з Києва** to mean "from Kyiv."

Let's look at the pattern for origins. To say "from," we use the preposition **з** (or its phonetic variations **із** and **зі**). 

Here are the most common country and city chunks you need to know:

*   **з України** — from Ukraine
*   **з Києва** — from Kyiv
*   **зі Львова** — from Lviv
*   **з Одеси** — from Odesa
*   **з Харкова** — from Kharkiv
*   **з Канади** — from Canada
*   **зі США** — from the USA (also commonly: **зі Штатів**)
*   **з Англії** — from England
*   **з Німеччини** — from Germany
*   **з Польщі** — from Poland

We also use this exact same pattern for everyday locations when you are walking or traveling away from them:

*   **з роботи** — from work
*   **зі школи** — from school
*   **з магазину** — from the store
*   **з банку** — from the bank

### The Rule of Euphony: з / із / зі

You might have noticed that we say **з Києва** but **зі Львова**, and **з роботи** but **зі школи**. Why does the preposition change? 

This is because of the Ukrainian rule of euphony (harmony of sound). The language naturally tries to avoid harsh combinations of consonants that are difficult to pronounce. 

*   We use **з** before most vowels and single consonants: **з України**, **з Канади**, **з роботи**.
*   We use **із** predominantly before words beginning with sibilant and fricative sounds (з, с, ц, ч, ш): **із золота**, **із семи**, **із цукром**. It also helps break up consonant clusters between words.
*   We use **зі** before words that start with **з**, **с**, **ш**, or **щ** followed by another consonant. This is why we say **зі школи** (from school), **зі США** (from the USA), and **зі Львова** (from Lviv). 

Do not stress over getting the euphony perfectly right every single time you speak. Native speakers automatically adjust these sounds for comfort. If you try to say "з школи," your mouth will naturally want to add a vowel to make it easier, and you will eventually say **зі школи**.

### Nationality and Language Links

When you introduce yourself, your country of origin is usually closely tied to your nationality and the language you speak. It is very helpful to learn these concepts together as a sequence. Let's look at how we build this chain of identity using vocabulary you learned in earlier modules.

Here is the progression for someone from Ukraine:
*   **Я з України.** (I am from Ukraine.)
*   **Я українець.** (I am Ukrainian. — male) / **Я українка.** (I am Ukrainian. — female)
*   **Я говорю українською.** (I speak Ukrainian.)

This pattern works for other origins as well. You state your origin with **з**, your identity as a noun, and your language as an instrumental form:
*   **Я з Канади.** (I am from Canada.)
*   **Я канадець.** (I am Canadian. — male) / **Я канадійка.** (I am Canadian. — female)
*   **Я говорю англійською.** (I speak English.)

### Contrasting Current Location and Origin

In the modern world, where people are from is often different from where they currently live. You can combine your knowledge of the **Де?** (Where?) and **Звідки?** (Where from?) structures to explain this. 

To do this, use the conjunction **але** (but).

*   **Я живу в Києві, але я зі Львова.** (I live in Kyiv, but I am from Lviv.)
*   **Вона зараз у Лондоні, але вона з України.** (She is in London now, but she is from Ukraine.)
*   **Ми живемо в Канаді, але ми з Одеси.** (We live in Canada, but we are from Odesa.)

Notice how the preposition changes. For the current living location, we use **в** or **у** with the Locative case form (**в Києві**). For the origin, we use **з** or **зі** with the Genitive chunk form (**зі Львова**). Mastering this contrast is a major milestone in speaking naturally.

| 7 | **Structural integrity** | 5% | All H2 headings from plan present? Word count in range? No duplicate sections? No meta-commentary? Clean markdown? |
| 8 | **Cultural accuracy** | 5% | Decolonized (Ukrainian on its own terms, never "like Russian but...")? Factually correct claims about Ukrainian? Respectful representation? |
| 9 | **Dialogue & conversation quality** | 10% | Dialogues natural and culturally appropriate? Real situations, real responses? Speaker roles clear? Not stilted or textbook-robotic? |

**Note:** Vocabulary tables (словник), video embeds, and external resource links are added by a downstream ENRICH step — do NOT penalize their absence or quality. If you see a vocabulary table with wrong translations, missing words, or formatting issues, that content was NOT written by the writer — it was generated deterministically by the ENRICH step. Do NOT flag словník problems as linguistic errors or deduct points from the writer. Report them separately under "ENRICH issues" in your findings (informational only, not scored).

### Step 4: Output raw scores

Output ONLY the raw 1-10 scores in the table below. Do NOT calculate the weighted total — the pipeline script handles the math deterministically. Just write your per-dimension scores.

### Step 5: List findings

For every issue found, provide:
```
[DIMENSION] [SEVERITY: critical/major/minor]
Location: [specific section/paragraph — quote the actual text]
Issue: [what's wrong]
Fix: [exactly how to fix it]
```

Critical = module cannot ship. Major = quality below standard. Minor = polish item.

### Step 6: Verdict

Base your verdict on the **severity of findings** — the pipeline calculates the weighted score separately.

| Verdict | Condition |
|---------|-----------|
| **PASS** | Zero critical findings, at most minor issues |
| **REVISE** | Has major findings but no criticals — fixable without rewrite |
| **REJECT** | Has any critical finding — fundamental problems requiring rewrite |

### Step 7: Fix it yourself (REVISE only)

If your verdict is **REVISE**, you MUST output a `<fixes>` block with exact find/replace pairs. The pipeline applies these deterministically — no LLM regeneration, no rewriting from scratch.

Rules for fixes:
- Each fix is a YAML entry with `find:` (exact text from the module) and `replace:` (corrected text)
- The `find` string must be an EXACT substring of the module content — copy-paste it
- Keep fixes minimal — change only what's wrong, preserve surrounding text
- Do NOT fix словник/vocabulary tables (those are generated by a downstream tool)
- For word count issues: add content as a new `insert_after:` entry instead of find/replace
