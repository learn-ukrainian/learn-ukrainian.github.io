## М'який знак (The Soft Sign — Ь)

Look at these two words: **стан** (state) and **стань** (stand!). One tiny letter at the end — **ь** — changes everything. That letter is the **м'який знак** (soft sign), and it is one of the most important marks in Ukrainian writing.

Here is the key fact: **ь** has no sound of its own. Zero. It is silent. Its only job is to soften the consonant that comes before it. Ukrainian distinguishes between **тверді** (hard) and **пом'якшені** (softened) consonants. In Grade 1 textbooks, Ukrainian children learn a simple notation: hard consonants are marked [–] and soft consonants are marked [=]. The soft sign is what creates that [=] sound.

Compare these pairs:

| Without Ь | With Ь |
|-----------|--------|
| **кін** (end, turn) | **кінь** (horse) |
| **рис** (rice) | **рись** (lynx) |

The meaning changes completely. The soft sign is not decoration — it is meaningful. If you forget to use it, you might accidentally ask for a "lynx" instead of "rice"! This is why paying attention to the [=] softened consonants is a critical first step in mastering Ukrainian pronunciation. It shows respect for the language's natural melody and ensures you are clearly understood.



When you pronounce a hard consonant, your tongue is relaxed. When you pronounce a softened consonant, the middle of your tongue raises toward the roof of your mouth, pressing slightly against the palate. This creates a subtle 'y' flavor right at the end of the consonant, melting it. It takes practice to hear it, but native speakers listen for this difference constantly.

Where does **ь** commonly appear? Look for these patterns:

- **-нь**: **день** (day), **кінь** (horse), **осінь** (autumn)
- **-ль**: **сіль** (salt), **біль** (pain)
- **-ть**: **мить** (moment)
- **-дь**: **мідь** (copper)
- **-зь**: **мазь** (ointment)

One important rule: **ь** appears only after consonants, never at the beginning of a word. You will also see it inside words: **учитель** (teacher), **батько** (father), **маленький** (small). In each case, the consonant right before **ь** becomes soft.

## Апостроф (The Apostrophe)

Ukrainian has another special mark that looks like a small raised comma between letters: the **апостроф** (apostrophe). Unlike **ь**, the apostrophe does not soften anything. It does the opposite — it keeps the consonant hard.

The rule comes straight from the Grade 1 textbook (Захарійчук, p. 97): the apostrophe appears after the consonants **б**, **п**, **в**, **м**, **ф**, **р** — and only before the vowels **я**, **ю**, **є**, **ї**. When you see the apostrophe, the consonant before it stays hard, and the vowel after it gets its full two-sound value.

Look at the difference. In the word **пісня** (song), there is no apostrophe, and the **н** before **я** is soft. But in **п'ять** (five), the apostrophe keeps the **п** hard, and **я** sounds like two sounds together.

Here are essential apostrophe words:

- **сім'я** (family) — the apostrophe separates **м** from **я**
- **м'ясо** (meat) — hard **м** before **я**
- **п'ять** (five) — hard **п** before **я**
- **комп'ютер** (computer) — a familiar word with an apostrophe
- **дев'ять** (nine) — hard **в** before **я**
- **м'який** (soft) — notice the irony: the word for "soft" has an apostrophe that keeps the consonant hard

Reading practice — say these words out loud, pausing slightly at the apostrophe: **п'ять**, **дев'ять**, **м'який**, **м'яч** (ball), **об'єкт** (object). The apostrophe is your signal to keep the consonant firm and give the vowel its full sound. On a Ukrainian keyboard, the apostrophe is usually found on the same key as the English tilde (~) or sometimes above the Tab key, depending on your layout. Because it appears in so many fundamental words, getting comfortable typing and pronouncing the apostrophe is an important early milestone for learners.



Think of the apostrophe as a tiny speed bump. You drive up to the consonant, stop for a microsecond, and then say the vowel as if it were at the beginning of a new word. Try saying "п... ять" and then slowly bring them closer together without blending the sounds. That crisp separation is the heart of the Ukrainian apostrophe.

## Дзвінкі і глухі (Voiced and Voiceless)

Ukrainian consonants come in pairs: **дзвінкі** (voiced) and **глухі** (voiceless). There is a simple physical test. Place your hand on your throat and say a sound. If you feel vibration, the consonant is voiced. No vibration — voiceless.

Here are the eight pairs:

| Дзвінкий (voiced) | Глухий (voiceless) |
|---|---|
| **Б** | **П** |
| **Д** | **Т** |
| **Г** | **Х** |
| **Ґ** | **К** |
| **З** | **С** |
| **Ж** | **Ш** |
| **ДЗ** | **Ц** |
| **ДЖ** | **Ч** |

Notice that **ДЗ** and **ДЖ** are each one sound written with two letters. They are single consonants, not combinations.

Now here is something critical that separates Ukrainian from Russian. In Russian, voiced consonants lose their voicing at the end of a word — the Russian word for "oak" sounds like *[дуп]*. **Ukrainian does not do this.** The word **дуб** (oak) is pronounced exactly as written — with a clear **б** at the end. The word **мороз** (frost) keeps its **з**. This is authentic Ukrainian pronunciation, and it is one of the easiest ways to sound natural.

Minimal pairs help train your ear to hear the difference between voiced and voiceless:

- **балка** (beam) vs **палка** (stick)
- **коза** (goat) vs **коса** (braid)

The first sound in each pair makes the entire word different. Voiced and voiceless are not interchangeable.



This rule of keeping final consonants voiced gives spoken Ukrainian a very strong, resonant, and musical quality. When a Ukrainian says **хліб** (bread) or **дід** (grandfather), the final vibration rings out clearly. Practice over-emphasizing that final buzz or hum until it feels natural. If you master this one rule, your accent will immediately sound more authentically Ukrainian. Unlike English, where final consonants can sometimes be swallowed or mumbled, Ukrainian demands that you pronounce the ends of your words with confidence and clarity. The voicing of that final consonant is a key part of the language's steady, rhythmic flow.

## Важкі звуки (Tricky Sounds)

Three Ukrainian sounds give English speakers the most trouble. None of them have exact English equivalents, but all of them are learnable.

### И

The Ukrainian **И** is not the English "ee" and not the English short "i" — it lives between them. To find it, start by smiling as if saying "ee," then pull your tongue slightly back and relax your lips. The sound you get is **И**. Practice with these words: **бик** (bull), **лист** (leaf), **зима** (winter), **тихо** (quietly), **синій** (blue). This sound appears constantly in Ukrainian, so getting comfortable with it early matters.

### Г and Ґ

Ukrainian has two letters that both look like they should sound like "g" — but they are completely different sounds.

**Г** is a voiced sound produced in the throat — like saying "h" but with your vocal cords vibrating. It is breathy and soft, nothing like the hard English "g." Words with **Г**: **гарно** (nicely, beautifully), **голова** (head), **гори** (mountains).

**Ґ** is the hard sound, like English "g" in "go." It is rare in Ukrainian and appears in only a handful of words: **ґанок** (porch), **ґудзик** (button), **ґречний** (polite). Most of the time, when you see a letter that looks like it should be "g," it is **Г** — the soft, breathy one.

### Р

Ukrainian **Р** is rolled, similar to the Spanish "r." The tongue tip vibrates against the ridge behind the upper teeth. Practice words: **риба** (fish), **ранок** (morning), **рука** (hand), **робота** (work). Even an imperfect **Р** is perfectly understood — do not stress about making it perfect from day one.

## Review Protocol

### Step 1: Scan for linguistic errors

Scan the Ukrainian text for errors. Report ONLY problems found — do not echo correct text.

**Four SEPARATE checks (these are four different problems):**
1. **Russianisms** — Russian words used instead of Ukrainian (кот→кіт, хорошо→добре, конечно→звичайно, сейчас→зараз)
2. **Surzhyk** — mixed Russian-Ukrainian forms (шо→що, чо→чому, тіпа→типу)
3. **Calques** — literal translations from Russian that sound wrong in Ukrainian (приймати душ→брати душ, приймати рішення→ухвалювати рішення)
4. **Paronyms** — similar-sounding words with different meanings used incorrectly (тактична≠тактовна, ефектний≠ефективний)

**Also check:**
- Russian characters (ы, э, ё, ъ) — these must NEVER appear
- Incorrect gender assignment or case endings
- Factually wrong claims about Ukrainian phonetics, grammar, or culture

**Do NOT check for stress marks** — stress annotation is handled by a separate deterministic tool after the review phase. Their absence is correct.

### Step 2: Check exercises

List every exercise block (`:::quiz`, `:::fill-in`, `:::match-up`, `:::group-sort`, `:::true-false`). These are filled exercises — a deterministic tool converted placeholders to real content.

For each exercise, check:
- Does the exercise test what was just taught? (language skill, not content recall)
- Is the logic correct? (correct answers are actually correct, distractors are plausible but wrong)
- Can a learner complete this with the knowledge taught so far in this module?
- Does the exercise match one of the plan's `activity_hints` in type and focus?
- Are there enough items per exercise? (check against plan's `activity_hints`)

Also check: Are there enough exercises total? The plan's `activity_hints` specifies the expected count.

**Note:** Exercises are generated by a deterministic tool from the writer's placeholders. If the exercise LOGIC is wrong (e.g., matching unrelated items), flag it — the tool's input data needs fixing. If the exercise FORMAT looks unusual, that is expected (the tool uses a specific DSL syntax).

### Step 3: Score on 9 dimensions

Rate each dimension 1-10 with SPECIFIC evidence (cite the section/paragraph, quote the actual text).

| # | Dimension | Weight | What to check |
|---|-----------|--------|---------------|
| 1 | **Plan adherence** | 15% | Every content_outline point covered? Section word budgets respected (±10%)? All plan references used? |
| 2 | **Linguistic accuracy** | 15% | All Ukrainian correct? No Russianisms/Surzhyk/calques? Correct phonetic descriptions? Gender/case correct? |
| 3 | **Pedagogical quality** | 15% | PPP (Present→Practice→Produce) applied? Textbook pedagogy used (Большакова, Захарійчук)? Grammar scope respected (no A2 in A1)? |
| 4 | **Vocabulary coverage** | 10% | All required vocab from plan used naturally in prose? Recommended vocab included? New words introduced in context, not as lists? |
| 5 | **Exercise quality** | 15% | Placeholders specific enough? Test the right skills? Placed after relevant teaching? Match plan's activity_hints? Sufficient items? |
| 6 | **Engagement & tone** | 10% | Interesting for teens/adults? Authoritative but warm (like a skilled teacher)? No LLM filler ("Good news!", "Don't panic!", "Fun fact!")? Cultural hooks? |
| 7 | **Structural integrity** | 5% | All H2 headings from plan present? Word count in range? No duplicate sections? No meta-commentary? Clean markdown? |
| 8 | **Cultural accuracy** | 5% | Decolonized (Ukrainian on its own terms, never "like Russian but...")? Factually correct claims about Ukrainian? Respectful representation? |
| 9 | **Dialogue & conversation quality** | 10% | Dialogues natural and culturally appropriate? Real situations, real responses? Speaker roles clear? Not stilted or textbook-robotic? |

**Note:** Vocabulary tables (словник), video embeds, and external resource links are added by a downstream ENRICH step — do NOT penalize their absence or quality. If you see a vocabulary table with wrong translations, missing words, or formatting issues, that content was NOT written by the writer — it was generated deterministically by the ENRICH step. Do NOT flag словník problems as linguistic errors or deduct points from the writer. Report them separately under "ENRICH issues" in your findings (informational only, not scored).

### Step 4: Output raw scores

Output ONLY the raw 1-10 scores in the table below. Do NOT calculate the weighted total — the pipeline script handles the math deterministically. Just write your per-dimension scores.

### Step 5: List findings

For every issue found, provide:
```
[DIMENSION] [SEVERITY: critical/major/minor]
Location: [specific section/paragraph — quote the actual text]
Issue: [what's wrong]
Fix: [exactly how to fix it]
```

Critical = module cannot ship. Major = quality below standard. Minor = polish item.

### Step 6: Verdict

Base your verdict on the **severity of findings** — the pipeline calculates the weighted score separately.

| Verdict | Condition |
|---------|-----------|
| **PASS** | Zero critical findings, at most minor issues |
| **REVISE** | Has major findings but no criticals — fixable without rewrite |
| **REJECT** | Has any critical finding — fundamental problems requiring rewrite |

### Step 7: Fix it yourself (REVISE only)

If your verdict is **REVISE**, you MUST output a `<fixes>` block with exact find/replace pairs. The pipeline applies these deterministically — no LLM regeneration, no rewriting from scratch.

Rules for fixes:
- Each fix is a YAML entry with `find:` (exact text from the module) and `replace:` (corrected text)
- The `find` string must be an EXACT substring of the module content — copy-paste it
- Keep fixes minimal — change only what's wrong, preserve surrounding text
- Do NOT fix словник/vocabulary tables (those are generated by a downstream tool)
- For word count issues: add content as a new `insert_after:` entry instead of find/replace
