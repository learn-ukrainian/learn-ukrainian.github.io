# Questions & Negation

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Asking questions and saying no are two of the most important survival skills in any language. Without them, you cannot navigate a city, buy food, or hold a basic conversation. Ukrainian makes this surprisingly easy by completely removing the need for English helper words like «do» or «does».

## Вступ: Основи заперечення та інтонації

Ukrainian is often easier than English. Why? Because we do not use «do» or «does». In English, you say «Do you know?» or «I do not know.» In Ukrainian, we just use the verb. This is a huge advantage for learners. You only need to know the verb and how to change your voice. This means you have fewer words to memorize and fewer grammar rules to juggle in your head.

Запере́чення в украї́нській мо́ві.
Negation in the Ukrainian language.

You need to know two essential words: **ні** and **не**. They sound similar, but they do completely different jobs.

**Ні** is the standalone answer «No.»
**Не** is the word «not.» It always goes immediately before the verb.

Ні, я не зна́ю.
No, I do not know.

Ні, я не розумі́ю.
No, I do not understand.

> [!warning]
> Do not mix them up! Saying «Ні знаю» instead of «Не знаю» is a very common mistake. Always use **не** right before the action.

Інтона́ція ду́же важли́ва.
Intonation is very important.

Because we do not have «do» to signal a question, our voice does the work. A normal statement has a falling tone at the end (↘). A question has a sharp, rising tone on the main word (↗).

Ти зна́єш. ↘
You know. (Statement)

Ти не зна́єш. ↘
You do not know. (Negative statement)

Ти зна́єш? ↗
Do you know? (Question)

Listen for that sharp jump in pitch. It tells you immediately that a question is being asked. This simple change turns any statement into a question without adding extra words. When you speak, try to make your falling tone very obvious for negative statements. This will help you sound confident. Conversely, your rising tone for questions should sound almost exaggerated at first.

## Презентація: Питальні конструкції

We will now explore how to build questions properly. There are three main ways to ask questions in Ukrainian.

### Yes/No Questions with Чи

Sometimes, relying only on your voice is not enough. When we want to be polite, clear, or formal, we use the word **чи**.

Чи ви розумі́єте?
Do you understand?

Чи це ка́ва?
Is this coffee?

Чи ти зна́єш?
Do you know?

The word **чи** acts like a clear signal flag at the beginning of the sentence. It politely announces, «A yes or no question is coming!» You will hear it often in shops, offices, train stations, and formal situations. It is the safest way to ask a question when you are still learning the language, because it guarantees the listener knows you are asking something. Practice responding to **чи** questions with confirmation using **так** (yes) or negation using **ні** (no).

Чи ти зна́єш? Так, я зна́ю.
Do you know? Yes, I know.

### Intonation-Only Questions

In casual conversation with friends or family, Ukrainians usually drop the word **чи**. We just use the statement order and raise our pitch.

Ви розумі́єте? ↗
Do you understand?

Це ка́ва? ↗
Is this coffee?

Ти зна́єш? ↗
Do you know?

This is extremely common. You must listen closely for the rising melody at the end of the phrase. When you speak, do not be afraid to exaggerate the rising tone. If your pitch stays flat, people will think you are just stating a fact.

> [!tip]
> Start your learning journey by using **чи** to be safe and polite. As you get comfortable with the Ukrainian melody, try using just your voice!

### Question Words

When you need specific information, you need question words. These words usually go at the beginning of the sentence.

Here are six important question words:

| Word | Meaning | Example | Translation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **що** | what | Що це? | What is this? |
| **хто** | who | Хто там? | Who is there? |
| **де** | where | Де ти? | Where are you? |
| **коли́** | when | Коли́ сніда́нок? | When is breakfast? |
| **як** | how | Як спра́ви? | How are things? |
| **скі́льки** | how much | Скі́льки це? | How much is this? |

Хто це?
Who is this?

Що ти ро́биш?
What are you doing?

Де туале́т?
Where is the toilet?

Коли́ ти вдо́ма?
When are you at home?

Notice how simple the structure is. You just put the question word in front. There is no confusing word order to remember.

> [!did-you-know]
> **The Street Sound**
> The standard dictionary word for «what» is **що**. But on the streets, almost everyone says **шо**. You will hear «Шо це?» or «Шо ти ро́биш?» everywhere in Ukraine. You should write **що**, but expect to hear **шо**!

Here are a few more examples using these core words.

Що ви зна́єте?
What do you know?

Хто він?
Who is he?

Де ма́ма?
Where is mom?

These small words are the keys to unlocking conversations. Master them, and you can navigate almost any basic situation in Ukraine, from finding your way on the street to having a chat over dinner. Remember to keep your sentences short and direct. As a beginner, clarity is much more important than complex grammar. These basic structures will serve you well.

## Практика: Тренування заперечень та запитань

Time to put everything together. We can take one simple idea and change it in four different ways.

Ця трансформа́ція ду́же проста́.
This transformation is very simple.

We begin with a basic positive statement.

Ти розумі́єш.
You understand.

Next, we make it negative by adding **не** before the verb.

Ти не розумі́єш.
You do not understand.

Then, we ask a formal question by adding **чи** at the start.

Чи ти розумі́єш?
Do you understand?

Finally, we create a casual question using only intonation.

Ти розумі́єш? ↗
Do you understand?

Practice this cycle in your head with other verbs. It builds a very strong foundation and trains your brain to stop looking for the English «do».

We will try one more transformation cycle with a new verb.

Він зна́є.
He knows.

Він не зна́є.
He does not know.

Чи він зна́є?
Does he know?

Він зна́є? ↗
Does he know?

See how predictable the pattern is? Once you learn this rhythm, you can apply it to any verb.

> [!culture]
> **У кафе́**
> At the café.
> Consider a short situation. Imagine you are entering a coffee shop.

Де ка́ва?
Where is the coffee?

Ка́ва там.
The coffee is there.

Чи є цу́кор?
Is there sugar?

Ні, цу́кру нема́є.
No, there is no sugar.

Що це?
What is this?

Це чай.
This is tea.

This short dialogue uses the tools we just learned. You will use these exact phrases frequently.

За́вжди та ніко́ли.
Always and never.

Sometimes, a simple «not» is not enough. You might want to say you «always» do something, or «never» do it. In Ukrainian, when we say «never», we use double negation. This means we use **ніко́ли** (never) AND **не** (not) together. In English this is wrong, but in Ukrainian it is required!

Я за́вжди тут.
I am always here.

Я ніко́ли не зна́ю.
I never know. (Literally: I never not know.)

Вона́ ніко́ли не розумі́є.
She never understands.

Embrace the double negative. It makes your Ukrainian sound very natural and expressive. Do not be afraid to say «ніко́ли не» — it is the only correct way!

# Підсумок: Культурний контекст та перевірка

Social context matters. How you ask a question depends on who you are talking to.

If you are speaking to a police officer, a shop assistant, or a stranger on the street, use **чи**. It shows respect and clarity. If you are sitting in a kitchen with friends, or talking to family, drop the **чи** and just raise your voice enthusiastically.

> [!culture]
> **The famous ALF quote**
> In the 1990s, the American TV show ALF was dubbed into Ukrainian. The translation was a masterpiece. Every Ukrainian remembers ALF's famous, dark-humor quote about cats. It is a perfect example of negation!
>
> Ти не лю́биш коті́в?
> You do not like cats?
> Ти про́сто не вмі́єш їх готува́ти!
> You simply do not know how to cook them!

This quote perfectly shows how we use **не** right before the verb. It also shows how intonation makes a question out of a statement.
