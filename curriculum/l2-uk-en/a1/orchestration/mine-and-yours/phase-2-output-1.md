# Mine and Yours

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Learning to say «mine» and «yours» helps you claim your things, talk about your family, and build a sense of personal space in Ukrainian. Unlike English, where «my» is always just «my», Ukrainian possession is a beautiful matching game where the pronoun dances with the object you own.

## Вступ: Чия це річ? (Introduction: Whose is this?)

Уяві́ть цю ситуа́цію. (Imagine this situation.)
Ви у бюро́ знахі́док. (You are in a lost and found office.)
Тут є телефо́н, па́спорт і су́мка. (Here is a phone, a passport, and a bag.)
Люди́на запи́тує. (A person asks.)
Чий це телефо́н? (Whose phone is this?)
Чия́ це су́мка? (Whose bag is this?)

You might notice something interesting right away. The English word «whose» stays exactly the same no matter what object we are talking about. But in Ukrainian, the question word actually changes its shape.

This happens because Ukrainian is a language of grammatical gender. Every single noun in the language belongs to a gender category: masculine, feminine, or neuter. When you want to talk about who owns an object, the «owner word» must match the gender of the object being owned.

This is the absolute most important rule of the entire lesson. The possessive pronoun agrees with the thing you possess, not with you as the owner.
If you are a tall, strong man, and you own a small bag (which is a feminine noun), the word for «my» must be feminine. The bag is the boss! It dictates the shape of the word that comes before it.

> [!tip]
> Think of possessive pronouns as mirrors. They reflect the grammatical gender of the noun that comes right after them. If the noun is feminine, the pronoun must be feminine too.

Let's look at the basic question words we use to ask «whose thing is this» in our office:
- Чий? (Whose? — used for a masculine object)
- Чия́? (Whose? — used for a feminine object)
- Чиє́? (Whose? — used for a neuter object)

It is time to discover how to answer these questions using our own possessive pronouns.

## Змінювані займенники (Variable Possessives: Мій, Твій, Наш, Ваш)

When we want to claim ownership, we have to choose the right version of a word to match our noun. Let's look at these variable possessive pronouns.

First, let's explore how to say «my». We have three different options depending on the noun's gender. We will start with masculine nouns.
Мі́й телефо́н. (My phone.)
Мі́й дім. (My house.)
Мі́й па́спорт. (My passport.)
Мі́й друг. (My friend.)

If the object is feminine, we must use the feminine form. Notice how it usually ends in the letter «я» or «а».
Моя́ ма́ма. (My mom.)
Моя́ су́мка. (My bag.)
Моя́ кімна́та. (My room.)
Моя́ робо́та. (My job.)

If the object is neuter, we must use the neuter form. This usually ends in the letter «е».
Моє́ мі́сто. (My city.)
Моє́ ім’я́. (My name.)

Notice how the pronoun always dances to the tune of the noun. A common error for English speakers is to match the pronoun to themselves. A male student might try to say «мій мама» because he is male. But the mother (ма́ма) is a feminine noun, so it absolutely must be «моя́ ма́ма», regardless of the gender of the speaker.

The exact same pattern applies to «your» when speaking to one person informally.
Тві́й друг. (Your friend. — masculine)
Твоя́ ма́ма. (Your mom. — feminine)
Твоє́ мі́сто. (Your city. — neuter)

For «our», we use a very similar set of endings.
Наш дім. (Our house. — masculine)
На́ша краї́на. (Our country. — feminine)
На́ше мі́сто. (Our city. — neuter)

And finally, for «your» when speaking formally or to multiple people, we use the formal versions.
Ваш квито́к. (Your ticket. — masculine)
Ва́ша адре́са. (Your address. — feminine)
Ва́ше прі́звище. (Your surname. — neuter)

> [!warning]
> The «Gender Mismatch» is the number one trap for beginners. Always check the ending of the noun before you choose your pronoun. If the noun ends in -а or -я, you almost always need the feminine form (моя́, твоя́, на́ша, ва́ша). Do not guess based on your own gender!

Here is a handy reference table to help you remember the variable forms:

| English | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---------|-----------|----------|--------|
| My      | мі́й       | моя́      | моє́    |
| Your    | тві́й      | твоя́     | твоє́   |
| Our     | наш       | на́ша     | на́ше   |
| Your    | ваш       | ва́ша     | ва́ше   |

## Незмінні форми (Invariable Possessives and Свій)

Are you feeling tired of constantly matching genders? I have some wonderful news for you. The Ukrainian pronouns for «his» and «her» are completely invariable. They do not change their shape, no matter what noun follows them. This works exactly like English!

For «his», we always use the word **його́**.
Його́ ба́тько. (His father. — masculine object)
Його́ маши́на. (His car. — feminine object)
Його́ мі́сто. (His city. — neuter object)

For «her», we always use the word **її́**.
Її́ па́спорт. (Her passport. — masculine object)
Її́ су́мка. (Her bag. — feminine object)
Її́ ім’я́. (Her name. — neuter object)

These forms are securely frozen. You never need to worry about the gender of the object when you are talking about what belongs to him or her. It is a moment of pure relief for language learners.

Now, let's talk about «their». The standard, beautiful literary word for «their» is **ї́хній**. Unlike «his» and «her», this word DOES change to match the object.
Ї́хній буди́нок. (Their house. — masculine)
Ї́хня сім’я́. (Their family. — feminine)
Ї́хнє рі́шення. (Their decision. — neuter)

> [!culture]
> You might sometimes hear people use the short word «їх» instead of «їхній» to mean «their». This is a Russianism and is considered incorrect in standard Ukrainian. Always choose the proper, elegant form «ї́хній», «ї́хня», «ї́хнє». It is an important marker of clean, decolonized speech.

Finally, Ukrainian has a very special reflexive possessive pronoun: **сві́й** (one's own).
English does not really have a direct equivalent to this word. We use «сві́й» when the subject of the sentence owns the object.
It matches genders exactly like «мі́й» and «тві́й»: сві́й, своя́, своє́.
Why do we need this special word? Look at this situation to see the difference.
Іва́н лю́бить свою́ робо́ту. (Ivan loves his own job.)
If we used the normal «his» instead, the meaning of the sentence changes completely.
Іва́н лю́бить його́ робо́ту. (Ivan loves his job — meaning he loves some other guy's job!)

Using «сві́й» prevents awkward confusion and makes it crystal clear that the person doing the action is also the actual owner of the object. Whenever you mean «one's own», reach for this reflexive pronoun.

## Культурний контекст (Cultural Context: Твій vs. Ваш)

In Ukrainian society, the choice between «тві́й» (your informal) and «ваш» (your formal) is about much more than just grammar. It is a direct reflection of social distance, respect, and etiquette.

You must use «ваш» and its feminine and neuter forms (ва́ша, ва́ше) when you are speaking to someone you respect. This category includes teachers, doctors, people older than you, and any adult stranger you meet in public. Using the formal address shows politeness and cultural awareness. You also use «ваш» whenever you are talking to two or more people at the same time, regardless of how intimately well you know them.

You use «тві́й», «твоя́», and «твоє́» only when speaking to close friends, immediate family members, peers of the same age, and young children.

The transition from «ваш» to «тві́й» is a significant cultural milestone in the evolution of Ukrainian relationships. We call this social ritual «перехі́д на ти» (switching to the informal pronoun). It is usually a very deliberate, planned moment. Often, the older person or the person of higher social status will explicitly invite the other person to switch to the informal address. They might say, «Let's switch to ty». Until that clear invitation is extended, it is always much safer to stick with the formal «ваш». It shows that you respect their personal boundaries and value traditional politeness.

# Підсумок

Let's take a comprehensive moment to review the main rules of possession we have explored today. You have learned a lot of essential grammar!

The absolute golden rule to remember is that most possessive pronouns must match the grammatical gender of the object being owned, not the person who owns it. The object is always the boss in this relationship. If the object is masculine, your pronoun is masculine. If the object is feminine, your pronoun must be feminine. If the object is neuter, the pronoun must be neuter.

We learned the core variable pronouns that dynamically change their shape to match their noun partners:
- Мі́й, моя́, моє́ (My).
- Тві́й, твоя́, твоє́ (Your informal).
- Наш, на́ша, на́ше (Our).
- Ваш, ва́ша, ва́ше (Your formal/plural).

We also discovered the wonderful invariable pronouns that stay completely frozen and never change, no matter what complicated noun follows them. These are great time-savers that you will learn to love!
- Його́ (His).
- Її́ (Her).

And we learned the literary standard for «their», which does return to the pattern of changing to match the object:
- Ї́хній, ї́хня, ї́хнє (Their).

Finally, we explored the unique reflexive pronoun сві́й, своя́, своє́ (one's own), which we use when the owner is the one performing the action in the sentence. This elegant tool keeps our sentences clear and prevents us from accidentally talking about someone else's personal belongings.

Before you finish this lesson, try this short self-check. Read the following questions carefully and think deeply about your answers to verify your true understanding of the material.

1. If you are a woman and you want to say «my passport» (па́спорт is masculine), do you use the feminine pronoun «моя́» because you are female, or the masculine pronoun «мі́й» because the passport is masculine?
2. What is the fundamental difference in behavior between the pronoun «його́» (his) and the pronoun «мі́й» (my)? Do they both change to match the noun they stand next to?
3. Why is it strictly considered incorrect to use the short word «їх» to mean «their» in proper standard Ukrainian? What is the correct word you should use instead?
4. What happens when you switch from using «ваш» to using «тві́й» with a Ukrainian colleague at work? What does this important cultural milestone represent in your evolving relationship?

Keep practicing this grammatical matching game every single day. Look at objects around your room and try claiming them with the correct pronoun. Very soon, matching the genders will feel completely natural and automatic!
