# Describing Things - Adjectives

> **Чому це важливо? — Why does this matter?**
>
> You already know how to name things and people, but the world is full of amazing details! Adjectives let you add color, size, and character to everything around you. By learning how to match describing words with objects, you can start painting vivid pictures with your Ukrainian language skills.

## Вступ: Світ прикметників (Introduction: The World of Adjectives)

Подиві́ться на кімна́ту. Вона́ вели́ка чи мала́? (Look at the room. Is it big or small?)
Ваш стіл стари́й чи нови́й? (Is your table old or new?)
Ми зна́ємо слова́. Тепе́р ми опи́суємо сві́т! (We know the words. Now we describe the world!)

In Ukrainian, adjectives are the ultimate copycats. They never stand alone. They must always match the noun they are describing. Do you remember the Gender Code? We learned earlier that every noun is either masculine, feminine, or neuter. Because adjectives are copycats, they change their endings to perfectly match the gender of the noun. They also change if the noun is plural.

To ask "What kind?" or "Which?", we use special question words. Notice how the question word itself changes its shape to match the gender!
- **Яки́й?** (What kind? — for masculine words)
- **Яка́?** (What kind? — for feminine words)
- **Яке́?** (What kind? — for neuter words)
- **Які́?** (What kind? — for plural words)

Let us travel to the heart of Kyiv. Imagine standing in front of Софі́йський собо́р (St. Sophia's Cathedral). It is a magnificent historical building. We can describe it as «стари́й» (ancient) and «вели́кий» (grand). To describe these beautiful historical landmarks correctly, we need to understand exactly how the endings of these descriptive words work. Let us dive into the rules of adjective agreement.

## Тверда група прикметників (Hard Stem Adjectives)

Most adjectives in Ukrainian belong to the "hard group". This simply means that their base ends in a hard sound, and they take standard, predictable endings. When you look up an adjective in a dictionary, you will always find it in its masculine, singular form.

Let us look at two of the most common adjectives you will ever use: **нови́й** (new) and **га́рний** (beautiful or nice). Here is how their endings change to match the noun perfectly.

| Gender | Question | Ending | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masculine | Яки́й? | **-ий** | нови́й дім (a new house) |
| Feminine | Яка́? | **-а** | нова́ кни́га (a new book) |
| Neuter | Яке́? | **-е** | нове́ вікно́ (a new window) |
| Plural | Які́? | **-і** | нові́ дру́зі (new friends) |

> [!tip]
> Think of these endings as a color code. You can imagine masculine as blue, feminine as red, neuter as yellow, and plural as green. When you learn a new noun, immediately tag it with its color so you know exactly which adjective ending to use.

A very common mistake for beginners is forgetting to change the ending. Because the dictionary gives you the masculine form, it is highly tempting to use it for absolutely everything.

> [!warning]
> Never say *нови́й маши́на*. The word «маши́на» is feminine. The adjective must copy it! The correct phrase is «нова́ маши́на».

Where do we put the adjective in a sentence? Usually, it goes right before the noun, exactly like in English. We call this the attributive position.
- Га́рна пого́да (Nice weather).
- Вели́кий дім (A big house).

However, you can also place the adjective after the noun to make a complete sentence. This is called the predicative position. Remember that Ukrainian does not use the word "is" in the present tense! The gender endings remain exactly the same.
- Пого́да га́рна (The weather is nice).
- Дім вели́кий (The house is big).

Let us add a few more essential opposites to your vocabulary toolkit. These words describe scale and quality.
- **вели́кий** (big) and **мали́й** (small)
- **до́брий** (good) and **пога́ний** (bad)
- **дороги́й** (expensive) and **деше́вий** (cheap)

Practice matching these with different nouns in your head. For example, «вели́ке мі́сто» (a big city) or «до́брий ра́нок» (good morning).

## М'яка група та множина (Soft Stem and Plurals)

Now that you have mastered the hard group, let us look at the "soft group". These adjectives are less common, but they are incredibly important. Their base ends in a soft sound, so their endings must also be soft to match the sound perfectly.

The most famous example for beginners is the color blue: **си́ній**. If you visit the Kyiv Metro, you will definitely ride the «си́ня лі́нія» (Blue Line). This is a perfect memory hook for the soft feminine ending!

Here is how the soft endings look. Notice how they closely correspond to the hard endings, but with a much softer sound.

| Gender | Question | Ending | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masculine | Яки́й? | **-ій** | си́ній зо́шит (a blue notebook) |
| Feminine | Яка́? | **-я** | си́ня лі́нія (the blue line) |
| Neuter | Яке́? | **-є** | си́нє мо́ре (the blue sea) |
| Plural | Які́? | **-і** | си́ні дні (blue days) |

> [!warning]
> Visual trap alert! Because the hard ending «-ий» and the soft ending «-ій» sound somewhat similar to untrained ears, many learners accidentally write *си́ний*. This is incorrect spelling. Always use «-ій» for soft masculine adjectives.

Лі́нія си́ня. Мо́ре си́нє. (The line is blue. The sea is blue.)
Дні си́ні. (The days are blue.)

Now, let us talk about the plural form. You might have noticed a fantastic shortcut in the tables above. Did you spot it?

All plural adjectives, whether they belong to the hard group or the soft group, take the exact same ending: **-і**. It does not matter if the original noun was masculine, feminine, or neuter. Once a noun becomes plural, the adjective ending is always -і. This makes describing groups of things incredibly easy and fast.

Let us look at some plural examples to prove this fantastic rule:
- га́рні дні (nice days) — from the masculine noun «день»
- га́рні дівча́та (beautiful girls) — from the feminine noun «ді́вчина»
- га́рні ві́кна (nice windows) — from the neuter noun «вікно́»

Always ensure that your adjective and noun agree in number. Do not use a singular adjective with a plural noun. If you see a plural noun, immediately reach for the -і ending!

## Практика: Люди і місця (Practice: People and Places)

Let us put your new descriptive powers to use in real-world contexts. We will start with a beautiful piece of Ukrainian culture.

Have you heard of Ма́вка (Mavka)? She is a famous figure from Ukrainian folklore. She is a mystical spirit of the forest, popularized by the brilliant writer Lesya Ukrainka in her famous play "Forest Song". How would we describe Mavka using our new words? Since she is a female figure, we must strictly use feminine adjective endings.

Ма́вка — це лісова́ дівчи́на. (Mavka is a forest girl.)
Вона́ ду́же га́рна. (She is very beautiful.)
Вона́ ціка́ва та молода́. (She is interesting and young.)

> [!culture]
> Mavka represents the soul of the forest and nature's untouched beauty. Describing beloved folklore characters is a wonderful and memorable way to practice feminine and masculine agreement naturally!

Now, let us switch roles completely. Imagine you are a successful real estate agent showing properties in the city center. You need to highlight the best features of the homes to your eager clients.

Це вели́кий буди́нок. (This is a big house.)
Буди́нок нови́й. (The house is new.)
Він ду́же дороги́й. (It is very expensive.)

Це нова́ кварти́ра. (This is a new apartment.)
Кварти́ра га́рна та деше́ва. (The apartment is nice and cheap.)

Your clients will also care about the local neighborhood. What if you take your clients out for lunch after viewing the apartments? You will definitely need to describe the food!

Борщ смачни́й. (The borscht is tasty.)
Це смачна́ ка́ва. (This is tasty coffee.)

Practicing with these distinct personas helps you remember which endings belong to which daily life situations. It makes the grammar feel alive and useful.

## Підсумок — Summary

Let us review what we have successfully accomplished today. You have learned that adjectives are copycats that must always match the gender and number of the nouns they describe. You learned to ask the crucial questions «Яки́й? Яка́? Яке́? Які́?» to find out exactly "what kind" of object you are looking at.

You also learned the two main groups of endings that you will use every day:
- Hard group endings: -ий, -а, -е, -і
- Soft group endings: -ій, -я, -є, -і

Most importantly, you discovered the golden rule of plurals: all plural adjectives simply end in -і.

**Self-Check Questions:**
Can you choose the correct adjective ending for these everyday nouns?
1. (Нови́й / Нова́ / Нове́) маши́на.
2. (Стари́й / Стара́ / Старе́) мі́сто.
3. (Га́рний / Га́рні / Га́рне) дру́зі.
4. (Вели́кий / Вели́ка / Вели́ке) дім.

*Answers: 1. Нова́ маши́на (feminine), 2. Старе́ мі́сто (neuter), 3. Га́рні дру́зі (plural), 4. Вели́кий дім (masculine).*
