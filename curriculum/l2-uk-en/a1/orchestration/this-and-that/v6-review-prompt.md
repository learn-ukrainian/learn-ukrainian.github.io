## Діалоги (Dialogues)

Imagine stepping into a bustling Ukrainian shop or a vibrant local market. You are looking for a specific item, but the shelves are full of similar products in different colors and sizes. How do you communicate exactly which one you want to buy? You use pointing words. In Ukrainian grammar, these are called demonstrative pronouns. 

Read the following conversation between a customer and a seller. Notice how the small pointing words change depending on the specific object they are discussing.

**Покупець:** Скільки коштує ця сумка? *(How much does this bag cost?)*


**Продавець:** Яка? Ця червона? *(Which one? This red one?)*


**Покупець:** Ні, та синя. *(No, that blue one.)*


**Продавець:** Та коштує двісті гривень. *(That one costs two hundred hryvnias.)*


**Покупець:** А цей рюкзак? *(And this backpack?)*


**Продавець:** Цей — сто п'ятдесят. *(This one — one hundred fifty.)*


</div>

In this first dialogue, the customer asks for the price using the phrase **Скільки коштує** (How much does [it] cost) followed by **ця сумка** (this bag). Because there are many bags, the seller asks for clarification: **Яка?** (Which one?). The seller immediately offers a guess by pointing: **Ця червона?** (This red one?). The customer corrects the seller by pointing to a different object further away on the shelf: **Ні, та синя.** (No, that blue one.). Notice how the word for "this" (**ця**) changes to "that" (**та**) as the focus shifts to an object that is physically further from the speakers. Finally, the customer points to another item nearby: **А цей рюкзак?** (And this backpack?). Because a backpack (**рюкзак**) is a masculine word, the word for "this" changes to **цей**.

## Цей, ця, це (This)

When an object is physically close to you, you point to it using the words **цей**, **ця**, and **це**. All three of these words translate to the English word "this". However, in Ukrainian, pointing words must perfectly reflect the grammatical gender of the noun they are attached to. As the Ukrainian textbook *Заболотний Grade 6* explains: вказівні займенники **цей**, **той** змінюються за родами (demonstrative pronouns 'this' and 'that' change by gender). This is a foundational rule of the language: the modifier must always mirror the object it describes.

Let us break this down by the three gender categories in the nominative (naming) case.

For masculine nouns, which typically end in a consonant, you must use **цей**. The word **цей** ends in a soft consonant sound, similar to the "y" in the English word "boy".
**Цей стіл** (this table)
**Цей рюкзак** (this backpack)
**Цей брат** (this brother)

For feminine nouns, which typically end in the vowels **-а** or **-я**, you must use **ця**. The word **ця** ends with the vowel sound "a", making it perfectly symmetrical with the feminine nouns it points to.
**Ця книга** (this book)
**Ця сумка** (this bag)
**Ця сестра** (this sister)

For neuter nouns, which typically end in the vowels **-о** or **-е**, you must use **це**.
**Це вікно** (this window)
**Це крісло** (this armchair)
**Це місто** (this city)

This pattern should feel incredibly familiar. It is the exact same underlying logic you use when asking **Який?** (Which/What kind?) or when saying **Мій** (My).

We can build more complex, descriptive phrases by stacking these words together. When you combine a demonstrative pronoun with an adjective and a noun, the word order in Ukrainian is identical to English. First comes the pointing word, then the descriptive word, and finally the object itself. Every single word in the chain must agree in gender.

**Цей великий червоний стіл.** (This big red table.) — All three words (**цей**, **великий**, **червоний**) take the masculine form to match **стіл**.
**Ця нова синя сумка.** (This new blue bag.) — All three words (**ця**, **нова**, **синя**) take the feminine form to match **сумка**.
**Це маленьке біле вікно.** (This small white window.) — All three words (**це**, **маленьке**, **біле**) take the neuter form to match **вікно**.

To make your speech sound even more authentic, you can pair these demonstrative pronouns with location words. Native speakers frequently use the pointing word **ось** (here is / look) or the location word **тут** (here).
**Ось цей стіл.** (Here is this table.)
**Ця сумка тут.** (This bag is here.)
These small additions make your sentences feel grounded and natural.

## Той, та, те (That)

While **цей**, **ця**, and **це** keep your focus close, the words **той**, **та**, and **те** cast your attention further away. These words translate to "that", and you use them to point at objects that are physically distant across a room, or to refer back to something that was mentioned earlier in a conversation. Just like the words for "this", they change their form to perfectly match the gender of the noun.

For masculine nouns, the pointing word becomes **той**.
**Той стіл** (that table)
**Той стілець** (that chair)

For feminine nouns, the pointing word becomes **та**.
**Та книга** (that book)
**Та сумка** (that bag)

For neuter nouns, the pointing word becomes **те**.
**Те вікно** (that window)
**Те крісло** (that armchair)

The real power of these words emerges when you need to draw a contrast. Imagine standing in a room and comparing two similar objects. You can use the word **а** (and/but, used specifically for contrast) to connect your two observations smoothly.

**Цей стілець новий, а той — старий.** (This chair is new, and that one is old.)
**Ця сумка червона, а та — синя.** (This bag is red, and that one is blue.)
**Це вікно велике, а те — маленьке.** (This window is big, and that one is small.)

Notice how you do not even need to repeat the noun in the second half of the sentence. The word **той**, **та**, or **те** carries the gender information so clearly that the listener instantly knows what you are talking about. You can also anchor these distant objects using the location word **там** (there).
**Той стіл там.** (That table is there.)
**Те крісло там.** (That armchair is there.)

There is one very important detail to watch out for. The word **та** has a double identity in Ukrainian. It is the feminine word for "that", but it is also a common conjunction meaning "and" (functioning exactly like **і** or **й**). You will always know which one is being used based entirely on the context of the sentence.

Look at this comparison:
**мама та тато** (mom and dad) — Here, **та** connects two people. It means "and".
**брат та сестра** (brother and sister) — Again, **та** is simply linking the two words together.
**та книга** (that book) — Here, **та** is pointing directly at a feminine noun. It acts as a demonstrative pronoun.
**та дівчина** (that girl) — The word **та** serves as a pointing word.

When you are choosing between two items, you will frequently use the question word **чи** (or). This word is essential for questions where you offer alternatives to a listener.

**Який стіл? Цей чи той?** (Which table? This one or that one?)
**Яка сумка? Ця червона чи та синя?** (Which bag? This red one or that blue one?)
**Яке вікно? Це велике чи те маленьке?** (Which window? This big one or that small one?)

Finally, we must clarify a very common point of confusion for beginners regarding the word **це**. This small word plays two slightly different roles depending on where it sits in a sentence. When **це** stands alone at the beginning of a sentence, it acts as a universal, unchanging pointer meaning "This is" or "It is". It does not change gender because it is merely introducing a concept.
**Це вікно.** (This is a window.)
**Це книга.** (This is a book.)
**Це стіл.** (This is a table.)

However, when **це** is attached directly to a neuter noun as a descriptive modifier, it specifically means "this [neuter object]".
**Це вікно велике.** (This window is big.)
**Це крісло нове.** (This armchair is new.)

Context will always make the meaning completely transparent.

## Підсумок — Summary

Let us bring all of these patterns together. As you progress through the Ukrainian language, you will quickly notice that grammar is not a random collection of disconnected rules, but rather a highly consistent, repeating mathematical system. The endings you learn for one group of words often apply directly and perfectly to another. This is especially true for gender agreement. 

Look at the synthesis table below. It combines the possessive pronouns you learned in previous modules, the descriptive question words, and the new demonstrative pointing pronouns you mastered today.

| Meaning | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| My | **мій** | **моя** | **моє** | Showing ownership |
| Which | **який** | **яка** | **яке** | Asking for details |
| This | **цей** | **ця** | **це** | Pointing nearby |
| That | **той** | **та** | **те** | Pointing far away |

Look closely at the vertical columns. The masculine column contains words that generally end in a consonant sound (**й**). The feminine column contains words that all end beautifully in the vowel sound **-а** or **-я**. The neuter column contains words that all end in the vowel sound **-е** or **-є**. This structural harmony means that once you know a noun is feminine, you automatically know to use **моя**, **яка**, **ця**, and **та**. The language builds upon itself logically and predictably.

Remember that once you have identified an object using **цей** or **той**, you do not need to keep repeating its name in the following sentences. You can simply refer back to it using the personal pronouns **він** (he/it), **вона** (she/it), or **воно** (it), just as you learned in earlier modules.
**Цей стіл новий. Він великий.** (This table is new. It is big.)
**Та книга стара. Вона цікава.** (That book is old. It is interesting.)

If you see a vocabulary table with wrong translations, missing words, or formatting issues, that content was NOT written by the writer — it was generated deterministically by the ENRICH step. Do NOT flag словník problems as linguistic errors or deduct points from the writer. Report them separately under "ENRICH issues" in your findings (informational only, not scored).

### Step 4: Output raw scores

Output ONLY the raw 1-10 scores in the table below. Do NOT calculate the weighted total — the pipeline script handles the math deterministically. Just write your per-dimension scores.

### Step 5: List findings

For every issue found, provide:
```
[DIMENSION] [SEVERITY: critical/major/minor]
Location: [specific section/paragraph — quote the actual text]
Issue: [what's wrong]
Fix: [exactly how to fix it]
```

Critical = module cannot ship. Major = quality below standard. Minor = polish item.

### Step 6: Verdict

Base your verdict on the **severity of findings** — the pipeline calculates the weighted score separately.

| Verdict | Condition |
|---------|-----------|
| **PASS** | Zero critical findings, at most minor issues |
| **REVISE** | Has major findings but no criticals — fixable without rewrite |
| **REJECT** | Has any critical finding — fundamental problems requiring rewrite |

### Step 7: Fix it yourself (REVISE only)

If your verdict is **REVISE**, you MUST output a `<fixes>` block with exact find/replace pairs. The pipeline applies these deterministically — no LLM regeneration, no rewriting from scratch.

Rules for fixes:
- Each fix is a YAML entry with `find:` (exact text from the module) and `replace:` (corrected text)
- The `find` string must be an EXACT substring of the module content — copy-paste it
- Keep fixes minimal — change only what's wrong, preserve surrounding text
- Do NOT fix словник/vocabulary tables (those are generated by a downstream tool)
- For word count issues: add content as a new `insert_after:` entry instead of find/replace
