## Презентація: Правила вживання та відмінювання

### The Golden Rule: -ся vs. -сь
While the suffix **-ся** [sʲɐ] is the default «face» of reflexive verbs, it actually has a twin: **-сь** [sʲ]. Choosing between them is like choosing between a light coat and a heavy jacket — it all depends on what comes before it! The rule is purely phonetic, designed to keep the language flowing smoothly without awkward clusters of sounds.

If the verb form ends in a **приголосний** (consonant), we use the full suffix **-ся**. If it ends in a **голосний** (vowel), we use the shorter **-сь**. This «vowel-vowel avoidance» is a common feature in Ukrainian that makes your speech sound much more natural and rhythmic.

| Ending Type | Suffix | Example Verb | IPA |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Consonant** | **-ся** | вмиваються | [wmɪˈʋɑjutʲsʲɐ] |
| **Vowel** | **-сь** | вмиваюся | [wmɪˈʋɑjusʲ] |

*   Я **вмиваюся** зранку. (I wash myself in the morning.)
*   Ми **вмиваємося** разом. (We wash ourselves together.)
*   Вони **вмиваються** швидко. (They wash themselves quickly.)

> [!tip]
> In very formal writing or poetry, you might occasionally see **-ся** used after a vowel. However, for everyday speaking and standard modern Ukrainian, always follow the «Vowel likes -сь, Consonant likes -ся» rule to sound like a local!

### Conjugating in the Present Tense
Conjugating a **зворотне дієслово** (reflexive verb) might look intimidating at first, but here is a secret: you already know how to do it! You simply conjugate the base verb as you normally would, and then «glue» the correct suffix onto the very end. The suffix always sits outside the conjugation, like a little tail that never falls off.

Let's look at **вмиватися** (to wash oneself). Notice how the base verb **вмивати** changes its ending for each person, and then we add either **-ся** or **-сь** depending on whether that ending is a vowel or a consonant. Pay close attention to the **наголос** (stress) in the table below, as it stays on the same syllable throughout the pattern.

| Person | Base + Ending | Reflexive Suffix | Full Form |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Я** (I) | вмива-ю | **-сь** | **вмиваюся** |
| **Ти** (You sing.) | вмива-єш | **-ся** | **вмиваєшся** |
| **Він/Вона** (He/She) | вмива-ється | **-ся** | **вмивається** |
| **Ми** (We) | вмива-ємо | **-сь** | **вмиваємося** |
| **Ви** (You plur.) | вмива-єте | **-сь** | **вмиваєтеся** |
| **Вони** (They) | вмива-ють | **-ся** | **вмиваються** |

*   Ти **вмиваєшся** холодною водою. (You wash yourself with cold water.)
*   Вона **вмивається** милом. (She washes herself with soap.)
*   Ви **вмиваєтеся** дуже довго! (You are washing yourselves for a very long time!)

### Three Types of Reflexive Meanings
Not all reflexive verbs work exactly the same way. To use them correctly, it helps to group them into three main categories. First, we have **власне-зворотні** (true reflexive) verbs, where you literally do the action to yourself, like **вмиватися** or **одягатися** (to dress oneself).

The second group consists of **взаємно-зворотні** (reciprocal) verbs. These describe an action that two or more people do to each other. You can't «greet yourself» or «meet yourself» in this sense; you need a partner! Common examples are **вітатися** (to greet each other) and **зустрічатися** (to meet each other). Finally, there are **лексикалізовані** (lexicalized) verbs. These are «reflexive by nature» — they always have the suffix, and their meaning doesn't necessarily imply «self.»

*   **одягатися** — [odʲɐˈɦɑtɪsʲɐ] — to dress oneself (True Reflexive)
*   **вітатися** — [ʋiˈtɑtɪsʲɐ] — to greet each other (Reciprocal)
*   **сміятися** — [smʲiˈjɑtɪsʲɐ] — to laugh (Lexicalized)

Usage note: Even though **сміятися** (to laugh) or **дивитися** (to watch) end in **-ся**, we don't translate them as «laughing oneself.» They are just built that way!

*   Він завжди **сміється**. (He always laughs.)
*   Ми **вітаємося** кожен день. (We greet each other every day.)
*   Я **дивлюся** цікаве кіно. (I am watching an interesting movie.)

### High-Frequency Verbs for Daily Life
To really feel comfortable in a Ukrainian-speaking environment, you should memorize a few «power verbs» that appear in almost every conversation. One of the most important is **називатися** (to be called/named). We use it for objects, titles, or sometimes even for ourselves in formal introductions.

Another essential verb is **вчитися** (to study/learn). Unlike the plain verb for «to teach,» **вчитися** shows that the learning process is happening within you. You will also often hear **повертатися** (to return), whether someone is coming back home or returning to a topic in a conversation. Using these correctly will make your A1 Ukrainian sound much more sophisticated and polished.

*   Як **називається** це місто? (What is this city called?)
*   Я **вчуся** кожного дня. (I study every day.)
*   Він **повертається** додому пізно. (He returns home late.)

**називатися** — [nɐzɪˈʋɑtɪsʲɐ]
**вчитися** — [ˈwtʃɪtɪsʲɐ]
**повертатися** — [poʋerˈtɑtɪsʲɐ]
