## Діалоги (Dialogues)

People constantly negotiate their time and actions. Expressing wants, needs, and capabilities is essential. Ukrainian expresses these ideas by pairing a conjugated modal verb with a base action, forming a compound verbal predicate (складений дієслівний присудок). Combining modal verbs with infinitive verbs creates dozens of meaningful sentences.

When planning a weekend — negotiating what to do, you must balance personal desires against daily obligations. One person might have free time, while the other has mandatory tasks.

> **Оля:** Що ти хочеш робити сьогодні? *(What do you want to do today?)*
> **Денис:** Я хочу гуляти. А ти? *(I want to walk. And you?)*
> **Оля:** Я не можу, я мушу працювати. *(I cannot, I must work.)*
> **Денис:** Шкода! *(Pity!)*

Another practical application occurs every time you visit a restaurant. In these situations, you need to clearly express what specific object you want to receive. The waiter uses their professional ability to offer suggestions and guide your choices.

> **Гість:** Добрий день! Я хочу каву. *(Good day! I want a coffee.)*
> **Офіціант:** Велику чи маленьку? *(Large or small?)*
> **Гість:** Велику. І ще я хочу їсти. Що ви можете порекомендувати? *(Large. And also I want to eat. What can you recommend?)*
> **Офіціант:** Можу порекомендувати борщ! *(I can recommend borscht!)*

These dialogues introduce three critical verbs: **хотіти** (to want), **могти** (to be able to), and **мусити** (to have to). Notice the word **шкода** (pity) when plans fail. We also see two grammatical patterns with **хотіти**. In the first, the verb is followed by an infinitive action, like **я хочу їсти** (I want to eat). In the second, the verb targets a noun directly. The guest says **я хочу каву**. The dictionary form is **кава** (coffee), but it changes its ending to **-у** because it is the direct object, showing the Accusative case.
