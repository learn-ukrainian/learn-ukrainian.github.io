## Вступ та теорія: Хто чи що?

### Warm-up: English scaffolding contrasting «The book» with «The brother»

In our previous lesson, we looked at objects. We learned how to say «I see a book». In Ukrainian, this is **«Я бачу книгу»** — [jɑ ˈbɑtʃʊ ˈknɪɦʊ]. The word **книга** — [ˈknɪɦɑ] (book) changed its ending. But what happens if you see a person? Imagine you are at the park. You see your brother. In English, we just say «I see the brother». The word "brother" stays the same whether he is doing something or something is happening to him. Ukrainian is different. It cares if the object of your sentence is a living person or a "thing".

Let's look at this contrast. When we talk about a book, it is a thing. When we talk about a brother, he is a person. This difference is very important for our grammar. In Ukrainian, we have two big groups of words. We have "Stone Words" for things. We have "Heart Words" for people and animals.

| Sentence Type | English | Ukrainian |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Inanimate (Thing)** | I see a book. | **Я бачу книгу.** — [jɑ ˈbɑtʃʊ ˈknɪɦʊ] |
| **Animate (Person)** | I see a brother. | **Я бачу брата.** — [jɑ ˈbɑtʃʊ ˈbrɑtɑ] |

### Concept: Defining «Істота» (Animate) - introduction of «Heart Words» vs «Stone Words» metaphor

Ukrainian grammar divides the world into two groups. The first group is **істота** — [iˈstɔtɑ] (animate). These are our "Heart Words". They have a heart. They can breathe. They can love. This group includes your **мама** — [ˈmɑmɑ] (mother), your **тато** — [ˈtɑtɔ] (father), and even your **кіт** — [ˈkit] (cat). When these "Heart Words" are the object of a sentence, they often change. They want to show they are alive!

The second group is **неістота** — [ne-iˈstɔtɑ] (inanimate). These are "Stone Words". They do not have a heart. They do not breathe. This group includes a **стіл** — [sʲtil] (table) or a **дім** — [dim] (house). As we saw before, masculine "Stone Words" do not change in the Accusative case. But our "Heart Words" are special. They follow a different rule because they are alive.

> [!observe]
> Think about the word. Does it have a heartbeat? If yes, it is a **Heart Word**. It will need a special ending in the Accusative case.

### Masculine Animate: Step-by-step logic for the Accusative = Genitive (-а/-я) rule

For masculine "Heart Words", the rule is special. The Accusative case (the object) looks exactly like the Genitive case. We call this the «Accusative = Genitive» rule. Usually, a masculine noun ends in a consonant. To make it an object, we add the ending **-а** — [ɑ]. If the word ends in a soft consonant, we add **-я** — [jɑ]. This helps the listener know you are talking about a person, not a thing.

Let's see how this works with our family words. Your **брат** — [brɑt] (brother) becomes **брата** — [ˈbrɑtɑ]. Your **тато** — [ˈtɑtɔ] (father) becomes **тата** — [ˈtɑtɑ]. Even though «тато» ends in -о, it is a masculine word for a person. So it follows this "alive" rule.

_Приклад:_
- **Я бачу брата.** — [jɑ ˈbɑtʃʊ ˈbrɑtɑ] (I see the brother.)
- **Ти знаєш тата.** — [tɪ ˈznɑjeʃ ˈtɑtɑ] (You know the father.)
- **Ми любимо друга.** — [mɪ ˈlʲʊbɪmɔ ˈdrʊɦɑ] (We love the friend.)

### The Animate Trap: Warning against applying the Nominative = Accusative rule to people

Many students fall into a trap. They remember that **стіл** (table) stays **стіл** in a sentence like «Я бачу стіл». They think people work the same way. This is a mistake! If you say **«Я бачу брат»**, a Ukrainian speaker will be confused. It sounds like your brother is a piece of furniture! 

Remember: people are never "things" in Ukrainian. You must use the **-а** or **-я** ending for masculine people. This shows respect for their "heart". It makes your Ukrainian sound natural and correct. Always check: is my object a person? If yes, use the ending.

_Приклад:_
- **Я бачу лікаря.** — [jɑ ˈbɑtʃʊ ˈlʲikɑrʲɑ] (I see the doctor.)
- **Вона знає вчителя.** — [vɔˈnɑ ˈznɑje ˈwtʃɪtelʲɑ] (She knows the teacher.)
- **Студент бачить сусіда.** — [stʊˈdent ˈbɑtʃɪtʲ sʊˈsʲidɑ] (The student sees the neighbor.)

### Feminine Animate: Comparison showing feminine nouns follow the standard -у/-ю pattern

Now, what about the ladies? Good news! Feminine nouns are much simpler. It does not matter if they are "Heart Words" or "Stone Words". They all follow the same rule we learned before. A feminine noun ending in **-а** changes to **-у**. A feminine noun ending in **-я** changes to **-ю**. 

Your **сестра** — [sesˈtrɑ] (sister) becomes **сестру** — [sesˈtrʊ]. Your **подруга** — [ˈpɔdrʊɦɑ] (female friend) becomes **подругу** — [ˈpɔdrʊɦʊ]. This is different from masculine nouns. Masculine nouns care if they are alive. Feminine nouns just care about their ending.

| Nominative (Subject) | Accusative (Object) | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **сестра** — [sesˈtrɑ] | **сестру** — [sesˈtrʊ] | sister |
| **подруга** — [ˈpɔdrʊɦɑ] | **подругу** — [ˈpɔdrʊɦʊ] | female friend |
| **мама** — [ˈmɑmɑ] | **маму** — [ˈmɑmʊ] | mother |

_Приклад:_
- **Я люблю сестру.** — [jɑ lʲʊˈblʲʊ sesˈtrʊ] (I love the sister.)
- **Він знає подругу.** — [vin ˈznɑje ˈpɔdrʊɦʊ] (He knows the female friend.)

### Animals: Grammatical animacy explained for pets and common animals

In Ukrainian, animals are part of the "Heart Words" group too! Your pets are treated like people in grammar. This means a masculine animal like **кіт** — [kit] (cat) or **пес** — [pes] (dog) will change its ending. They are alive, so they take the **-а** ending.

If you have a cat, you say **«Я маю кота»**. Note that **кіт** changes to **кот-** before the ending. This is a small vowel change. If you see a dog, you say **«Я бачу пса»**. Here, the **е** in **пес** disappears. These are "Heart Words" with a little extra character!

_Приклад:_
- **Я маю кота.** — [jɑ ˈmɑjʊ kɔˈtɑ] (I have a cat.)
- **Ти бачиш пса.** — [tɪ ˈbɑtʃɪʃ psɑ] (You see a dog.)
- **Ми любимо кота.** — [mɪ ˈlʲʊbɪmɔ kɔˈtɑ] (We love the cat.)
