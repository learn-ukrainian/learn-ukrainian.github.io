## Практика

### Хто чи що? (Who or What?)
Now that we know animals join the "Heart Word" club, let's organize our world. Before you change any ending, you must ask one simple question: is this a living being? In Ukrainian, we ask **Хто це?** [xtɔ t͡sɛ] (Who is this?) for people and animals, and **Що це?** [ʃt͡ʃɔ t͡sɛ] (What is this?) for everything else.

Imagine you are looking at a photo album. If you see a brother, you ask "Who?". If you see a house, you ask "What?". This distinction is your traffic light. Green light (Alive) means masculine nouns must change. Red light (Not Alive) means masculine nouns stay the same.

- **брат** [brat] (brother) → **Хто?** (Change is coming!)
- **телефон** [tɛlɛˈfɔn] (phone) → **Що?** (No change)
- **кіт** [kʲit] (cat) → **Хто?** (Change is coming!)
- **парк** [park] (park) → **Що?** (No change)

### Змінюємо закінчення (Changing the Endings)
Once you identify a "Who" word, you apply the rule. This is the moment of transformation. For masculine words (he-words), we add the sound **-а** or **-я**. It is like giving them a special badge because they are alive. For feminine words (she-words), the rule is simple: **-а** always turns into **-у**, whether it is a mom or a book.

Let's look at **лікар** [ˈlʲikar] (doctor). Is it a "Who"? Yes. Is it masculine? Yes. So when you wait for him, you wait for **лікаря** [ˈlʲikarʲa] (note the soft sign creates the -я sound). But if you wait for a bus (**автобус**), you just wait for **автобус**.

> [!observe]
> **Look at Dad!**
> The word **тато** [ˈtatɔ] (dad) looks like a feminine word because it ends in **-о** (similar to **-а** in rhythm), but it is 100% masculine. However, because he is a person, he gets the genitive-style ending **-а**.
> **Я бачу тата** [ja ˈbatʃʊ ˈtata].
> Do not say "Я бачу тато". That sounds like he is an object!

### Активні дієслова (Active Verbs)
To practice these changes, we need verbs that interact with people. You cannot just "be" a person; you have to see them, know them, or love them. The four most common verbs for this level are **бачити** [ˈbatʃɪtɪ] (to see), **знати** [ˈznatɪ] (to know), **любити** [lʲʊˈbɪtɪ] (to love), and **чекати** [tʃɛˈkatɪ] (to wait for).

When you use these verbs with people, the Accusative case activates immediately. It is automatic. You cannot "love" (Nominative). You must "love" (Accusative).

- **Я знаю брата** [ja ˈznaju ˈbrata]. (Not "брат")
- **Ми любимо маму** [mɪ lʲʊˈbɪmɔ ˈmamʊ]. (Not "мама")
- **Вона чекає вчителя** [vɔˈna tʃɛˈkaje ˈwtʃɪtɛlʲa]. (Not "вчитель")

### Займенники: Його та Її (Him and Her)
A very common mistake is using "he" or "she" as an object. In English, you say "I know **him**", not "I know **he**". Ukrainian is the same. You must use the Accusative forms of pronouns.

For "he" (**він**), the object form is **його** [jɔˈɦɔ].
For "she" (**вона**), the object form is **її** [jiˈji].

These forms are essential when you don't want to repeat a name. If someone asks "Do you know Ivan?", you answer "Yes, I know **him**".

- **Ти знаєш Івана?** [tɪ ˈznajeʃ iˈvana]
- **Так, я знаю його** [tak ja ˈznaju jɔˈɦɔ].
- **Я бачу її** [ja ˈbatʃʊ jiˈji].

### Будуємо речення (Building Sentences)
Now let's put it all together into full sentences. Your goal is to combine a subject (Nominative), an active verb, and a person-object (Accusative). This structure helps you talk about your family and friends.

Remember the "Goldilocks" check for masculine nouns:
1. Is it a person? (Yes -> Change to **-а**)
2. Is it a thing? (Yes -> No change)

Let's look at some family scenarios:
- **Я маю друга** [ja ˈmaju ˈdruɦa]. (I have a male friend)
- **Я маю подругу** [ja ˈmaju ˈpɔdruɦʊ]. (I have a female friend)
- **Він шукає лікаря** [vʲin ʃʊˈkaje ˈlʲikarʲa]. (He is looking for a doctor)

Notice how **друг** becomes **друга**, but **подруга** becomes **подругу**. The logic is consistent!
