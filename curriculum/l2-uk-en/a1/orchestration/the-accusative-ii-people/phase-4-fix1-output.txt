# The Accusative II: People

Welcome back! You have already mastered the Accusative Case for inanimate objects (things like *coffee*, *book*, *metro*). You know that for masculine objects, nothing changes, but for feminine objects, the ending shifts to **-у** or **-ю**.

Now, we enter the world of **living beings**: people and animals.

In Ukrainian grammar, this distinction is vital. When we interact with people—loving them, seeing them, knowing them—we use the "Animate" category. The question changes from **Що?** (What?) to **Кого?** (Whom?).

Let's learn how to speak about the people in your life.

## Теорія: Істоти та знахідний відмінок

The rule for people (and animals) is slightly different from objects. This is called the **Category of Animacy** (*Категорія істот* [kateˈɦɔrʲijɐ isˈtɔt]).

### 1. The Masculine Change
This is the biggest change from what you learned before.
For **inanimate** masculine objects (bus, park), the word didn't change.
For **animate** masculine nouns (brother, dad, actor), the word **changes**. It adopts the ending of the Genitive case.

> **Rule**: Masculine Animate nouns add **-а** (hard) or **-я** (soft).

| Base (Nominative) | Ending | Accusative (Кого?) | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **брат** [brat] | (hard) + **а** | **брата** [ˈbrɑtɐ] | brother |
| **син** [sɪn] | (hard) + **а** | **сина** [ˈsɪnɐ] | son |
| **студент** [stuˈdɛnt] | (hard) + **а** | **студента** [stuˈdɛntɐ] | student |
| **дідусь** [dʲiˈdusʲ] | (soft) + **я** | **дідуся** [dʲiduˈsʲɑ] | grandpa |
| **водій** [voˈdʲij] | (soft) + **я** | **водія** [vodʲiˈjɑ] | driver |

> [!note]
> Think of it this way: In English, we say "I see **him**" (not "I see he"). In Ukrainian, that **-а/-я** ending is the "him" signal for masculine nouns.

### 2. The Feminine Consistency
Good news! Feminine nouns behave exactly the same way for people as they do for objects. You simply change **-а** to **-у** and **-я** to **-ю**.

| Base (Nominative) | Ending | Accusative (Кого?) | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **мама** [ˈmɑmɐ] | **а** → **у** | **маму** [ˈmɑmu] | mom |
| **сестра** [seˈstrɑ] | **а** → **у** | **сестру** [seˈstru] | sister |
| **донька** [ˈdɔnʲkɐ] | **а** → **у** | **доньку** [ˈdɔnʲku] | daughter |
| **бабуся** [bɐˈbusʲɐ] | **я** → **ю** | **бабусю** [bɐˈbusʲu] | grandma |
| **Марія** [mɐˈrʲijɐ] | **я** → **ю** | **Марію** [mɐˈrʲiju] | Maria |

### 3. Summary of Endings

| Gender | Nominative (Who?) | Accusative (Whom?) | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | Consonant | **+ а / я** | брат → **брата** |
| **Feminine** | -а / -я | **-у / -ю** | сестра → **сестру** |

## Практика

To practice this, we need verbs that interact with people. The most common ones are:

*   **знати** [ˈznɑtɪ] — to know (a person)
*   **бачити** [ˈbɑt͡ʃɪtɪ] — to see
*   **любити** [lʲuˈbɪtɪ] — to love
*   **слухати** [ˈsluxɐtɪ] — to listen to
*   **розуміти** [rozuˈmʲitɪ] — to understand

### Exercise 1: Seeing People
Let's look at some sentences. Notice how the endings change depending on who we see.

*   **Я бачу студента.** [jɑ ˈbɑt͡ʃu stuˈdɛntɐ] — I see a student (male).
*   **Я бачу студентку.** [jɑ ˈbɑt͡ʃu stuˈdɛntku] — I see a student (female).
*   **Ми бачимо батька.** [mɪ ˈbɑt͡ʃɪmo ˈbɑtʲkɐ] — We see father.
*   **Ви бачите маму?** [vɪ ˈbɑt͡ʃɪte ˈmɑmu] — Do you see mom?

### Exercise 2: Knowing People
Using the verb **знати** (to know).

| Nominative | Sentence | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Олег** | Я знаю **Олега**. | I know Oleh. |
| **Ірина** | Ти знаєш **Ірину**? | Do you know Iryna? |
| **сусід** | Він знає **сусіда**. | He knows the neighbor (male). |
| **сусідка** | Вона знає **сусідку**. | She knows the neighbor (female). |

> [!tip]
> **Proper Names**
> Names change too!
> *   Ivan → **Ivana** (Іван → Івана)
> *   Oksana → **Oksanu** (Оксана → Оксану)
> *   Andrii → **Andriia** (Андрій → Андрія)
> *   Yuliia → **Yuliiu** (Юлія → Юлію)

### Exercise 3: Loving Family
The verb **любити** is perfect for family members.

*   **Я люблю маму і тата.** [jɑ lʲuˈblʲu ˈmɑmu i ˈtɑtɐ]
    *   (I love mom and dad.)
*   **Вона любить брата.** [voˈnɑ lʲuˈbɪtʲ ˈbrɑtɐ]
    *   (She loves [her] brother.)
*   **Ми любимо бабусю і дідуся.** [mɪ ˈlʲubɪmo bɐˈbusʲu i dʲiduˈsʲɑ]
    *   (We love grandma and grandpa.)

> [!caution]
> **Masculine Nouns ending in -а/-я**
> Some masculine words like **тато** (dad), **дідусь** (grandpa), or names like **Микола** look feminine or soft.
> *   **Тато** ends in **-о**, but in Accusative it follows the masculine animate rule (hard + a): **тата**.
> *   **Микола** ends in **-а**, so it declines like a feminine noun: **Миколу**.
> *   **Дідусь** is soft masculine: **дідуся**.
>
> Don't worry too much about the exceptions yet, just remember **тата** and **дідуся**.

## Використання

Let's put this into real-world contexts. You will use the Accusative case daily when talking about your social circle.

### Scenario A: Introducing Friends
Imagine you are at a party. You want to ask if someone knows your friend.

— **Ти знаєш Максима?** [tɪ ˈznɑjeʃ mɐkˈsɪmɐ]
— (Do you know Maksym?)
— **Ні, я не знаю Максима. А ти знаєш Ольгу?** [nʲi, jɑ nɛ ˈznɑju mɐkˈsɪmɐ. ɑ tɪ ˈznɑjeʃ ˈɔlʲɦu]
— (No, I don't know Maksym. And do you know Olha?)
— **Так, я знаю Ольгу.** [tɑk, jɑ ˈznɑju ˈɔlʲɦu]
— (Yes, I know Olha.)

### Scenario B: Whom are you waiting for?
The verb **чекати** (to wait for) also uses the Accusative case for people.

— **Кого ти чекаєш?** [koˈɦɔ tɪ t͡ʃeˈkɑjeʃ]
— (Whom are you waiting for?)
— **Я чекаю клієнта.** [jɑ t͡ʃeˈkɑju klʲiˈjɛntɐ]
— (I am waiting for a client [male].)
— **А я чекаю подругу.** [ɑ jɑ t͡ʃeˈkɑju ˈpɔdruɦu]
— (And I am waiting for a friend [female].)

### Scenario C: Listening to Music (Artists)
When we listen to an artist, we treat them as a person, not just a sound.

*   **Я слухаю "Океан Ельзи".** (Band name treated as inanimate/abstract usually, but often personalized).
*   **Я слухаю Джамалу.** [jɑ ˈsluxɐju d͡ʒɐˈmɑlu] — I listen to Jamala.
*   **Ми слухаємо Тараса Шевченка.** [mɪ ˈsluxɐjemo tɐˈrɑsɐ ʃeu̯ˈt͡ʃɛnkɐ] — We listen to Taras Shevchenko (recitation).

## Культурний погляд

In Ukrainian culture, addressing people correctly is a sign of respect (*повага*). When you use the formal titles **Пан** (Mr.) or **Пані** (Ms./Mrs.), you must decline them too!

### The Double Change
If you see "Mr. Tkachenko," both words change.

*   Nominative: **Пан Ткаченко**
*   Accusative: **Пана Ткаченка** [ˈpɑnɐ tkɐˈt͡ʃɛnkɐ]

If you see "Mrs. Koval," both parts change (if the surname is declinable, though female surnames ending in consonants usually don't change. Let's stick to first names for simplicity at A1).

*   Nominative: **Пані Олена**
*   Accusative: **Пані Олену** [ˈpɑnʲi oˈlɛnu]

> **Note**: The title **Пані** is indeclinable! It stays **Пані** in all cases.
> *   Я бачу **пані** Софію. (I see Ms. Sofiia).
>
> But **Пан** acts like a regular masculine noun.
> *   Я бачу **пана** Андрія. (I see Mr. Andrii).

### Cultural Context: "Visiting"
Ukrainians love to visit and host guests. You might hear:
*   **Ми чекаємо гостя.** [mɪ t͡ʃeˈkɑjemo ˈɦɔsʲtʲɐ] — We are expecting a guest.
*   **Зустрічати гостя.** [zustrʲiˈt͡ʃɑtɪ ˈɦɔsʲtʲɐ] — To meet/welcome a guest.

Remember, seeing a person involves acknowledging them correctly. Getting the ending right shows you care about *whom* you are speaking to.

# Summary

You have taken a big step today. You can now talk about people, not just things!

**Key Takeaways:**

1.  **The Question**: Use **Кого?** (Whom?) for people and animals.
2.  **Masculine Rule**: Animate nouns add **-а** (hard) or **-я** (soft).
    *   *брат* → *брата*
3.  **Feminine Rule**: Same as objects. **-а** → **-у**, **-я** → **-ю**.
    *   *сестра* → *сестру*
4.  **Verbs**: Use with *любити*, *знати*, *бачити*, *чекати*, *слухати*.

| Gender | Nominative (Dictionary) | Accusative (Used in sentence) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masculine** | друг (friend) | Я маю **друга**. |
| **Feminine** | подруга (friend) | Я маю **подругу**. |
| **Masculine** | тато (dad) | Я люблю **тата**. |
| **Feminine** | мама (mom) | Я люблю **маму**. |

Practice these endings when talking about your family and friends. Next time you see your friend, think: "Я бачу друг**а**!" or "Я бачу подруг**у**!".
