## Summary

Now that you understand the cultural importance of names and titles, let’s bring all our new grammar tools together. You have taken a big step today! You can now talk about the people you love, know, and see.

### Живі «істоти» (Living Beings)
We learned that Ukrainian grammar treats people and animals differently from objects. We call them **істоти** (animate nouns). These words answer the question **«Хто?»** (Who?), not **«Що?»** (What?). Think of them as "Heart Words."

*   **Лікар** (doctor) — це істота (animate).
*   **Кіт** (cat) — це теж істота (animate).
*   **Стіл** (table) — це неістота (inanimate).

It is important to feel this difference. When you see a person, your grammar brain should switch to "Animate Mode."

### Чоловічий рід: Велика зміна (Masculine Change)
This was our main rule today. For masculine nouns (people and animals), the Nominative form acts like the **Genitive**. The word MUST change. We usually add the ending **-а** or **-я**.

*   **Брат** (brother) $\rightarrow$ **Я бачу брата.**
*   **Друг** (friend) $\rightarrow$ **Я чекаю друга.**
*   **Вчитель** (teacher) $\rightarrow$ **Ми слухаємо вчителя.**

> [!tip]
> **Don't fall into the trap!**
> Never use the base form for a man.
> *   ❌ **Wrong:** Я знаю **Іван**.
> *   ✅ **Right:** Я знаю **Івана**.

### Жіночий рід: Стабільність (Feminine Stability)
The good news is that feminine nouns are consistent. Whether it is a book or a sister, the rule is the same. Hard **-а** changes to **-у**, and soft **-я** changes to **-ю**.

*   **Мама** (mom) $\rightarrow$ **Я люблю маму.**
*   **Сестра** (sister) $\rightarrow$ **Я бачу сестру.**
*   **Катя** (Katya) $\rightarrow$ **Я знаю Катю.**

You do not need to learn a special rule here. Just remember: feminine nouns love the letter **-у** in the Accusative case!
