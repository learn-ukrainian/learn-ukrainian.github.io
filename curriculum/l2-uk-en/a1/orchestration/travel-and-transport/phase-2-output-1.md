# Подорожі та транспорт

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Navigating a new environment is one of the most empowering things you can do as a language learner. Knowing how to name vehicles, ask for directions, and describe your journey will make you feel confident and independent when exploring Ukrainian cities and landscapes. 

## Транспорт

Exploring a new city means learning how to use its public transportation network. Ukraine has a fantastic, diverse system of transit options in its cities, from deep underground trains to electric vehicles on the bustling streets. Let's learn the names of the most common vehicles you will see. 

The great news for beginners is that many of these transport words sound very similar to their English equivalents. They are international words that have been adapted to the Ukrainian alphabet and pronunciation. Read these words aloud and notice where the stress marks fall:

**авто́бус** (bus)
**тролейбу́с** (trolleybus)
**трамва́й** (tram)
**метро́** (subway / underground)
**таксі́** (taxi)
**маши́на** (car)

The word «тролейбу́с» refers to a large bus that runs quietly on electric wires suspended above the street. They are very common, highly efficient, and environmentally friendly. A «трамва́й» runs on rails, usually right in the middle or along the side of the road. The word for car, «маши́на», literally translates to "machine," which makes it incredibly memorable!

> [!culture]
> The Kyiv Metro is famous worldwide. The «Арсена́льна» (Arsenalna) station on the red line is one of the deepest underground transit stations in the world, located 105.5 meters below the surface. The escalator ride down takes over five minutes!

One extremely important grammar rule for beginners to remember: the words «метро́» and «таксі́» are special. Because they are borrowed foreign words ending in vowels that do not fit the usual Ukrainian noun patterns, they never change their endings. They are "frozen" words. Whether you are in the metro, looking at the metro, or waiting for the metro, the word stays exactly the same. This is a rare and wonderful gift in Ukrainian grammar!

Let's look at some simple statements using these new words. Notice how the sentence structure is straightforward and logical.

**Ось авто́бус.** (Here is a bus.)
**Там стої́ть трамва́й.** (There stands a tram.)
**Де метро́?** (Where is the subway?)
**Я ба́чу таксі́.** (I see a taxi.)
**Це моя́ маши́на.** (This is my car.)

## Вокзал та аеропорт

When you travel between cities or countries, you will need to navigate larger transport hubs. The vocabulary for these locations is essential for any traveler moving across Ukraine. Let's look at the main places where journeys begin and end.

**вокза́л** (train station)
**аеропо́рт** (airport)
**зупи́нка** (stop — for a bus or tram)
**ста́нція** (station — for the metro)
**квито́к** (ticket)
**валі́за** (suitcase)

The word «вокза́л» specifically refers to a large central train station, the bustling heart of intercity travel. If you are just waiting on the street corner for a local city bus, you are standing at a «зупи́нка». To ride any of these public vehicles, you will always need a «квито́к». Always remember to keep your ticket safe during the entire ride, as inspectors often check them. When packing your «валі́за», remember that traveling light makes navigating the wide stairs at the station much easier. Also, keep in mind that Ukraine has a vast network of intercity buses that also use stations, often located right next to the train hub.

> [!tip]
> Pay attention to the pronunciation of «вокза́л». The letter **л** at the end is hard, giving the word a solid, grounded sound. Do not confuse it with «зупи́нка», which is a much smaller place!

Now we need an action verb to get us moving. In English, we use the verb "to go" for almost everything, whether we are walking, flying, or driving. In Ukrainian, we are very specific. If you are using a vehicle to travel, you must use the verb «ї́хати» (to ride / to go by vehicle). Do not use this verb if you are simply traveling on foot!

**я ї́ду** (I am riding / going by vehicle)
**він ї́де** (he is riding)
**вона́ ї́де** (she is riding)
**ми ї́демо** (we are riding)
**вони́ ї́дуть** (they are riding)

Let's combine our new locations with our new verb. We will use the prepositions «в» (in/into) or «на» (on/onto) to show our destination. Do not worry about the complex grammar of destinations yet; just observe how the phrases are put together clearly.

**Я ї́ду на вокза́л.** (I am going to the train station.)
**Вона́ ї́де в аеропо́рт.** (She is going to the airport.)
**Ми ма́ємо квито́к.** (We have a ticket.)
**Де моя́ валі́за?** (Where is my suitcase?)

## Напрямки

Once you are in a taxi or asking a friendly local for directions on the street, you need to know how to navigate. Directional words in Ukrainian are mostly adverbs. This is fantastic news for beginners, because it means they are simple vocabulary items that never change their form. You just learn them and use them exactly as they are.

**пря́мо** (straight)
**напра́во** (to the right)
**налі́во** (to the left)
**туди́** (there / in that direction)
**сюди́** (here / in this direction)

If you take a taxi in Ukraine, you can easily guide the driver using these simple words. Just add «бу́дь ла́ска» (please) to be polite, and you can comfortably navigate anywhere in the city. 

> [!warning]
> Be careful with the word «пря́мо» (straight). English speakers often confuse it with "right" because of the letters "pr". Remember: «пря́мо» means straight ahead, while «напра́во» means to the right side!

The words «туди́» and «сюди́» are incredibly useful when you want to indicate the direction of movement. This is slightly different from just pointing out a static location. They specifically mean "to that place" and "to this place". If you cannot remember the exact name of a street, simply pointing and saying "there" is a perfectly valid and highly effective communication strategy for a beginner.

Let's see how these words describe motion in action. We can use our vehicle words and our verb «ї́хати» to paint a vivid picture of traffic moving dynamically through a city space.

**Авто́бус ї́де пря́мо.** (The bus is going straight.)
**Маши́на ї́де налі́во.** (The car is going left.)
**Таксі́ ї́де напра́во.** (The taxi is going right.)
**По́їзд ї́де туди́.** (The train is going there.)
**Трамва́й ї́де сюди́.** (The tram is coming here.)

## Подорожі

Now that we know the vehicles, the transit stations, and the directions, we can talk about the experience of the journey itself. Traveling is an adventure, and knowing how to describe your trip makes the experience much richer and easier to share with new friends.

**по́дорож** (journey / trip)
**доро́га** (road / way)
**пасажи́р** (passenger)
**шви́дко** (fast / quickly)
**пові́льно** (slowly)
**дале́ко** (far)
**бли́зько** (near)

A «по́дорож» is the trip you take, encompassing the entire experience from start to finish. The «доро́га» is the physical road you travel on, or the time spent in transit. Whenever you are riding a bus or relaxing on a train, you are a «пасажи́р». 

> [!did-you-know]
> «Укрзалізни́ця» (Ukrainian Railways) is the absolute backbone of the country. Even in the most difficult times, these trains are famous for running exactly on schedule, keeping people connected across vast distances. Train travel is deeply woven into modern Ukrainian culture.

We also need a very important question word to ask about destinations: "Where to?". In English, we often just use "where" for both a fixed location and a moving direction. In Ukrainian, there is a specific word that means exactly "Where to?": «куди́».

**Куди́ ї́де по́їзд?** (Where is the train going?)
**Куди́ ї́де маши́на?** (Where is the car going?)

Let's answer these questions and describe how our vehicles are moving through the landscape. Notice how adverbs like «шви́дко» and «дале́ко» add rich detail to our simple, effective sentences.

**По́їзд ї́де в Ки́їв.** (The train is going to Kyiv.)
**Він ї́де шви́дко.** (It is going fast.)
**Авто́бус ї́де дале́ко.** (The bus is going far.)
**Трамва́й ї́де пові́льно.** (The tram is going slowly.)
**Вокза́л — це бли́зько.** (The station is near.)
**Це га́рна по́дорож.** (This is a good journey.)

## Практика

Let's bring everything together. Reading a short, continuous story is the best way to see how words interact with each other in natural contexts. Read the following text slowly. Do not worry if you do not translate every single word instantly; try to picture the scene in your mind. Focus on the flow of the journey.

**Анто́н — пасажи́р. Він ма́є квито́к.**
**Анто́н ї́де на вокза́л. Він ї́де на таксі́.**
**Таксі́ ї́де шви́дко. Маши́на ї́де пря́мо, а по́тім налі́во.**
**Ось вокза́л. Анто́н ба́чить вели́кий по́їзд.**
**По́їзд ї́де в Оде́су. Це дале́ко.**
**Це бу́де га́рна по́дорож.**

Here is the English translation so you can check your understanding of the narrative:

Anton is a passenger. He has a ticket. Anton is going to the train station. He is going by taxi. The taxi is going fast. The car goes straight, and then to the left. Here is the station. Anton sees a large train. The train is going to Odesa. It is far. This will be a good journey.

Read the Ukrainian text one more time, out loud. Feel the rhythm of the sentences and celebrate how much you can already understand without needing the English crutch!

# Підсумок

You have successfully learned the essential vocabulary to navigate travel in Ukraine! You can now identify vehicles on the street, find stations, ask about directions, and describe your journey clearly. This is a massive step toward independence in your language learning adventure.

4. What is the special Ukrainian question word for "Where to?"
