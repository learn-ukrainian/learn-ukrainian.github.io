# Дослідження: Yesterday - Past Tense

## State Standard Reference
§4.2.4.1: "утворення форм минулого часу дієслова: читав, читала, читало, читали; сміявся, сміялася, сміялося, сміялися; був, була, було, були"
Alignment: This module directly addresses the formation of the past tense for standard verbs (читав), introduces the past forms of the foundational verb 'бути' (був/була/було/були), and covers gender and number agreement as strictly required by the standard for A1.

## Cultural Hooks
1. **Printing of the Apostol (1574)**: On February 15, 1574, Ivan Fedorov printed the first book in Ukraine, the "Apostol", in Lviv. This serves as a concrete, historical past event to anchor the grammar concept.
2. **ZUNR Language Law (1919)**: On February 15, 1919, the West Ukrainian People's Republic (ЗУНР) passed a law elevating Ukrainian to the state language, providing an empowering narrative context for practicing past events ("ЗУНР прийняла закон").

## Common Learner Errors
1. **Gender Mismatch in the 1st Person**: Female learners defaulting to the masculine form (saying «Я читав» instead of «Я читала»). — *Why it happens*: Overgeneralizing the dictionary form or lacking awareness that past tense verbs must agree with the speaker's gender, not the grammatical person.
2. **English Calque "Was doing"**: Saying «Я був працював» to translate "I was working". — *Why it happens*: Direct translation of the English past continuous structure. Ukrainian natively handles this with the imperfective past («Я працював»).
3. **Preposition Overuse with Time Markers**: Saying «в минулому тижні» for "last week". — *Why it happens*: Interference from English ("in the past..."). Ukrainian uses the genitive case directly without a preposition («минулого тижня»).

## Notes for Content Writing
- **Decolonized Framing**: Frame the Ukrainian past tense positively as an elegant, simplified system (relying purely on gender/number, without conjugation for person) — a beautiful and efficient relic of the Old East Slavic L-participle system. Avoid using Russian grammar as a baseline for comparison.
- Ensure explicit English scaffolding in the introduction per A1 immersion rules (35-40% for late A1), clearly setting the stage before transitioning to Ukrainian examples.
