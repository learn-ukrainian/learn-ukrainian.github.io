## Вступ: Що було вчора? (Introduction: What Happened Yesterday?)

In English, we have many ways to talk about the past. In Ukrainian, we have one main **мину́лий час** (past tense) that does most of the heavy lifting. This makes it a very powerful tool for you! To see why this matters, let's look at a significant moment in Ukrainian history. On February 15, 1574, Ivan Fedorov **надрукува́в** (printed) the first book in Lviv. It was called the «Apostol». 

> **(Львівський музе́й / Lviv Museum)**
>
> — Що він зроби́в? (What did he do?)
> — Він **надрукува́в** пе́ршу кни́гу. (He printed the first book.)
> — Це було́ у Льво́ві? (Was it in Lviv?)
> — Так, це було́ у Льво́ві. (Yes, it was in Lviv.)

To talk about such events, you need time expressions. In Ukrainian, these are quite simple. Unlike English, you don't use a preposition like «in» for the word **мину́лий** (last/past). You simply say the phrase!

*   **вчо́ра** — yesterday
*   **вчо́ра зра́нку** — yesterday morning
*   **вчо́ра вве́чері** — yesterday evening
*   **мину́лого ти́жня** — last week
*   **мину́лого мі́сяця** — last month
*   **мину́лого ро́ку** — last year

Don't worry about the grammar of **мину́лого** yet; just treat these as fixed phrases for now. You are already expanding your horizons!

## Основи минулого часу (Grammar: Past Tense Formation)

Forming the past tense is very different from the present tense. In the present, you change the ending based on the person (**я, ти, він**...). In the **мину́лий час**, the verb changes based on **gender** (masculine, feminine, or neuter) and **number** (singular or plural). This is a quick win for you because the endings are very consistent!

To form the past tense, you take the infinitive (the dictionary form like **чита́ти**), remove the **-ти**, and add these suffixes:

| Gender/Number | Ending | Example: **чита́ти** (to read) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Чолові́чий рід** (Masculine) | **-в** | він **чита́в** |
| **Жіно́чий рід** (Feminine) | **-ла** | вона́ **чита́ла** |
| **Сере́дній рід** (Neuter) | **-ло** | воно́ **чита́ло** |
| **Множина́** (Plural) | **-ли** | вони́ **чита́ли** |

Take a look at how this works with some of your favorite verbs:

*   Він **працюва́в** у о́фісі. (He worked in the office.)
*   Вона́ **працюва́ла** вдо́ма. (She worked at home.)
*   Дити́на **спа́ла** до́вго. (The child slept for a long time.)
*   Ми **чита́ли** ціка́ві нови́ни. (We read interesting news.)
*   Ви **роби́ли** ка́ву вчо́ра? (Did you make coffee yesterday?)

> [!warning] **The "Gender Trap"**
> Since the past tense depends on gender, you must match the verb to yourself! If you identify as a woman, you must say **«Я чита́ла»**. If you are a man, say **«Я чита́в»**. English speakers often forget this because "I read" is the same for everyone. Take your time to get this right!

Also, be careful not to translate word-for-word from English. If you want to say «I was working», you simply use the past tense: **«Я працюва́в»**. Never say ~~«Я був працював»~~—that is a mistake your tutor will help you avoid!

## Складні випадки та практика (Irregular Verbs and Practice)

Even in the museum of grammar, there are some "unique exhibits"—irregular verbs! The most important one is **бу́ти** (to be). Its past tense forms are the foundation for many stories.

*   Він **був** у музе́ї. (He was in the museum.)
*   Вона́ **була́** на робо́ті. (She was at work.)
*   Мі́сто **було́** старе́. (The city was old.)
*   Вони́ **були́** вчо́ра тут. (They were here yesterday.)

Another interesting case is the verb **ї́сти** (to eat). Notice how the root changes slightly. And for verbs of motion like **йти** (to go on foot), the change is even bigger!

*   Він **їв** сма́чний борщ. (He ate delicious borscht.)
*   Вона́ **ї́ла** я́блуко. (She ate an apple.)
*   Він **ішо́в** додо́му. (He was going home.)
*   Вона́ **ішла́** в магази́н. (She was going to the store.)

> On February 15, 1919, the West Ukrainian People's Republic (ZUNR) passed a law making Ukrainian the state language. We can say: **«ЗУНР прийняла́ зако́н»** (ZUNR adopted the law). History and language always walk together!

Let's practice these forms in a museum setting. Imagine you are looking at old photographs from **мину́лого ро́ку**.

*   Мину́лого ти́жня я **пив** чай тут. (Last week I drank tea here.)
*   Вона́ **пила́** ка́ву вчо́ра вве́чері. (She drank coffee yesterday evening.)
*   Студе́нтка **диви́лася** на карти́ну. (The student watched the painting.)
*   Ми **диви́лися** нови́ни рані́ше. (We watched the news earlier.)
*   Хло́пець **ходи́в** у па́рк. (The boy went to the park.)

> [!practice] **Reading Practice**
> 1. Вчо́ра я **був** удо́ма і **чита́в** кни́гу. (Yesterday I was at home and read a book.)
> 2. Вона́ **працюва́ла** мину́лого ти́жня. (She worked last week.)
> 3. Ми **ї́ли** в рестора́ні вчо́ра вве́чері. (We ate in a restaurant yesterday evening.)
> 4. Ти **пила́** сі́к чи во́ду? (Did you drink juice or water?)
> 5. Вони́ **диви́лися** но́вий фільм. (They watched a new film.)
> 6. Дити́на **спа́ла**, а я **роби́в** ка́ву. (The child slept, and I made coffee.)
