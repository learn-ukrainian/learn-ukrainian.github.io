## 3. Cultural Hook

### The "Exam Ticket" Tradition (–ë—ñ–ª–µ—Ç–∏)
In the Ukrainian educational system, examinations (especially oral ones) traditionally use "–±—ñ–ª–µ—Ç–∏" (tickets). Each student draws a random ticket containing 2-3 questions/tasks. For an A1 learner, simulating this "ticket" system adds an authentic Ukrainian academic flavor to the final assessment, even for a language exam.

Ukrainians place a high value on formal address in educational settings. ## 4. Pedagogical Notes

### Key Differences from English
- **Case Endings**: English uses word order and prepositions for functions that Ukrainian handles with inflection (cases). A1 students often forget to change endings (e.g., *—è –±–∞—á—É –∫–Ω–∏–≥–∞* instead of *–∫–Ω–∏–≥—É*).
- **Gender Agreement**: Unlike English, every noun has gender, and adjectives/possessives must agree. This is the most frequent source of errors at A1.
- **Verb Conjugation**: Ukrainian verbs have distinct endings for all persons, whereas English is relatively invariant (except 3rd person singular).

### Common Learner Errors
- **Genitive vs. Accusative**: Confusing the endings for animate vs. inanimate objects in the Accusative, or using the wrong case for negation (*—è –Ω–µ –±–∞—á—É –∫–Ω–∏–≥—É* instead of *–∫–Ω–∏–≥–∏*).
- **Omission of 'to be'**: Since the present tense of 'to be' (*—î*) is usually omitted in Ukrainian, English speakers often try to insert it where it doesn't belong or struggle with sentences where it is implied.
- **Word Order**: While Ukrainian is flexible, A1 students may produce "English-translated" sentences that sound unnatural.

### Teaching Sequence Recommendations
The final exam should mirror the **PPP (Present-Practice-Produce)** methodology used throughout A1. It should begin with simple recognition/identification (receptive skills) and move towards production (writing a short self-bio or engaging in a simple dialogue).
