# Shopping and Market

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> You need to buy food, water, and daily essentials. In Ukraine, shopping is a mix of modern supermarkets and traditional outdoor markets. Knowing how to ask for prices and quantities is a core survival skill. Let's learn the basics of a successful shopping trip!

## Скільки коштує? (How Much?)

Ми йдемо́ в магази́н. Ми хо́чемо купи́ти проду́кти. Це ду́же важли́во. Пе́рше запита́ння — про гро́ші. Ми за́вжди запи́туємо ці́ну. Скі́льки це кошту́є? Скі́льки кошту́є хліб? Скі́льки кошту́є вода́? Скі́льки кошту́є ка́ва?

We are going to the store. We want to buy groceries. This is very important. The first question is about money. We always ask the price. How much does this cost? How much does bread cost? How much does water cost? How much does coffee cost?

The verb **коштува́ти** means «to cost». It is your absolute best friend when shopping. You will hear and use the form **кошту́є** for a single item, and **кошту́ють** for multiple items. To ask a price, use the question word **скі́льки** (how much). This is the most important phrase you can learn for daily life.

В Украї́ні ми використо́вуємо гри́вні. Одна́ гри́вня. Дві гри́вні. П'ять гри́вень. Де́сять гри́вень. Скі́льки кошту́є борщ? Борщ кошту́є сімдеся́т гри́вень. Скі́льки кошту́є чай? Чай кошту́є со́рок п'ять гри́вень. Скі́льки кошту́ють я́блука? Вони́ кошту́ють три́дцять гри́вень.

In Ukraine, we use hryvnias. One hryvnia. Two hryvnias. Five hryvnias. Ten hryvnias. How much does the borsch cost? The borsch costs seventy hryvnias. How much does tea cost? The tea costs forty-five hryvnias. How much do the apples cost? They cost thirty hryvnias.

Let's review the money rules from our numbers lesson. The word for the Ukrainian currency, **гри́вня**, changes its ending depending on the number that comes right before it. This is a very common grammatical pattern in our language. If the price ends in one, we say **одна́ гри́вня**. If the price ends in two, three, or four, we say **дві гри́вні**, **три гри́вні**, **чоти́ри гри́вні**. For numbers five and above, or for prices ending in zero, we use the form **гри́вень**. For example, **де́сять гри́вень** or **сто гри́вень**.

Notice how we use the plural form **кошту́ють** for the apples, but the singular **кошту́є** for tea and borsch. This distinction will help your speech sound natural.

[!warning]
> Be careful with number agreement! A common mistake is saying «двадцять гривня». The correct phrase is «двадцять гри́вень» because the number ends in zero. Take a moment to memorize these three forms.

## У магазині (In the Store)

Я хо́чу купи́ти молоко́. Я хо́чу купи́ти сир. Да́йте молоко́, будь ла́ска. Да́йте сир, будь ла́ска. У вас є вода́? У вас є сві́жий хліб? Так, у нас є хліб. Ні, хлі́ба нема́є.

I want to buy milk. I want to buy cheese. Give me milk, please. Give me cheese, please. Do you have water? Do you have fresh bread? Yes, we have bread. No, there is no bread.

When you know exactly what you want, you can say **я хо́чу купи́ти** (I want to buy). The verb **купи́ти** means «to buy».
Another very common and perfectly polite way to ask for things is the phrase **Да́йте, будь ла́ска**. This translates to «Give me, please». While «give me» sounds quite demanding in English, in Ukrainian it is the standard, polite way to request an item from a vendor at a kiosk or market. Treat this as a fixed, ready-to-use chunk of language.

To ask if a store carries an item, use the phrase **У вас є...?** (Do you have...?). Literally, this phrase translates to «At you is...?». You can also simply ask **Є...?** (Is there...?) if you want to be even more brief.

[!tip]
> English speakers often try to use the verb «ма́ти» (to have) when shopping. Do not say «Ви ма́єте хліб?». Always use the existence structure: «У вас є хліб?». This is how native speakers talk.

До́брий день! У вас є ка́ва? Так, є. Скі́льки кошту́є ка́ва? Ка́ва кошту́є со́рок гри́вень. Да́йте ка́ву, будь ла́ска. Ось, будь ла́ска. Ва́ша ре́шта. Дя́кую! До поба́чення!

Good afternoon! Do you have coffee? Yes, we do. How much does coffee cost? Coffee costs forty hryvnias. Give me coffee, please. Here you go. Your change. Thank you! Goodbye!

Here is a simple flow for a complete transaction. You greet the vendor, check for the item, ask the price, request it, receive your change (**ре́шта**), and say thank you. The word **ось** means «here is» when handing something over. If you prefer paying with plastic, a **ка́ртка** (card) is accepted almost everywhere in modern Ukraine.

## Кількість та одиниці (Quantities and Units)

Ми купу́ємо рі́зні проду́кти. Ми використо́вуємо рі́зні слова́. Кілогра́м. Літр. Па́чка. Пля́шка. Шту́ка. Я хо́чу купи́ти кілогра́м я́блук. Да́йте літр молока́, будь ла́ска. Мені́ потрі́бна па́чка цу́кру. Да́йте пля́шку води́.

We buy different products. We use different words. Kilogram. Litre. Packet. Bottle. Piece. I want to buy a kilogram of apples. Give me a litre of milk, please. I need a packet of sugar. Give me a bottle of water.

You cannot always buy just «one» of something. We use specific units of measurement for different types of goods at the market and the supermarket.
**Кілогра́м** is used for weight, like fresh meat, fruit, or vegetables.
**Літр** is for liquids, like milk, juice, or oil.
**Па́чка** is a packet or a box, often used for tea, cookies, or sugar.
**Пля́шка** is a bottle, typically used for water or lemonade.
**Шту́ка** means a «piece» or a single item, like one lemon or one avocado.

When you combine a unit of measurement with a product, the product word changes its ending. This is the Genitive case, which shows the «of» relationship in Ukrainian grammar. For now, just remember these forms as fixed, everyday phrases. **Кілогра́м я́блук** means «a kilogram of apples». **Літр молока́** means «a litre of milk».

Да́йте два кілогра́ми помідо́рів. Я хо́чу три лі́три води́. Да́йте п'ять кілогра́мів карто́плі.

Give me two kilograms of tomatoes. I want three litres of water. Give me five kilograms of potatoes.

Units of measurement change endings with numbers, just like the currency does!
For numbers two, three, and four, we say **два кілогра́ми** or **два лі́три**.
For five and above, we say **п'ять кілогра́мів** or **п'ять лі́трів**.

[!culture]
> When you shop at a Ukrainian outdoor market, or «ри́нок», buying produce by the kilogram is the absolute standard. Vendors will often weigh your items on a small digital scale right in front of you.

## Засоби гігієни (Hygiene Products)

Мені́ потрі́бне ми́ло. Я купу́ю зубну́ па́сту. Мені́ потрі́бен шампу́нь. Де мій рушни́к? Я купу́ю туале́тний папі́р.

I need soap. I am buying toothpaste. I need shampoo. Where is my towel? I am buying toilet paper.

Grocery stores are not the only places we shop. You also need to know the names of essential hygiene products for your daily routine. Let's look at the most common ones.
**Ми́ло** is soap. It is a neuter noun.
**Зубна́ па́ста** is toothpaste. It consists of two words.
**Шампу́нь** is shampoo. Remember that this word is masculine in Ukrainian, which surprises many learners!
**Рушни́к** is a towel. You use this after washing.
**Туале́тний папі́р** is toilet paper.

Де тут апте́ка? Де тут магази́н? Де мо́жна купи́ти ми́ло? Ми́ло мо́жна купи́ти в магази́ні або́ в апте́ці.

Where is the pharmacy here? Where is the store here? Where can one buy soap? One can buy soap in the store or in the pharmacy.

If you are in a new city, you will definitely need to find these items quickly. You can easily combine your hygiene vocabulary with simple location questions. Ask **Де тут...?** (Where is the... here?) or use the phrase **Де мо́жна купи́ти...?** (Where can one buy...?). A pharmacy is called an **апте́ка**, and a regular store is a **магази́н**.

## Практика (Practice)

У магази́ні. До́брий день! У вас є зубна́ па́ста? Так, є. Скі́льки вона́ кошту́є? П'ятдеся́т гри́вень. До́бре. Да́йте, будь ла́ска. Ось, будь ла́ска. Ва́ша ре́шта.

In the store. Good afternoon! Do you have toothpaste? Yes, we do. How much does it cost? Fifty hryvnias. Good. Give me, please. Here you go. Your change.

На ри́нку. До́брий ра́нок! Скі́льки кошту́ють помідо́ри? Вісімдеся́т гри́вень кілогра́м. Да́йте два кілогра́ми, будь ла́ска. Ось, будь ла́ска. Що ще? Дя́кую, це все.

At the market. Good morning! How much do the tomatoes cost? Eighty hryvnias a kilogram. Give me two kilograms, please. Here you go. What else? Thank you, that is all.

Let's practice what we have learned today. Read these two short dialogues out loud to build your speaking confidence. In the first dialogue, the customer shops at the **магази́н** (store) for hygiene products. In the second, they are buying fresh food at the **ри́нок** (market).

Мій спи́сок проду́ктів. Оди́н хліб. Літр молока́. Два кілогра́ми я́блук. Па́чка ча́ю. Пля́шка води́. Туале́тний папі́р.

My shopping list. One bread. A litre of milk. Two kilograms of apples. A packet of tea. A bottle of water. Toilet paper.

A shopping list is a fantastic way to practice reading quantities and products together. Try writing your own list in Ukrainian before you go to the supermarket next time!

Before moving on to the next topic, verify your new skills. Can you do the following tasks without looking back?
1. Ask how much a bottle of water costs.
2. Ask the vendor if they have fresh bread, without using the verb «ма́ти».

4. Say the price «forty hryvnias» with the correct ending for the currency.

If you can do all of these, you are completely ready for your next shopping trip!
