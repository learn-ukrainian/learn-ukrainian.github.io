## Cultural Hooks
1. **The Origins of Cyrillic**: The alphabet is named after Saint Cyril (9th century), although he likely created the earlier Glagolitic script. The Cyrillic script as we know it was developed by his students, such as Clement of Ohrid, in the First Bulgarian Empire.
2. **The "I" Identity and the Letter Ґ**: The dotted «І» is a visual hallmark of Ukrainian typography, distinguishing it from neighboring alphabets and linking it visually to European traditions. The letter «Ґ» (hard G) serves as a symbol of linguistic resilience; it was repressed by Soviet authorities in 1933 in an attempt to forcibly assimilate Ukrainian typography, but was triumphantly restored to the alphabet in 1990.

## Common Learner Errors
1. **The 'H' Trap (Н)** → Pronouncing Cyrillic Н as English /h/ instead of /n/. Result: mispronouncing "он" as "oh".
2. **The 'P' Trap (Р)** → Pronouncing Cyrillic Р as English /p/ instead of the rolled /r/. Result: reading "парк" as "papk".
3. **The 'C' Trap (С)** → Pronouncing Cyrillic С as English /k/ instead of /s/. Result: misreading "сокіл".

## Notes for Content Writing
- **Decolonized Framing**: Introduce the Ukrainian alphabet as an independent, complete system. When applying the "Exclusion Rule" to letters like 'ё', 'ы', 'э', frame it affirmatively: "The Ukrainian language has its own distinct phonetic space. Letters used in neighboring alphabets (such as ё, ы, э) do not exist in Ukrainian."
- **Pacing**: A1 immersion is 10-50%. Heavy English scaffolding is required for explanations, but all examples must be in Ukrainian with English translations.
- **IPA Rule**: Provide IPA only for the first occurrence of new words, never for full sentences.
