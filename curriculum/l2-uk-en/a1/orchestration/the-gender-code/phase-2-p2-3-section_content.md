## Класифікація: Чотири родини (відміни)

### Family 1: The «Open/A» Group
As we saw with our basic gender rules, nouns ending in **-а** or **-я** are the most common feminine words. In Ukrainian grammar, we call this group **Family 1** (Перша відміна). Most of these words are feminine, like **мама** [ˈmɑmɑ] (mother) or **книга** [ˈknɪɦɑ] (book). However, this family also welcomes a few «masculine guests»—men whose names or roles end in -а or -о. For example, **тато** [ˈtɑtɔ] (father) and the name **Микола** [mɪˈkɔlɑ] (Mykola) live here because they share the same endings, even though they are masculine people.

When you see a word in this family, think of it as the «open» group because your mouth stays open for that final sound. Whether it is a soft **земля** [zɛmˈlʲɑ] (earth) or a hard **мама**, the pattern is the same. Just remember: even if **тато** is in this family for grammar, he is still «він» (he).

**Це моя мама.** [tsɛ mɔˈjɑ ˈmɑmɑ] — This is my mother.
**Тут моя земля.** [tut mɔˈjɑ zɛmˈlʲɑ] — My land is here.
**Це мій тато.** [tsɛ mij ˈtɑtɔ] — This is my father.

### Family 2: The «City and Home» Group
This is the largest and most diverse family in Ukrainian, known as **Family 2** (Друга відміна). It is a special partnership between two types of words: Masculine nouns that end in a «stop» (consonant) and Neuter nouns that end in **-о** or **-е**. Think of it as the family that builds your world—it contains your **дім** [dim] (house), your **стіл** [stil] (table), and your **місто** [ˈmisto] (city).

This group is very predictable. If you have a masculine word like **брат** [brɑt] (brother) or a neuter word like **вікно** [ʋɪkˈnɔ] (window), they follow the same grammatical logic. It is like a big neighborhood where houses (masculine) and parks (neuter) exist side-by-side. 

**Тут мій дім.** [tut mij dim] — My house is here.
**Це велике місто.** [tsɛ ʋɛˈlɪkɛ ˈmisto] — This is a big city.
**Там моє вікно.** [tɑm mɔˈjɛ ʋɪkˈnɔ] — My window is there.

### Family 3: The «Hidden Feminine» Group
Sometimes, a word looks masculine because it ends in a consonant, but it is actually feminine! These rare words belong to **Family 3** (Третя відміна). The most famous member is **ніч** [nitʃ] (night). Unlike **дім** (masculine), **ніч** is feminine. You can often spot these because they end in a soft sign or certain consonants like «ч» or «в».

These words are like secrets—you have to look closer to see their true gender. A common example you will use is **ніч**. Even though it stops with a consonant sound, we say «добра ніч» (good night) because the «night» is feminine.

> [!warning]
> Don't get caught by the «Stop» Trap! Most nouns ending in a consonant are masculine (**брат**, **стіл**), but a few common words like **ніч** are feminine. Always check if you should use **моя**!

**Це моя ніч.** [tsɛ mɔˈjɑ nitʃ] — This is my night.
**Тут моя любов.** [tut mɔˈjɑ lʲuˈbɔu̯] — My love is here.
**Це добра ніч.** [tsɛ ˈdɔbrɑ nitʃ] — This is a good night.

### Family 4: The «Name» Trap
The final group, **Family 4** (Четверта відміна), is quite small but contains a very important «trap» word: **ім'я** [imˈjɑ] (name). While it ends in **-я** (which usually means feminine), it is actually Neuter! This family is mostly for neuter words that describe young things or abstract concepts that change their stem when you add more information.

For a beginner, the most important thing is to remember that **ім'я** is neuter. When you ask someone their name, you are using a «воно» (it) word, not a «вона» (she) word. This is why we say **моє ім'я** (my name).

**Це моє імʼя.** [tsɛ mɔˈjɛ imˈjɑ] — This is my name.
**Твоє імʼя гарне.** [tʋɔˈjɛ imˈjɑ ˈɦɑrnɛ] — Your name is beautiful.
**Моє імʼя — Андрій.** [mɔˈjɛ imˈjɑ ɑnˈdrij] — My name is Andriy.
