## Наголос (Stress)

The concept of **наголос** (stress) is fundamental in Ukrainian. As Заболотний Grade 5, p.73 notes, the Ukrainian language has exactly 38 sounds. When you speak, **наголос** determines which syllable in a word is pronounced louder and longer. This forms the rhythmic heart of Ukrainian pronunciation. Ukrainian stress is **вільний** (free). It can fall on any syllable in a word. It is also **рухомий** (mobile), meaning it shifts between forms of the exact same word. For example, look at the word for leg: **нога** (stress on the last syllable). But when we talk about plural legs, it becomes **ноги** (stress on the first syllable). This system is completely unlike French, where stress is always fixed on the last syllable, or Czech, where it is always on the first. In Ukrainian, the stress dances around.

Why does this matter? Stress actually changes meaning. There are minimal pairs of words that look identical but mean completely different things based on their stress alone. We call these pairs **омографи** (homographs). Consider real pairs you will encounter. First, look at **замок** (castle) with stress on the first syllable, versus **замок** (lock) with stress on the second syllable. Next, compare **мука** (torment) versus **мука** (flour). Another clear example is **атлас** (atlas) versus **атлас** (satin). Getting the stress wrong means you are saying a completely different word. This is not just a slight accent issue; wrong stress equals the wrong word entirely.

You might wonder how to know where the stress goes. In writing, stress marks (') appear in textbooks and dictionaries to help learners. You will see them often as you study. However, these marks are NOT used in everyday Ukrainian text. You will not see them on street signs, in menus, or in books. As a learner, you must memorize the stress when you learn a new word. Always check an online dictionary like goroh.pp.ua for correct stress when you are unsure.

:::caution
**Common Mistake**
Do not assume a word has the same stress as its English or similar-sounding equivalent in another language. Always learn the unique Ukrainian **наголос**.
:::

There are common A1 stress patterns, but there is no magic shortcut here. Words must be learned individually. Many common words have first-syllable stress. Examples include **мама** (mother), **тато** (father), **ранок** (morning), **кава** (coffee), and **книжка** (book). Notice how the first part of the word is loud and long. Contrast these with words that have last-syllable stress. Examples include **вода** (water), **зима** (winter), **рука** (hand), **метро** (metro), and **кафе** (cafe). Listen to how the energy shifts to the end of the word.

## Інтонація (Intonation)

We will now discuss **інтонація** (melody). Ukrainian uses intonation to distinguish different sentence types. You can use the exact same words, but with a different melody, you convey a completely different meaning. A basic statement uses a falling intonation. It sounds like you are landing on the final thought. Look at this statement: **Це кава.** ↘ (This is coffee). The pitch goes down at the end. To make a yes/no question, you use a rising intonation on the stressed syllable. You do not change the word order at all. **Це кава?** ↗ (Is this coffee?). Finally, an exclamation uses a strong, sharp fall to express emotion. **Як гарно!** ↘↘ (How beautiful!).

There is a critical difference between yes/no questions and questions that contain specific question words. Question words include **хто** (who), **що** (what), **де** (where), and **коли** (when). When you use these words, the question actually has a falling intonation. The question word itself does all the work of showing that you are asking something. For example: **Що це?** ↘ (What is this?). The pitch goes down. **Де метро?** ↘ (Where is the metro?). Again, the pitch goes down. Contrast this with yes/no questions, which do not have these question words. Yes/no questions always require a rising melody. **Це метро?** ↗ (Is this the metro?).

:::tip
**Intonation Rule**
Think of a question word (**хто**, **що**, **де**, **коли**) as doing the heavy lifting. Because the word itself asks the question, your melody can simply fall at the end.
:::

As Авраменко Grade 5, p.19 explains, we classify sentences by their main purpose. There are three main types. First, we have **розповідні** (declarative) sentences, which tell you facts. Second, we have **питальні** (interrogative) sentences, which ask questions. Third, we have **спонукальні** (imperative) sentences, which give commands. Any of these sentence types can also be **окличні** (exclamatory), which is a separate dimension that adds strong emotion. For A1 learners, focus on identifying the three punctuation patterns that match these melodies. We use a period (.) for statements. We use a question mark (?) for questions. We use an exclamation mark (!) for emotions or commands. The punctuation tells you exactly which melody to use.

## Читаємо вголос (Reading Aloud)

Here is the method for reading multisyllable words with the correct stress. The technique is simple but powerful. First, break the word into syllables. Second, find the stressed syllable. Finally, read the whole word at a natural speed, giving that stressed syllable extra power. We can practice this with some examples. Take the word for Ukrainian: **у-кра-їн-ська**. The stress is on the **ї**. Say it out loud. Next, look at the word for photograph: **фо-то-гра-фі-я**. The stress falls on the third **а**. Say it: **фотографія**. Now try the word for rest: **від-по-чи-нок**. The stress is on the **и**. Break it, find the stress, and say it smoothly.

Following the stress-drill approach in ULP Season 1, Episode 5, here is a word stress reading practice section. Read this list of words aloud. You need to deliberately exaggerate the stressed syllable to train your ear. Start with the capital of Ukraine: **Ки-їв**. The stress is on the first syllable. Remember, **Київ — столиця України** (Kyiv is the capital of Ukraine). Next, try milk: **мо-ло-ко** (stress on the last **о**). Now morning: **ран-ок** (stress on **а**). Coffee: **ка-ва** (stress on **а**). Water: **во-да** (stress on **а**). Winter: **зи-ма** (stress on **а**). And Ukrainian: **у-кра-їн-ська** (stress on **ї**). Find the loud part, and own it.

## Підсумок — Summary

Let's recap the core concepts of this module with a comprehensive self-check. Answering these questions helps solidify your understanding of Ukrainian phonetics.

*   What is **наголос**? It is the stress in a word that makes one specific syllable sound louder, longer, and clearer than the surrounding syllables. It is the rhythmic anchor of every Ukrainian word.
*   Is Ukrainian stress fixed in one place? No, it is free (**вільний**) and mobile (**рухомий**). It can appear anywhere and can shift when a word changes form.
*   Can stress change the meaning of a word entirely? Yes, absolutely. Remember the minimal pairs we studied. The word **замок** (castle) is a completely different object from **замок** (lock). The stress is the only phonetic feature that separates them.
*   What intonation do you use for a yes/no question? You must use a rising intonation (↗) on the stressed syllable. Your voice goes up, waiting for an answer.
*   What intonation do you use for a statement or a question with a question word? You use a falling intonation (↘). The question words (**хто**, **що**, **де**, **коли**) already do the asking for you, so your melody simply lands.

Now, for your final read-aloud practice block. This is where you bring stress and melody together. Apply the correct melody and word stress to these short sentences. Read them out loud right now, exaggerating the pitch:

**Це аптека?** ↗ *(Is this a pharmacy?)*
**Так, це аптека.** ↘ *(Yes, this is a pharmacy.)*
**Як гарно!** ↘↘ *(How beautiful!)*

If you can hear and produce the difference between these three distinct melodies, you have built a strong foundation. You know how to make a word loud, and you know how to make a sentence ask or tell.

## Verification Tools (Shell Commands)

You have access to verification tools via shell commands. Use these to verify
Ukrainian vocabulary, check grammar, and search textbook content **as you write**.

**IMPORTANT:** Run verification commands BEFORE finalizing any Ukrainian text.
Batch multiple verifications together to minimize round-trips.

### 1. Batch-verify Ukrainian words exist in VESUM (preferred — one call for many words)

```bash
.venv/bin/python -c "
import sys; sys.path.insert(0, 'scripts')
from rag.query import verify_words
results = verify_words(['слово1', 'слово2', 'слово3'])
for w, matches in results.items():
    if matches:
        print(f'{w}: FOUND — lemma={matches[0][\"lemma\"]}, pos={matches[0][\"pos\"]}')
    else:
        print(f'{w}: NOT FOUND — may not exist in standard Ukrainian')
"
```

**WHEN to use these tools:**

1. **Before writing any Ukrainian text** — batch-verify all key vocabulary with
   `verify_words` (tool 1). Any word that returns NOT FOUND must be replaced.
2. **When you suspect a Russicism or calque** — search the style guide (tool 6)
   and verify the correct Ukrainian form with `verify_word` (tool 2).
3. **When writing for A1/A2** — check CEFR levels (tool 4) to ensure vocabulary
   is level-appropriate.
4. **When unsure about a case ending or conjugation** — use `verify_lemma` (tool 3)
   to see the full paradigm.
5. **When covering a grammar topic** — search textbooks (tool 5) to see how
   Ukrainian school textbooks teach it.
6. **When you need the precise Ukrainian meaning** — use СУМ-11 (tool 7).
7. **When looking for natural Ukrainian expressions** — search idioms (tool 8).

**Batching rule:** Collect all words you want to verify, then run ONE `verify_words`
call instead of multiple `verify_word` calls. This is faster and uses fewer tokens.
