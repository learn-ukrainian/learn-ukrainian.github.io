## Наголос (Stress)

The concept of **наголос** (stress) is fundamental in Ukrainian. The Ukrainian language has exactly 38 sounds. When you speak, **наголос** determines which syllable in a word is pronounced louder and longer. This forms the rhythmic heart of Ukrainian pronunciation. Ukrainian stress is **вільний** (free). It can fall on any syllable in a word. It is also **рухомий** (mobile), meaning it shifts between forms of the exact same word. For example, look at the word for leg: **нога** (stress on the last syllable). But when we talk about plural legs, it becomes **ноги** (stress on the first syllable). This system is completely unlike French, where stress is always fixed on the last syllable, or Czech, where it is always on the first. In Ukrainian, the stress dances around.

Why does this matter? Stress actually changes meaning. There are minimal pairs of words that look identical but mean completely different things based on their stress alone. We call these pairs **омографи** (homographs). Consider real pairs you will encounter. First, look at **замок** (castle) with stress on the first syllable, versus **замок** (lock) with stress on the second syllable. Next, compare **мука** (torment) versus **мука** (flour). Another clear example is **атлас** (atlas) versus **атлас** (satin). Getting the stress wrong means you are saying a completely different word. This is not just a slight accent issue; wrong stress equals the wrong word entirely.

You might wonder how to know where the stress goes. In writing, stress marks (') appear in textbooks and dictionaries to help learners. You will see them often as you study. However, these marks are NOT used in everyday Ukrainian text. You will not see them on street signs, in menus, or in books. As a learner, you must memorize the stress when you learn a new word. Always check an online dictionary like goroh.pp.ua for correct stress when you are unsure.

:::caution
**Common Mistake**
Do not assume a word has the same stress as its English or similar-sounding equivalent in another language. Always learn the unique Ukrainian **наголос**.
:::

There are common A1 stress patterns, but there is no magic shortcut here. Words must be learned individually. Many common words have first-syllable stress. Examples include **мама** (mother), **тато** (father), **ранок** (morning), **кава** (coffee), and **книжка** (book). Notice how the first part of the word is loud and long. Contrast these with words that have last-syllable stress. Examples include **вода** (water), **зима** (winter), **рука** (hand), **метро** (metro), and **кафе** (cafe). Listen to how the energy shifts to the end of the word.

## Інтонація (Intonation)

We will now discuss **інтонація** (melody). Ukrainian uses intonation to distinguish different sentence types. You can use the exact same words, but with a different melody, you convey a completely different meaning. A basic statement uses a falling intonation. It sounds like you are landing on the final thought. Look at this statement: **Це кава.** ↘ (This is coffee). The pitch goes down at the end. To make a yes/no question, you use a rising intonation on the stressed syllable. You do not change the word order at all. **Це кава?** ↗ (Is this coffee?). Finally, an exclamation uses a strong, sharp fall to express emotion. **Як гарно!** ↘↘ (How beautiful!).

There is a critical difference between yes/no questions and questions that contain specific question words. Question words include **хто** (who), **що** (what), **де** (where), and **коли** (when). When you use these words, the question actually has a falling intonation. The question word itself does all the work of showing that you are asking something. For example: **Що це?** ↘ (What is this?). The pitch goes down. **Де метро?** ↘ (Where is the metro?). Again, the pitch goes down. Contrast this with yes/no questions, which do not have these question words. Yes/no questions always require a rising melody. **Це метро?** ↗ (Is this the metro?).

:::tip
**Intonation Rule**
Think of a question word (**хто**, **що**, **де**, **коли**) as doing the heavy lifting. Because the word itself asks the question, your melody can simply fall at the end.
:::

We classify sentences by their main purpose. There are three main types. First, we have **розповідні** (declarative) sentences, which tell you facts. Second, we have **питальні** (interrogative) sentences, which ask questions. Third, we have **спонукальні** (imperative) or **окличні** (exclamatory) sentences, which give commands or show strong emotion. For A1 learners, focus on identifying the three punctuation patterns that match these melodies. We use a period (.) for statements. We use a question mark (?) for questions. We use an exclamation mark (!) for emotions or commands. The punctuation tells you exactly which melody to use.

## Читаємо вголос (Reading Aloud)

Here is the method for reading multisyllable words with the correct stress. The technique is simple but powerful. First, break the word into syllables. Second, find the stressed syllable. Finally, read the whole word at a natural speed, giving that stressed syllable extra power. We can practice this with some examples. Take the word for Ukrainian: **у-кра-їн-ська**. The stress is on the **ї**. Say it out loud. Next, look at the word for photograph: **фо-то-гра-фі-я**. The stress falls on the third **а**. Say it: **фотографія**. Now try the word for rest: **ві-дпо-чи-нок**. The stress is on the **и**. Break it, find the stress, and say it smoothly.

Here is a word stress reading practice section. Read this list of words aloud. You need to deliberately exaggerate the stressed syllable to train your ear. Start with the capital of Ukraine: **Ки-їв**. The stress is on the first syllable. Remember, **Київ — столиця України** (Kyiv is the capital of Ukraine). Next, try milk: **мо-ло-ко** (stress on the last **о**). Now morning: **ран-ок** (stress on **а**). Coffee: **ка-ва** (stress on **а**). Water: **во-да** (stress on **а**). Winter: **зи-ма** (stress on **а**). And Ukrainian: **у-кра-їн-ська** (stress on **ї**). Find the loud part, and own it.

## Підсумок — Summary

Let's recap the core concepts of this module with a comprehensive self-check. Answering these questions helps solidify your understanding of Ukrainian phonetics.

*   What is **наголос**? It is the stress in a word that makes one specific syllable sound louder, longer, and clearer than the surrounding syllables. It is the rhythmic anchor of every Ukrainian word.
*   Is Ukrainian stress fixed in one place? No, it is free (**вільний**) and mobile (**рухомий**). It can appear anywhere and can shift when a word changes form.
*   Can stress change the meaning of a word entirely? Yes, absolutely. Remember the minimal pairs we studied. The word **замок** (castle) is a completely different object from **замок** (lock). The stress is the only phonetic feature that separates them.
*   What intonation do you use for a yes/no question? You must use a rising intonation (↗) on the stressed syllable. Your voice goes up, waiting for an answer.
*   What intonation do you use for a statement or a question with a question word? You use a falling intonation (↘). The question words (**хто**, **що**, **де**, **коли**) already do the asking for you, so your melody simply lands.

## Live Verification Tools (MCP)

You have access to RAG-powered MCP tools to verify Ukrainian language constructs **live as you write**. The research phase is already complete; use these tools strictly for targeted verification to ensure zero Russianisms, accurate grammar, and authentic usage.

**Core Tools:**

- `mcp_rag_search_text` — Ukrainian school textbooks (Grades 1-11, 23K chunks).
- `mcp_rag_search_literary` — Primary literary sources (chronicles, poetry, legal texts).
- `mcp_rag_query_pravopys` — Official Ukrainian orthography rules (Правопис 2019).
- `mcp_rag_query_wikipedia` — Ukrainian Wikipedia.

**Dictionary Tools (NEW — use these for quality):**
- `mcp_rag_search_style_guide` — **Антоненко-Давидович (279 entries). HIGH PRIORITY.** Identifies calques and Russianisms. Use when unsure if a phrase is natural Ukrainian.
- `mcp_rag_query_cefr_level` — PULS CEFR vocabulary (5.9K words). Check if a word is level-appropriate (A1/A2/B1 etc.).
- `mcp_rag_search_definitions` — СУМ-11 (127K entries). Look up exact Ukrainian definitions.
- `mcp_rag_search_etymology` — Грінченко (67K entries). Historical forms, etymology.
- `mcp_rag_search_idioms` — Фразеологічний (25K entries). Find natural Ukrainian idioms.
- `mcp_rag_search_synonyms` — Ukrajinet WordNet (122K synsets). Synonyms, antonyms.
- `mcp_rag_translate_en_uk` — Балла EN→UK (79K entries). English→Ukrainian translations.
- `mcp_rag_query_grac` — GRAC corpus (2B tokens). Check word frequency, collocations, concordance. Use when unsure if a collocation is natural.
- `mcp_rag_query_ulif` — ULIF morphological paradigms. Full declension/conjugation tables. Use when verify_lemma isn't enough.
- `mcp_rag_query_r2u` — Russian→Ukrainian equivalents. Use when you suspect a word might be a Russicism — finds the proper Ukrainian alternative.

**WHEN to use tools (Specific Triggers):**

1. **Suspected Russianisms or Surzhyk (HIGH PRIORITY):**
   - *Trigger:* You are about to use a word that sounds similar to Russian, a calque, or you are unsure of its exact Ukrainian equivalent.
   - *Action:* Use `mcp_rag_search_style_guide` first (it knows calques). Then `mcp_rag_query_r2u` for the proper Ukrainian equivalent. Then verify with `mcp_rag_verify_words`.
   - *Example:* Checking *приймати участь* (calque) → *брати участь* (correct).

2. **Vocabulary Level Check:**
   - *Trigger:* You are writing for A1/A2 and want to ensure words are level-appropriate.
   - *Action:* Use `mcp_rag_query_cefr_level` to verify the word's CEFR level.

3. **Grammar & Morphology Doubts:**
   - *Trigger:* You are unsure about a case ending, irregular plural, or conjugation.
   - *Action:* Use `mcp_rag_verify_lemma` to pull the complete declension/conjugation.

4. **Natural Expressions:**
   - *Trigger:* You need a natural idiom or collocation for a dialogue.
   - *Action:* Use `mcp_rag_search_idioms` for Ukrainian expressions, `mcp_rag_search_synonyms` for word variety.

5. **Drafting Grammar Rules:**
   - *Trigger:* You are explaining a spelling or phonetic rule.
   - *Action:* Use `mcp_rag_query_pravopys` to confirm the exact 2019 standard.

6. **Checking Collocations & Frequency:**
   - *Trigger:* You want to confirm a word combination is actually used by native speakers.
   - *Action:* Use `mcp_rag_query_grac` with mode='collocations' to see real-world usage.

**MANDATORY Verification (these are NOT optional):**

7. **Letter/Sound Decomposition (ALWAYS VERIFY):**
