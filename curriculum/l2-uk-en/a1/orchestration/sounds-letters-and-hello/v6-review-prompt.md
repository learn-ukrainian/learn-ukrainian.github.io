## –Ч–≤—ГћБ–Ї–Є —Ц –ї—ЦћБ—В–µ—А–Є (Sounds and Letters)

Look at the text on this page. What you are seeing are letters. Now, say a word out loud. What you just produced is a sound. This distinction is the absolute foundation of the Ukrainian language. There is a golden rule taught to every Ukrainian student in the fifth grade, from the textbook by Zabolotnyi: **–Ч–≤—Г–Ї–Є –Љ–Є —З—ГћБ—Ф–Љ–Њ –є –≤–Є–Љ–Њ–≤–ї—ПћБ—Ф–Љ–Њ, –∞ –±—ГћБ–Ї–≤–Є –±–∞ћБ—З–Є–Љ–Њ –є –њ–ЄћБ—И–µ–Љ–Њ** (We hear and pronounce sounds, but we see and write letters). Think of it like music: the sound is the physical note you play on an instrument, while the letter is simply the sheet music written on paper to record it. Ukrainian teachers drill this difference from the very first day of grade school because confusing the visual symbol with the physical sound causes immediate pronunciation errors. 

You might wonder how a language can have a different number of sounds and letters. The math of Ukrainian is interesting: the alphabet has exactly 33 letters, but the spoken language has 38 distinct sounds. This mismatch occurs because certain "smart" letters can represent two sounds at the exact same time. For example, the letters **–ѓ**, **–Ѓ**, **–Д**, and **–З** can hold a combination of sounds, acting like a hidden "y" sound before a vowel. Conversely, one specific letterвАФthe soft sign, written as **–ђ**вАФmakes absolutely no sound of its own. It acts only as a silent modifier, slightly changing how you pronounce the consonant that stands directly before it. Remember that letters are just the visual clothing that sounds wear.

Because sounds and letters are strictly separate concepts, Ukrainian demands linguistic accuracy when you discuss them. In a fifth-grade textbook, the author Litvinova asks students a pedagogical question: **–І–Є –Љ–ЊћБ–ґ–љ–∞ –≥–Њ–≤–Њ—А–ЄћБ—В–Є ¬Ђ–≥–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ–∞ћБ –ї—ЦћБ—В–µ—А–∞¬ї?** (Is it possible to say "vowel letter"?). ## –У–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ—ЦћБ –Ј–≤—Г–Ї–Є (Vowel Sounds)

## –Я—А–ЄћБ–≥–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ—Ц –Ј–≤—Г–Ї–Є (Consonant Sounds)

The partners to the vowels are the **–њ—А–Є–≥–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ—Ц –Ј–≤—Г–Ї–Є** (consonant sounds). The author Bolshakova describes them in contrast to vowels: **–Я—А–Є–≥–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ—Ц –і–µ—А–µ–љ—З–∞ћБ—В—М —Ц —В–Є—Е–µћБ–љ—М–Ї–Њ —И–µ–ї–µ—Б—В—ПћБ—В—М, –≥–ЊћБ–ї–Њ—Б–љ–Њ —Б–≤–Є—Б—В—ПћБ—В—М —Ц —И–Є–њ–ї—ПћБ—В—М** (Consonants rattle and quietly rustle, loudly whistle and hiss). Consonants are formed very differently from vowels. Instead of free-flowing air, consonants are made by creating a physical obstruction in your mouth using your tongue, your teeth, or your lips. They consist of voice mixed with noise, or sometimes just noise alone. Because the air is blocked or squeezed, you cannot sing a pure consonant sound like [–Ї] or [—В].

The Ukrainian language features 32 consonant sounds produced by 22 consonant letters. A major feature of these sounds is the hard versus soft distinction. Many consonants come in pairs: a **—В–≤–µ—А–і–ЄћБ–є** (hard) version and a **–Љ'—П–Ї–ЄћБ–є** (soft) version. Soft consonants are palatalized, meaning the middle of your tongue raises toward the roof of your mouth, similar to the "n" in the English word "new". The Zakhariichuk textbook notation marks hard sounds with a single dash [вАУ] and soft sounds with a double dash [=]. This hard and soft distinction does not exist in English, but it is uniquely Slavic and completely changes the meaning of a Ukrainian word.

You will meet the primary consonant letters through video practice: **–Ь**, **–Э**, **–Ґ**, **–°**, **–Ы**, **–Ъ**, and **–†**. Pay special attention to the letter **“Р**, which makes a hard [g] sound. This letter is uniquely Ukrainian and has a history of being suppressed during the Soviet era, making it an important symbol of linguistic identity. You will also encounter the letter **–©**, which is a "double letter" that always represents two sounds together: [—И—З]. Finally, you will see the soft sign, **–ђ**. As a reminder, this letter is the silent "helper" that changes the consonant standing directly before it from a hard sound into a soft one.
