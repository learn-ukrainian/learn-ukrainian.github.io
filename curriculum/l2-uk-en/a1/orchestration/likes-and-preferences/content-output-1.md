## Мені подобається (I like)

When expressing that something appeals to you in Ukrainian, the grammatical structure flips completely compared to English. Instead of saying "I like coffee", the structure translates closer to "Coffee is pleasing to me". The subject of the sentence is the actual thing that is pleasing, not the person who likes it.

This is called a Dative construction. You use a specific pronoun form to show *to whom* the object is pleasing, followed by the verb **подо́батися** (to appeal) and the noun or action. This means the person is receiving the good feeling, rather than actively doing the liking.

* **Мені́ подо́бається ка́ва.** — I like coffee.
* **Мені́ подо́бається чита́ти.** — I like to read.

Notice that you can follow the verb directly with an infinitive action, such as **чита́ти** (to read). This is extremely common when talking about your hobbies and daily routines.

To say who likes something, you must use the correct pronoun form. Here is the full paradigm for these pronouns:

| Pronoun | Dative Form | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| я | **мені́** | **Мені́ подо́бається ка́ва.** | I like coffee. |
| ти | **тобі́** | **Тобі́ подо́бається чай.** | You like tea. |
| він | **йому́** | **Йому́ подо́бається му́зика.** | He likes music. |
| вона | **їй** | **Їй подо́бається сніг.** | She likes snow. |
| ми | **нам** | **Нам подо́бається лі́то.** | We like summer. |
| ви | **вам** | **Вам подо́бається мі́сто.** | You (formal/pl) like the city. |
| вони | **їм** | **Їм подо́бається о́сінь.** | They like autumn. |

The verb **подо́батися** also changes depending on whether the thing being liked is singular or plural. If the object is singular or an infinitive action, use **подо́бається**. If you like multiple things, the verb must be plural: **подо́баються**.

* **Мені́ подо́бається ціка́вий фільм.** — I like an interesting film.
* **Мені́ подо́баються ці кві́ти.** — I like these flowers.
* **Йому́ подо́баються нові́ книжки́.** — He likes new books.

## Я люблю (I love)

While the previous construction is great for general appeal, sometimes you want to express a stronger, more active emotion. The verb **люби́ти** (to love) is used for active enjoyment or deep affection. This verb is incredibly useful for expressing strong positive feelings about food, activities, or people in your life. Do not be afraid to use it often!

Unlike the passive construction above, **люби́ти** works exactly like the English verb "to love". The person is the active subject, and the thing loved takes a special object form (the Accusative). The grammatical subject is the person who is feeling the love.

* **Я люблю́ ка́ву.** — I love coffee.
* **Я люблю́ ма́му.** — I love mom.
* **Я люблю́ чита́ти.** — I love to read.

The verb belongs to the second conjugation group. Notice the spelling change in the first person singular form, where an extra letter appears to make pronunciation smoother:

| Pronoun | Verb Form |
|---|---|
| я | **люблю́** |
| ти | **лю́биш** |
| він/вона/воно | **лю́бить** |
| ми | **лю́бимо** |
| ви | **лю́бите** |
| вони | **лю́блять** |

There is an important difference in meaning between these two concepts. When you say **мені́ подо́бається**, you are describing a state of finding something appealing or pleasant. It is often used for new experiences or general preferences. When you say **я люблю́**, you are describing an active, ongoing attachment or habit.

* **Мені́ подо́бається ця кни́га.** — I find this book appealing.
* **Я люблю́ цю кни́гу.** — I love this book.
* **Мені́ подо́бається смачни́й борщ.** — I like tasty borscht.
* **Я люблю́ смачни́й борщ.** — I love tasty borscht.

> [!tip]
> Reserve the active verb for things you truly adore or do frequently. For everyday polite conversation about things you find nice, the passive construction is your best friend.

## Я хочу (I want)

To express desire, use the verb **хоті́ти** (to want). This verb is irregular, meaning its endings follow a unique pattern. It is incredibly useful for everyday situations, such as ordering food at a restaurant or suggesting an activity to a friend. Learning this verb early will help you navigate many practical situations in Ukraine.

The most common way to use this verb is to follow it directly with an infinitive action. This lets you explain exactly what you intend to do next, making your communication much clearer.

* **Я хо́чу ї́сти.** — I want to eat.
* **Я хо́чу піти́.** — I want to go.
* **Він хо́че чита́ти.** — He wants to read.

Here is the complete conjugation pattern for this irregular verb. Pay close attention to the vowel changes in the plural forms, as they shift noticeably:

| Pronoun | Verb Form |
|---|---|
| я | **хо́чу** |
| ти | **хо́чеш** |
| він/вона/воно | **хо́че** |
| ми | **хо́чемо** |
| ви | **хо́чете** |
| вони | **хо́чуть** |

You can also use this verb directly with an object. Just like with our active verb for love, the object takes a specific form to show it is being wanted. You will practice this form more later, but memorize these common examples for now so you can request things politely.

* **Я хо́чу ка́ву.** — I want coffee.
* **Я хо́чу квито́к.** — I want a ticket.
* **Вона́ хо́че чай.** — She wants tea.

Compare this directly with the passive construction we learned first. Passive preferences do not require this special object form. This distinction is vital for sounding natural.

* **Мені́ подо́бається ка́ва.** — I like coffee.
* **Я хо́чу ка́ву.** — I want coffee.

## Порівняння (Comparing likes)

Once you can state your own preferences, the next step is asking others about theirs. Building dialogues about shared and different tastes is essential for making friends. You will find that Ukrainians are very hospitable and will frequently ask what you like or want. Showing interest in their answers is a great way to build rapport.

To turn statements into questions, simply use rising intonation or the question word **чи**. You can also ask directly about someone else using short question tags.

* **А ти?** — And you?
* **А вам?** — And you? (formal/plural)
* **Тобі́ подо́бається му́зика?** — Do you like music?
* **Ти лю́биш чита́ти?** — Do you love to read?
* **Ви хо́чете ї́сти?** — Do you want to eat?

Let us look at a short dialogue building on these phrases. Notice how the speakers compare their tastes naturally, creating a flowing conversation:

> — **Приві́т! Тобі́ подо́бається ця ка́ва?**
> — **Так, смачна́ ка́ва. А тобі́?**
> — **Мені́ теж. Я дуже люблю́ ка́ву.**

Understanding cultural context is helpful when discussing preferences. Ukrainians love to gather for meals and hot drinks, especially in the colder months. Finding out what people enjoy eating or doing in their free time naturally introduces new vocabulary you will see in upcoming modules. Being able to compare your likes with others will make these social interactions much more engaging and authentic.

* **Ми хо́чемо піти́ в кафе́.** — We want to go to a cafe.
* **Їм подо́бається смачни́й торт.** — They like tasty cake.
* **Я люблю́ чай.** — I love tea.
* **Це нудни́й фільм, я хо́чу піти́.** — This is a boring film, I want to go.

## Практика (Practice)

Now it is time to practice choosing the correct construction. Depending on the context, you must decide whether to describe a passive appeal, an active love, or a direct desire.

Look at the following pattern examples to see how the choice changes the meaning slightly:

* **Мені́ подо́бається суп.** — I find the soup appealing.
* **Я люблю́ суп.** — I love soup.
* **Я хо́чу суп.** — I want soup.

Consider the context carefully. If a friend asks you to watch a movie, but you have no interest, you might use an adjective to explain why you want to leave:

> — **Ти хо́чеш диви́тися фільм?**
> — **Ні, це нудни́й фільм. Я хо́чу спа́ти.**

Try to fill in the gaps mentally when you hear these conversations. Paying attention to context clues makes it easier:

> — **Що тобі́ подо́бається?**
> — **Мені́ подо́бається ________.**
> — **Що ти лю́биш?**
> — **Я люблю́ ________.**

Practice these forms daily. Discuss your preferences, from a tasty drink to an interesting book. 

* **Це ціка́вий текст.** — This is an interesting text.
* **Ця му́зика гарна.** — This music is beautiful.
