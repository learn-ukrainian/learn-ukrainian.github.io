# Prohibitions and Signs

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Уявіть, що ви гуляєте мі́стом. Ви ба́чите бага́то рі́зних знакі́в і написі́в.
> Imagine you are walking around the city. You see many different signs and inscriptions.
> Understanding these signs is essential for your safety and daily navigation in Ukraine. It helps you know where to go, what to avoid, and when shops are actually open.

## Заборони (Prohibitions)

Ми ча́сто ба́чимо зна́ки заборо́ни на ву́лицях.
We often see prohibition signs on the streets.
Вони́ є в па́рках і шко́лах.
They are in parks and schools.
Ці зна́ки допомага́ють нам бу́ти у безпе́ці.
These signs help us stay safe.

Public signs in Ukraine have their own special language. They do not usually talk to you directly. Instead of using a direct command, they use the infinitive form of the verb. This makes the sign sound official and impersonal. It is a completely standard way to write rules for public spaces.

Ось кі́лька ду́же важли́вих знакі́в.
Here are a few very important signs.
Їх тре́ба зна́ти напа́м'ять.
You need to know them by heart.

| Знак | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Не кури́ти!** | No smoking! |
| **Не входи́ти!** | Do not enter! |
| **Не торка́тися!** | Do not touch! |

Notice how each verb ends in **-ти** or **-тися**. This is the infinitive form. We translate this into English as "No smoking" or "Do not enter". You will never see these verbs conjugated for a specific person on an official sign.

Ми мо́жемо порівня́ти ці фо́рми.
We can compare these forms.
Signs use the infinitive, but people use the imperative.
When a person speaks to you, they will give a direct personal command. When a printed sign communicates a rule, it stays neutral.

**Не торка́тися!** (Do not touch!)
This is what you read on a museum display behind a glass case. It applies to absolutely everyone visiting the museum.

**Не чіпа́й!** (Do not touch!)
This is what a person says directly to you if you are about to drop their fragile coffee cup. It is personal, urgent, and immediate.

Ви тако́ж поба́чите зна́ки на́прямку.
You will also see direction signs.
Вони́ вка́зують нам доро́гу.
They show us the way.

| Знак | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Вхід** | Entrance |
| **Ви́хід** | Exit |
| **Вхід заборо́нено!** | Entrance forbidden! |

If you see **Вхід заборо́нено!**, do not go through that door. It literally means "Entrance is forbidden". The word **заборо́нено** is a special impersonal form meaning "forbidden". It is used everywhere from construction sites to private office corridors.

> [!tip]
> Memorize the visual difference between **Вхід** (Entrance) and **Ви́хід** (Exit). They look very similar because they share the same root, but mixing them up in a busy metro station can send you walking in the wrong direction against a massive crowd of people!

Зна́ки — це важли́ва части́на мі́ста.
Signs are an important part of the city.
Без них ду́же ва́жко орієнтува́тися.
Without them it is very difficult to navigate.
Ми чита́ємо їх щодня́.
We read them every day.
You will find these infinitive prohibitions in parks, hospitals, schools, and government buildings. Whenever you see a red circle with a line through it, look for a verb ending in **-ти**. It will always tell you what you cannot do.

## Попередження (Warnings)

Іно́ді зна́ки не про́сто забороня́ють.
Sometimes signs do not just prohibit.
Вони́ попереджа́ють нас про небезпе́ку.
They warn us about danger.
Це ду́же важли́ва інформа́ція.
This is very important information.

Warning signs often use short, exclamatory words. These are usually adverbs or short adjectives that function as a loud, standalone alert. You need to recognize these words instantly without thinking about the English translation, because they protect your personal safety in unfamiliar environments.

| Слово | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Ува́га!** | Attention! |
| **Обере́жно!** | Careful! / Caution! |
| **Небезпе́чно!** | Dangerous! |

Ці слова́ ду́же коро́ткі й зрозумі́лі.
These words are very short and clear.
Ви ча́сто поба́чите їх вели́кими лі́терами.
You will often see them in capital letters.

You will frequently see these exclamations combined with short noun phrases. The noun and adjective will agree in gender, just like in normal sentences. These warnings are designed to be read in a fraction of a second.

**Мо́кра підло́га** (Wet floor)
This is a feminine noun phrase. You will usually see it on a bright yellow sign inside a supermarket, a restaurant, or a modern office building.

**Висо́ка напру́га** (High voltage)
This is another feminine phrase. You will see this specific warning on electrical boxes or near power lines. It is always accompanied by a sharp yellow lightning bolt symbol. Never ignore it!

> [!culture]
> If you ride the Kyiv Metro, you will hear and see one iconic warning every single day: **Не притуля́тися** (Do not lean). It is written on the doors of every metro train. This classic infinitive prohibition is so well-known that locals instantly recognize it as a symbol of the city's transport system.

За́вжди зверта́йте ува́гу на ці зна́ки.
Always pay attention to these signs.
Вони́ бережу́ть ва́ше здоро́в'я.
They protect your health.
Ніко́ли не ігнору́йте їх.
Never ignore them.

## Розклади та оголошення (Schedules and notices)

Ко́жен магази́н ма́є свій ро́зклад.
Every store has its own schedule.
Це текст на дверя́х.
This is the text on the doors.
Вам тре́ба вмі́ти йо́го чита́ти.
You need to be able to read it.

Reading business schedules is a practical reading skill you will use every single day. You will usually find a small printed sign on the door or window of a shop, pharmacy, bank, or local clinic. If you cannot read it, you might wait outside for nothing.

| Термін | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Ро́зклад** | Schedule |
| **Годи́ни робо́ти** | Operating hours |
| **Вихідни́й** | Day off / Closed day |
| **Пере́рва** | Break / Lunch break |

> [!did-you-know]
> The **пере́рва** (lunch break) is a very important cultural phenomenon in Ukraine. While large supermarkets stay open all day, smaller shops, post offices, and local pharmacies strictly close for an hour, usually between 13:00 and 14:00. If you arrive during this time, you will just have to wait outside!

Час робо́ти пи́шуть ду́же про́сто.
Working hours are written very simply.
Ось ду́же популя́рний форма́т.
Here is a very popular format.

**З 9:00 до 18:00** (From 9:00 to 18:00)

This is a fixed, practical chunk of language. The prepositions **з** (from) and **до** (to) both require the genitive case. For now, do not worry about the complex grammar rules. Just memorize this exact structure as a visual pattern you will see on glass doors everywhere.

Ви тако́ж поба́чите і́нші важли́ві напи́си.
You will also see other important inscriptions.
Це звича́йні інформаці́йні оголо́шення.
These are common informational notices.
Лю́ди чита́ють їх щодня́.
People read them every day.

- **Оголо́шення** (Announcement)
- **А́кція** (Sale / Promotion)
- **Зни́жка** (Discount)

Якщо́ ви на вокза́лі, ро́зклад бу́де і́ншим.
If you are at the train station, the schedule will be different.
Там є ро́зклад ру́ху.
There is a timetable there.
Ви купу́єте квито́к.
You buy a ticket.
You will see words from our travel lesson, like **відпра́влення** (departure) and **прибуття́** (arrival). These words tell you exactly when your train will leave the platform and when it will reach your destination.

## На вулиці (On the street)

Як знайти́ потрі́бну буді́влю?
How to find the necessary building?
Тре́ба чита́ти ву́личні вказівники́.
You need to read street pointers.
Вони́ є на ко́жному буди́нку.
They are on every house.

Street signs and address plates in Ukraine use standard abbreviations. You need to know both the full word and its short form to navigate effectively. If you are looking for a specific address using a map application, the physical signs will match these exact abbreviations.

| Слово | Скорочення | Meaning |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **ву́лиця** | вул. | street |
| **пло́ща** | пл. | square |
| **прову́лок** | пров. | lane |

На́приклад, ви шука́єте пло́щу.
For example, you are looking for a square.
Ви ба́чите на́пис: пл. Незале́жності.
You see the sign: Independence Square.
Це центр мі́ста.
This is the center of the city.

When walking or driving, you will interact with traffic and transport signs. They help pedestrians and drivers share the road safely.

- **Стоп** (Stop)
- **Зупи́нка** (Stop — for public transport, like a bus stop)
- **Пішохі́дний перехі́д** (Pedestrian crossing)
- **Перехі́д** (Transfer or crossing, often underground in the metro)

> [!warning]
> The biggest trap for learners is reading signs on shop doors! If a shop is open, the sign must say **Відчи́нено**. If it is closed, it must say **Зачи́нено**.
> You might hear some people use the words *відкри́то* and *закри́то*. This is incorrect. Those words are Russian interference (Surzhyk). We only use «відкри́то» for things like a discovered planet or an opened book, never for doors or businesses!

Украї́нська мо́ва ду́же то́чна.
The Ukrainian language is very precise.
Две́рі мо́жна ли́ше відчиня́ти й зачиня́ти.
Doors can only be opened and closed.
Тому́ ми ба́чимо на́пис «Зачи́нено».
That is why we see the sign "Closed".
Пам'ята́йте це пра́вило.
Remember this rule.

## Практика (Practice)

Ви ви́вчили ду́же важли́ві слова́.
You have learned very important words.
Тепе́р ви мо́жете гуля́ти мі́стом.
Now you can walk around the city.
Ви розумі́єте зна́ки.
You understand the signs.

Let us review the most important concept from this lesson. Public signs and printed warnings use a specific register that is entirely different from spoken language. Knowing the difference helps you sound natural and read comfortably.

| Context | Form | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Public Sign** | Infinitive / Impersonal | **Не торка́тися!** |
| **Spoken Command** | Imperative | **Не чіпа́й!** |
