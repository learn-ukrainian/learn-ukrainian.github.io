## Транспорт (Transport Types)

Ukraine has an extensive and varied public transport system. To navigate it smoothly, you need to recognize the different vehicles and know exactly how to say you are traveling by them. Let us build your transport vocabulary.

For getting around within a city, you have several primary options:
- **автобус** (bus, m)
- **тролейбус** (trolleybus, m)
- **трамвай** (tram, m)
- **метро** (metro, n)
- **маршрутка** (minibus, f)
- **таксі** (taxi, n)

The **автобус** is standard in every city. A **тролейбус** looks like a bus but runs quietly on electricity drawn from overhead wires; they are incredibly common across the country. A **трамвай** runs on steel rails through the city streets, adding a historic charm to cities like Lviv, Kyiv, and Odesa. The **метро** is the rapid underground subway system, currently operating in Kyiv, Kharkiv, and Dnipro. 

The **маршрутка** is a true cultural icon. These yellow minibuses operate on specific routes, but unlike regular municipal buses, they often stop exactly where you ask them to along the road. Finally, you can always take a **таксі** for a private, direct ride. 

Note that the words **метро** and **таксі** are borrowed from other languages and end in vowels. In Ukrainian grammar, this means they are indeclinable—their endings never change, no matter what grammatical case you use them in.

For traveling longer distances between cities, you will rely on:
- **потяг** (train, m)
- **автобус** (bus, m)
- **літак** (plane, m)

The **потяг** is the classic, romantic way to travel across Ukraine. The national railway network is vast and reliable. For international distances, you might take a **літак**.

When you want to say "by bus" or "by metro", Ukrainian uses two distinctly different grammatical patterns. You must memorize which transport uses which pattern. They both mean exactly the same thing in English ("by..."), but they are constructed differently.

**Pattern 1: The Instrumental Chunk**
Most standard, declinable vehicles take an instrumental case ending (usually **-ом** or **-єм**) without any preposition at all. Treat these as fixed chunks for now.
- **їхати автобусом** (to go by bus)
- **їхати тролейбусом** (to go by trolleybus)
- **їхати трамваєм** (to go by tram)
- **їхати потягом** (to go by train)

Examples in action:
- Я їду **автобусом**. *(I am going by bus.)*
- Ми їдемо **потягом**. *(We are going by train.)*
- Вона їде **трамваєм**. *(She is going by tram.)*

**Pattern 2: На + Locative Chunk**
A few specific vehicles use the preposition **на** followed by the locative case. This pattern is strictly required for indeclinable borrowed words, and it is also the standard way to talk about traveling by a personal car.
- **їхати на метро** (to go by metro)
- **їхати на таксі** (to go by taxi)
- **їхати на машині** (to go by car)

Examples in action:
- Він їде **на метро**. *(He is going by metro.)*
- Ти їдеш **на таксі**. *(You are going by taxi.)*
- Вони їдуть **на машині**. *(They are going by car.)*

Always link the transport type with its specific pattern in your mind. When you want to say "by bus" (means of transport), use **автобусом** — not "на автобусі." The form "на автобусі" does exist, but it means "on the bus" (a physical location). For the means of getting somewhere, stick to the instrumental: **автобусом**.

## Корисні фрази (Useful Phrases)

You know the vehicles, and you know how to talk about riding them. Now, let us look at the essential, practical phrases you need at the station, on the street, and during your actual journey.

When you are trying to find your transport, your most important word is **зупинка** (stop or station, f).
- Де **зупинка**? *(Where is the stop?)*
- Де **зупинка** автобуса? *(Where is the bus stop?)*

Once you arrive, you need a **квиток** (ticket, m). Buying one at a station counter or from a driver is straightforward. You state the quantity, the destination, and add the polite "please".
- Один **квиток**, будь ласка. *(One ticket, please.)*
- Один **квиток** до Одеси. *(One ticket to Odesa.)*

If you do not know the price beforehand, simply ask:
- Скільки коштує **квиток**? *(How much is a ticket?)*

If you are waiting at the railway station, watching the large departure boards, you might need to ask someone nearby about the schedule:
- Коли наступний **потяг**? *(When is the next train?)*

Once you are actually sitting on the bus or the tram, you might feel a bit lost as the city rolls by. It is completely normal and expected to ask fellow passengers for help. If you do not recognize the street outside the window, you can ask:
- Яка це **зупинка**? *(What stop is this?)*

If you are unsure whether it is your turn to leave the vehicle, you can ask a specific verification phrase. We use the word **мені** (to me) here as part of a fixed chunk.
- **Мені** виходити тут? *(Do I get off here?)*

Sometimes, you need broader directions before you even find the transport. As we saw in the very first dialogue, the best way to ask is using the phrase **як дістатися до** (how to get to).
- Вибачте, **як дістатися до** парку? *(Excuse me, how do I get to the park?)*
- Вибачте, **як дістатися до** центру? *(Excuse me, how do I get to the center?)*

When someone answers your question, listen carefully for these three directional words. They are absolutely crucial for your navigation.
- **прямо** (straight)
- **направо** (right)
- **наліво** (left)

You will often hear a combination of these words in a single response.
- Йдіть **прямо**, а потім **наліво**. *(Go straight, and then left.)*
- Зупинка там, **направо**. *(The stop is there, to the right.)*

Mastering these phrases gives you immense freedom. You no longer need to rely entirely on your phone's digital map application. You can interact with people, ask for guidance, and navigate the city like a true local.

If you see a vocabulary table with wrong translations, missing words, or formatting issues, that content was NOT written by the writer — it was generated deterministically by the ENRICH step. Do NOT flag словník problems as linguistic errors or deduct points from the writer. Report them separately under "ENRICH issues" in your findings (informational only, not scored).

### Step 4: Output raw scores

Output ONLY the raw 1-10 scores in the table below. Do NOT calculate the weighted total — the pipeline script handles the math deterministically. Just write your per-dimension scores.

### Step 5: List findings

For every issue found, provide:
```
[DIMENSION] [SEVERITY: critical/major/minor]
Location: [specific section/paragraph — quote the actual text]
Issue: [what's wrong]
Fix: [exactly how to fix it]
```

Critical = module cannot ship. Major = quality below standard. Minor = polish item.

### Step 6: Verdict

Base your verdict on the **severity of findings** — the pipeline calculates the weighted score separately.

| Verdict | Condition |
|---------|-----------|
| **PASS** | Zero critical findings, at most minor issues |
| **REVISE** | Has major findings but no criticals — fixable without rewrite |
| **REJECT** | Has any critical finding — fundamental problems requiring rewrite |

### Step 7: Fix it yourself (REVISE only)

If your verdict is **REVISE**, you MUST output a `<fixes>` block with exact find/replace pairs. The pipeline applies these deterministically — no LLM regeneration, no rewriting from scratch.

Rules for fixes:
- Each fix is a YAML entry with `find:` (exact text from the module) and `replace:` (corrected text)
- The `find` string must be an EXACT substring of the module content — copy-paste it
- Keep fixes minimal — change only what's wrong, preserve surrounding text
- Do NOT fix словник/vocabulary tables (those are generated by a downstream tool)
- For word count issues: add content as a new `insert_after:` entry instead of find/replace
