Time is the essential scaffolding of our daily lives, giving structure to everything we do. In Ukrainian, asking about time and scheduling events relies on a few very specific, highly predictable patterns. ## Котра година? (What Time Is It?)

When you look at your watch and a friend asks **Котра година?** (What time is it?), Ukrainian approaches the answer a bit differently than English does. In English, you use cardinal numbers—the numbers used for counting quantities, and you say "It is one o'clock." In Ukrainian, you literally say "It is the first hour," using ordinal numbers—the numbers used for ordering things in a sequence.

Because the word **година** is a feminine noun, all the numbers used to tell the hour must also be in their feminine form. If you look closely at the endings of these words, you will notice they all end in the letter "а" or "я", which is the classic hallmark of a feminine word in Ukrainian. 

According to Ukrainian school textbooks (such as *Захарійчук Grade 4, p.117*), learners memorize these feminine ordinal numbers as fixed vocabulary. You do not need to worry about the underlying grammar of ordinals right now; simply learn them as the names of the hours on the clock face.

Here are the first three hours:
*   1:00 — **перша** (1st)
*   2:00 — **друга** (2nd)
*   3:00 — **третя** (3rd)

And here is the rest of the 12-hour clock face:
*   4:00 — **четверта** (4th)
*   5:00 — **п'ята** (5th)
*   6:00 — **шоста** (6th)
*   7:00 — **сьома** (7th)
*   8:00 — **восьма** (8th)
*   9:00 — **дев'ята** (9th)
*   10:00 — **десята** (10th)
*   11:00 — **одинадцята** (11th)
*   12:00 — **дванадцята** (12th)

If someone asks the time at exactly 3:00, you simply say: **Третя**. Or, if you want to be fully complete and formal, you can say: **Третя година**.

### Half Hours and Quarters

Life rarely happens exactly on the hour. To express half-hours, Ukrainian uses the word **пів** (half) combined with the preposition **на** (onto) and the *next* hour. You are literally saying "half onto the [next] hour." It takes a little practice to look ahead rather than looking back.

*   1:30 — **пів на другу** (half onto the second)
*   4:30 — **пів на п'яту** (half onto the fifth)
*   8:30 — **пів на дев'яту** (half onto the ninth)

This "looking forward" pattern is extremely common. You will also occasionally hear native speakers use quarters with the word **чверть** (quarter). At the A1 level, you should focus your energy on mastering the full hours and the **пів на** pattern. However, it is very helpful to recognize quarters when you hear them in conversation.

*   2:15 — **чверть на третю** (quarter onto the third)
*   2:45 — **за чверть третя** (in a quarter, the third)

For now, practice looking at analog clocks in your daily life and stating the full hour or the half-hour using the patterns you have just learned.

## О котрій? (At What Time?)

Telling someone what time it *is* is only half the battle. Most of the time, you actually need to tell someone what time something *happens*. To say "at" a specific hour, Ukrainian uses the preposition **о** (or **об**) combined with a special locative ending for the number. 

Just like the hours themselves, do not worry about the underlying case grammar right now. Simply treat these as memorized, ready-to-use chunks. When answering the question **О котрій?** (At what time?), use these specific forms:

*   at 1:00 — **о першій**
*   at 2:00 — **о другій**
*   at 3:00 — **о третій**
*   at 4:00 — **о четвертій**
*   at 5:00 — **о п'ятій**
*   at 6:00 — **о шостій**
*   at 7:00 — **о сьомій**
*   at 8:00 — **о восьмій**
*   at 9:00 — **о дев'ятій**
*   at 10:00 — **о десятій**
*   at 11:00 — **об одинадцятій**
*   at 12:00 — **о дванадцятій**

Notice the small but important change for 11:00. We use **об** instead of **о** because the next word begins with a vowel (**одинадцятій**). This phonetic adjustment makes the pronunciation much smoother and easier to say quickly.

*   Я працюю **о дев'ятій**. (I work at nine.)
*   Ми снідаємо **о восьмій**. (We eat breakfast at eight.)
*   Зустрінемося **об одинадцятій**? (Shall we meet at eleven?)

This concept might seem like a small detail, but think of it as a specific tool for a specific job. **Котра година?** is the tool you use to read the clock face. **О котрій?** is the tool you use to mark an event on your calendar. Mixing them up is one of the most common mistakes for beginners, so keep this distinction clear.

### Times of Day

Because casual conversations almost always use the 12-hour clock, you will frequently need to specify the time of day to avoid confusion. To do this, we take the four main parts of the day and use their genitive forms to mean "of the morning," "of the afternoon," and so on.

Here are the base words and how they change when used together with a specific time:
*   the word **ранок** (morning, m) becomes **ранку** (in the morning)
*   the word **день** (day, m) becomes **дня** (in the afternoon)
*   the word **вечір** (evening, m) becomes **вечора** (in the evening)
*   the word **ніч** (night, f) becomes **ночі** (at night)

You simply add these words immediately after the hour chunk. Notice that these forms are fixed. You do not need to alter them based on the hour you are talking about. Whether it is one in the afternoon or three in the afternoon, the word **дня** remains exactly the same.

*   **о сьомій ранку** (at 7 AM)
*   **о третій дня** (at 3 PM)
*   **о десятій вечора** (at 10 PM)
*   **о другій ночі** (at 2 AM)

Ukrainian textbooks (such as *Літвінова Grade 6, pp.245-246*) present the full system of time prepositions and expressions, including two special standalone words for exact midday and exact midnight. **Опівдні** (at noon) and **опівночі** (at midnight) act as complete, standalone chunks that do not need any extra prepositions.

*   Я обідаю **опівдні**. (I eat lunch at noon.)
*   Я сплю **опівночі**. (I sleep at midnight.)

## Підсумок — Summary

You now have the essential vocabulary tools to navigate daily schedules, arrange important meetings, and answer one of the most common questions in any language. The Ukrainian system for telling time might feel slightly different at first because it relies heavily on ordinal numbers instead of cardinal ones, but the patterns are highly consistent and logical once you practice them.

Let's review the core differences between the two main questions you will hear and use every single day.

When you want to know what the clock says right now, you ask:
*   **Котра година?** (What time is it?)
*   **Десята.** (Ten o'clock.)

When you want to know when a specific event is happening, you ask:
*   **О котрій?** (At what time?)
*   **О десятій.** (At ten.)

To ensure your meaning is perfectly clear when using the 12-hour clock in conversation, always remember to attach the correct time of day to your schedule:
*   **ранку** (in the morning)
*   **дня** (in the afternoon)
*   **вечора** (in the evening)

Output ONLY the raw 1-10 scores in the table below. Do NOT calculate the weighted total — the pipeline script handles the math deterministically. Just write your per-dimension scores.

### Step 5: List findings

For every issue found, provide:
```
[DIMENSION] [SEVERITY: critical/major/minor]
Location: [specific section/paragraph — quote the actual text]
Issue: [what's wrong]
Fix: [exactly how to fix it]
```

Critical = module cannot ship. Major = quality below standard. Minor = polish item.

### Step 6: Verdict

Base your verdict on the **severity of findings** — the pipeline calculates the weighted score separately.

| Verdict | Condition |
|---------|-----------|
| **PASS** | Zero critical findings, at most minor issues |
| **REVISE** | Has major findings but no criticals — fixable without rewrite |
| **REJECT** | Has any critical finding — fundamental problems requiring rewrite |

### Step 7: Fix it yourself (REVISE only)

If your verdict is **REVISE**, you MUST output a `<fixes>` block with exact find/replace pairs. The pipeline applies these deterministically — no LLM regeneration, no rewriting from scratch.

Rules for fixes:
- Each fix is a YAML entry with `find:` (exact text from the module) and `replace:` (corrected text)
- The `find` string must be an EXACT substring of the module content — copy-paste it
- Keep fixes minimal — change only what's wrong, preserve surrounding text
- Do NOT fix словник/vocabulary tables (those are generated by a downstream tool)
- For word count issues: add content as a new `insert_after:` entry instead of find/replace
