## Обов'язок, Дозвіл та Порада

You now know how to say what you *can* physically do (**могти**) and what skills you have mastered (**вміти**). But life is not just about ability. Sometimes you have the skill to drive, but the traffic light is red. Sometimes you are tired, but you have a deadline.

In this section, we step into the world of rules, permissions, and necessities. We will learn how to say what is necessary, what is allowed, and what is strictly forbidden.

### Necessity: Треба [trɛbɑ]

The most common way to say "it is necessary" or "one needs to" in Ukrainian is the small, unchanging word **треба** [ˈtrɛbɑ]. It never changes form. You do not conjugate it. You simply add an action (infinitive) after it.

Think of it as a universal "Needs to be done" sign. It is impersonal, meaning it focuses on the necessity of the action itself.

*   **Треба працювати.** [ˈtrɛbɑ prɑt͡sʲʊˈwɑtɪ] — It is necessary to work.
*   **Треба спати.** [ˈtrɛbɑ ˈspɑtɪ] — One needs to sleep.
*   **Треба йти додому.** [ˈtrɛbɑ jˈtɪ dɔˈdɔmʊ] — It is necessary to go home.

> [!warning]
> **No Person Needed**
> In English, we usually say "**I** need" or "**You** need." In Ukrainian A1, we often just state the necessity: **Треба чекати** (It is necessary to wait).
>
> Later, you will learn how to add a person using a special case, but for now, keep it simple and impersonal. Just **Треба** + Action.

### Strong Obligation: Мусити [musɪtɪ]

When the obligation comes from inside usually because of a strong rule or urgent need, we use the verb **мусити** [ˈmusɪtɪ] (to have to / must). Unlike **треба**, this is a real verb. It changes (conjugates) to match the person.

Use this when you have no choice. It is strong and definite.

| Person | Form | IPA |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Я (I) | **мушу** | [ˈmuʃʊ] |
| Ти (You) | **мусиш** | [ˈmusɪʃ] |
| Він / Вона (He/She) | **мусить** | [ˈmusɪtʲ] |
| Ми (We) | **мусимо** | [ˈmusɪmɔ] |
| Ви (You all/formal) | **мусите** | [ˈmusɪtɛ] |
| Вони (They) | **мусять** | [ˈmusʲɑtʲ] |

*   **Я мушу знати правду.** [ja ˈmuʃʊ ˈznɑtɪ ˈprɑwdʊ] — I must know the truth.
*   **Ми мусимо допомогти.** [mɪ ˈmusɪmɔ dɔpɔmɔɦˈtɪ] — We must help.
*   **Ти мусиш слухати.** [tɪ ˈmusɪʃ ˈsluxɑtɪ] — You must listen.

### Duty and Gender: Повинен [pɔwɪnɛn]

There is another elegant way to express obligation: "obliged to" or "should." This is **повинен** [pɔˈwɪnɛn].

It behaves like an adjective! It changes based on **gender**, not just the person. This is very common in formal settings or when talking about duties.

*   **Він повинен** [win pɔˈwɪnɛn] — He is obliged / He should.
*   **Вона повинна** [wɔˈnɑ pɔˈwɪnnɑ] — She is obliged / She should.
*   **Вони повинні** [wɔˈnɪ pɔˈwɪnːi] — They are obliged / They should.

*   **Він повинен працювати тут.** [win pɔˈwɪnɛn prɑt͡sʲʊˈwɑtɪ tut] — He should work here.
*   **Вона повинна купити хліб.** [wɔˈnɑ pɔˈwɪnnɑ kuˈpɪtɪ xlʲib] — She must buy bread.

### Permission: Можна [mɔʒnɑ]

How do you ask "May I?" or say "It is allowed"? Use the invariable word **можна** [ˈmɔʒnɑ]. Like **треба**, it does not change.

It is your "Green Light." You see a sign "Parking" — **Тут можна паркувати** (One can park here). You want to enter a room — **Можна?** (May I?).

*   **Тут можна сидіти.** [tut ˈmɔʒnɑ sɪˈdʲitɪ] — One may sit here.
*   **Можна запитати?** [ˈmɔʒnɑ zɑpɪˈtɑtɪ] — May I ask?
*   **Можна увійти?** [ˈmɔʒnɑ uwiˈjtɪ] — May I come in?

### Prohibition: Не можна [nɛ mɔʒnɑ]

To forbid something, simply add **не**. **Не можна** [nɛ ˈmɔʒnɑ] means "It is not allowed" or "One must not." It is your "Red Light."

*   **Тут не можна курити.** [tut nɛ ˈmɔʒnɑ kuˈrɪtɪ] — Smoking is not allowed here.
*   **Не можна говорити голосно.** [nɛ ˈmɔʒnɑ ɦɔwɔˈrɪtɪ ˈhɔlɔsnɔ] — One must not speak loudly.

> [!culture]
> **The Polite Question**
> In Ukraine, privacy and personal space are respected. Before you take an empty chair at a café, open a window on a bus, or enter an office, just ask: «**Можна?**» (May I?).
>
> It is the magic key that opens doors. The answer is usually a warm «**Можна!**» or «**Будь ласка**» (Please).

### Friendly Advice: Варто [wɑrtɔ]

Finally, what if it is not a rule, but just a good idea? Use **варто** [ˈwɑrtɔ] (it is worth / one should). It is softer than **мусити** or **повинен**. It is friendly advice from a neighbor.

*   **Варто спробувати.** [ˈwɑrtɔ sprɔbuˈwɑtɪ] — It is worth trying.
*   **Варто подивитися цей фільм.** [ˈwɑrtɔ pɔdɪˈwɪtɪsʲɑ t͡sɛj fʲilʲm] — It is worth watching this film.
*   **Не варто чекати.** [nɛ ˈwɑrtɔ t͡ʃɛˈkɑtɪ] — It is not worth waiting.
