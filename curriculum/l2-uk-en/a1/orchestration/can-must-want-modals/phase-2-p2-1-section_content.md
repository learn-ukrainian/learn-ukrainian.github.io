## Могти та Вміти: Ваші перші кроки

Imagine you are standing at the fence, talking to your neighbor. You want to ask for help, or perhaps share a hidden talent you have. In English, we use the word "can" for almost everything: "I can help," "I can swim," "I can hear you." But Ukrainian is more precise. We distinguish between physical possibility and learned skills. Let's explore these two essential tools for your daily conversations.

### Могти: Physical Possibility and Permission

When you want to express that something is physically possible, permitted, or within your power right now, you use the verb **могти** [mohˈtɪ]. Think of this as "to be able to" or "to be in a position to."

This verb is irregular, so pay attention to the sound changes. The stem changes from **г** to **ж** in most forms.

| Person | Ukrainian | IPA | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | **Я можу** | [ja ˈmɔʒʊ] | I can / I am able |
| You (singular) | **Ти можеш** | [tɪ ˈmɔʒɛʃ] | You can |
| He/She | **Він/Вона може** | [win / wɔˈnɑ ˈmɔʒɛ] | He/She can |
| We | **Ми можемо** | [mɪ ˈmɔʒɛmɔ] | We can |
| You (formal/plural) | **Ви можете** | [vɪ ˈmɔʒɛtɛ] | You can |
| They | **Вони можуть** | [wɔˈnɪ ˈmɔʒʊtʲ] | They can |

Use this verb when asking favors or stating facts about the current situation. It does not imply you learned a skill; it just means nothing is stopping you.

_Examples:_
*   **Я можу це зробити.** [ja ˈmɔʒʊ tsɛ zrɔˈbɪtɪ] — I can do this.
*   **Ви можете допомогти?** [vɪ ˈmɔʒɛtɛ dɔpɔmhˈtɪ] — Can you help?
*   **Ми не можемо чекати.** [mɪ nɛ ˈmɔʒɛmɔ tʃɛˈkɑtɪ] — We cannot wait.

### Вміти: Learned Skills and Know-How

Now, imagine you want to brag about your talents. Maybe you paint, cook borshch, or speak Ukrainian. Here, physical possibility is not the point. The point is your knowledge and training. For this, we use the verb **вміти** [ˈwmitɪ].

This verb translates closer to "to know how" or "to have the skill." It follows a softer conjugation pattern called the Second Conjugation. Note how the vowel **є** [jɛ] appears in the endings.

| Person | Ukrainian | IPA | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | **Я вмію** | [ja ˈwmijʊ] | I know how |
| You (singular) | **Ти вмієш** | [tɪ ˈwmijɛʃ] | You know how |
| He/She | **Він/Вона вміє** | [win / wɔˈnɑ ˈwmijɛ] | He/She knows how |
| We | **Ми вміємо** | [mɪ ˈwmijɛmɔ] | We know how |
| You (formal/plural) | **Ви вмієте** | [vɪ ˈwmijɛtɛ] | You know how |
| They | **Вони вміють** | [wɔˈnɪ ˈwmijʊtʲ] | They know how |

Use **вміти** whenever you discuss hobbies, professions, or languages.

_Examples:_
*   **Я вмію читати.** [ja ˈwmijʊ tʃɪˈtɑtɪ] — I know how to read.
*   **Ти вмієш готувати?** [tɪ ˈwmijɛʃ hɔtʊˈwɑtɪ] — Do you know how to cook?
*   **Вона гарно вміє співати.** [wɔˈnɑ ˈhɑrnɔ ˈwmijɛ sʲpiˈwɑtɪ] — She knows how to sing well.

### The Vital Difference: Possibility vs. Skill

The most common mistake for beginners is mixing these two up because English uses "can" for both. To choose the correct Ukrainian word, ask yourself a simple question: "Did I learn this at school or through practice?"

If the answer is yes, use **вміти**. If the answer is "It is physically possible right now," use **могти**.

> [!tip]
> **The "Pool Test"**
> Imagine you are standing in front of a swimming pool.
> *   You took swimming lessons for years. Do you have the skill? Yes. So, **я вмію плавати** (I know how to swim).
> *   But today the pool is closed, or perhaps you forgot your swimsuit. Is it possible to swim right now? No. So, **я не можу плавати** (I cannot swim [right now]).
>
> You can possess a skill (**вміти**) but lack the opportunity (**могти**) to use it.

This distinction adds clarity to your speech. It tells your neighbor not just what you *will* do, but what you *are capable* of doing.

_Compare these situations:_
*   **Я вмію писати.** [ja ˈwmijʊ pɪˈsɑtɪ] — I am literate (I have the skill).
*   **Я можу писати тут.** [ja ˈmɔʒʊ pɪˈsɑtɪ tut] — I have permission/space to write here.
*   **Він не вміє водити.** [win nɛ ˈwmijɛ wɔˈdɪtɪ] — He cannot drive (he never learned).
*   **Він не може водити.** [win nɛ ˈmɔʒɛ wɔˈdɪtɪ] — He cannot drive (maybe he is sick or lost his keys).
