## Світ кольорів: Узгодження прикметників

### The Golden Rule of Description
Imagine you are painting a picture. In English, you use the same paint for everything: a *red* bus, a *red* pen, a *red* apple. The word "red" never changes. But Ukrainian is different. In Ukrainian, the paint must match the canvas! This is the Golden Rule of adjectives: **The adjective must agree with the noun in gender.**

If the noun is masculine, the adjective wears a masculine uniform. If the noun is feminine, the adjective changes its ending to match. It is like a dance partner following the lead. The noun leads, and the adjective follows.

This might seem strange at first, but it gives the language a beautiful rhythm. You already know that **стіл** (table) is masculine and **кава** (coffee) is feminine. Now we will see how colors change to fit these words.

### Meeting the Palette
Let’s look at the most common colors. We will start with the masculine forms, as these are the base forms you find in the dictionary. Listen to the music of these words.

*   **білий** [ˈb⁽ʲ⁾ilɪj] — white
*   **чорний** [ˈtʃɔrnɪj] — black
*   **червоний** [tʃerˈwɔnɪj] — red
*   **синій** [ˈsɪnʲij] — dark blue
*   **зелений** [zeˈlɛnɪj] — green
*   **жовтий** [ˈʒɔwtɪj] — yellow

Notice that most of them end in **-ий**. This is the standard ending for masculine adjectives. But look at **синій**. It ends in **-ій** because the sound [nʲ] is soft. This is a small detail, but an important one for your accent.

### The Magic of Endings
Now, let’s see the Golden Rule in action. We will take the color **червоний** (red) and paint three different objects. Watch how the ending changes.

For masculine nouns, we use the base form **-ий**:
*   **Це червоний автобус.** (This is a red bus.)
*   **Там стоїть червоний стілець.** (A red chair stands there.)

For feminine nouns, the ending changes to **-а**. This sounds open and musical:
*   **Це червона ручка.** (This is a red pen.)
*   **Моя машина червона.** (My car is red.)

For neuter nouns, the ending becomes **-е**:
*   **Це червоне яблуко.** (This is a red apple.)
*   **Вино червоне.** (The wine is red.)

> [!tip]
> **Rhythm Check**
> To remember this, think of the rhyme in the endings.
> *   Masculine: **Він** (He) — **червоний**
> *   Feminine: **Вона** (She) — **червона**
> *   Neuter: **Воно** (It) — **червоне**
> The vowel often matches the final sound of the pronoun!

### More Than Just Paint: The Soul of Color
In Ukraine, colors are not just visual decoration. They carry deep meaning, especially in our embroidery and traditions. When you describe something, you often invoke these ancient symbols.

> [!culture]
> **The Language of Symbols**
> *   **Червоний** (Red) symbolizes energy, life, and love. It also protects against evil. That is why **вишиванка** (embroidered shirt) often has red thread.
> *   **Чорний** (Black) is not about sadness. It symbolizes the fertile soil (earth) and wisdom. It creates a powerful contrast with white and red.
> *   **Білий** (White) represents purity and divinity. A clean white shirt is the canvas for the soul.

So when you say **«Це червона вишиванка»**, you are saying it is full of life and protection.

### Practice in the Wild
Let’s look at the world around us. Nature gives us the best examples of these colors. Try to match the gender in your head as you read these descriptions.

*   **Сніг білий.** (The snow is white.) — Masculine
*   **Трава зелена.** (The grass is green.) — Feminine
*   **Сонце жовте.** (The sun is yellow.) — Neuter

Notice **синій** (dark blue) again. Because it is soft, its endings are slightly different: **синій** (masc.), **синя** (fem.), **синє** (neut.).
*   **Небо синє.** (The sky is dark blue.)

Use these patterns when you shop or describe your home. Start with the noun, identify its gender, and let the adjective follow.
