Ukrainian adjusts these small words based on the surrounding sounds. The core physical principle is simple: avoid clusters of the same type. When consonants pile up, Ukrainian inserts a vowel sound to break them apart. When a vowel follows a vowel, a light consonant glide smooths the transition. We also see the rule for "in/to" applying again: **у театр** (because "театр" starts with a consonant and the previous word ends in a consonant) but **в офіс** (because "офіс" starts with a vowel). 

These are not strict grammatical rules meant to trick you; they are physical pronunciation helpers. They exist simply because human mouths prefer alternating vowels and consonants.

## У чи В? (У or В?)

As noted in the Ukrainian textbook *Авраменко Grade 5* (p. 117): "Чергування у–в забезпечує милозвучність мови." (The alternation of у–в ensures the euphony of the language). 

The core rule is simple: avoid consonant clusters. You use **у/в** (in/at — alternating preposition) depending on what makes the sentence flow without stopping. 

Use **в** after a vowel and before a consonant. The vowel before it gives your voice enough momentum to pronounce the consonant without needing an extra vowel sound.
*   Вона живе **в** Києві. *(She lives in Kyiv.)*
*   Я працюю **в** банку. *(I work in a bank.)*
*   Ми йдемо **в** парк. *(We are going to the park.)*

Use **у** between two consonants. If the previous word ends in a consonant, and the next word starts with a consonant, saying **в** would force you to pronounce three consonants in a row. Ukrainian uses **у** to break them up.
*   Тарас **у** Львові. *(Taras is in Lviv.)*
*   Максим **у** банку. *(Maksym is in the bank.)*
*   Брат **у** школі. *(The brother is at school.)*

Before a vowel, **в** is used regardless of what comes before:
*   Він працює **в** офісі. *(He works in an office.)*
*   Потім **в** університет. *(Then to the university.)*

This alternation applies to both the preposition (meaning "in" or "at") and the prefix for words like **вже/уже** (already). You can say **Він уже тут** or **Вона вже тут** — both mean "already", but they change to fit the surrounding letters.

There are a few important exceptions to know. At the start of a sentence, if the next word begins with a consonant, you always use **У**:
*   **У** мене є кіт. *(I have a cat.)*
*   **У** Києві тепло. *(It is warm in Kyiv.)*

Similarly, after a pause or a comma, the flow of the sentence resets, so you treat it like the start of a sentence:
*   Так, **у** нас є проблема. *(Yes, we have a problem.)*

Do not overthink it. Native speakers use euphony instinctively because it feels physically easier to say. The goal is to produce sentences that SOUND smooth, not to do complex mental math before every sentence.

## І чи Й? З, із, чи зі?

The same principle applies to other common connecting words. The textbook *Літвінова Grade 5* (p. 176) explains the alternation for "and". 

The word **і/й** (and — alternating conjunction) connects words and ideas. 

Use **і** after a consonant or at the start of a sentence. This acts as a vowel bridge after a hard consonant sound.
*   брат **і** сестра *(brother and sister)*
*   Тарас **і** Максим *(Taras and Maksym)*
*   день **і** ніч *(day and night)*

Use **й** after a vowel, especially before another vowel. This provides a soft, gliding consonant sound that smooths the transition to the next word.
*   мама **й** тато *(mom and dad)*
*   вона **й** він *(she and he)*
*   кава **й** молоко *(coffee and milk)*

At the start of a sentence, you always use **І**:
*   **І** він прийшов. *(And he arrived.)*

Another important set of alternations involves the word for "with" or "from". According to *Літвінова Grade 5* (p. 177), the word **з/із/зі** (with/from — alternating preposition) has three forms to handle different combinations of letters. This is a slightly smaller rule than the **у/в** alternation, but it is very important for natural, authentic speech.

Use **з** before vowels and most single consonants. This is the most common form.
*   Він **з** Одеси. *(He is from Odesa.)*
*   Я йду **з** другом. *(I am going with a friend.)*
*   Вона **з** Києва. *(She is from Kyiv.)*

Use **із** between consonants to avoid a difficult cluster.
*   Максим **із** Семеном. *(Maksym with Semen.)*
*   лист **із** Харкова *(a letter from Kharkiv)*

Use **зі** before words starting with difficult consonant combinations, especially those beginning with **з**, **с**, **ш**, **щ**.
*   Вона працює **зі** мною. *(She works with me.)*
*   Вітаю **зі** святом! *(Congratulations with the holiday!)*
*   Він іде **зі** школи. *(He is coming from school.)*

Pronouncing "з святом" is physically difficult and sounds like a stutter. Saying "зі святом" introduces a clear, bright vowel that makes the phrase roll off the tongue.

## Підсумок — Summary

Ukrainian is built for smooth, melodic speech. Euphony (*милозвучність*) is the natural adjustment of small connecting words to prevent harsh consonant clusters or awkward vowel collisions. 

Remember the three main euphony pairs and triplets we covered today:

1.  **у/в** — avoid consonant+consonant: 
    *   Тарас **у** Львові. (consonant - **у** - consonant)
    *   Вона **в** Києві. (vowel - **в** - consonant)
2.  **і/й** — avoid vowel+vowel: 
    *   брат **і** сестра (consonant - **і** - consonant)
    *   мама **й** тато (vowel - **й** - consonant)
3.  **з/із/зі** — before difficult clusters: 
    *   **з** другом (standard consonant)
    *   брат **із** сестрою (between consonants)
    *   **зі** мною (before a difficult cluster)

Self-check your understanding. Which is correct: "Я живу в Києві" or "Я живу у Києві"? Because "живу" ends in a vowel, **в** is correct. What about "Мама і тато" or "Мама й тато"? Because "Мама" ends in a vowel, **й** is the smooth choice. 

The best way to practice euphony is to read your Ukrainian sentences aloud. Try it with the examples from this module. Do they flow smoothly? Do you have to stop or stutter to get the consonants out? If the sentence feels physically difficult to say, you probably need to switch a connecting word. Trust your mouth!
