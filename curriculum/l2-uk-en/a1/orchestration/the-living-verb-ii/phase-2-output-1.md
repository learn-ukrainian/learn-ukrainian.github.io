# The Living Verb II

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> You already know how to talk about daily actions using Class I verbs. Now we explore the second major family of Ukrainian action words. Class II verbs cover ongoing states and the most fundamental human interactions.

## Вступ: Тріада гостинності

You already know how to talk about your daily actions using Class I verbs. Now, we are ready to explore the second major family of Ukrainian action words. We call these Class II verbs, or verbs ending in **-ити**.

Why do we need a second group? While Class I verbs cover a lot of general activities, Class II verbs often describe ongoing, habitual states and some of the most fundamental human interactions. 

In Ukrainian culture, building connections relies on a very simple concept. We can call it the «Triad of Hospitality».

Украї́нці лю́блять госте́й. (Ukrainians love guests.)
Ми лю́бимо ї́сти. (We love to eat.)
Ми лю́бимо пи́ти. (We love to drink.)
Ми лю́бимо говори́ти. (We love to speak.)

When you visit a Ukrainian home, these three actions — eating, drinking, and speaking — form the core of your experience. You cannot separate social bonding from sharing a meal and a good conversation.

> [!culture]
> Hospitality is a central value in Ukraine. Offering food and drink is a primary way to show respect and care. Refusing a meal can sometimes be perceived as rejecting the host's goodwill, so it is always polite to accept at least a little bit!

Before we look at the endings, remember a key concept about the present tense. In Ukrainian, present tense verbs describe actions that are happening right now or actions that happen regularly as a habit. They focus on the process of doing something.

Я ба́чу стіл. (I see a table.)
Я ба́чу хліб. (I see bread.)
Я ба́чу во́ду. (I see water.)

When you say you do these things, you are describing your ongoing reality. Now, let's see how these words change their shapes to match who is doing the action.

## Презентація: Моделі та закінчення

Let's look at the patterns for Class II verbs. You will notice they are very similar to what you already know, but with a crucial vowel shift.

Instead of the «е» or «є» sounds you saw in Class I, Class II verbs use «и» or «і» in most of their forms. This vowel difference is the main key to telling them apart.

Let's use the verb **говори́ти** (to speak) as our model. It is regular and very useful.

Я говорю́ украї́нською. (I speak Ukrainian.)
Ти говори́ш до́бре. (You speak well.)
Він говори́ть ти́хо. (He speaks quietly.)

Notice the endings for these forms:
*   Я (I) drops the -ити and adds **-ю** (or sometimes **-у**).
*   Ти (You singular) adds **-иш**.
*   Він/Вона/Воно (He/She/It) adds **-ить**.
*   Ми (We) adds **-имо**.
*   Ви (You plural/formal) adds **-ите**.
*   Вони (They) adds **-ять** (or sometimes **-ать**).

Here is the full table for **говори́ти**:

| Pronoun | Form | Ending |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Я | я говорю́ | -ю |
| Ти | ти говори́ш | -иш |
| Він / Вона | він говори́ть | -ить |
| Ми | ми говори́мо | -имо |
| Ви | ви говори́те | -ите |
| Вони | вони говоря́ть | -ять |

> [!tip]
> A great way to remember the difference between the two verb classes is to compare the «you» forms. Class I has «ти чита́єш» (you read). Class II has «ти говори́ш» (you speak). If you hear an «е/є» sound, it is Class I. If you hear an «и/і» sound, it is Class II!

Here is another common verb, **вчи́ти** (to teach or to learn):

Вона́ вчить слова́. (She learns words.)
Вони́ вчать мо́ву. (They learn a language.)

Notice that for **вчи́ти**, the «я» form takes **-у** instead of **-ю** (я вчу), and the «вони» form takes **-ать** instead of **-ять** (вони вчать). This is a natural spelling adjustment after the letter «ч».

## Презентація: Мутації та Неправильні дієслова

We now arrive at a fascinating feature of Ukrainian pronunciation. Sometimes, the last consonant of the verb stem changes when we say «I do it». This only happens in the «я» (I) form. We call this a consonant mutation.

Let's look at the verb **ходи́ти** (to walk or to go regularly).

Він хо́дить до шко́ли. (He walks to school.)
Ти хо́диш у парк. (You walk to the park.)

When I do the walking, the «д» changes to a «дж» sound.

Я ходжу́. (I walk.)
Я ходжу́ до шко́ли. (I walk to school.)

This happens with **сиді́ти** (to sit) as well.

Я сиджу́. (I sit.)
Вона́ сиди́ть. (She sits.)

Another type of mutation involves lips. When a verb stem ends in a labial consonant — like б, п, в, м, or ф — it is physically difficult to say the «ю» ending right after it. To make pronunciation easier and smoother, Ukrainian inserts a soft «л» sound. We call this the «Labial L».

Look at **роби́ти** (to do):

Ти ро́биш. (You do.)
Я роблю́. (I do.)

And **люби́ти** (to love):

Ми лю́бимо приро́ду. (We love nature.)
Я люблю́ те́бе. (I love you.)

Without the «л», saying the forms feels awkward. The «л» acts as a bridge for your tongue.

> [!did-you-know]
> The verb **люби́ти** (to love) shares an ancient root with the word **лю́ди** (people). In a deep historical sense, to love means to belong to a community of people!

Some verbs are simply unique and do not follow normal rules. The most important one in the Triad of Hospitality is **ї́сти** (to eat). You must memorize its forms.

Я їм. (I eat.)
Ти їси́. (You eat.)
Він їсть. (He eats.)
Ми їмо́. (We eat.)
Ви їсте́. (You eat.)
Вони́ їдя́ть. (They eat.)

The verb **пи́ти** (to drink) is short and punchy.

Я п'ю во́ду. (I drink water.)
Він п'є ка́ву. (He drinks coffee.)
Вони́ п'ють. (They drink.)

Finally, let's look at sight. **Ба́чити** means to see — it describes the physical ability or the result of sight.

Я ба́чу стіл. (I see a table.)

When you want to describe the process of watching something, you will use a different verb, **диви́тися** (to watch), which we will explore later. For now, focus on **ба́чити** for perceiving things with your eyes.

## Практика: Уникнення типових помилок

When learning Class II verbs, it is easy to accidentally mix the rules. The most common mistake is using Class I endings where they do not belong. 

We must train our ears to expect the «и» sound. Let's compare correct and incorrect forms.

Він ро́бить. (He does.)
Він ро́бе. (Incorrect.)

Ти говори́ш. (You speak.)
Ти говори́єш. (Incorrect.)

Ми пла́тимо. (We pay.)
Ми пла́темо. (Incorrect.)

You should always pause and check if the verb requires an «и» before you speak.

Another common slip is forgetting the consonant mutations in the «я» form. We must practice these until they feel natural.

Я ходжу́. (I walk.)
Я сиджу́. (I sit.)
Я плачу́. (I pay.)
Я прошу́. (I ask.)

> [!warning]
> If you ignore the mutation and say forms like «я ходю» or «я сидю», people will understand you, but it sounds like baby talk. The mutations give your speech an authentic, mature sound.

Let's put our new verbs into action. We use these verbs constantly to describe our daily routines. 

Я роблю́ дома́шнє завда́ння. (I do homework.)
Він лю́бить приро́ду. (He loves nature.)
Ми говори́мо украї́нською. (We speak Ukrainian.)
Вони́ лю́блять чита́ти. (They love to read.)

These simple sentences form the foundation of your ability to converse. Pay attention to the endings and mutations, and your sentences will sound natural and clear.

# Підсумок

You have now mastered the two major verb classes in the present tense. Remember the golden rule: Class I verbs rely on «е» and «є» in their endings, while Class II verbs rely on «и» and «і». 

Understanding this distinction, along with the special «я» form mutations and the essential irregular verbs for eating and drinking, opens up a massive part of the language for you.

Here are a few questions to test your knowledge:

1. How do you say «I love» and «I do» in Ukrainian?
2. What is the «я» form of **ходи́ти** and **сиді́ти**?
3. How do you conjugate the verb **ї́сти** for «I», «you», and «he»?
4. What vowel defines the endings for Class II verbs?

If you can answer these easily, you are ready to move forward. Keep observing the verbs you encounter!
