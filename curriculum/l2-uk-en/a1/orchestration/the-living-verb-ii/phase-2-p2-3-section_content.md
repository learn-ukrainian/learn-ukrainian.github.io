## Культурна довідка: Давні корені дієслова

These two verbs are essential for hospitality, so keep them close!

> [!cultural]
> In Ukrainian culture, offering food and drink is a sacred duty of a host. You will often hear **“Прошу до столу!”** (Please come to the table!) followed quickly by **“Ви будете їсти?”** (Will you eat?). Knowing how to answer with **“Я їм...”** or **“Я п’ю...”** is the first step to being a great guest.

As you sit at the table and enjoy a meal with your Ukrainian friends, you are participating in a tradition that stretches back over a thousand years. The very words you use to describe your actions—the **дієслова** (verbs) ending in **-ити**—are living echoes of the ancient language of Kyivan Rus’. It is fascinating to think that when you say **«Я люблю...»** or **«Ми говоримо...»**, you are using structures that have remained remarkably stable since the 10th century.

### Історична лінія: 1000 років стабільності

The **-ити** ending you are learning today is a direct descendant of the Proto-Slavic infinitive form `*-iti`. While other parts of the language have shifted and evolved, this specific class of verbs has acted as a linguistic anchor for centuries. In the era of Kyivan Rus’, speakers used these same patterns to describe their daily lives, their faith, and their laws. When you look at ancient birch bark manuscripts or the **«Руська Правда»** (Rus' Justice)—the oldest law code of the region—you find these familiar verb forms everywhere.

For a beginner, this history is a great comfort. It means the system you are mastering isn't a modern invention or a complex "patchwork" of rules; it is a tried-and-tested engine of communication that has served millions of people for over a millennium. Understanding this "heritage link" helps us see the Ukrainian language not just as a set of grammar rules, but as a continuous thread of human history.

| Period | Form | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Proto-Slavic (Ancient) | `*lubiti` | to love |
| Kyivan Rus' (11th c.) | **любити** — [lʲuˈbɪtɪ] | to love |
| Modern Ukrainian | **любити** — [lʲuˈbɪtɪ] | to love |

### Етимологія: Любити означає бути з людьми

One of the most beautiful connections in the Ukrainian language is found in the word **любити** (to love). If you look closely at the root, you will see it shares a deep historical connection with the word **люди** (people). In the Slavic worldview, the concept of "loving" is linguistically inseparable from the concept of "being among people" or "belonging to a community." To love is, in a sense, to recognize the humanity in others.

This connection reflects the deep social interconnectedness found in Ukrainian traditions. Whether it is a village gathering or a modern family dinner, the act of "loving" is often expressed through community and shared presence. When you tell someone **«Я люблю тебе»** (I love you), you are using a word that roots your affection in the very idea of what it means to be human.

> [!fact]
> The verb **любити** — [lʲuˈbɪtɪ] is used for both people and things you enjoy (like hobbies). However, for deep romantic love or intense devotion, Ukrainians also use the verb **кохати** — [koˈxatɪ]. As a beginner, **любити** is your "all-purpose" word for showing appreciation!

### Слов’янська родина: Мовний міст

Because these **-ити** verbs are so ancient, they serve as a wonderful "bridge" to other Slavic languages. If you ever travel to Poland, Czechia, or Slovakia, you will find that your Ukrainian knowledge gives you a massive head start. The stems and endings often look like siblings or cousins, reflecting their shared ancestry. This kinship is part of what makes learning Ukrainian so rewarding—it opens the door to a whole family of cultures.

In the table below, notice how the "Second Conjugation" pattern (the "i/y" sound in the endings) remains a common feature across these neighbors. Even if the spelling changes slightly, the "music" of the conjugation is the same.

| English | Ukrainian | Polish | Czech |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| He/She speaks | **Він говорить** | *On mówi* | *On mluví* |
| They speak | **Вони говорять** | *Oni mówią* | *Oni mluví* |
| He/She does | **Він робить** | *On robi* | *On robí* |

> [!myth-buster]
> You might hear that Slavic languages are "impossible" to learn because of their verbs. But look at the patterns! Whether in Kyiv, Warsaw, or Prague, the **-ити** family follows a logical, historical rhythm. Once you find the beat, you can dance in any Slavic ballroom!
