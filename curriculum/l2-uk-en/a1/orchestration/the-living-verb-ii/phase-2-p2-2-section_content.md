## Практика та застосування у мовленні

Now that you can tell the «и-family» apart from the «е-family», let's see how these verbs behave in real life and how you can start using them today.

### Identifying the Conjugation Families
Think of verbs as neighbors in a small village. Some belong to the **Перша дієвідміна** [ˈpɛrʃɐ dʲijɛʋidʲˈminɐ] (First Conjugation) family, while others, like the ones we are meeting today, are part of the **Друга дієвідміна** [ˈdruɦɐ dʲijɛʋidʲˈminɐ] (Second Conjugation). To help them find their way home, you simply look at their "last name"—the **інфінітив** (infinitive) ending. If a verb ends in **-ати**, it usually belongs to the first group. If it ends in **-ити**, it joins our current family. Sorting them correctly is the first step to speaking like a local and avoiding that clumsy "hat on your foot" feeling.

_Приклади:_
**Я роблю.** [ja rɔˈblʲʊ] — I am doing.
**Ти бачиш.** [tɪ ˈbatʃɪʃ] — You see.
**Він ходить.** [ʋin ˈxɔdɪtʲ] — He walks.

[!observe]
Look at the verb **робити** [rɔˈbɪtɪ] (to do). Can you see the **-и-** in the middle of almost every form? This is the signature of our new family!

### High-Frequency Verb Pairings
In Ukrainian, some words are best friends and almost always appear together. These are called collocations. For example, when you talk about speaking, you often say **говорити повільно** [ɦɔwɔˈrɪtɪ pɔˈʋilʲnɔ] (to speak slowly). Another essential pair is **робити помилки** [rɔˈbɪtɪ pɔˈmɪlkɪ] (to make mistakes). Don't worry, everyone makes them when they start! By learning these common pairs, your speech will sound much more natural and fluid to your neighbors.

_Приклади:_
**Ми говоримо багато.** [mɪ ɦɔˈwɔrɪmɔ bɐˈɦatɔ] — We speak a lot.
**Я роблю фото.** [ja rɔˈblʲʊ ˈfɔtɔ] — I am taking a photo.
**Вона платить гроші.** [ʋɔˈna plɐˈtɪtʲ ˈɦrɔʃi] — She is paying money.

### Spotlight on the Third Person
The third person forms—he, she, it, and they—are the ones you will use most often when describing the world around you. In the **Друга дієвідміна**, the singular ending is usually **-ить** [ɪtʲ]. When you move to the plural "they," it changes to **-ять** [jatʲ]. It is a small shift, but it helps your listener know exactly how many people you are talking about. It’s like a secret code for plurals that makes your descriptions clear and professional.

| Singular (Він/Вона) | Plural (Вони) | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Він любить** [ʋin ˈlʲʊbɪtʲ] | **Вони люблять** [ʋɔˈnɪ ˈlʲʊblʲatʲ] | He loves / They love |
| **Вона бачить** [ʋɔˈna ˈbatʃɪtʲ] | **Вони бачать** [ʋɔˈnɪ ˈbatʃatʲ] | She sees / They see |
| **Воно стоїть** [ʋɔˈnɔ stɔˈjitʲ] | **Вони стоять** [ʋɔˈnɪ stɔˈjatʲ] | It stands / They stand |

### Common Sentence Blueprints
Now, let’s put everything together into simple sentences. You can use a few basic blueprints to talk about your day. A very popular one is starting with **Я люблю...** [ja lʲʊˈblʲʊ] followed by another verb to show what you enjoy doing. You can also describe what is happening right in front of you using **Ти бачиш...** [tɪ ˈbatʃɪʃ]. These patterns are like Lego blocks; once you have the base, you can build any sentence you want to share with a friend.

_Приклади:_
**Я люблю пити каву.** [ja lʲʊˈblʲʊ ˈpɪtɪ ˈkawʊ] — I love to drink coffee.
**Ти бачиш парк.** [tɪ ˈbatʃɪʃ park] — You see the park.
**Ми бачимо море.** [mɪ ˈbatʃɪmɔ ˈmɔrɛ] — We see the sea.

[!culture]
Ukrainians love to host guests. If a neighbor sees you and says **«Ходити в гості!»** [xɔˈdɪtɪ ʋ ˈɦɔsʲtʲɪ], they are inviting you over for tea and a chat. It is a cornerstone of our hospitality and a great chance to practice your verbs!

### Special Guests: Eating and Drinking
Finally, we have two verbs that are absolutely vital for survival and social life: **їсти** [ˈjis tɪ] (to eat) and **пити** [ˈpɪtɪ] (to drink). While **пити** follows the rules of the First Conjugation fairly well, **їсти** is an ancient "bonus verb" with its own unique rhythm. It doesn't fit the standard patterns perfectly because it is so old. However, because we use it every single day, you will find it becomes second nature very quickly.

**Conjugation of їсти (to eat):**
- **Я їм** [ja jim] — I eat
- **Ти їси** [tɪ jiˈsɪ] — You eat
- **Він/Вона їсть** [ʋin ˈjistʲ] — He/She eats
- **Ми їмо** [mɪ jiˈmɔ] — We eat
- **Ви їсте** [ʋɪ jisˈtɛ] — You (plural) eat
- **Вони їдять** [ʋɔˈnɪ jiˈdʲatʲ] — They eat

**Conjugation of пити (to drink):**
- **Я п'ю** [ja pju] — I drink
- **Ти п'єш** [tɪ pjeʃ] — You drink
- **Він/Вона п'є** [ʋin pje] — He/She/It drinks
