## Розминка: Що ми їмо та п'ємо?

### Eating and Drinking: Our Daily Routine
In our previous lessons, we learned how to handle numbers and money. Now, it is time to use those skills for something very practical: shopping for food! Every day, we engage in the simple but essential acts of eating and drinking. In Ukraine, food is not just fuel; it is a way to show hospitality and connect with others. When you visit someone's home, you will often hear questions about what you would like to have. Starting with these basic habits helps us build the foundation for any social interaction. We will focus on the simple things we consume at home or buy at a small local store. Let’s look at how we describe these daily actions in Ukrainian.

In this section, we use simple phrases to describe what is on our table. We talk about «хліб» — [xlʲib] (bread), «молоко» — [mɔ.lɔ.ˈkɔ] (milk), and «вода» — [ʋɔ.ˈda] (water). These are the building blocks of a typical day. Ukrainian families value fresh ingredients, and you will see people buying these items every morning.

_Приклади:_
- **Ми їмо хліб.** — [mɪ ˈjis.mɔ xlʲib] — We eat bread.
- **Ти п'єш воду.** — [tɪ pjeʃ ʋɔ.ˈdu] — You drink water.
- **Вона їсть яблуко.** — [ʋɔ.ˈna jistʲ ˈjab.lu.kɔ] — She eats an apple.

### The Action Verbs: To Eat, To Drink, and To Love
To talk about our food habits, we need three primary verbs: «їсти» (to eat), «пити» (to drink), and «любити» (to love). In Ukrainian, these verbs belong to different groups, and their endings change based on the person. The verb «їсти» — [ˈjis.tɪ] is unique because it has very short forms. The verb «пити» — [ˈpɪ.tɪ] changes its stem slightly when we conjugate it. Finally, «любити» — [lʲu.ˈbɪ.tɪ] is essential for expressing what we enjoy. Notice how the letter «л» — [lʲ] appears in the "I" form of «любити». This is a common pattern for many Ukrainian verbs.

| Person | **їсти** (to eat) | **пити** (to drink) | **любити** (to love) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Я** (I) | **їм** — [jim] | **п'ю** — [pju] | **люблю** — [lʲu.ˈblʲu] |
| **Ти** (You) | **їси** — [ji.ˈsɪ] | **п'єш** — [pjeʃ] | **любиш** — [ˈlʲu.bɪʃ] |
| **Він/Вона** (He/She) | **їсть** — [jistʲ] | **п'є** — [pje] | **любить** — [ˈlʲu.bɪtʲ] |
| **Ми** (We) | **їмо** — [ji.ˈmɔ] | **п'ємо** — [pje.ˈmɔ] | **любимо** — [ˈlʲu.bɪ.mɔ] |
| **Ви** (You pl.) | **їсте** — [ji.ˈstɛ] | **п'єте** — [pje.ˈtɛ] | **любите** — [ˈlʲu.bɪ.tɛ] |
| **Вони** (They) | **їдять** — [ji.ˈdʲatʲ] | **п'ють** — [pjutʲ] | **люблять** — [ˈlʲu.blʲatʲ] |

Using these verbs is straightforward. You simply place the verb after the person and follow it with the food or drink. We keep our sentences short and clear.

### Expressing Preferences: My Favorite Drinks
When we use the verb «любити» (to love) to talk about drinks, the words for those drinks might change their endings. This happens because the drink is the object of our action. This is called the **Accusative case**. For words ending in «-а», like «кава» — [ˈka.ʋa] (coffee) or «вода» — [ʋɔ.ˈda] (water), the ending changes to «-у». However, words like «сік» — [sʲik] (juice) or «чай» — [tʃaj] (tea) stay exactly the same. We use these patterns to tell our friends what we want to drink.

[!tip]
In Ukrainian, when you love a feminine word (ending in **-а**), it changes to **-у**.
- **кава** (coffee) → Я люблю **каву**.
- **вода** (water) → Я люблю **воду**.
Masculine drinks like **сік** (juice) or **чай** (tea) do not change their endings!

_Приклади:_
- **Я люблю каву.** — [ja lʲu.ˈblʲu ˈka.ʋu] — I love coffee.
- **Ми не любимо сік.** — [mɪ nɛ ˈlʲu.bɪ.mɔ sʲik] — We do not love juice.
- **Він п'є тепле молоко.** — [ʋin pje ˈtɛ.plɛ mɔ.lɔ.ˈkɔ] — He drinks warm milk.

Let's look at a quick conversation you might have at a neighbor's house:
— **Ти любиш чай?** (Do you love tea?)
— **Ні, я люблю каву.** (No, I love coffee.)
— **Будь ласка, кава і цукор.** (Please, coffee and sugar.)
