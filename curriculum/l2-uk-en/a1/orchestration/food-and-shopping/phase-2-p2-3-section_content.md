## Практика: У магазині та культурні традиції

### Складання списку покупок
When you prepare to go to the store, it is helpful to have a **список покупок** (shopping list). In Ukrainian, we often think about what we have and what we need. When we talk about a specific quantity of an item, like a «кілограм» (kilogram) or a «пляшка» (bottle), we use the **Genitive case** for the food itself. This is because we are saying «a kilogram *of* apples» or «a bottle *of* water». For masculine and neuter nouns, the ending often changes to **-а** or **-я**, while feminine nouns change to **-и** or **-і**.

Let's look at how we build these phrases:
- **кілограм яблук** — [kiloˈɦram ˈjabluk] (a kilogram of apples)
- **пляшка води** — [ˈplʲaʃka woˈdɪ] (a bottle of water)
- **пачка цукру** — [ˈpatʃka ˈtsukru] (a pack of sugar)

Notice that for sugar, we use **цукру** — [ˈtsukru]. Using these correct endings helps you sound like a natural shopper!

**Приклади:**
- Я купую **кілограм яблук**. (I buy a kilogram of apples.)
- Ти купуєш **літр молока**. (You buy a liter of milk.)
- Ми маємо **пачку цукру**. (We have a pack of sugar.)

### Діалог: У продуктовому магазині
When you are finally at the store, you might need to speak with the **продавець** (shopkeeper). Standard phrases make the process smooth and polite. You can start by asking for what you need or asking about the price. Remember, in Ukraine, we pay in **гривні** — [ˈɦrɪwnʲi] (hryvnias).

Here is a simple dialogue you might hear at a local shop:
- **Покупець:** Добрий день! Скільки коштує це молоко? (Buyer: Good day! How much does this milk cost?)
- **Продавець:** Добрий день! Воно коштує тридцять гривень. (Seller: Good day! It costs thirty hryvnias.)
- **Покупець:** Добре. Я купую пляшку молока і батон хліба. (Buyer: Good. I am buying a bottle of milk and a loaf of bread.)
- **Продавець:** Будь ласка. Це сорок гривень. (Seller: Here you go. That is forty hryvnias.)

Using these phrases helps you practice both your numbers and your new food vocabulary in a real-world setting.

[!tip]
In Ukrainian shops, it is common to say «Будь ласка» — [budʲ ˈlaska] when giving money or receiving a product. It translates to «Please», but in this context, it works like «Here you go».

### Українські традиції: Хліб на столі
In Ukraine, **хліб** — [xlʲib] (bread) is much more than just food. It is a symbol of life, hospitality, and home. There is an old saying: «Хліб — усьому голова» (Bread is the head of everything), meaning it is the most important thing on the table. When guests arrive, they are traditionally greeted with «хліб і сіль» (bread and salt) on a beautiful embroidered towel called a «рушник».

Different types of bread are used for different occasions:
- **Батон** — [baˈtɔn]: A white, oblong loaf common for daily sandwiches.
- **Паляниця** — [palʲɐˈnɪtsʲɐ]: A traditional round hearth bread, now a famous symbol of Ukrainian identity.
- **Чорний хліб** — [ˈtʃɔrnɪj xlʲib]: Dark rye bread, perfect for eating with a bowl of hot borscht.

[!culture]
Ukrainian borscht is never complete without bread. Most people eat it with rye bread and sometimes «пампушки» — [pamˈpuʃkɪ], which are small garlic rolls. This combination is a true taste of Ukraine!

### Готуємо вечерю
Now that we have our ingredients, what can we make? A typical Ukrainian dinner is simple but hearty. We use **картопля** — [karˈtɔplʲa] (potatoes) as a base for many dishes. You might buy a **кілограм картоплі** (a kilogram of potatoes) and some **м'ясо** — [ˈmjasɔ] (meat) to make a delicious roast.

Think about what is in your bag:
- **сир** — [sɪr] (cheese)
- **яйце** — [jajˈtsɛ] (egg)
- **курка** — [ˈkurka] (chicken)

With these items, you can prepare a wonderful meal for your family or friends. Learning these words allows you to follow simple recipes and understand what goes into famous dishes like «вареники» — [waˈrɛnɪkɪ].

**Приклади:**
- Мама готує **м'ясо**. (Mom is cooking meat.)
- Тато купує **сир**. (Dad is buying cheese.)
- Я люблю **свіжі яйця**. (I love fresh eggs.)
