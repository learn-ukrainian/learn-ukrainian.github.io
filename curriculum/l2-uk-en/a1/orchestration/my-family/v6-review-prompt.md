## Діалоги (Dialogues)

Your phone buzzes. A friend wants to see photos of your family. How do you talk about the people in those pictures? These three dialogues show you how Ukrainians introduce their families — from a simple question about siblings to a full family introduction.

### Dialogue 1 — Brothers and sisters

<div class="dialogue">


**Оксана:** У тебе є брати чи сестри? *(Do you have brothers or sisters?)*


**Андрій:** Так, у мене є два брати. *(Yes, I have two brothers.)*


**Андрій:** І одна сестра. *(And one sister.)*


**Оксана:** Ого! У мене тільки один брат. *(Wow! I only have one brother.)*


**Оксана:** Як його звати? *(What's his name?)*


**Андрій:** Коля. *(Kolya.)*


</div>

Notice how Оксана asks **«У тебе є брати чи сестри?»** — this is the standard way to ask "Do you have brothers or sisters?" The word **чи** (or) connects the two options. Андрій answers with **«Так, у мене є...»** (Yes, I have...). When Оксана asks about names, she uses the familiar phrase **«Як його звати?»** (What's his name?) — you already know **«Як тебе звати?»** from Module 5, and **його** (his) simply replaces **тебе** here.

### Dialogue 2 — A family photo

<div class="dialogue">


**Марина:** Це моя сім'я на фотографії. *(This is my family in the photo.)*


**Даниїл:** Класно! Хто це? *(Cool! Who is this?)*


**Марина:** Це моя мама Оксана. *(This is my mom Oksana.)*


**Марина:** Це мій тато Євген. *(This is my dad Yevhen.)*


**Марина:** Це моя сестра Катя. *(This is my sister Katya.)*


**Марина:** І мої брати — Іван і Денис. *(And my brothers — Ivan and Denys.)*


**Даниїл:** А це твоя бабуся? *(And is this your grandmother?)*


**Марина:** Так, її звати Тетяна. *(Yes, her name is Tetiana.)*


</div>

Here you see the pattern **Це + possessive + family member**: **«Це моя мама»**, **«Це мій тато»**, **«Це моя сестра»**. Марина points at each person and names them. Notice how **моя** appears with **мама** and **сестра** (both feminine), while **мій** appears with **тато** (masculine). When there are multiple people, Марина switches to **мої** (my, plural): **«мої брати»**. Даниїл asks **«Це твоя бабуся?»** using **твоя** (your, feminine) — the informal form for someone you know well.

### Dialogue 3 — Introducing yourself and your family

<div class="dialogue">


**Тарас:** Привіт! Мене звати Тарас. *(Hi! My name is Taras.)*


**Тарас:** Моя мама — вчителька. *(My mom is a teacher.)*


**Тарас:** Мій тато — інженер. *(My dad is an engineer.)*


**Тарас:** У мене є один брат. *(I have one brother.)*


**Тарас:** Його звати Олег. *(His name is Oleh.)*


</div>

This is a connected self-introduction — the kind you might give when meeting someone new. Тарас combines everything from Modules 1–5 (greetings, his name) with new family vocabulary. By the end of this module, you will be able to give a similar introduction about your own family.

## Сім'я (Family Vocabulary)

Ukrainian has two words for "family": **сім'я** and **родина**. Both are common, and Ukrainians use them interchangeably in everyday speech. Notice the apostrophe in **сім'я** — you first saw this special mark in earlier modules. It separates the consonant from the soft vowel that follows.

Here are the core family members every beginner needs:

| Ukrainian | English |
|-----------|---------|
| **мама** / **мати** | mother |
| **тато** / **батько** | father |
| **брат** | brother |
| **сестра** | sister |
| **син** | son |
| **дочка** / **донька** | daughter |

Everyday speech favors **мама** and **тато** — warm, familiar words. The forms **мати** and **батько** are more formal or literary. The plural **батьки** (parents) comes from **батько**. For extended family:

| Ukrainian | English |
|-----------|---------|
| **бабуся** / **баба** | grandmother |
| **дідусь** / **дід** | grandfather |
| **тітка** | aunt |
| **дядько** | uncle |
| **дружина** | wife |
| **чоловік** | husband (also: man) |

:::note
Ukrainian has no single word for "grandparents." You always say **бабуся і дідусь** — both names together.
:::

## У мене є (I have)

English says "I have a brother." Ukrainian thinks about this completely differently. There is no verb "to have" in this construction. Instead, Ukrainian says "At me there-is a brother" — **«У мене є брат»**. The thing you possess simply *exists near you*.

For now, you need three forms of this pattern:

| Ukrainian | English | When to use |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **У мене є** | I have | talking about yourself |
| **У тебе є** | You have (informal) | asking a friend |
| **У вас є** | You have (formal) | asking politely |

To ask a question, simply raise your intonation at the end: **«У тебе є сестра?»** ↗ — "Do you have a sister?" No extra words needed, no word order change. Just your voice going up.

How do you answer? **«Так, у мене є сестра.»** (Yes, I have a sister.) For "no," keep it simple at this stage: **«Ні.»** or **«Ні, у мене тільки один брат.»** (No, I only have one brother.) The word **тільки** (only) is very useful here.

### Numbers preview: один/одна, два/дві

When counting family members, Ukrainian numbers change by gender. **Один** (one) is masculine: **«один брат»**. **Одна** (one) is feminine: **«одна сестра»**. The same split happens with "two": **два брати** (two brothers, masculine), **дві сестри** (two sisters, feminine). You do not need to memorize rules — just notice the pattern in the dialogues above and let it become natural.

## Мій, моя, моє (Possessive Pronouns)

When Марина says **«Це мій тато»** and then **«Це моя мама»**, why does "my" change? Because Ukrainian possessive pronouns match the gender of the thing you possess — not your own gender. It does not matter if you are male or female. What matters is the noun that follows.

| Possessive | Gender | Example |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **мій** | masculine | **мій брат** (my brother) |
| **моя** | feminine | **моя сестра** (my sister) |
| **моє** | neuter | **моє місто** (my city) |
| **мої** | plural | **мої батьки** (my parents) |

The same pattern applies to "your" (informal): **твій** (m), **твоя** (f), **твоє** (n), **твої** (pl). So **«Це твій тато?»** (Is this your dad?) uses **твій** because **тато** is masculine, while **«Це твоя мама?»** uses **твоя** because **мама** is feminine.

Two possessives are easier — they never change form. **Його** (his) stays **його** regardless of the noun: **його брат**, **його сестра**, **його місто**. The same goes for **її** (her): **її тато**, **її мама**, **її місто**. These are invariable — one form for all genders.

:::tip
How to choose the right possessive: look at the noun AFTER it. Masculine noun → **мій/твій**. Feminine noun → **моя/твоя**. Neuter noun → **моє/твоє**. Plural → **мої/твої**.
:::

## Підсумок — Summary

You can now talk about your family in Ukrainian. Test yourself with these questions:

**Name five family members in Ukrainian.** Can you say мама, тато, брат, сестра, бабуся without hesitation? Add дідусь, дядько, тітка if you can.

**Say "I have a sister" in Ukrainian.** Remember — Ukrainian does not use a verb for "have." The answer is **«У мене є сестра.»**

**What is the difference between мій and моя?** Both mean "my," but **мій** goes with masculine nouns (**мій брат**) and **моя** goes with feminine nouns (**моя сестра**).

Now try this: introduce your family in four or five sentences. Use the pattern from Dialogue 3 as your model:

> **Мене звати ___. Моя мама — ___. Мій тато — ___. У мене є ___.**

Say it out loud. Do not translate from English — start from the Ukrainian patterns you have learned. Picture the person, then say the Ukrainian sentence. **«Це моя мама. Це мій тато. У мене є брат.»** This is how you begin to think in Ukrainian — situation first, Ukrainian words next, no English in between.

2. **Surzhyk** — mixed Russian-Ukrainian forms (шо→що, чо→чому, тіпа→типу)
3. **Calques** — literal translations from Russian that sound wrong in Ukrainian (приймати душ→брати душ, приймати рішення→ухвалювати рішення)
4. **Paronyms** — similar-sounding words with different meanings used incorrectly (тактична≠тактовна, ефектний≠ефективний)

**Also check:**
- Russian characters (ы, э, ё, ъ) — these must NEVER appear
- Incorrect gender assignment or case endings
