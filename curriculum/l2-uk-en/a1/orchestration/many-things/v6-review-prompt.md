## Діалоги (Dialogues)

When you first begin learning any new language, your initial vocabulary is often limited to single, isolated items. You learn the word for a single apple, a single cup, or a single book. However, reality is rarely so isolated. We live in a world of collections, groups, and multiples. To truly describe your daily life and environment, you must be able to count and group objects. You need to talk about your keys, your shoes, your notebooks, and your friends.

This brings us to a fundamental grammatical concept. In Ukrainian grammar, we call the singular form **однина** (singular). We call the plural form **множина** (plural). As Ukrainian textbooks for young learners state: **один предмет → багато предметів** (one object → many objects). When we change a word from singular to plural in Ukrainian, the ending of the word changes to reflect this new quantity.

Imagine you are walking into a school for the first time. The room is full of furniture. Read the following dialogue between a teacher and a student. Notice exactly how the words for the objects change when they refer to more than one item. Pay close attention to the endings.

## Один → багато (Singular → Plural)

Ukrainian creates plural nouns systematically. In English, you typically just add an "s" to the end of a word. In Ukrainian, the process is slightly more complex but highly logical. It depends heavily on the gender of the noun.

There are three main plural patterns for the nominative case. The nominative case is the dictionary form of a word. It is used when the objects are the main subject of your sentence, like when you are simply naming what you see.

### Masculine Nouns

For masculine words, the most common plural ending is the letter **и**. However, some words take the softer letter **і**. Take a look at some everyday examples you already know.
- **стіл** (table) → **столи** (tables)
- **зошит** (notebook) → **зошити** (notebooks)
- **телефон** (phone) → **телефони** (phones)

Sometimes, a vowel inside the word simply drops out when we make it plural. You saw this in the word for chair. The singular is **стілець** (chair), but the plural is **стільці** (chairs). The letter **е** disappears entirely. This happens to make the word easier and faster to pronounce. It is a very common phonetic shift in Ukrainian vocabulary.

### Feminine Nouns

Feminine nouns follow a very similar pattern to masculine nouns. Most feminine words end in the vowel **-а** in the singular form. To form the plural, you remove that **-а** and replace it with **-и**.
- **лампа** (lamp) → **лампи** (lamps)
- **сумка** (bag) → **сумки** (bags)
- **книга** (book) → **книги** (books)
- **ручка** (pen) → **ручки** (pens)

Notice that the word **річ** (thing) does not end in **-а**. It is a feminine word, but it ends in a consonant. Its plural takes the soft vowel **-і** to become **речі** (things). You will encounter these unique words as you expand your vocabulary.

### Neuter Nouns

Neuter nouns behave entirely differently. Most neuter words end in the vowels **-о** or **-е** in the singular. To form the plural, you change the **-о** to an **-а**. This creates a very distinct sound difference.
- **вікно** (window) → **вікна** (windows)
- **дзеркало** (mirror) → **дзеркала** (mirrors)
- **крісло** (armchair) → **крісла** (armchairs)
- **ліжко** (bed) → **ліжка** (beds)

### A Helpful Guideline

You might wonder when to exactly use **-и** and when to use **-і**. There is a phonetic guideline that can help you. After the specific consonants **г**, **к**, and **х**, Ukrainian words almost always take the ending **-и**.
- **книга** (book) → **книги** (books)
- **ручка** (pen) → **ручки** (pens)

After most other consonants, the ending will be either **-и** or **-і** depending on whether the preceding consonant is hard or soft.
- **стіл** (table) → **столи** (tables)
- **стілець** (chair) → **стільці** (chairs)

For neuter words, the rule is straightforward for now: neuter words ending in **-о** change to **-а**. Neuter words ending in **-е** change to **-я**, which we will practice more in later modules as you learn more advanced vocabulary.

Full declension rules are complex, and exceptions always exist in natural languages. For now, the best strategy is to learn each plural form together with its noun. When you memorize a new word, memorize its plural form alongside it. Treat them as a pair.

## Прикметники у множині (Adjectives in Plural)

We use adjectives to describe the qualities, sizes, and colors of objects. When an object becomes plural, the adjective describing it must also become plural. This grammatical matching is called adjective agreement. The adjective must agree with the noun it describes.

In singular, you learned that adjectives change their endings based on the gender of the noun. You had to choose carefully:
- **який?** (what kind? — masculine) → **веселий** (cheerful)
- **яка?** (what kind? — feminine) → **весела** (cheerful)
- **яке?** (what kind? — neuter) → **веселе** (cheerful)

In the plural form, adjectives become much simpler. In the plural form, ALL adjectives take the exact same ending: **-і**. This happens regardless of the noun's original gender! You do not need to worry if the plural objects were originally masculine, feminine, or neuter. They all share one common, unifying adjective ending.

The plural question word is **які?** (what kind?).
- **які?** (what kind? — plural) → **веселі** (cheerful)

This universal rule works consistently across different genders. Notice how the ending remains identical.
- **великий стіл** (big table) → **великі столи** (big tables)
- **нова книга** (new book) → **нові книги** (new books)
- **чисте вікно** (clean window) → **чисті вікна** (clean windows)

The ending is consistently **-і**. This makes describing groups of objects straightforward. You only have to remember one sound.

### Colors in Plural

You can easily apply the colors you learned previously to plural nouns. The same rule applies to all descriptive adjectives, including colors. If you want to describe the colors of several items at once, simply use the **-і** ending. Review these colors and apply them to plural nouns.
- **червоні ручки** (red pens)
- **сині зошити** (blue notebooks)
- **білі стіни** (white walls)
- **чорні стільці** (black chairs)

Whether you are pointing at masculine chairs or feminine walls, the color adjective ends in **-і**. It is a beautifully consistent system.

### Demonstratives and Possessives

We also use pointer words, called demonstratives, to indicate specific objects in space. In the singular, you learned **цей** (this) and **той** (that). These words also have dedicated plural forms that you must use when pointing at multiple things.

You use **ці** (these) for objects that are near you, right at your fingertips. You use **ті** (those) for objects that are farther away, across the room.
- **Ці столи великі.** (These tables are big.)
- **Ці книги нові.** (These books are new.)
- **Ті вікна чисті.** (Those windows are clean.)
- **Ті стільці старі.** (Those chairs are old.)

Possessive words follow the exact same logic. If you want to say that multiple things belong to you, use the plural possessive form **мої** (my).
- **мої зошити** (my notebooks)
- **мої речі** (my things)
- **мої сумки** (my bags)

Whenever you have a plural noun, ensure that every single word describing it or pointing to it matches in the plural form. The adjective takes **-і**, the demonstrative is **ці** or **ті**, and the possessive is **мої**. They all link together in a chain of agreement.

## Підсумок — Summary

You now possess the grammatical tools to describe your environment in much richer, more accurate detail. Moving from one object to many objects unlocks a vast new range of expression and allows you to talk about the real world as it is. The core patterns for creating plurals allow you to describe any space accurately.

First, nouns change their endings to indicate plurality. The exact transformation depends on the noun's gender and ending, but you will quickly recognize the common patterns:
- Masculine and feminine nouns typically end in **-и** or **-і** (for example: **столи**, **книги**, **ручки**, **стільці**).
- Neuter nouns typically end in **-а** or **-я** (for example: **вікна**, **дзеркала**).

Second, adjectives are beautifully simple in the plural. They completely ignore gender boundaries. Every single adjective takes the exact same ending **-і**:
- **великі** (big)
- **нові** (new)
- **червоні** (red)
- **сині** (blue)

Finally, always remember your plural pointers and possessives:
- **ці** (these)
- **ті** (those)
- **мої** (my)

You can combine all these grammatical elements to build highly descriptive, accurate sentences. For example, you can say:
- **Ці нові книги — мої.** (These new books are mine.)
- **Ті великі столи — чисті.** (Those big tables are clean.)

Practice these patterns until they feel completely natural. Understanding plurals is a major step toward real fluency. Keep identifying objects and describing their colors and sizes. The more you observe your world in Ukrainian, the faster you will learn to think in the language.

| 4 | **Vocabulary coverage** | 10% | All required vocab from plan used naturally in prose? Recommended vocab included? New words introduced in context, not as lists? |
| 5 | **Exercise quality** | 15% | Placeholders specific enough? Test the right skills? Placed after relevant teaching? Match plan's activity_hints? Sufficient items? |
| 6 | **Engagement & tone** | 10% | Interesting for teens/adults? Authoritative but warm (like a skilled teacher)? No LLM filler ("Good news!", "Don't panic!", "Fun fact!")? Cultural hooks? |
| 7 | **Structural integrity** | 5% | All H2 headings from plan present? Word count in range? No duplicate sections? No meta-commentary? Clean markdown? |
| 8 | **Cultural accuracy** | 5% | Decolonized (Ukrainian on its own terms, never "like Russian but...")? Factually correct claims about Ukrainian? Respectful representation? |
| 9 | **Dialogue & conversation quality** | 10% | Dialogues natural and culturally appropriate? Real situations, real responses? Speaker roles clear? Not stilted or textbook-robotic? |

**Note:** Vocabulary tables (словник), video embeds, and external resource links are added by a downstream ENRICH step — do NOT penalize their absence or quality. If you see a vocabulary table with wrong translations, missing words, or formatting issues, that content was NOT written by the writer — it was generated deterministically by the ENRICH step. Do NOT flag словník problems as linguistic errors or deduct points from the writer. Report them separately under "ENRICH issues" in your findings (informational only, not scored).

### Step 4: Output raw scores

Output ONLY the raw 1-10 scores in the table below. Do NOT calculate the weighted total — the pipeline script handles the math deterministically. Just write your per-dimension scores.

### Step 5: List findings

For every issue found, provide:
```
[DIMENSION] [SEVERITY: critical/major/minor]

```

Critical = module cannot ship. Major = quality below standard. Minor = polish item.

### Step 6: Verdict

Base your verdict on the **severity of findings** — the pipeline calculates the weighted score separately.

| Verdict | Condition |
|---------|-----------|
| **PASS** | Zero critical findings, at most minor issues |
| **REVISE** | Has major findings but no criticals — fixable without rewrite |
| **REJECT** | Has any critical finding — fundamental problems requiring rewrite |

### Step 7: Fix it yourself (REVISE only)

If your verdict is **REVISE**, you MUST output a `<fixes>` block with exact find/replace pairs. The pipeline applies these deterministically — no LLM regeneration, no rewriting from scratch.

Rules for fixes:
- Each fix is a YAML entry with `find:` (exact text from the module) and `replace:` (corrected text)
- The `find` string must be an EXACT substring of the module content — copy-paste it
- Keep fixes minimal — change only what's wrong, preserve surrounding text
- Do NOT fix словник/vocabulary tables (those are generated by a downstream tool)
- For word count issues: add content as a new `insert_after:` entry instead of find/replace
