## Вступ — Introduction

Welcome to the beginning of your Ukrainian journey! I am excited to guide you through this first crucial step. The Ukrainian language uses the Cyrillic script, which might look a bit unfamiliar at first glance, but do not worry! It is an incredibly logical and beautiful system. The Cyrillic alphabet descended from the Greek alphabet via the First Bulgarian Empire.

> [!culture] The Origins of Cyrillic
> The Cyrillic script was created by students of Saints Cyril and Methodius in the 9th century. It is NOT derived from Latin — it descends from the Greek alphabet. Ukrainian Cyrillic has its own unique letters like Ґ, Є, Ї, and І that reflect sounds specific to the Ukrainian language!

There are exactly 33 letters in the Ukrainian alphabet. What makes this so exciting for you as a learner? It is highly phonetic! This means that each letter usually maps to one single sound. Think about English, where a combination like «ough» can sound five different ways. You will not face that struggle here. You do not have to memorize all 33 letters today. We will master each group together step by step. Here is the full 33-letter alphabet chart:

## Букви і звуки — Letters and Sounds

As we start looking at the alphabet, we need to understand a very important distinction: the difference between letters (**бу́кви**) and sounds (**зву́ки**). Letters are the written symbols you see on the page or screen. Sounds are what you actually hear and pronounce when you speak. They are connected, but they are not the exact same thing. In Ukrainian, there are 38 distinct phonemes (the sounds), but we only use 33 letters to represent them.

The key insight that will make your learning process so much smoother is this: Ukrainian spelling is incredibly phonetic. In almost every case, one letter represents exactly one sound. This is a huge advantage for you! It makes reading Ukrainian FAR easier than reading English. Once you learn the 33 letters and the sounds they make, you will be able to accurately sound out almost any word you encounter, even if you do not know what it means yet!

However, there are a few special cases to keep in mind for later. Some letters do double duty. For example, the iotated vowels (Я, Ю, Є, Ї) can sometimes represent two sounds at once depending on their position in a word. Also, there is a special character called the soft sign (Ь), which acts as a modifier and has no sound of its own; it simply softens the consonant right before it. Do not worry about these exceptions right now! We will cover those details in later modules.

## Голосні та приголосні — Vowels and Consonants

To master the alphabet, it helps to categorize the letters. In Ukrainian, just like in English, we divide letters into vowels (**голосні́**) and consonants (**приголосні́**). Let's break down what this means.

There are 10 vowel letters in total. These are categorized into 6 base vowels (А, О, У, Е, И, І) and 4 iotated vowels (Я, Ю, Є, Ї). Vowels are produced using voice only, with absolutely no obstruction of the airflow from your lips, tongue, or teeth.

> [!tip] The Syllable Rule
> Here is a golden rule for your pronunciation practice: every single Ukrainian syllable has exactly one vowel. If you see a word with three vowels, like **молоко́**, you instantly know it has three syllables: мо-ло-ко!

On the other hand, there are 22 consonant letters, plus the soft sign modifier (Ь). When you pronounce consonants, the airflow is obstructed in some way—perhaps by your lips, your tongue touching your palate, or your teeth coming together.

Here is your preview chart organized by category. Do not try to memorize everything today; this is just a map of where we are going:

| Category | Letters |
|---|---|
| Голосні (Base) | А, О, У, Е, И, І |
| Голосні (Iotated) | Я, Ю, Є, Ї |
| Приголосні | Б, В, Г, Ґ, Д, Ж, З, Й, К, Л, М, Н, П, Р, С, Т, Ф, Х, Ц, Ч, Ш, Щ |
| Modifier | Ь |

We will master the vowels in Module 2, the consonants in Module 3, and the special signs in Module 4.

> [!culture] Молоко́ — Three Syllables, Three Vowels
> The word **молоко́** (milk) is a perfect example of the syllable rule in action. With three vowels (о-о-о), you get three syllables: мо-ло-ко. Milk products are central to Ukrainian cuisine — from **сметана** (sour cream) to **сир** (cottage cheese) to **масло** (butter). You will learn to read all of these!

> [!did-you-know] Phonetic Advantage
> Unlike English, where «c» can sound like «k» (cat) or «s» (city), each Ukrainian letter has ONE consistent sound. Once you learn these 10 letters, you will read them the same way every single time — no guessing!

> [!tip] Shape Mnemonics
> To remember the vowels, notice that **А**, **О**, and **У** look similar to English letters (but У sounds like «oo»!). For **І**, think of the English letter I — they even sound similar. The consonants **М**, **К**, **Т** also look like their English counterparts, making them easy first friends.

> [!warning] False Friends
> Watch out for letters that look like English but sound completely different! **Н** is the sound /n/ (not H!). Meanwhile, **С** is always /s/ (never C or K). And **У** sounds like /oo/ (not Y!). Do not let your English habits trick you!

| Ukrainian Letter | Looks Like | Actually Sounds Like |
|---|---|---|
| Н | English H | English N |
| С | English C | English S |
| У | English Y | "oo" as in moon |

> [!culture] Мак — The Ukrainian Poppy
> The poppy flower (**мак**) holds a special place in Ukrainian culture. It appears in traditional embroidery (**вишивка**), folk songs, and is a symbol of remembrance. You will see poppies woven into Ukrainian wreaths (**вінок**) worn during holidays.

Now for the exciting part: reading! Let's do a detailed phonetic walkthrough. Take the letter М and the letter А. When you blend them, you get М+А→МА. Now put two of those together: МА+МА→**ма́ма** (mom). You just read your first word! Let's try another: К+І→**кі**, add Т, К+І+Т→**кіт** (cat). You build from letters to syllables, and then to words.

Using ONLY these 10 letters, you can already decode all of these vocabulary words: **ма́ма** (mom), **та́то** (dad), **кіт** (cat), **молоко́** (milk), **ма́сло** (butter), **о́ко** (eye), **ніс** (nose), **мі́сто** (city), **ліс** (forest), **сон** (dream), **мак** (poppy), **сік** (juice), **са́ло** (lard), **стіл** (table), **тут** (here), **там** (there), **сом** (catfish), and **кіно́** (cinema). ## Перші слова — First Words in Context

Now that you can decode words, let's put them into context. To build our first sentences, I will introduce three very helpful sight words. Sight words contain letters we haven't learned yet, so just recognize them as whole shapes for now: **приві́т** (hello), **дя́кую** (thank you), and **це** (this is). Additionally, you can already fully decode our survival words for yes and no: **так** (yes) and **ні** (no).

Let's practice reading some short sentences mixing our decodable words and these new sight words. Take your time, sound out the letters, and remember that you are doing great!

*   **Ма́ма** тут.
*   **Кіт** там.
*   **Це** **молоко́**.
*   **Це** **ма́сло**.
*   **Це** **мі́сто**.
*   **Приві́т**! **Це** **та́то**.

Now let's look at some micro-dialogues. In Ukrainian, you can turn a statement into a question simply by changing the intonation of your voice—no extra grammar required right now!

> **(Вдома / At home)**
> — **Це** **кіт**?
> — **Так**, **це** **кіт**.
>
> **(На вулиці / Outside)**
> — **Це** **мі́сто**?
> — **Ні**, **це** **ліс**.
>
> **(За столом / At the table)**
> — **Це** **молоко́**?
> — **Так**, **дя́кую**.

You are officially reading and understanding Ukrainian in context. Keep practicing these small sentences until the letters feel familiar. Take it step by step!

> [!culture] Your First Ukrainian Saying
> **Око за око** — «An eye for an eye.» You can already read **око** using your 10 letters! The word **за** introduces one new letter (З) — you will learn it soon. Ukrainian has hundreds of vivid proverbs (**прислів'я**), and you will discover more as your alphabet grows.

> [!tip] Practice Strategy
> Try covering the English translation and reading the Ukrainian first. If you can sound out the word before peeking at the meaning, you are building real reading skills!

> [!fun-fact] Сало — A National Treasure
> The word **са́ло** (lard) is one of the most iconic Ukrainian foods. Ukrainians have dozens of ways to prepare it, and it has been a staple of the diet for centuries. You just read it using only your 10 letters!
