# Tomorrow - Future Tense

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Talking about the past helps us share stories, but talking about the future lets us build our lives together. When you can express your plans, hopes, and intentions, you open the door to real connection with others. Knowing how to use the future tense is your key to making arrangements and looking forward to what comes next.

## Вступ (Introduction)

We have already explored how to talk about the things we did in the past. We learned how to say what happened yesterday, last week, or a long time ago. The timeline of our lives moves forward, and it is equally important to discuss where we are going. Today, we shift our focus from the past to the future.

We all have plans. Sometimes our plans are very concrete, and sometimes they are just dreams or intentions. Think about your timeline. Right now, you are reading this text. Yesterday, you perhaps worked or rested. But what happens next? Where will you be tomorrow? What will you do? 

In Ukrainian culture, looking toward the future often carries a sense of hope and resilience. A very common phrase you might hear is «Завтра буде новий день». This translates to "Tomorrow will be a new day." It is a gentle reminder that no matter what challenges we face today, tomorrow brings new opportunities and a fresh start. 

> [!culture]
> When someone has a difficult day, friends often say «Завтра буде новий день» to offer comfort. It shows optimism and a forward-looking mindset.

To express these hopes and plans, we need the right tools. We will learn a simple and highly effective way to build future tense sentences. This method uses a special helper word combined with the basic dictionary form of the verbs you already know. You will soon see that building future actions is actually quite logical and straightforward. 

Let's prepare to talk about your next steps. We can start by looking at the grammar formula that makes this possible. 

## Презентація граматики: Буду + Інфінітив (Grammar: Буду + Infinitive)

To talk about actions that will happen in the future, we use a compound structure. This means we combine two words to create one meaning. The formula is very simple: we take a form of the verb «бути» (to be) and add an infinitive. An infinitive is the basic dictionary form of a verb, which usually ends in -ти.

First, we need to know how to change the verb «бути» for the future. It works as a helper verb, very much like "will" in English. 

Here is the complete conjugation table for our helper verb:

| Subject | Verb Form | Example | Meaning |
|---------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Я | **бу́ду** | Я бу́ду працюва́ти. | I will work. |
| Ти | **бу́деш** | Ти бу́деш чита́ти. | You will read. |
| Він/Вона/Воно | **бу́де** | Він бу́де спа́ти. | He will sleep. |
| Ми | **бу́демо** | Ми бу́демо ї́сти. | We will eat. |
| Ви | **бу́дете** | Ви бу́дете слу́хати. | You will listen. |
| Вони | **бу́дуть** | Вони бу́дуть зна́ти. | They will know. |

You simply choose the correct form of the helper verb and attach the action you plan to do. «Я бу́ду» means "I will," and «працюва́ти» means "to work." Together, «Я бу́ду працюва́ти» means "I will work."

> [!warning]
> **Learner Error:** There is a strict rule about which verbs can follow the «бу́ду» forms. The helper verb «бу́ду» only ever pairs with imperfective verbs. These are the regular verbs you have been learning so far, which describe ongoing or repeated actions. 
> 
> You must say «Я бу́ду чита́ти» (I will read). You must never use a perfective verb here. Saying something like «Я бу́ду прочита́ти» is always incorrect. The helper «бу́ду» demands an ongoing action verb.

We can easily make these sentences negative by adding «не» before the helper verb.

* Я не бу́ду працюва́ти. (I will not work.)
* Ми не бу́демо спа́ти. (We will not sleep.)

Asking a question is just as easy. We simply change our voice tone or put the question word at the beginning.

* Що ти бу́деш роби́ти? (What will you do?)
* Де ви бу́дете жи́ти? (Where will you live?)

> [!tip]
> **FYI: The Synthetic Future**
> While reading Ukrainian books or news, you might see words like «працюва́тиму» or «чита́тимеш». This is another way to form the future tense. It merges the verb and the helper into one single word. For your own speaking, using «бу́ду працюва́ти» is perfectly natural and correct. You only need to recognize these single-word forms when you see them. 

## Лексика та культурний контекст: Плани на майбутнє (Vocabulary and Plans)

Now that we know how to form the verbs, we need time words to say exactly when these actions will happen. Time markers give our sentences clear context.

The most common future marker is **за́втра** (tomorrow). We often combine it with times of the day to be more specific.

* **за́втра вра́нці** (tomorrow morning)
* **за́втра вде́нь** (tomorrow during the day)
* **за́втра вве́чері** (tomorrow evening)

If something happens the day after tomorrow, we use the word **післяза́втра**. If an event is approaching, we use **ско́ро** (soon). We can emphasize this by saying **ду́же ско́ро** (very soon) or asking **як ско́ро?** (how soon?).

When we want to say "next week" or "next year," Ukrainian grammar requires a small adjustment. We cannot just translate the words directly into their basic forms. We must use the Genitive case to show the time when something occurs.

* **насту́пного ти́жня** (next week)
* **насту́пного ро́ку** (next year)
* **насту́пного ра́зу** (next time)

> [!warning]
> **Learner Error:** English speakers often try to use the basic Nominative case for these phrases. Never say «насту́пний ти́ждень» when you mean "I will do it next week." Always use the Genitive forms «насту́пного ти́жня» and «насту́пного ро́ку» to indicate the time of an action.

Sometimes we know exactly what will happen, and sometimes we only have a desire. It is important to distinguish between definite plans and intentions. 

When we are certain about an action, we use our new future tense: «Я бу́ду працюва́ти» (I will work). This shows a solid commitment. 

However, if we are just expressing a desire, we use the verb **хоті́ти** (to want). We use it with an infinitive.
* Я хо́чу зна́ти. (I want to know.)
* Я ду́же хо́чу спа́ти. (I really want to sleep.)

If we have an intention but it is not a fixed arrangement yet, we use the verb **збира́тися** (to be going to).
* Я збира́юся ї́хати. (I am going to travel.)
* Ми збира́ємося вчи́ти. (We are going to learn.)

> [!culture]
> Ukrainians can be quite cautious when talking about future plans. A famous proverb says: «Не кажи "гоп", по́ки не переско́чиш». This literally means "Don't say 'hop' until you've jumped over." It is a reminder not to celebrate or declare your plans as a guaranteed success before they actually happen. Because of this, people often prefer to say «Я збира́юся...» rather than declaring «Я бу́ду...» for things that are uncertain.

## Практика та підсумок (Practice and Summary)

Let's look at how people talk about their plans in everyday situations. When we organize our time, we often use words like **план** (plan) and the phrase **ма́ти пла́ни** (to have plans). We can also **склада́ти план** (to make a plan) or discuss our **пла́ни на майбу́тнє** (plans for the future). We use the word **по́тім** (then, later) to show the sequence of events.

Consider this small exchange about the weekend:

— Які́ ти ма́єш пла́ни на насту́пні вихідні́? (What plans do you have for the next weekend?)
— Я збира́юся ї́хати в го́ри, а по́тім бу́ду відпочива́ти. (I am going to travel to the mountains, and then I will rest.)

Or a conversation about an evening:

— Що ти бу́деш роби́ти за́втра вве́чері? (What will you do tomorrow evening?)
— Я бу́ду чита́ти нову́ кни́гу. (I will read a new book.)

Imagine an event planner organizing a work week. They need to coordinate schedules clearly. They might say:

Я склада́ю план насту́пного ти́жня. За́втра вра́нці ми бу́демо працюва́ти. По́тім ми бу́демо обі́дати. Післяза́втра вони́ бу́дуть слу́хати презента́цію. Насту́пного ро́ку ми збира́ємося ма́ти нови́й прое́кт.

Notice how the planner uses the correct forms of «бу́ду», the Genitive time marker «насту́пного ти́жня», and expresses intentions with «збира́ємося».

We can express our own schedules smoothly when we combine these elements. Our sentences become richer and more informative.
