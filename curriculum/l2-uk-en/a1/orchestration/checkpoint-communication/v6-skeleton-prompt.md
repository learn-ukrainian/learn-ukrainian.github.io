# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: Checkpoint Communication



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

Checkpoint communication modules serve as cumulative reviews where learners integrate previously learned grammar and vocabulary into practical, goal-oriented dialogues. The pedagogical approach, as seen in Ukrainian educational materials, is heavily context-driven and functional. Dialogues are not abstract grammar drills; they model real-life situations like making plans, inviting friends, and arranging meetings (Source 1, 2, 10).

The core method is built around listening to or reading a model dialogue, then breaking it down into functional chunks. For instance, the Ukrainian Lessons Podcast (ULP) consistently presents a full dialogue first, asks comprehension questions, and only then dissects the grammar and vocabulary (Source 1, 10, 18). This "whole-to-part" approach mirrors natural language acquisition.

Ukrainian textbooks for native speakers also emphasize dialogue as a central activity. They use dialogues to introduce new concepts and then immediately ask students to analyze, transform, or role-play them (Source 7, 12, 31). The goal is active use, not passive knowledge. Exercises often involve transforming a dialogue into a monologue, which forces the learner to process and re-synthesize the information, confirming comprehension (Source 6, 12).

Greetings and politeness are foundational. Materials for both native speakers and L2 learners introduce the distinction between formal and informal address early on, using vocative case for names (`–Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В, –Ю–ї—О`) and polite forms for strangers or elders (`–®–∞–љ–Њ–≤–љ–Є–є –°–µ—А–≥—Ц—О –Т–∞—Б–Є–ї—М–Њ–≤–Є—З—Г`) (Source 3, 7, 27). This isn't just a grammar point; it's a core element of communicative competence in Ukrainian culture.

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

A logical sequence for an A1 learner to acquire checkpoint communication skills should build from simple exchanges to more complex negotiations.

1.  **Step 1: Basic Greetings, Farewells, and Politeness Formulas.** This is the absolute foundation.
    *   **Informal:** `–Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В!` (Hello!), `–Я–∞-–њ–∞!` (Bye-bye!) (Source 1).
    *   **Formal/Neutral:** `–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М!` (Good day!), `–Ф–Њ –њ–Њ–±–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П!` (Goodbye!) (Source 19, 27).
    *   **Core Politeness:** `–Ф—П–Ї—Г—О` (Thank you), `–С—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞` (Please/You're welcome) (Source 1, 19).

2.  **Step 2: Core Status Check Questions.** The most common conversational openers.
    *   `–ѓ–Ї —Б–њ—А–∞–≤–Є?` (How are things?), `–ѓ–Ї —В–Є?` (How are you?) (Source 1, 2).
    *   Common responses: `–Т—Б–µ –і–Њ–±—А–µ`, `–Э–Њ—А–Љ–∞–ї—М–љ–Њ`, `–І—Г–і–Њ–≤–Њ` (Source 1, 2, 8).

3.  **Step 3: Making Simple, Direct Invitations.** The focus is on a clear proposal.
    *   Use of imperative or suggestive forms: `–•–Њ–і—Ц–Љ–Њ –≤ –Ї—Ц–љ–Њ!` (Let's go to the cinema!) (Source 20).
    *   Direct question form: `–•–Њ—З–µ—И –њ—Ц—В–Є –Ј—Ц –Љ–љ–Њ—О?` (Do you want to go with me?) (Source 2).
    *   Initial responses: `–Ґ–∞–Ї, –і–∞–≤–∞–є` (Yes, let's), `–Ф–Њ–±—А–µ` (Okay) (Source 2).

4.  **Step 4: Negotiating Time and Place.** This is the "checkpoint" skill, requiring specific grammar.
    *   Asking about time: `–Ю –Ї–Њ—В—А—Ц–є –≥–Њ–і–Є–љ—Ц?` (At what time?) or `–Э–∞ –Ї–Њ—В—А—Г –≥–Њ–і–Є–љ—Г?` (For what time?) (Source 1, 10).
    *   Telling time using `–Њ` + ordinal number in the Locative case: `–Њ —В—А–µ—В—Ц–є` (at three), `–Њ —Б—М–Њ–Љ—Ц–є` (at seven) (Source 1, 10).
    *   Asking about place: `–Ф–µ –Ј—Г—Б—В—А—Ц–љ–µ–Љ–Њ—Б—М?` (Where will we meet?).
    *   Responding with `–≤/—Г` or `–љ–∞` + Locative case: `—Г –њ–∞—А–Ї—Г`, `–љ–∞ —Д–µ—Б—В–Є–≤–∞–ї—Ц` (Source 18).

5.  **Step 5: Simple Apologies and Explanations for Refusal.**
    *   Politely declining: `–Э–∞ –ґ–∞–ї—М, –љ–µ –Љ–Њ–ґ—Г` (Unfortunately, I can't) (Source 20).
    *   Giving a simple reason: `–Я–Њ–≥–∞–љ–Њ –њ–Њ—З—Г–≤–∞—О—Б—П` (I feel unwell), `–ѓ –Ј–∞–є–љ—П—В–∞` (I'm busy) (Source 2, 20).
    *   Simple apology: `–Т–Є–±–∞—З` (Sorry - informal), `–Я—А–Њ–±–∞—З` (Forgive me) (Source 7).

6.  **Step 6: Formal vs. Informal Register.** Introduce the concept of `—В–Є` (informal 'you') vs. `–≤–Є` (formal/plural 'you').
    *   Contrast informal `–Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В, –Ґ–∞—А–∞—Б–µ` with formal `–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М, –°–µ—А–≥—Ц—О –Т–∞—Б–Є–ї—М–Њ–≤–Є—З—Г` (Source 1, 3).
    *   Explain that `–≤–Є` is used with strangers, elders, and in professional contexts. Show how verb endings and pronouns change: `–•–Њ—З–µ—И?` (—В–Є) vs. `–•–Њ—З–µ—В–µ?` (–≤–Є).

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

Teaching Ukrainian communication requires a deliberate de-linking from Russian linguistic habits, which are often mistakenly presented as a "shortcut" to learners.

1.  **The Vocative Case is Non-Negotiable and a Key Differentiator:** Russian has lost the vocative case in everyday speech, retaining it only in archaic or religious contexts. Ukrainian, however, uses it constantly and actively (`–і—А—Г–ґ–µ`, `–Ю–ї–µ–љ–Њ`, `–њ–∞–љ–µ`). Emphasize that this is a core, living feature of Ukrainian grammar, not an optional flourish. Its absence in modern Russian is a point of divergence, not a model to follow (Source 5).
2.  **Teach `–Я–∞–љ–µ / –Я–∞–љ—Ц` as the Default Formal Address:** The use of `–Я–∞–љ–µ` (Sir/Mr.) and `–Я–∞–љ—Ц` (Madam/Mrs./Ms.) followed by a first name or title is the standard, polite, European way of formal address in Ukrainian (Source 3). Avoid introducing the Russian-style `–Ж–Љ'—П + –њ–Њ-–±–∞—В—М–Ї–Њ–≤—Ц` (Name + Patronymic) as the primary model. While patronymics are used (`–°–µ—А–≥—Ц—О –Т–∞—Б–Є–ї—М–Њ–≤–Є—З—Г`), they often appear in more official or older-generation contexts. The `–Я–∞–љ/–Я–∞–љ—Ц` model is more versatile, modern, and aligns Ukrainian with its Central European cultural context.
3.  **Avoid Russian Phonetic Analogies:** Do not teach Ukrainian sounds by comparing them to Russian ones (e.g., "Ukrainian `–Є` is like Russian `—Л`"). This creates phonetic interference and hinders the development of an authentic Ukrainian accent. Teach each sound on its own terms, using minimal pair drills based on Ukrainian words.
4.  **Calques and Surzhyk:** Actively teach against common Russian calques in conversational phrases. A prime example is the incorrect use of `*–Ф–∞–≤–∞–є!` for "bye" (a Russianism) instead of the proper `–Я–∞-–њ–∞` or `–Ф–Њ –њ–Њ–±–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П`. The brief should explicitly list and forbid such constructions.

1.  **Dialogue Completion and Role-Play (Source 22, 31):** This exercise format requires active production and understanding of conversational flow.
    *   **Prompt:** *–Я–Њ–±—Г–і—Г–є—В–µ –і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥ –Ј–∞ –Ј—А–∞–Ј–Ї–Њ–Љ. –†–Њ–Ј—Ц–≥—А–∞–є—В–µ –і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥ —Ц–Ј —Б—Г—Б—Ц–і–Њ–Љ / —Б—Г—Б—Ц–і–Ї–Њ—О –Ј–∞ –њ–∞—А—В–Њ—О.* (Construct a dialogue based on the model. Act out the dialogue with your deskmate.)
    *   **Example Model:**
        вАФ –ѓ–Ї —В–µ–±–µ –Ј–≤—Г—В—М?
        вАФ –Ь–µ–љ–µ –Ј–≤—Г—В—М вА¶ .
        вАФ –ѓ–Ї —В–≤–Њ—Ф –њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ?
        вАФ –Ь–Њ—Ф –њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ вА¶ .
    *   **Pedagogical Value:** This simple format is easily adaptable for arranging a meeting. E.g., "вАФ –•–Њ–і—Ц–Љ–Њ –≤ –Ї—Ц–љ–Њ. –Ю –Ї–Њ—В—А—Ц–є –≥–Њ–і–Є–љ—Ц? вАФ ...". It moves from controlled practice to free production.

2.  **Dialogue Punctuation and Analysis (Source 7):** This focuses on the written conventions of dialogue, especially the vocative case comma.
    *   **Prompt:** *–Я–µ—А–µ–њ–Є—И—Ц—В—М –і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥, —А–Њ–Ј—Б—В–∞–≤–ї—П—О—З–Є –њ—А–Њ–њ—Г—Й–µ–љ—Ц —А–Њ–Ј–і—Ц–ї–Њ–≤—Ц –Ј–љ–∞–Ї–Є. –•—В–Њ –Ј –њ–Њ–і—А—Г–ґ–Њ–Ї –њ–Њ—А—Г—И–Є–≤ –µ—В–Є–Ї–µ—В —Г —Б–њ—Ц–ї–Ї—Г–≤–∞–љ–љ—Ц?* (Rewrite the dialogue, inserting the missing punctuation marks. Which of the friends violated etiquette in the conversation?)
    *   **Example Text:** `вАФ –Р–ї–ї–Њ! –Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В –Ю–ї—О –њ—А–Є–≤—Ц—В–∞–ї–∞—Б—П –Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–∞.` (Needs comma after `–Я—А–Є–≤—Ц—В` and `–Ю–ї—О`).
    *   **Pedagogical Value:** This connects the grammatical rule (vocative case) to its function in polite communication and its visual representation in text. It also introduces the meta-level of analyzing social appropriateness.

3.  **Dialogue Transformation (Source 12):** This task tests deep comprehension by forcing a change in perspective.
    *   **Prompt:** *–Ґ—А–∞–љ—Б—Д–Њ—А–Љ—Г–є—В–µ –і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥ —Г –Љ–Њ–љ–Њ–ї–Њ–≥ —Ц –њ–µ—А–µ–Ї–∞–ґ—Ц—В—М –є–Њ–≥–Њ –≤—Ц–і —Ц–Љ–µ–љ—Ц: –Р. –Ф–∞–≤–Є–і–∞ (–≤—Ц–і –њ–µ—А—И–Њ—Ч –Њ—Б–Њ–±–Є); –С. –Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–Є (–≤—Ц–і —В—А–µ—В—М–Њ—Ч –Њ—Б–Њ–±–Є).* (Transform the dialogue into a monologue and retell it from the perspective of: A. David (first person); B. Oksana (third person).)
    *   **Pedagogical Value:** A learner cannot complete this without fully understanding who said what and what the overall context is. It's an excellent comprehension check for a checkpoint module.

4.  **Functional Questions about a Dialogue (Source 1, 10, 18):** A straightforward and effective way to check listening or reading comprehension.
    *   **Prompt:** *–°–ї—Г—Е–∞–є—В–µ –і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥ —Ц –і–∞–є—В–µ –≤—Ц–і–њ–Њ–≤—Ц–і—М –љ–∞ –Ј–∞–њ–Є—В–∞–љ–љ—П: –Ъ–Њ–ї–Є –±—Г–і–µ –≤–µ—З—Ц—А–Ї–∞?* (Listen to the dialogue and answer the question: When will the party be?) (Source 1). *–©–Њ –≤–Њ–љ–Є –±—Г–і—Г—В—М —А–Њ–±–Є—В–Є?* (What are they going to do?) (Source 10).
    *   **Pedagogical Value:** It anchors the learning purpose. The learner is listening not just to words, but for specific, functional information needed to answer the question, mimicking real-life listening.

# –Я–µ–і–∞–≥–Њ–≥—Ц–Ї–∞ A1: Checkpoint First Contact



## –Ь–µ—В–Њ–і–Є—З–љ–Є–є –њ—Ц–і—Е—Ц–і (Methodological Approach)

The Ukrainian pedagogical approach to teaching initial introductions is fundamentally communicative and context-driven. Even from the first lesson, the goal is to enable a learner to participate in a simple, formulaic dialogue (`–і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥`). The core concepts of **—Ц–Љ'—П** (first name), **–њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ** (surname), and **–њ–Њ –±–∞—В—М–Ї–Њ–≤—Ц** (patronymic) are introduced as functional chunks of language needed to complete a real-world task, such as introducing oneself or filling out a simple form (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `4-klas-ukrayinska-mova-varzatska-2021-1_s0159`, `6-klas-ukrmova-zabolotnyi-2020_s0032`).

Ukrainian textbooks for early grades (1-2) establish this pattern by immediately presenting model dialogues. They use a "question-and-answer" format that is easy to memorize and adapt (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0107`, `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0014`). For example, the structure `вАФ –ѓ–Ї —В–µ–±–µ –Ј–≤—Г—В—М? вАФ –Ь–µ–љ–µ –Ј–≤—Г—В—М ... .` is presented as a fixed pair to be practiced with a partner (`–†–Њ–Ј—Ц–≥—А–∞–є—В–µ –і—Ц–∞–ї–Њ–≥ —Ц–Ј —Б—Г—Б—Ц–і–Њ–Љ / —Б—Г—Б—Ц–і–Ї–Њ—О –Ј–∞ –њ–∞—А—В–Њ—О`) (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0014`).

Key methodological principles are:
1.  **Dialogue First:** The primary mode of instruction is the dialogue or poly-dialogue (`–њ–Њ–ї—Ц–ї–Њ–≥`), where students learn by playing roles in a given situation (`–°–Є—В—Г–∞—Ж—Ц—П`) (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0106`, `5-klas-ukrmova-avramenko-2022_s0011`). This makes the language immediately useful.
2.  **Structural Repetition:** Core phrases like `–Ь–µ–љ–µ –Ј–≤–∞—В–Є...` and `–Ь–Њ—Ф –њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ...` are drilled through repetition, not grammatical analysis at first. The focus is on automaticity. (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0106`).
3.  **Immediate Introduction of Capitalization:** From the outset, learners are shown that names, patronymics, and surnames are proper nouns written with a capital letter (`–њ–Є—И—Г—В—М –Ј –≤–µ–ї–Є–Ї–Њ—Ч –ї—Ц—В–µ—А–Є`) (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `2-klas-ukrmova-kravcova-2019-1_s0070`, `2-klas-ukrmova-bolshakova-2019-2_s0023`). This is treated as a fundamental orthographic rule, not an advanced topic.
4.  **Implicit Grammar:** The accusative case in `–Ь–µ–љ–µ –Ј–≤–∞—В–Є...` and the vocative case in direct address (`–Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–Њ!`) are introduced implicitly through model phrases. Formal grammatical explanation is delayed until the learner is comfortable with the functional use of the phrases (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0106`, `6-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2023_s0148`).

## –Я–Њ—Б–ї—Ц–і–Њ–≤–љ—Ц—Б—В—М –≤–≤–µ–і–µ–љ–љ—П (Introduction Sequence)

The introduction of "first contact" language should follow a logical progression from simple to complex, mirroring the approach in Ukrainian native-speaker textbooks.

1.  **Step 1: Foundational Phrases & Pronouns.** Start with greetings (`–Ф–Њ–±—А–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М!`) and the core construction `–Ь–µ–љ–µ –Ј–≤–∞—В–Є...` (My name is...). This immediately introduces the personal pronoun in the accusative case (`–Љ–µ–љ–µ`) in a fixed, unanalyzed chunk (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0106`). Contrast `–ѓ–Ї —В–µ–±–µ –Ј–≤–∞—В–Є?` (informal 'you') with `–ѓ–Ї –≤–∞—Б –Ј–≤–∞—В–Є?` (formal/plural 'you').

2.  **Step 2: Adding the Surname.** Introduce the concept of `–њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ` (surname) with the parallel construction `–Ь–Њ—Ф –њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ...` (My surname is...). Practice this in a simple dialogue format (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0014`, `5-klas-ukrmova-uhor-2022-1_s0107`). At this stage, learners practice asking and answering both questions in a sequence.

3.  **Step 3: The Vocative Case (–Ъ–ї–Є—З–љ–Є–є –≤—Ц–і–Љ—Ц–љ–Њ–Ї) for Direct Address.** This is a critical element of natural Ukrainian speech and must be introduced early. Instead of just saying a name, learners must be taught to use the vocative form to call someone.
    *   For feminine names ending in `-–∞`, it changes to `-–Њ`: `–Р–љ–љ–∞ вЖТ –Р–љ–љ–Њ!`, `–Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–∞ вЖТ –Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–Њ!` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2023_s0148`).
    *   For masculine names ending in a consonant, it changes to `-–µ`: `–Ґ–∞—А–∞—Б вЖТ –Ґ–∞—А–∞—Б–µ!`, `–Я–∞–≤–ї–Њ вЖТ –Я–∞–≤–ї–µ!` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2023_s0148`).
    *   Introduce formal address with `–њ–∞–љ/–њ–∞–љ—Ц`: `–њ–∞–љ–µ –Ж–≤–∞–љ–µ–љ–Ї—Г`, `–њ–∞–љ—Ц –Ю–Ї—Б–∞–љ–Њ` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-litvinova-2023_s0148`). This immediately elevates the learner's politeness and authenticity.

4.  **Step 4: Introducing the Patronymic (–Я–Њ –±–∞—В—М–Ї–Њ–≤—Ц).** Explain that `–њ–Њ –±–∞—В—М–Ї–Њ–≤—Ц` is a name derived from one's father's name and is used in formal or respectful situations. Show the full formal structure: `–Я—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ, –Ж–ЉвАЩ—П, –Я–Њ –±–∞—В—М–Ї–Њ–≤—Ц` (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `2-klas-ukrmova-bolshakova-2019-2_s0023`). Explain the common suffixes: `-–Њ–≤–Є—З` (masculine) and `-—Ц–≤–љ–∞` (feminine) (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0016`). The goal at A1 is recognition, not productive use. Learners should understand what it is when they see it on a form or hear it in a formal introduction.

5.  **Step 5: Contextual Application.** Embed these skills in practical scenarios like booking a table (`–°–Ї–∞–ґ—Ц—В—М –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞ –≤–∞—И–µ –њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ`) or making a doctor's appointment (`–≤–∞—И–µ –њ—А—Ц–Ј–≤–Є—Й–µ —Ц–Љ'—П —Ц –љ–Њ–Љ–µ—А —В–µ–ї–µ—Д–Њ–љ—Г –±—Г–і—М –ї–∞—Б–Ї–∞`) (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `ext-ulp_youtube-120`, `ext-ulp_youtube-58`). This reinforces the utility of the language.

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

Teaching Ukrainian from a decolonized perspective is non-negotiable. This is especially important in foundational topics where Russian-centric habits can form.

1.  **Teach Ukrainian on Its Own Terms:** Never introduce Ukrainian letters or sounds as "like the Russian X." Learners must build a clean Ukrainian phonetic and orthographic foundation from zero. Russian has different letters (e.g., `—Л`, `—Н`) and different pronunciations for shared letters (e.g., `–Є`, `–≥`). Using Russian as a reference point pollutes the learning process from day one.
2.  **Patronymics are East Slavic, Not Russian:** Explicitly state that patronymics (`–њ–Њ –±–∞—В—М–Ї–Њ–≤—Ц`) are a feature of Ukrainian, Belarusian, and Russian cultures. Frame it as a shared heritage, not a Russian import. Highlight the distinct Ukrainian suffixes (`-–Њ–≤–Є—З`, `-—Ц–≤–љ–∞`) as seen in textbooks (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `6-klas-ukrmova-betsa-2023_s0016`).
3.  **Correct Transliteration:** Emphasize the official Ukrainian transliteration system (and the common informal one) which differs from Russian. Key examples: `–У` is `H`, not `G`; `–Ш` is `Y`, not `I`; `–Ж` is `I`. This prevents learners from writing Ukrainian names with Russian spelling conventions.
4.  **Surname Origins:** When discussing surnames, highlight authentic Ukrainian origins related to professions (`–Ъ–Њ–≤–∞–ї—М`, `–С–Њ–љ–і–∞—А`, `–У–Њ–љ—З–∞—А`), features, or Cossack history, not just those shared with Russian (–Ф–ґ–µ—А–µ–ї–Њ: `2-klas-ukrmova-bolshakova-2019-2_s0025`, `3-klas-ukrainska-mova-vashulenko-2020-2_s0158`).
