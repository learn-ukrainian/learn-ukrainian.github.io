## Дієвідмінювання дієслів теперішнього часу

As we see with **батько** and **тато**, grammar often has patterns that reflect reality, and this logic extends to how we describe actions. Just as nouns follow clear gender rules, Ukrainian verbs follow organized patterns called conjugations. Understanding these groups helps you predict the ending of almost any action you want to describe. In this module, we consolidate the patterns you have practiced.

### Перша дієвідміна (-ати)
Verbs in the **Перша дієвідміна** (First Conjugation) group are the most common in the language. You can easily recognize them by the **-ати** ending in their basic form. To use them in a sentence, you drop the **-ти** and add specific personal endings like **-ю**, **-єш**, and **-ють**. For example, the verb **читати** — [tʃɪˈtatɪ] (to read) becomes **я читаю** — [ja tʃɪˈtaju] (I read). Note that **ч** — [tʃ] sounds like «ch» in «church». Other important verbs like **знати** — [znatɪ] (to know) and **писати** — [pɪˈsatɪ] (to write) also belong here. While most are regular, **писати** changes its sound to **пишу** — [pɪˈʃu] (I write), where **ш** — [ʃ] is like «sh» in «shoe». The most important feature is the plural «they» ending, which is usually **-ють**. This group is the foundation of your Ukrainian communication.

*   **Я читаю текст.** — [ja tʃɪˈtaju tekst] (I read the text.)
*   **Ти знаєш відповідь.** — [tɪ ˈznajeʃ ˈʋidpoʋidʲ] (You know the answer.)
*   **Вони знають все.** — [ʋoˈnɪ ˈznajutʲ ʋsɛ] (They know everything.)

### Друга дієвідміна (-ити)
The **Друга дієвідміна** (Second Conjugation) group follows a slightly different set of rules. These verbs typically end in **-ити** or **-іти**. A vital verb in this category is **говорити** — [ɦoʋoˈrɪtɪ] (to speak). Note that **г** — [ɦ] is a voiced sound like «h» in «ahead». While the first group uses an «e» sound in the middle of endings, this group prefers the «y» sound. The biggest distinction appears in the «they» form. Instead of the **-ють** ending we saw before, these verbs use **-ять**. For instance, we say **вони говорять** — [ʋoˈnɪ ɦoˈʋɔrʲatʲ] (they speak). Mastering this difference between the plural endings is a key milestone for every student. It shows you truly understand the two main verb families.

*   **Він говорить повільно.** — [ʋin ɦoˈʋɔrɪtʲ poˈʋilʲno] (He speaks slowly.)
*   **Ми говоримо добре.** — [mɪ ɦoˈʋɔrɪmo ˈdɔbrɛ] (We speak well.)
*   **Вона говорить.** — [ʋoˈna ɦoˈʋɔrɪtʲ] (She speaks.)

### Підмет та присудок
To build a correct sentence, you must understand the **підмет** (subject) and the **присудок** (predicate). The **підмет** is the noun or pronoun that performs the action. The **присудок** is the action itself, which we express with a verb. In Ukrainian, these two parts must always agree in person and number. If you change the **підмет**, the verb ending in the **присудок** must change too. This agreement is like a grammar contract between the subject and the action. It ensures that the listener always knows exactly who is doing what, even if the sentence is very short. This clarity is a beautiful feature of the language.

*   **Студент читає.** — [stuˈdent tʃɪˈtaje] (The student reads.)
*   **Я пишу.** — [ja pɪˈʃu] (I write.)
*   **Друзі знають.** — [ˈdruzʲi ˈznajutʲ] (The friends know.)

[!myth-buster]
Many students think that the verb ending is just an extra detail. In reality, the ending is so strong that you can often drop the pronoun. **Читаю** — [tʃɪˈtaju] already means «I read»!
