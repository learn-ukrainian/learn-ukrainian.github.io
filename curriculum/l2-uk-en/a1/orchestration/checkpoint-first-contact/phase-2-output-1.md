## Навичка 1: Читання та Рід (Skill 1: Reading and Gender)

The single most important skill at this stage is reading the Ukrainian alphabet confidently, completely without any transliteration. You are now reading real Cyrillic!

Це слова́. (These are words.)

Let us focus on two specific letters that frequently trick English speakers: **И** and **І**. They look different, but their sounds can be confusing. The letter **І** sounds sharp and high, exactly like the "ee" in the English word "bee." The letter **И**, however, sounds a bit lower and more relaxed, similar to the short "i" in the English word "bit."

Look closely at these words and notice the difference:

| Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **кіт** | cat |
| **кит** | whale |
| **мі́сто** | city |
| **ми** | we |

> [!warning]
> The visual and auditory «И vs І» trap is extremely common! Always check carefully. A «кіт» (cat) is a small pet, but a «кит» (whale) is far too big for your house!

Now, let us review the rules for noun genders. In the Ukrainian language, every single noun has a gender assigned to it. You can almost always guess this gender simply by looking at the very last letter of the word.

Це пра́вило. (This is the rule.)

- If a word ends in a consonant, it is usually masculine.
- If a word ends in the vowel **-а** or **-я**, it is usually feminine.
- If a word ends in the vowel **-о** or **-е**, it is usually neuter.

Let us look at some clear examples:

| Word | Gender |
|---|---|
| **хліб** (bread) | Masculine (ends in a consonant) |
| **ма́ма** (mom) | Feminine (ends in -а) |
| **мі́сто** (city) | Neuter (ends in -о) |

> [!tip]
> Watch out for the tricky word **та́то** (dad). It ends in **-о**, so it visually looks like a neuter word, but it is actually a masculine noun because it refers to a male person! Meaning overrides the ending here.

Let us practice identifying gender in short phrases.

Це мій та́то. (This is my dad.)
Він тут. (He is here.)
Це моя́ ма́ма. (This is my mom.)
Вона́ там. (She is there.)
Це вели́ке мі́сто. (This is a big city.)
Воно́ тут. (It is here.)

Always check the final letter first, but always keep those logical human exceptions in mind!

## Навичка 2: Прикметники, Множина та Питання (Skill 2: Adjectives, Plurals and Questions)

Once you confidently know the gender of a noun, you can easily describe it using adjectives. Adjectives are words like "big," "good," or "black." In Ukrainian, adjectives must perfectly match the noun they are describing.

Це ду́же важли́во! (This is very important!)

If the noun is feminine, the adjective must change to its feminine form. If the noun is plural, the adjective must also take a plural form. Let us observe the adjective **вели́кий** (big) and see how it changes.

- Masculine: **вели́кий хліб** (big bread)
- Feminine: **вели́ка ка́ва** (big coffee)
- Neuter: **вели́ке мі́сто** (big city)
- Plural: **вели́кі міста́** (big cities)

To make nouns plural, we generally change their final letter. Masculine and feminine nouns very often take the endings **-и** or **-і**. Neuter nouns, on the other hand, often change their ending to **-а** or **-я**.

Це коти́. (These are cats.)
Це ма́ми. (These are moms.)
Це міста́. (These are cities.)

Now, let us practice asking some basic, highly useful questions.

Що це? (What is this?)

You can ask about people or animals using the question word **хто** (who). You can ask about inanimate objects using **що** (what). If you want to ask about a location, you simply use **де** (where).

Хто це? (Who is this?)
Це та́то. (This is dad.)
Що це? (What is this?)
Це до́брий хліб. (This is good bread.)
Де вели́кий кіт? (Where is the big cat?)
Кіт тут. (The cat is here.)

If your answer to a question is negative, you will use **ні** (no) at the beginning of the sentence, and the negative particle **не** (not) right before the word you are negating.

Це ма́ма? (Is this mom?)
Ні, це не ма́ма. (No, this is not mom.)
Це мі́сто? (Is this a city?)
Ні, це не мі́сто. (No, this is not a city.)
Кіт там? (Is the cat there?)
Ні, кіт не там. (No, the cat is not there.)

> [!did-you-know]
> In the Ukrainian present tense, you completely skip words like "is" or "are." You do not say "The cat is here"; you simply say "Cat here" («Кіт тут»). This makes your sentences surprisingly short, punchy, and direct!

Remember, flawlessly matching your adjectives to your nouns is the absolute key to sounding natural. Always pause and ask yourself: what is the gender of this noun, and is it singular or plural? Once you know that, the rest is easy.

## Культурний контекст: Кафе (Cultural Context: Cafe)

Let us take a break from grammar and talk about something truly delicious. Ukraine boasts an incredibly rich coffee culture, especially in the beautiful western city of Lviv.

Це мі́сто Львів. (This is the city of Lviv.)
Це до́бра ка́ва. (This is good coffee.)

Did you know that a Ukrainian merchant named Yuriy Kulchytsky played a major role in bringing coffee to Europe? He helped open one of the very first coffeehouses in Vienna way back in the year 1683! Since that historical moment, drinking coffee has become a huge, beloved part of Ukrainian daily life and social interaction.

When you go to a local cafe, you will definitely need some essential vocabulary.

Де кафе́? (Where is the cafe?)
Кафе́ там. (The cafe is there.)
Що це? (What is this?)
Це чо́рна ка́ва. (This is black coffee.)

When you have finished your drink and are ready to pay, you will need to ask for the bill.

Це мій раху́нок. (This is my bill.)
Де раху́нок? (Where is the bill?)

Finally, there is one absolutely essential phrase for Ukrainian dining etiquette. Before you take your first sip of coffee or eat your meal, you should always wish your companions a good appetite.

Смачно́го! (Bon appetit!)
Це ду́же до́бре. (This is very good.)

> [!culture]
> Saying «Смачно́го!» shows great politeness and respect. It is a warm, friendly way to connect with the people around you before sharing food or drinks together.
