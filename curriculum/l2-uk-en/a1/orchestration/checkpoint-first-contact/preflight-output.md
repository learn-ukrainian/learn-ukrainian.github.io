## Навичка 1: Читання та Рід (Skill 1: Reading and Gender)

The first skill we need to verify is your "ear" and your "eye." Reading Ukrainian without a safety net of transliteration is a huge milestone. One of the most common challenges for English speakers is the **фонологічний виклик** (phonological challenge) of distinguishing between our two "i" sounds.

Remember the "bit" vs. "bee" test? In Ukrainian, we have **И** and **І**. The letter **И** /ɪ/ is lower and more relaxed, like the vowel in "bit" or "sit." The letter **І** /i/ is high and tense, like "bee" or "see." Let's look at some **мінімальні пари** (minimal pairs) to see if your brain can tell them apart.

- **лис** (fox) — uses the relaxed **и**.
- **ліс** (forest) — uses the sharp **і**.

Can you feel the difference? If you say **лис** like **ліс**, you're talking about a forest instead of a fox! Now, try reading these high-frequency words from the official standards. Notice how the sounds shift:

- **Ки́їв** (Kyiv) — starts with the relaxed **и**, ends with the sharp **і**.
- **Ки́ївський** (Kyiv-style/related to Kyiv)
- **Украї́на** (Ukraine) — that **ї** is actually two sounds in one!
- **украї́нець** (Ukrainian person, male)
- **Оле́на** (Olena)
- **олі́вець** (pencil)

[!warning]
Don't rush! Beginners often try to read **и** as a long "ee" sound because of how it looks in other alphabets. Keep **и** short and "lazy" in your mouth, and save the "smile" for the letter **і**.

Once you can read the words, you need to know their "social group"—their **рід** (gender). In Ukrainian, every noun belongs to one of three categories: masculine (**чоловічий рід**), feminine (**жіночий рід**), or neuter (**середній рід**). Most of the time, the ending tells you the secret. Consonants usually mean masculine, **-а/-я** means feminine, and **-о/-е** means neuter.

But wait! There is a famous "trap" word you learned: **та́то** (dad). Even though it ends in **-о**, which usually signals a neuter noun like **мі́сто** (city), **та́то** is strictly masculine. Why? Because the biological meaning overrides the ending rule. You wouldn't call your dad "it"!

| Чоловічий рід (M) | Жіночий рід (F) | Середній рід (N) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **та́то** (dad) | **кни́га** (book) | **мі́сто** (city) |
| **олі́вець** (pencil) | **Украї́на** (Ukraine) | **вікно́** (window) |
| **стіл** (table) | **су́кня** (dress) | **кафе́** (cafe) |

You should now be able to recognize all 33 letters of the alphabet. No more looking at "Latin" guides! If you can look at the word **Ки́їв** and recognize the letters without hesitation, you have cleared the biggest hurdle in the A1.1 level.

## Навичка 2: Прикметники та Множина (Skill 2: Adjectives and Plurals)

Now that you can identify a noun and its gender, we need to make sure your adjectives are "agreeing" with them. An adjective in Ukrainian is a team player; it changes its ending to match the noun it describes. This is **узго́дження** (agreement).

Think back to Module 11. If we have the adjective **нови́й** (new), it has three main shapes for singular nouns:
- Masculine: **-ий** (**нови́й стіл**)
- Feminine: **-а** (**но́ва кни́га**)
- Neuter: **-е** (**но́ве мі́сто**)

Let's look at some of our favorite review words in action. Notice how **вели́кий** (big) and **га́рний** (beautiful/nice) shift their endings:

- **вели́кий дім** — a big house (M)
- **га́рна су́кня** — a beautiful dress (F)
- **мале́нька кни́га** — a small book (F)
- **вели́ке кафе́** — a big cafe (N)

But the world isn't just made of single objects. We need **множина́** (plural). When a noun becomes plural, the adjective also changes to a universal plural ending: **-і**.

- **но́ві столи́** — new tables
- **га́рні кни́ги** — beautiful books
- **вели́кі міста́** — big cities

[!did-you-know]
Did you notice the **чергува́ння** (alternation) in the last example? The word **мі́сто** (city) has the stress on the "i" in singular, but it jumps to the "a" in plural: **міста́**. Also, remember **стіл** (table) changes its vowel: **стіл** becomes **столи́**. The "i" only likes to hang out in closed syllables!

Finally, let's check your "fashion sense" with colors from Module 12. Colors are adjectives too! They must match the clothes.

> **The Closet Check:**
> - **Хто це?** (Who is this?) — **Це Олена.**
> - **Що це?** (What is this?) — **Це черво́на су́кня.** (This is a red dress.)
> - **Що це?** (What is this?) — **Це си́ні штани́.** (These are blue pants - plural!)
> - **Що це?** (What is this?) — **Це бі́лий све́тр.** (This is a white sweater.)

If you can answer **«так»** (yes) or **«ні»** (no) to whether an adjective matches its noun, you've mastered the logic of the Ukrainian phrase.

## Культурний контекст: Кафе (Cultural Context: Cafe)

To really see these words come alive, we need to step outside the hostel and head to a **кафе́** (cafe). In Ukraine, especially in the city of Lviv, coffee isn't just a drink—it's a ritual with a deep history that connects Ukraine to the heart of Europe.

You might be surprised to learn that a Ukrainian man named Yuriy Kulchytskyi is credited with popularizing coffee in Vienna! After the Siege of Vienna in 1683, he opened one of the city's first coffee houses. Back in Lviv, this tradition lives on in legendary spots like **«Під синьою пляшкою»** (Under the Blue Bottle). When you order a **чорна ка́ва** (black coffee) in Ukraine, you are participating in a centuries-old European tradition.

[!culture]
In Lviv, they don't just "drink" coffee; they "place" it (**піти на каву**). It is an invitation to talk, to share secrets, and to slow down.

When you enter a cafe, your A1.1 skills become your "survival kit." You need to know the **етикет** (etiquette) of ordering. The most important phrase is one you'll hear every time food is served: **«Смачно́го!»** (Bon appetit!). It’s the sound of Ukrainian hospitality.

To order politely, you don't need complex verbs yet. You can use these simple patterns we've practiced:

- **Да́йте, будь ла́ска, ка́ву.** — Give [me], please, a coffee.
- **Мо́жна раху́нок?** — May [I have] the bill?
- **Де ка́ва?** — Where is the coffee?

Remember to use **«так»** and **«ні»** clearly. If the waiter asks if you want sugar, a simple **«Так, будь ла́ска»** or **«Ні, дя́кую»** goes a long way. This is the "First Contact" level: clear, polite, and functional. You aren't debating philosophy yet; you are getting your coffee and enjoying the atmosphere of a **нови́й** and **га́рний** city.

## Інтеграційне завдання (Integration Task)

Now, let's put it all together. Imagine you are sitting in that Lviv cafe. You have a menu in front of you, and you are describing what you see to a friend. This task integrates your knowledge of **Це** (This is), gender, and adjective agreement.

Look at these items and see if you can identify the "wrong" descriptions:
1. **Це вели́кий торт.** (This is a big cake.) — Masculine noun, masculine adjective. **Так!**
2. **Це но́ва кафе́.** (This is a new cafe.) — Wait! **кафе́** is neuter. It should be **но́ве кафе́**. **Ні!**
3. **Це черво́на ка́ва.** (This is a red coffee.) — Grammatically correct (feminine), but maybe a strange drink!
4. **Це мале́нька кни́га.** (This is a small book.) — Feminine noun, feminine adjective. **Так!**

| Pattern: Це + Adjective + Noun | English Translation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Це вели́кий стіл.** | This is a big table. |
| **Це но́ва ка́ва.** | This is a new coffee. |
| **Це га́рне мі́сто.** | This is a beautiful city. |

Before you move to the next module, ask yourself these three questions. If you can answer them, you are ready for A1.2!

2. Do I know why **та́то** is masculine but **мі́сто** is neuter?
3. Can I change **нови́й** to match **кни́га**?

If you said **«так»** to all three, you have officially mastered the "First Contact" phase. You have the vocabulary to describe what things are (**що**), who people are (**хто**), and where things are located (**де**). You can describe colors, sizes, and quantities. You are no longer a stranger to the sounds of Ukraine.
