## Презентація (Presentation)

To begin our journey into Ukrainian numbers, we will start with the absolute basics: the numbers from zero to twenty. Take your time with these, as they form the foundation for all larger numbers you will learn later. Pay special attention to the number **п'ять** (five). For English speakers, the apostrophe in this word is often a pronunciation trap. It represents a glottal break, meaning you must pause slightly and pronounce the consonant hard, like [pjat']. 

> [!tip] Pronunciation Trick
> To pronounce **п'ять** correctly, pause slightly after the "p" sound! This tiny pause is the secret to sounding like a native speaker.

Let's look at the basic numbers:

* **оди́н** (one)
* **два** (two)
* **три** (three)
* **чоти́ри** (four)
* **п'ять** (five)
* **шість** (six)
* **сім** (seven)
* **ві́сім** (eight)
* **дев'ять** (nine)
* **де́сять** (ten)

When we move to the teen numbers (from eleven to nineteen), you simply add the suffix **-на́дцять** to the base number. The stress always falls on the first **а** in the suffix: **одина́дцять** (eleven), **двана́дцять** (twelve). 

Now, let's look at the tens, from twenty to one hundred. There is an important spelling trap here that catches many learners! When writing numbers like fifty or sixty, you must drop the soft sign from the middle of the word. While **п'ять** has a soft sign, **п'ятдеся́т** (fifty) does not!

* **два́дцять** (twenty)
* **три́дцять** (thirty)
* **со́рок** (forty)
* **п'ятдеся́т** (fifty)
* **шістдеся́т** (sixty)
* **сімдеся́т** (seventy)
* **вісімдеся́т** (eighty)
* **дев'яно́сто** (ninety)
* **сто** (one hundred)

Next, we must discuss the magical "1-2-5 Rule" for the Ukrainian currency, the **гри́вня** (hryvnia). In English, we simply say "one dollar" and "two dollars." In Ukrainian, the ending of the noun changes based on the number. You must memorize these three chunk patterns:

| Number Ends In | Currency Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **гри́вня** | **оди́н раз, одна́ гри́вня** |
| 2, 3, 4 | **гри́вні** | **дві гри́вні, три гри́вні** |
| 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 0 | (Genitive form) | **п'ять** (five hryvnias), **де́сять** (ten hryvnias) |

Finally, to ask about prices, you need the essential question word **скі́льки** (how much) and the verb **коштувати** (to cost). Use these basic constructions:

* **Скі́льки ко́шту́є?** (How much does it cost?)
* **Скі́льки це ко́шту́є?** (How much does this cost?)
* **Яка ціна́?** (What is the price?)

Let's see these in context:

> — **Вибачте, скільки коштує хліб?** (Excuse me, how much does bread cost?)
> — **Хліб коштує двадцять гривень.** (Bread costs twenty hryvnias.)
> — **А молоко?** (And milk?)
> — **Молоко коштує сорок гривень.** (Milk costs forty hryvnias.)
> — **Дякую!** (Thank you!)

## Практика (Practice)

Now that we have covered the theory, it is time to put your new knowledge into practice! Counting and arithmetic are excellent ways to train your brain to think in Ukrainian numbers. Let's do some interactive exercises adding up prices in a typical grocery basket. We will use realistic prices you might see in a Ukrainian supermarket today. 

Look at these simple math problems and try to read the full equations aloud in Ukrainian:

* **Хліб ко́шту́є дві гри́вні.** (Bread costs two hryvnias.)
* **Молоко́ ко́шту́є дві гри́вні.** (Milk costs two hryvnias.)
* **Ра́зом: чоти́ри гри́вні.** (Together: four hryvnias.)

A very common mistake for English speakers is mixing up the gender of the number two. In Ukrainian, the word **гри́вня** is feminine. Therefore, you must use the feminine form of two, which is **дві**. Never say "два гривні"! Let's contrast this with a masculine word like "dollar".

| Masculine (два) | Feminine (дві) |
|---|---|
| **два до́лари** | **дві гри́вні** |
| **два дні** | **дві хвили́ни** |

Let's model a typical shopping situation. Imagine you are at a local bazaar. You might want to express your budget constraints using words like **до́рого** (expensive) or **де́шево** (cheap). You also need to know how to ask for your **зда́ча** (change) after handing over a large bill like **сто** (one hundred) hryvnias.

> — **Яка ціна́?** (What is the price?)
> — **Три гри́вні.** (Three hryvnias.)
> — **Це ду́же до́рого!** (This is very expensive!)

> — **Скі́льки це ко́шту́є?** (How much does this cost?)
> — **Дві гри́вні.** (Two hryvnias.)
> — **Є зда́ча?** (Is there change?)

Let's try another one at a busy market:

> — **Добрий день. Скільки коштує сир?** (Good afternoon. How much does the cheese cost?)
> — **Сир коштує сто гривень.** (The cheese costs one hundred hryvnias.)
> — **А вода?** (And the water?)
> — **Вода коштує двадцять гривень.** (The water costs twenty hryvnias.)
> — **Добре, дякую.** (Good, thank you.)

To solidify your memory, let's do a drill on the 1-2-5 rule. Imagine you are picking random quantities of items. Watch how the currency word transforms instantly! 

* **три гри́вні** (three hryvnias) -> Rule 2-4
* **де́сять** (ten hryvnias) -> Rule 5+
* **одна́ гри́вня** (one hryvnia) -> Rule 1
* **п'ять** (five hryvnias) -> Rule 5+

Keep practicing these combinations until they feel completely natural. Repetition is the key to fluency!

## Культурний контекст (Cultural Context)

Understanding the culture behind the language makes learning much more rewarding. Let's explore the fascinating history of the Ukrainian currency, the **гри́вня**. The name itself carries centuries of rich heritage. It derives from the ancient word "гри́ва" (mane or neck). During the times of Kyivan Rus, long before modern paper money existed, a "hryvnia" referred to a valuable silver neck-ornament or a solid silver ingot used as a measure of wealth and trade. This ancient term is entirely native to Ukraine and represents a proud, independent monetary history.

When Ukraine regained its independence, the national currency was reintroduced in 1996. The official symbol for the hryvnia is **₴**. If you look closely at its design, it features a cursive, italic Cyrillic letter "г" (representing the first letter of the word). The two horizontal stripes crossing it are an internationally recognized symbol of financial stability, similar to the designs of the Euro (€) or the Japanese Yen (¥). You will see this symbol everywhere on price tags across the country.

The smaller subunit of the hryvnia is the **копі́йка** (kopeck). One hryvnia consists of one hundred kopecks. While physical kopeck coins are becoming less common due to inflation, the word survives strongly in Ukrainian culture. Speaking of money, consider the traditional phrase:

* **Час — це гроші.** (Time is money.)

This piece of wisdom beautifully illustrates a modern attitude toward value. Remembering this phrase is a great way to practice your new vocabulary!
