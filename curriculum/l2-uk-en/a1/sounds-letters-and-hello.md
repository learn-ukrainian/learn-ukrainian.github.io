## Зву́ки і лі́тери (Sounds and Letters)

In Ukrainian, sounds and letters are different things. As the textbook by Заболо́тний (Grade 5, page 83) states: "Звуки ми чу́ємо й вимовля́ємо, а бу́кви ба́чимо й пи́шемо." We hear and pronounce a **звук** (sound), but we see and write a **лі́тера** (letter). A letter is a written symbol. A sound is what you actually hear and say. This distinction matters immediately, because reading, spelling, and sound analysis all depend on separating what is written from what is pronounced.

You might assume that every letter matches exactly one sound, but the numbers tell a different story. The Ukrainian language has exactly 33 letters, but it has 38 distinct sounds. Why is there a mismatch? The answer lies in how certain letters behave. Some letters have the special ability to represent two different sounds at the same time. Specifically, the letters Я, Ю, Є, and Ї can represent a combination of two sounds depending on where they appear in a word. On the other hand, one specific letter makes no sound at all. The soft sign, written as Ь, is completely silent. Its only job is to soften the consonant that comes right before it. Because of these rules, you must be careful with your terminology. The textbook by Litvinova (Grade 5, page 130) asks students a very specific pedagogical question: "Чи мо́жна говори́ти «голосна́ літера»?" (Can we say "vowel letter"?). The answer is an absolute no. Sounds are either vowels or consonants, not letters. Letters only represent sounds. Calling a symbol on a page a "vowel" is factually incorrect in Ukrainian linguistics.

To start reading, you need to meet the Ukrainian alphabet, known as the **абе́тка** or алфа́ві́т. It contains all 33 letters in a specific order: А, Б, В, Г, Ґ, Д, Е, Є, Ж, З, И, І, Ї, Й, К, Л, М, Н, О, П, Р, С, Т, У, Ф, Х, Ц, Ч, Ш, Щ, Ь, Ю, Я. Each letter has its own specific name. For English speakers, a major advantage is that Ukrainian spelling is highly phonetic. This means that what you see is usually very close to what you hear. Unlike English, where the letter "a" sounds completely different in "cat," "father," and "late," Ukrainian letters are relatively consistent. There are no silent letters hiding in words to trick you (except for the functional role of the soft sign Ь). Pronunciation patterns are much more predictable than in English, so once you learn the specific sounds that these 33 symbols represent, you will usually be able to make a strong first reading attempt aloud.
 For example, knowing the sounds allows you to read the word **та́то** (father) perfectly on the first try.

## Голосні́ звуки (Vowel Sounds)

The first family is the **голосни́й** (vowel sound). A vowel is made with voice and free airflow, so you can stretch it and sing it. As the textbook by Большако́ва (Grade 1, page 24) teaches: "Голосні почу́єш в пісні́..." That is the key beginner test: if you can hold the sound, you are dealing with a vowel. In the examples below, you will hear this clearly in **ма́ма**, **молоко́**, and **Уля**.
 

There are exactly 6 vowel sounds in the Ukrainian language: [а], [о], [у], [е], [и], and [і]. However, as discussed earlier, the alphabet has 10 letters that are used to represent these 6 sounds: А, О, У, Е, И, І, Я, Ю, Є, and Ї. The extra four letters (Я, Ю, Є, Ї) are called "iotated" letters. They perform "two jobs," often representing a hidden "y" sound combined with a basic vowel sound. Their exact mechanics appear in depth in the next module. The most important rule to remember is that every single Ukrainian word must have at least one vowel sound. Vowels are the absolute heart of every syllable. A syllable cannot exist without a vowel to give it shape and volume. 

In Ukrainian schools, children learn to map out the sounds in words using special symbols. The textbook by Захарійчук (Grade 1, page 13) introduces a simple sound notation system. In these sound models, every vowel sound is marked with a heavy dot [•]. You can practice hearing these vowel sounds right now. Listen carefully to the word **мама** (mother). You can clearly hear two distinct [а] sounds: мА-мА. Next, listen to the word **молоко** (milk). It contains three identical [о] sounds strung together: мО-лО-кО. Finally, listen to the name **Уля**. It gives you a clear [у] example: У-ля.

## При́голосні звуки (Consonant Sounds)

The second family is the **при́голосний** (consonant sound). A consonant is made with noise, or with voice plus noise, because the lips, tongue, or teeth interrupt the airflow. The textbook by Большакова (Grade 1, page 24) captures this well: "Приголосні деренча́ть і тихе́нько шелестя́ть, го́лосно свистя́ть і шипля́ть." A simple beginner test is that you cannot sing [к] or [п] the way you can sing [а]. Consonants give words their structure; vowels give them voice.
 

The math for consonants is slightly more complex than for vowels. The Ukrainian language has 32 distinct consonant sounds, but these are derived from only 22 consonant letters in the alphabet. This is mostly because many Ukrainian consonants come in distinct pairs: тверді́ (hard) and м'які́ (soft). For example, a hard "t" and a soft "t" are considered completely separate sounds, even though they might be written using the same base letter. The notation system from Захарійчук (Grade 1, page 15) shows this clearly: hard consonant sounds are marked with a single dash [–], while soft consonant sounds are marked with a double dash [=]. This hard and soft distinction is central to Ukrainian phonetics and unfamiliar to most English speakers, which means you will need to train your ear to hear the difference.
 You can hear a hard consonant in **сон** (dream), which starts with a hard [с-] sound. Conversely, you can hear a soft consonant in **дім** (house), which starts with a soft [д=] sound, or **ніс** (nose), which starts with a soft [н=] sound.

You will meet the consonant letters through the pronunciation videos below: М, Н, С, К, Л, Р, Б, В, Д, П, Т, Г, Ґ, З, Ж, Ш, Х, Й, Ч, Щ, Ц, and Ф. Watch how the speaker shapes her mouth for each one. There are three special letters to pay close attention to. The letter Ґ with its little upturned tail is a distinctive letter in the Ukrainian alphabet and represents a hard "g" sound. The letter Щ is highly efficient because it always represents two distinct sounds packed together: [шч]. Finally, the soft sign, Ь (м'яки́й знак), makes absolutely no sound of its own, but it serves the crucial purpose of softening the consonant that comes immediately before it.

## Приві́т! (Hello!)

Understanding the mechanics of sounds and letters prepares you for your very first Ukrainian conversation. The fundamental phrases that people use every single day provide the best starting point. Imagine the setting of the first day in a Ukrainian language class. The teacher walks into the room and immediately begins speaking to the students to establish a welcoming atmosphere. 

> **Вчи́тель:** До́брий день! *(Good afternoon!)*
> **У́чні:** Добрий день! *(Good afternoon!)*
> **Вчитель:** **Привіт**! **Як спра́ви**? *(Hi! How are you?)*
> **Учні:** **До́бре**, дякую! *(Fine, thank you!)*

In this short exchange, you see the formal greeting "Добрий день" used respectfully with a teacher, followed by the informal greeting **привіт** (hi). When someone asks **як справи** (how are you), you can answer **добре** (fine, good), **чудо́во** (great), or **норма́льно** (okay), and ask the question back with **А у тебе́?** (And you?).

Consider another situation. Two new classmates meet in the hallway just before their first Ukrainian lesson begins. They want to introduce themselves and be friendly.

> **Марко́:** **Привіт**! Як тебе зва́ти? *(Hi! What is your name?)*
> **Софія:** **Привіт**! Ме́не звати Софія. А тебе? *(Hi! My name is Sofia. And you?)*
> **Марко:** Мене звати Марко. *(My name is Marko.)*

This is the very first social application of the sounds you have just learned. By putting these specific sounds together, Marko and Sofia successfully introduce themselves.

As the conversation continues, Sofia expresses her happiness at meeting her new classmate.

> **Софія:** Ра́да тебе ба́чити! *(Glad to see you!)*
> **Марко:** Ра́дий тебе бачити! *(Glad to see you!)*

Notice carefully how they say the exact same English phrase, but the Ukrainian words are slightly different. Sofia, a female speaker, says "рада." Marko, a male speaker, says "радий." The Ukrainian language has gendered forms for many words. This is your first encounter with grammatical gender, which will become a major structural topic starting in module M08. For now, observe that speakers adapt their words based on who they are.

Applying these rules directly, a "звукови́й ана́ліз" (sound analysis) on the word **привіт** breaks down the letters to reveal the exact sounds inside:

* П represents the sound [п] (приголосний)
* р represents the sound [р] (приголосний)
* и represents the sound [и] (**голосний**)
* в represents the sound [в] (приголосний)
* і represents the sound [і] (**голосний**)
* т represents the sound [т] (приголосний)

Counted out, the word contains exactly two **голосні** (vowels) and four приголосні (consonants). Because it has two vowel sounds, the word has two syllables. Every type of sound you learned about in this module appears right here in this one simple greeting.

## Підсумок (Summary)

This module established the foundation of Ukrainian phonetics. Review the core concepts with a few self-check questions to verify this foundation. 

First, how many letters are in the Ukrainian alphabet? There are exactly 33 літери. 
Second, how many sounds are there in the Ukrainian language? There are 38 зву́ків. 
Why are the numbers different? The numbers differ because iotated letters (Я, Ю, Є, Ї) can make two distinct sounds, many consonants come in hard and soft pairs, and the soft sign (Ь) makes absolutely no sound at all. 
What are голосні? These are vowel sounds made only with your voice, meaning they can be easily sung without any airflow obstruction. 
What are приголосні? These are consonant sounds made with a physical obstruction in the mouth, which means they cannot be sung. 
Can you say "голосна літера"? Absolutely not. Sounds are голосні, not letters; letters merely represent sounds on paper. 
Finally, what does **привіт** mean? It is the informal greeting for "hi." And how do you answer **як справи**? You can say **добре**, **чудово** (great, wonderful), or **нормально** (okay).
