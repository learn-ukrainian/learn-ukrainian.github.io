## Навичка 1: Дієслова

Verbs are the engine of every sentence. In Ukrainian, verbs change their endings based on who is doing the action. Let's review the two main conjugation classes and how to handle reflexive verbs. 

### Модель: Class I Verbs (-ати)

Class I verbs, like **чита́ти** (to read) and **писа́ти** (to write), typically end in -ати. They share a recognizable pattern of endings across all pronouns. Let's look at how they form their present tense.

The core rule is to drop the -ти ending from the infinitive and add the specific person ending. Notice the vowels «ю», «є», and «у» that dominate this conjugation class. These vowels are the signature sound of Class I. Let's look at the paradigm for **чита́ти**:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example | Translation |
|---------|--------|---------|-------------|
| Я | -ю | Я читаю | I read |
| Ти | -єш | Ти читаєш | You read (informal) |
| Він/Вона/Воно | -є | Він читає | He reads |
| Ми | -ємо | Ми читаємо | We read |
| Ви | -єте | Ви читаєте | You read (formal/plural) |
| Вони | -ють | Вони читають | They read |

Notice how the stem "чита-" remains constant, while the ending shifts to match the pronoun. This system allows Ukrainian to be highly precise. In fact, because the ending tells you exactly who is doing the action, Ukrainians often drop the pronoun entirely! Saying «Читаю книгу» is just as correct as saying «Я читаю книгу».

Here are a few examples in context:

*   Я читаю книгу. (I am reading a book.)
*   Ти пишеш лист? (Are you writing a letter?)
*   Він дуже добре пише. (He writes very well.)
*   Ми читаємо новини щодня. (We read the news every day.)
*   Вони не читають цей журнал. (They are not reading this magazine.)

You will use these verbs constantly when discussing your daily routine, your hobbies, or your work. Grasping this pattern is one of the most important steps you can take toward fluency.

### Практика: Class II Verbs (-ити)

Class II verbs, such as **говори́ти** (to speak), usually end in -ити. They have a slightly different set of endings. The most critical difference is in the 3rd person plural (Вони). 

While Class I verbs end in -уть or -ють for "They", Class II verbs end in -ать or -ять. Let's examine the paradigm for **говори́ти**:

| Pronoun | Ending | Example | Translation |
|---------|--------|---------|-------------|
| Я | -ю | Я говорю | I speak |
| Ти | -иш | Ти говориш | You speak (informal) |
| Він/Вона/Воно | -ить | Вона говорить | She speaks |
| Ми | -имо | Ми говоримо | We speak |
| Ви | -ите | Ви говорите | You speak (formal/plural) |
| Вони | -ять | Вони говорять | They speak |

Notice how the «и» vowel replaces the «є» from Class I. This shift is a key identifier for this verb class.

Let's see these in action with varied sentence structures:

*   Я говорю українською. (I speak Ukrainian.)
*   Ти говориш дуже швидко. (You speak very fast.)
*   Вони говорять про роботу. (They are speaking about work.)
*   Ми не говоримо про це. (We are not speaking about this.)
*   Вона говорить тихо. (She speaks quietly.)

Why do we have two classes? It is a natural evolution of the language's phonetic history. While it might seem like extra work now, recognizing these two patterns allows you to confidently guess the conjugation of hundreds of new verbs you will encounter later.

> [!warning]
> Watch out for the "Вони" form! A very common learner mistake is mixing up the plural endings. Remember: Class I takes -уть/-ють (Вони читають), while Class II takes -ать/-ять (Вони говорять). 

### Самоперевірка: Reflexive Verbs (-ся/-сь)

Reflexive verbs show that an action is directed back at the subject, or they indicate a routine state. They end in the particle -ся or -сь. 

The rule for attaching this particle is simple and based strictly on sound. If the conjugated verb ends in a consonant, you add **-ся**. If the conjugated verb ends in a vowel, you add **-сь**. This is a perfect example of Ukrainian euphony — the language naturally adjusts itself to sound melodious and easy to pronounce.

Let's conjugate a reflexive verb to see this pattern clearly:

*   Я смію**сь** — I laugh - ends in the vowel 'ю', so we add 'сь'.
*   Ти смієш**ся** — You laugh - ends in the consonant 'ш', so we add 'ся'.
*   Він смієть**ся** — He laughs - ends in the consonant 'ть', so we add 'ся'.
*   Ми сміємо**сь** — We laugh - ends in the vowel 'о', so we add 'сь'.
*   Ви смієте**сь** — You laugh - ends in the vowel 'є', so we add 'сь'.
*   Вони сміють**ся** — They laugh - ends in the consonant 'ть', so we add 'ся'.

Let's look at some full sentence examples that show routine actions:

1.  Як ти називаєшся? (What are you called?)
2.  Я сміюсь дуже голосно. (I laugh very loudly.)
3.  Вони зустрічаються в кафе. (They meet in the cafe.)
4.  Ми часто зустрічаємось. (We often meet.)
5.  Він дивиться телевізор. (He watches television.)

These verbs are incredibly common in Ukrainian. While English might use phrases like "to get dressed" or "to wake up," Ukrainian often uses a single reflexive verb. For example, «Я одягаюсь» means "I dress myself," and «Я прокидаюсь» means "I wake myself up." Understanding this concept will unlock a massive amount of everyday vocabulary for you.

## Навичка 2: Питання та заперечення

Asking questions and expressing negation are fundamental to having a real conversation. Let's ensure you can smoothly gather information and state what isn't true.

### Модель: Question Words

Question words are your keys to unlocking information in any language. In Ukrainian, they usually come at the very beginning of a sentence. Let's review the most essential question words: **хто** (who), **що** (what), **де** (where), **куди** (where to), and **звідки** (from where).

Notice a crucial distinction that Ukrainian makes: it separates location from direction. English uses "where" for both "Where are you?" and "Where are you going?". Ukrainian uses де for a static location and куди for the direction of movement. Similarly, звідки specifies the origin point.

Let's see a mini-dialogue to illustrate how хто and що work together in conversation:

— Хто це говорить? (Who is speaking?)
— Це мій друг Іван. (This is my friend Ivan.)
— Що ти зараз читаєш? (What are you reading right now?)
— Я читаю нову книгу. (I am reading a new book.)
— Що він хоче? (What does he want?)
— Він хоче їсти. (He wants to eat.)

And another dialogue focusing on locations and directions:

— Де ти? (Where are you?)
— Я тут. (I am here.)
— Де кафе? (Where is the cafe?)
— Кафе там, на вулиці. (The cafe is there, on the street.)
— Звідки ти? (Where are you from?)
— Я з України. (I am from Ukraine.)
— Куди ти йдеш зараз? (Where are you going right now?)
— Я йду додому. (I am going home.)

Using these question words accurately will allow you to navigate cities, meet people, and understand your surroundings without confusion.

### Практика: Yes/No Questions with "Чи"

When you want to ask a general question that requires a "yes" or "no" answer, Ukrainian offers a very elegant solution. You do not need to change the word order or use complex helper verbs. 

You can simply use the question particle **чи** at the beginning of your statement, or just rely on a rising intonation at the end of the sentence. 

Here is a comparison showing how easy it is to turn a statement into a question. Notice how the word order remains completely unchanged:

| Statement | Question | Translation |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Ти читаєш книгу. | Чи ти читаєш книгу? | Are you reading a book? |
| Вона говорить українською. | Чи вона говорить українською? | Does she speak Ukrainian? |
| Це твоя кава. | Чи це твоя кава? | Is this your coffee? |
| Вони працюють тут. | Чи вони працюють тут? | Do they work here? |

> [!myth-buster]
> You might think you need a complicated grammatical inversion to ask a question, like swapping the subject and verb. But Ukrainian word order is flexible! A simple "Чи" or a raised pitch at the end of the sentence is all you need. This system is incredibly learner-friendly.

### Самоперевірка: Negation and Reasons

Saying "no" or explaining why you do something is just as important as asking questions. To make a sentence negative, you simply place the particle **не** directly before the verb.

*   Я **не** хочу їсти. (I do not want to eat.)
*   Він **не** читає новини. (He is not reading the news.)

When you want to explain the reason behind your action or your negation, you use the conjunctions **тому що** (because) or the more colloquial **бо** (because). 

Let's connect some sentences to see how we build logical arguments:

*   Я п'ю каву, **тому що** я хочу спати. (I am drinking coffee because I want to sleep.)
*   Він не йде в кіно, **бо** він працює. (He is not going to the cinema because he is working.)
*   Мені подобається ця книга, **бо** вона дуже цікава. (I like this book because it is very interesting.)
*   Ми не говоримо українською, **бо** ми ще вчимося. (We are not speaking Ukrainian because we are still learning.)

These conjunctions allow you to build complex, compound sentences. You are no longer just stating facts; you are expressing your thoughts, your reasoning, and your worldview with much greater depth.

## Навичка 3: Уподобання та присвійні

Now let's review how to talk about the things you like, the things you want, and the things that belong to you. This is how you personalize your conversations.

### Модель: Expressing Preferences

Ukrainian has three distinct ways to express preferences or desires: **Мені подобається** (I like), **Я люблю** (I love/like strongly), and **Я хочу** (I want). Let's untangle exactly when to use each one.

The construction **Мені подобається** literally translates to "To me is pleasing." It takes the Dative case for the person experiencing the liking. We use this for general preferences, aesthetic appreciation, or everyday items like food, music, and places. The object that you like is actually the grammatical subject of the sentence.

*   Мені подобається кава. (I like coffee.)
*   Тобі подобається чай. (You like tea.)
*   Йому подобається місто. (He likes the city.)
*   Їй подобається музика. (She likes the music.)

It is a common error for learners to use «Я подобаю каву». This is a direct translation from an English structure and is grammatically incorrect in Ukrainian. Remember, the object (coffee) is what is pleasing, and it is pleasing to you. Always say «Мені подобається кава».

**Я люблю** is a much stronger emotional statement. It uses the Accusative case for the object. We use it for deep emotional attachments, hobbies you are passionate about, or people. You would use this for your family, your favorite lifelong hobbies, or your deep love for a country.

*   Я люблю Україну. (I love Ukraine.)
*   Вона любить читати. (She loves to read.)
*   Ми любимо музику. (We love music.)

**Я хочу** is an irregular verb used to express immediate desires, needs, or intentions. It is usually followed by an infinitive verb or a noun. It is practical and direct.

*   Я хочу їсти. (I want to eat.)
*   Ти хочеш каву. (You want coffee.)
*   Вони хочуть спати. (They want to sleep.)

> [!culture]
> In a cafe, saying «Я хочу каву» (I want a coffee) is perfectly polite and natural in Ukrainian. It does not sound rude or demanding like "I want" might sound in English. Ukrainians value directness in service situations.

### Практика: Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns tell us who owns something. They must agree in gender (masculine, feminine, neuter) and number (singular or plural) with the noun they are describing, not with the person who owns it.

Let's review the forms for **мій** (my) and **твій** (your - informal). Notice how the ending of the pronoun changes to mirror the gender of the noun.

| Gender/Number | Mій (My) | Твій (Your) | Example Noun |
|---------------|----------|-------------|--------------|
| Masculine | мій | твій | телефон (telephone) |
| Feminine | моя | твоя | робота (work) |
| Neuter | моє | твоє | місто (city) |
| Plural | мої | твої | друзі (friends) |

Here are some inline examples demonstrating agreement in full sentences. Pay attention to how the pronoun and the noun lock together as a matching pair:

*   Це мій телефон, а це мій друг. (This is my telephone, and this is my friend.)
*   А де твій будинок? (And where is your house?)
*   Це моє рідне місто. (This is my native city.)
*   Твоя книга лежить там. (Your book is lying there.)
*   Тут живуть мої друзі. (My friends live here.)
*   Моя робота дуже цікава. (My work is very interesting.)

These small words are crucial for making your sentences precise and clear. They show relationships and ownership seamlessly.

### Самоперевірка: Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative pronouns point out specific objects, helping you distinguish one item from another. Like possessive pronouns, they must agree perfectly with the noun's gender and number. We use **цей** to point to things that are near you (this/these), and **той** to point to things that are further away (that/those).

Here is the summary table for demonstrative pronouns, showing the different forms for each gender:

| Gender/Number | Цей (This) | Той (That) | Example Noun |
|---------------|------------|------------|--------------|
| Masculine | цей | той | стіл (table) |
| Feminine | ця | та | вулиця (street) |
| Neuter | це | те | вікно (window) |
| Plural | ці | ті | люди (people) |

Let's look at how they function in sentences. Notice how the demonstrative pronoun often pairs with a description:

*   Цей стілець дуже зручний, а ця книга дуже цікава. (This chair is very comfortable, and this book is very interesting.)
*   Мені подобається та вулиця. (I like that street.)
*   Той будинок дуже старий. (That house is very old.)
*   Ця кава смачна, а те молоко холодне. (This coffee is tasty, and that milk is cold.)
*   Ці студенти читають, а ті студенти пишуть. (These students are reading, and those students are writing.)
*   Я хочу цей торт. (I want this cake.)

> [!fact]
> The word "це" is incredibly versatile. Besides meaning "this" (neuter), it also serves as the universal word for "it is" or "this is" in sentences like «Це мій брат» (This is my brother), regardless of the noun's gender!

## Інтеграційне завдання

Now it is time to put everything together. Language is not just about isolated grammar points; it is about how these elements combine in real-life situations. 

### A Cafe Scenario

Let's look at a realistic dialogue where two friends, Олена and Марк, meet at a cafe. Watch how they use verbs, questions, preferences, and pronouns naturally. They are combining all the rules we have discussed into a flowing conversation.

— Привіт, Марку! Як ти? (Hi Mark! How are you?)
— Привіт, Олено! Я добре. А ти? (Hi Olena! I am well. And you?)
— Теж добре. Що ти хочеш пити? (Also well. What do you want to drink?)
— Я хочу каву. Я хочу спати. А ти? (I want coffee. I want to sleep. And you?)
— Мені подобається зелений чай. (I like green tea.)
— Де ми сидимо? (Where are we sitting?)
— Цей стілець зручний, але той стіл кращий. (This chair is comfortable, but that table is better.)
— Добре. Чи ти читаєш зараз нову книгу? (Good. Are you reading a new book right now?)
— Так, я читаю. Ця книга дуже цікава! (Yes, I am reading. This book is very interesting!)
— Хто це пише? (Who writes this?)
— Це дуже хороший автор. (This is a very good author.)

This simple conversation uses almost every skill we have reviewed. They asked questions using що, де, and хто. They expressed desires and preferences using хочу and подобається. They gave reasons using тому що. They used demonstrative pronouns like цей and та, and possessive pronouns like мій. Finally, they conjugated verbs like читати and хотіти perfectly. This is the power of mastering A1.2 grammar! You are no longer just memorizing words; you are communicating.

### Moving Forward

You have built a very solid grammatical foundation. You know how to build the core of a sentence: you can choose a subject, conjugate the verb to match it, and add details using pronouns and conjunctions. You know how to describe the subject and state its preferences.

But what happens when you want to directly interact with an object? What if you want to read the newspaper, rather than just saying "I am reading"? What if you want to see a friend, or buy water? To do this, we need a new linguistic tool.

In the next module, we will introduce the Accusative case (A1.3 Cases & Navigation). This grammatical case will allow your verbs to directly impact the nouns around them. It is the bridge between the actor and the object of the action. Your sentences are about to become much more dynamic and capable of expressing complex interactions in the real world. Get ready to expand your Ukrainian even further!
