## Dialogues

When we learn a new language, one of the most exciting milestones is breaking out of the present moment. Until now, you have been able to describe what is happening right now and what happened in the past. But what about your hopes, your schedule, and your upcoming weekend? 

To talk about what comes next, we need to ask about a **план** (plan). Let us listen to two colleagues, Anna and Maksym, discussing their schedules for the next day. Notice how they form their sentences.

<div class="dialogue">


**Анна:** Що ти бу́деш роби́ти за́втра? *(What will you do tomorrow?)*


**Максим:** Завтра я бу́ду працюва́ти. *(Tomorrow I will work.)*


**Анна:** А вве́чері? *(And in the evening?)*


**Максим:** Ввечері я буду готува́ти вече́рю. *(In the evening I will cook dinner.)*


**Анна:** А що бу́де робити Оле́на? *(And what will Olena do?)*


**Максим:** Вона́ буде чита́ти. *(She will read.)*


**Анна:** А ви бу́дете гуля́ти? *(And will you walk?)*


**Максим:** Так, ми бу́демо гуляти в парку! *(Yes, we will walk in the park!)*


</div>

In this conversation, the central question is **Що ти будеш робити?** (What will you do?). The verb **робити** means "to do" or "to make." Maksym answers using a consistent pattern: a helper verb followed by the main action. 

Now, let us listen to another conversation. Ihor and Mariia are looking forward to the end of the week.

<div class="dialogue">


**Ігор:** Що ви будете робити на вихідни́х? *(What will you do on the weekend?)*


**Марія:** У субо́ту ми будемо відпочива́ти. *(On Saturday we will rest.)*


**Ігор:** А в неді́лю? *(And on Sunday?)*


**Марія:** У неділю я буду готувати, а чолові́к буде гуляти з ді́тьми. *(On Sunday I will cook, and my husband will walk with the children.)*


**Ігор:** Звучи́ть до́бре! А я буду диви́тися футбо́л. *(Sounds good! And I will watch football.)*


**Марія:** Ти за́вжди́ будеш дивитися футбол! *(You will always watch football!)*


</div>

Mariia uses the verb **відпочивати** (to rest) to describe her Saturday plans. When Ihor shares his plan, Mariia responds with the phrase **Звучить добре!** (Sounds good!). She also teases him that he will always watch his favorite sport, **футбол** (football).

In both of these natural planning conversations, the speakers use a very specific two-word combination to talk about the future. Let us break down exactly how this works.

## Майбу́тній час (Future Tense)

Ukrainian actually has TWO future tenses. However, at the A1 level, we focus on learning ONE — the analytic future tense. 

In Ukrainian schools, children in Grade 3 and 4 learn that this is called **майбутній час** (future tense). More specifically, this form is the compound or analytic future. It is built using the helper verb **буду** (I will — a form of the verb "to be") plus the infinitive of the main verb. This is structurally identical to the English "will" + verb.

The magic of the analytic future is that the main verb never changes. It stays frozen in its infinitive dictionary form (always ending in **-ти**, like **читати**, **гуляти**, **працювати**). Only the helper verb changes to match the person performing the action.

Here is the complete conjugation pattern for the analytic future:

| Особa (Person) | Допомі́жне́ дієсло́во (Helper) | Інфініти́в (Infinitive) | Переклад (Translation) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Я** (I) | **буду** | **читати** | I will read |
| **Ти** (You, informal) | **будеш** | **читати** | You will read |
| **Він / Вона / Воно** (He / She / It) | **буде** | **читати** | He / She / It will read |
| **Ми** (We) | **будемо** | **читати** | We will read |
| **Ви** (You, formal/plural) | **будете** | **читати** | You will read |
| **Вони** (They) | **будуть** | **читати** | They will read |

Look closely at the table. The infinitive **читати** remains exactly the same in every single row. If you know the basic forms of **бути** (to be) in the future tense, you can immediately speak in the future tense using any verb you know.

### Comparing the Three Tenses

You now have a complete timeline at your disposal. Let us compare all three tenses using the same verb so you can see the mechanical differences.

*   **Минулий час** (Past tense): The ending of the verb changes based on the **gender** of the speaker.
    *   **Я чита́в кни́жку.** (I read a book. — masculine speaker)
    *   **Я чита́ла книжку.** (I read a book. — feminine speaker)
*   **Теперішній час** (Present tense): The ending of the verb changes based on the **person**.
    *   **Я чита́ю книжку.** (I am reading a book. / I read a book.)
*   **Майбутній час** (Future tense): We use two words. The helper verb changes based on the person, and the main verb is the infinitive.
    *   **Я буду читати книжку.** (I will read a book.)

*Note: The second future tense in Ukrainian is the synthetic future (e.g., **прочитаю**). It is built into a single word and is used for completed actions. This form exists, but it is A2 level material. For now, the analytic future is all you need to perfectly express your plans.*

## Пра́ктика (Practice)

Now that we know the formula, let us apply it to our core verbs. Because the infinitive never changes, you can rapidly build a massive vocabulary of future actions. 

Here is how our most common verbs look in the future tense:

*   **читати** (to read) → **я буду читати**, **ти будеш читати**, **вона буде читати**
*   **працювати** (to work) → **ми будемо працювати**, **ви будете працювати**
*   **готувати** (to cook) → **я буду готувати**, **він буде готувати**
*   **гуляти** (to walk) → **ми будемо гуляти**, **вони будуть гуляти**
*   **дивитися** (to watch) → **я буду дивитися**, **ти будеш дивитися**
*   **говорити** (to speak) → **ми будемо говорити**, **ви будете говорити**

To make these sentences useful in the real world, we need time markers. These are the words that set the scene for the future.

*   **завтра** (tomorrow)
*   **ввечері** (in the evening)
*   **у суботу** (on Saturday)
*   **наступного ти́жня** (next week — **наступний** means "next", and **тиждень** means "week")

Let us build complete, natural sentences about the future combining our verbs and our time markers. Ukrainian word order is flexible, but it is very common to put the time word at the beginning of the sentence to establish when the action will happen.

*   **Завтра я буду працювати з дев'я́тої до п'я́тої.** (Tomorrow I will work from nine to five.)
*   **Ввечері ми будемо дивитися фільм.** (In the evening we will watch a movie.)
*   **У суботу вони будуть гуляти в парку.** (On Saturday they will walk in the park.)
*   **Що ви будете ї́сти на вечерю?** (What will you eat for dinner?)

Notice that even reflexive verbs like **дивитися** follow the exact same rule. The entire verb, including the reflexive **-ся** ending, stays frozen in the infinitive. You simply place **буду** in front of it.

## Summary

You have unlocked the ability to discuss your plans, your schedule, and your upcoming events. The analytic future tense is powerful because it is completely predictable. 

Remember the core formula: **буду / будеш / буде / будемо / будете / будуть** + **infinitive**.

The infinitive never changes. The only word you need to conjugate is the helper verb.

You now possess the tools to navigate across all three essential timeframes. When you look at an action, you can place it perfectly on the timeline:
1.  **Учора я читав.** (Past — requires gender agreement)
2.  **Зараз я читаю.** (Present — requires person agreement)
3.  **Завтра я буду читати.** (Future — uses **буду** plus the infinitive)

Whenever someone asks you about your schedule, they will use this fundamental question:
**Що ти будеш робити?** (What will you do?)

Your answer will almost always begin with:
**Я буду...** (I will...) followed by whatever action you have planned.
