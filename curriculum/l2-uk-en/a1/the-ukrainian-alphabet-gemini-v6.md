## Зву́ки і лі́тери (Sounds and Letters)

Welcome to Ukrainian! The very first step on our journey is understanding a core principle taught to every Ukrainian child in Grade 1. It is the golden rule of Ukrainian phonetics:

> Ми чу́ємо і вимовля́ємо звуки, а ба́чимо і пи́шемо літери.

This means: we hear and pronounce **звуки** (sounds), but we see and write **літери** (letters). <!-- adapted from: unknown, Grade 1, p.24 -->

Why does this distinction matter? Because the Ukrainian alphabet has 33 **літери**, but the language uses 38 **звуки**. They don't match up exactly one-to-one, and understanding why is the key to perfect pronunciation.

Ukrainian sounds are divided into two main families:
*   **Голосні́** (vowels): These sounds are made using only your voice. The air flows freely through an open mouth with no obstruction. You can sing them easily. In Ukrainian, every single syllable has exactly one **голосни́й звук** at its core.
*   **При́голосні** (consonants): These sounds are made with voice and noise, or just noise. Your lips, teeth, or tongue create an obstruction for the air.

## Голосні звуки (Vowel Sounds)

In Ukrainian, there are only 6 vowel sounds (**голосні звуки**): [а], [о], [у], [е], [и], [і].
However, there are 10 vowel letters (**літери**): **А**, **О**, **У**, **Е**, **И**, **І**, **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, **Ї**. <!-- adapted from: unknown, Grade 2, p.34 -->

Why the mismatch? It is because four letters — **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, **Ї** — are what we call "double-duty" or iotated letters. In many situations, they represent *two* sounds combined (a short "y" sound plus a vowel).

Here is how the iotated vowels work:
*   **Я** = [йа] (like "ya" in "yacht"). It makes two sounds at the start of a word or after another vowel. But after a consonant, it does something special: it softens the consonant and just makes the [а] sound.
*   **Ю** = [йу] (like "you"). It works the same way: two sounds, or a softener plus [у].
*   **Є** = [йе] (like "ye" in "yellow"). Same rule: two sounds, or a softener plus [е].
*   **Ї** = [йі] (like "yee" in "yield"). This letter is unique to Ukrainian! Unlike the others, **Ї** *always* represents two sounds. It never softens a consonant.

### The Critical Difference: И vs І

Pay special attention to **И** and **І**. Mixing these up will change the meaning of words.
*   **И** is a lower, relaxed sound, formed deeper in your mouth (somewhere between the English "i" in "bit" and "ee" in "feet", but further back).
*   **І** is a high, tight, bright sound (very close to the English "ee" in "meet").

Look at these minimal pairs where only the vowel changes:
*   **кит** (whale) vs **кіт** (cat)
*   **дим** (smoke) vs **дім** (house) <!-- adapted from: unknown, Grade 1, p.25 -->

## Приголосні: знайо́мі та фальши́ві дру́зі (Consonants: Familiar and False Friends)

Now let's look at the **приголосні** (consonant) letters. We can group them by how they look to an English speaker.

### Group 1: The Familiar Friends
Some letters look exactly like Latin letters and sound similar enough to start reading immediately:
**А**, **О**, **К**, **М**, **Т**, **Е**.

This means you can already read your first Ukrainian words!
> **ма́ма** (mother)
> **та́то** (father)

*A quick note on perfect pronunciation:* The Ukrainian **Т** is dental (your tongue must touch the back of your upper teeth, not the gum ridge like in English). Also, **К** and **Т** are unaspirated, meaning there is no puff of air when you say them. Native speakers hear the difference, but for now, the English approximations will serve you well.

### Group 2: The False Friends
This is the biggest trap for English speakers. These letters look like English letters, but they make completely different sounds. You must train your brain to break its old habits.

*   **В** looks like a "B" but sounds like [в] (English "v").
    > **вода́** (water)
*   **Н** looks like an "H" but sounds like [н] (English "n").
*   **Р** looks like a "P" but sounds like [р] (a rolled "r", like in Spanish).
    > **рука́** (hand)
*   **С** looks like a "C" but sounds like [с] (always "s", never a "k" sound).
*   **У** looks like a "Y" but sounds like [у] (like the "oo" in "boot").
*   **Х** looks like an "X" but sounds like [х] (like the Scottish "loch", a strong, breathy "h" from the back of the throat).
    > **ха́та** (house, traditional)

Drill these false friends. When you see **вода**, if your mouth tries to say "boda", stop and correct it.

### Group 3: New Cyrillic Shapes
These are completely new letters that have no exact Latin equivalent.

*   **Б** [б] (b as in boy)
*   **Г** [г] (a soft, breathy "h", not a hard "g")
*   **Ґ** [ґ] (a hard "g" as in go — this letter is quite rare)
*   **Д** [д] (d as in dog)
    > **дім** (house, modern)
*   **Ж** [ж] (like the "s" in "pleasure")
*   **З** [з] (z as in zoo)
    > **зима́** (winter)
*   **Й** [й] (a very short "y" sound)
*   **Л** [л] (l as in light)
*   **П** [п] (p as in pot)
*   **Ф** [ф] (f as in fox)
*   **Ц** [ц] (like the "ts" in "cats")
*   **Ч** [ч] (like the "ch" in "church")
*   **Ш** [ш] (like the "sh" in "ship")
    > **шко́ла** (school)
*   **Щ** [шч] (This letter *always* makes two sounds together: a "sh" plus a "ch" [ш+ч]).

> **кни́га** (book)
> **мі́сто** (city)

## М'яки́й знак (The Soft Sign — Ь)

We have covered 32 letters. The 33rd letter is special: **Ь** (м'який знак).

The soft sign is unique because it has **NO sound of its own**. You will never pronounce a "Ь".

So what does it do? Its job is to soften the consonant that comes right before it, making that sound lighter and gentler. This is why Ukrainian has more sounds than letters — many consonants have both a "hard" version and a "soft" version, and the **Ь** tells you which one to use. <!-- adapted from: unknown, Grade 1, p.15 -->

We will explore softening deeply in the next module. For now, just know that **Ь** exists, it is silent, and it changes the consonant before it.

## Чита́ємо ви́віски (Reading Signs)

The best way to practice the alphabet is environmental reading. In Ukraine, Grade 1 reading books (букварі́) start with recognizing familiar words. Let's practice reading some common signs (**вивіски**) you will see on the streets.

Sound out each letter, blend them into syllables, and read the whole word. Pay attention to the false friends!

> **апте́ка** (pharmacy)
> **банк** (bank)
> **кафе́** (café)
> **по́шта** (post office)
> **метро́** (metro)
> **школа** (school)
> **зупи́нка** (bus stop)

Now, let's read some major Ukrainian city names. Each one tests different letter combinations:

> **Ки́їв**
> **Львів**
> **Оде́са**
> **Харків**
> **Дніпро́**
> **Полта́ва**
> **Черні́гів**

### Your First Sentences

We can already make complete Ukrainian sentences without using any verbs! We just need the word **Це** (This is / It is).

> Це банк.
> Це кафе.
> Це Київ.
> Це мама.

> Що це?
> Це метро.

> Хто це?
> Це тато.

> Так, це школа.
> Ні, це не пошта.
