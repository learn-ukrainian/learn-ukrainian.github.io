## Pedagogical Excellence Examples

### Example 1: M16 - Etymology Insight (lines 39-42)
> **Did You Know?**
>
> The word немає (there isn't) literally comes from не (not) + має (has). So "немає часу" = "doesn't-have time-genitive". This is why you need genitive here — the noun is the object of "having" that got negated!

**Why it's excellent:** Provides linguistic reasoning, not just rules. Shows WHY genitive is used. Aids retention through understanding.

---

### Example 2: M17 - Three-Rule Number Agreement (lines 134-157)
> **Rule 1: Number 1 → Nominative singular**
> **Rule 2: Numbers 2, 3, 4 → Nominative plural**
> **Rule 3: Numbers 5-20, 30-100 → Genitive plural**

**Why it's excellent:** Clear categorization with examples for each. Shows gender agreement for 1. Notes gender distinction for 2. Connects to genitive case from M16.

---

### Example 3: M18 - Food as Identity (lines 281-285)
> For Ukrainians, food is deeply connected to **identity** and **home**. Борщ isn't just soup—it's a symbol of family, tradition, and survival. During the Holodomor (1932-33 famine-genocide), millions of Ukrainians starved while grain was exported. Today, every bowl of borsch carries that historical memory.

**Why it's excellent:** Elevates food from vocabulary to cultural identity. Explains Holodomor with appropriate gravity. Shows food = resistance and survival.

---

### Example 4: M19 - Politeness Hierarchy (lines 165-172)
> **Common Mistake: "Я хочу" vs "Я візьму"**
>
> In English, we say "I want coffee." In Ukrainian, saying **"Я хочу каву"** is grammatically correct but sounds DEMANDING—like a child wanting candy.
>
> **Polite way:** "Я візьму каву, будь ласка." (I'll take coffee, please.)
> **Even more polite:** "Можна каву?" (May I have coffee?)

**Why it's excellent:** Prevents major cultural error. Shows grammar ≠ appropriateness. Provides three-tier politeness system. Explains cultural perception.
