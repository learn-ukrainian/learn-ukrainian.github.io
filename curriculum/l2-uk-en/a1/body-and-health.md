## Вступ: Частини тіла та народна мудрість (Introduction: Body Parts & Folk Wisdom)

Welcome back! So far, we have talked about our daily routines, going to cafes, and asking for directions. Today, we are going to dive into a very important topic: health and the human body. Knowing how to talk about your body is a vital survival skill in any language, whether you are buying simple medicine or visiting a clinic. 

First, let us learn some of the most high-frequency body parts in Ukrainian. The word for head is **голова́**. We also have **рука́** and **нога́**. There is an important difference here between Ukrainian and English that you should flag for yourself right now. In English, you have different words for "arm" and "hand". In Ukrainian, **рука́** covers both the arm and the hand! Similarly, **нога́** covers both the leg and the foot. It is all one continuous concept in everyday speech. If you need to specify the exact part, there are medical terms, but for daily use, **рука́** and **нога́** are all you need. We also have **живі́т**, which means stomach or belly.

Let us look at some important visual pairs for body parts. Ukrainian has some special plural forms for paired body parts that you simply need to memorize. 
*   **о́ко** (eye) becomes **о́чі** (eyes)
*   **ву́хо** (ear) becomes **ву́ха** (ears)

Do you remember our prepositions from module thirty? You already know how to talk about location using the question word **де** (where). You can easily combine these body parts with the preposition **на**.
*   **на голові́** — on the head
*   **на рука́х** — on the hands (or arms)
*   **на нога́х** — on the feet (or legs)

In Ukrainian school and sports culture, there is a very famous proverb that children learn early on. It perfectly captures the concept of being healthy. The proverb is:
*   «**Здоро́вий дух у здоро́вому ті́лі**» (A healthy spirit in a healthy body).

This phrase introduces the word **здоро́вий** in a memorable frame. Whenever Ukrainians talk about exercise or physical education, you will likely hear this proverb. It is a fantastic cultural hook that connects language learning to a positive lifestyle!

## Презентація: Як ми говоримо про біль (Presentation: Expressing Pain & Symptoms)

Now that we know the basic body parts, how do we say that something hurts? This is where Ukrainian grammar gets very interesting and differs significantly from English. In English, you might say "I have a headache" or "My leg hurts." In Ukrainian, the core grammar structure is «**У мене́ боли́ть** + Subject». 

Literally translated, this means "At me hurts the body part." In this sentence, the body part is the grammatical subject. You are the experiencer, not the actor. Let us look at the verb **боли́ть** (it hurts). Because the body part is the subject, the verb must agree with it. If one thing hurts, we use the singular **боли́ть**. If multiple things hurt, we use the plural form **боля́ть**. 

Let us practice this with some examples to make the pattern clear:
*   «**У мене́ боли́ть голова́.**» — I have a headache. (Literally: At me hurts the head.)
*   «**У мене́ боли́ть живі́т.**» — My stomach hurts. 
*   «**У мене́ боля́ть о́чі.**» — My eyes hurt. (Notice the plural **боля́ть** matching **о́чі**.)
*   «**У мене́ боля́ть но́ги.**» — My legs hurt.

A very common learner error is to mismatch the agreement. You might be tempted to say ~~«У мене́ боля́ть голова́»~~, thinking about the English phrase "My head hurts," but this is incorrect. Always remember: singular body part means **боли́ть**, plural body parts mean **боля́ть**. Contrast the incorrect ~~«У мене́ боля́ть голова́»~~ with the correct «**У мене́ боли́ть голова́**». The body part dictates the verb.

Beyond specific pain, how do you express your general state of feeling? The State Standard requires us to learn impersonal phrases for state. If you want to say "I feel bad," you must use the dative pronoun **мені́** with an adverb.
*   «**Мені́ пога́но.**» — I feel bad. 
*   «**Мені́ до́бре.**» — I feel good.

Drill this carefully to avoid the direct translation error of saying ~~«Я пога́но»~~. In Ukrainian, «**Я пога́но**» is not a valid sentence because states of being happen *to* you. Treat «**Мені́ пога́но**» as a frozen chunk of vocabulary for now. 

Let us add some more symptom vocabulary:
*   **температу́ра** — fever or temperature (collocations: **висо́ка температу́ра**, **мі́ряти температу́ру**)
*   **ка́шель** — cough (collocations: **си́льний ка́шель**)
*   **не́жить** — runny nose
*   **головни́й біль** — headache 

> [!cultural-note]
> When you have these symptoms, Ukrainians often turn to home remedies before visiting a clinic. The Ukrainian tradition of **траволікува́ння** (phytotherapy or herbal medicine) is very strong. If you tell a Ukrainian friend «**Мені́ пога́но**», they will immediately suggest drinking tea! The most popular remedies include tea with **мали́на** (raspberry), **кали́на** (viburnum berries), or a large spoonful of **мед** (honey). «**Чай з мали́ною**» is practically the official national cure for a common cold.

## Діалог: У лікаря та в аптеці (Dialogue: At the Doctor's & Pharmacy)

When raspberry tea is not enough, it is time to visit a professional. Your first point of contact is usually the **сіме́йний лі́кар** (family doctor). Here, we must address another very common learner error. The word for the person is **лі́кар** (doctor), but the word for the building is **ліка́рня** (hospital). 

If you are going to the doctor, you are moving toward a person, so you use the genitive case with the preposition **до**.
*   Correct: «**Я йду до лі́каря.**» — I am going to the doctor.
*   Correct: «**Я йду до ліка́рні.**» — I am going to the hospital.
*   Incorrect: ~~«Я йду в лі́кар.»~~ — This means you are physically walking inside the doctor, which is impossible!

After seeing the doctor, you will likely need to visit the pharmacy. Navigating Ukrainian streets to find one is easy. You might see these signs:
*   **апте́ка** — full pharmacy
*   **зеле́ний хрест** — illuminated green cross
*   **апте́чний пункт** — smaller dispensary kiosk, often located inside a clinic.

Let us look at some functional language for the pharmacy. The word for medicine is **лі́ки**. In Ukrainian, **лі́ки** is a plural-only noun, just like "clothes" or "scissors" in English. You will use it to ask for medicine against specific symptoms.
*   «**Да́йте, будь ла́ска, лі́ки від ка́шлю.**» — Please give me medicine for a cough.
*   «**Да́йте лі́ки від головно́го бо́лю.**» — Give me medicine for a headache.

You also need to understand the difference between getting medicine **за реце́птом** (with a prescription) versus **без реце́пту** (without a prescription). 

Let us read a typical dialogue at the polyclinic, where the doctor asks «**Що вас турбу́є?**» (What bothers you?).

> — **Добри́день. Що вас турбу́є?**
> — Good afternoon. What bothers you?
> — **Добри́день. Мені́ пога́но. У мене́ висо́ка температу́ра і ка́шель.**
> — Good afternoon. I feel bad. I have a high fever and a cough.
> — **У вас боли́ть го́рло?**
> — Does your throat hurt?
> — **Так, ду́же боли́ть.**
> — Yes, it hurts a lot.
> — **Ось реце́пт. Купі́ть ці лі́ки в апте́ці.**
> — Here is the prescription. Buy this medicine at the pharmacy.

## Практика: Пояснення причин (Practice: Explaining Causes)

Sometimes you need to explain to your boss, teacher, or friend why you cannot meet them. To do this, we construct basic complex sentences using the conjunctions **бо** and **тому́ що**, which both mean "because." 

According to the State Standard, this is an essential skill for this level. You can link two simple sentences together using **бо**.
*   «**Я не прийшо́в, бо я хво́рий.**» — I did not come because I am sick.
*   «**Я вдо́ма, тому́ що у мене́ температу́ра.**» — I am at home because I have a fever.

Let us discuss an important distinction between two related words: **хво́рий** and **хворі́ти**. 
*   **хво́рий** is an adjective that describes a state of being sick right now. («**Він хво́рий.**» — He is sick.)
*   **хворі́ти** is a verb that describes the ongoing process or frequency of being ill. («**Він ча́сто хворі́є.**» — He gets sick often.)

Also, we must address one more pitfall. Never say ~~«Я болю́»~~! In Ukrainian, this would mean "I cause pain." If you are the one experiencing the pain, you must use the impersonal structure we practiced earlier: «**Мені́ боли́ть**» or «**У мене́ боли́ть**». 

Let us look at a roleplay contrasting a casual dialogue with a friend about feeling sick versus a more formal explanation.

**Casual Dialogue with a Friend:**
> — **Приві́т! Ти йдеш у кіно́ сього́дні?**
> — Hi! Are you going to the cinema today?
> — **Ні, я вдо́ма, бо я хво́рий.**
> — No, I am at home because I am sick.
> — **Що ста́лося? Що боли́ть?**
> — What happened? What hurts?
> — **У мене́ ду́же боли́ть го́рло і си́льний не́жить.**
> — My throat hurts very much, and I have a bad runny nose.
> — **Пий чай з мали́ною! Оду́жуй шви́дко!**
> — Drink tea with raspberry! Get well soon!

In this casual setting, the friend suggests the traditional **чай з мали́ною** and wishes a quick recovery. The sentences are short and directly explain the cause of absence using **бо**.

Let us summarize the key points of this module. When describing symptoms, always remember that the body part is the subject of your sentence. The verb **боли́ть** must match whether that body part is singular or plural. «**У мене́ боли́ть го́рло**» (singular) versus «**У мене́ боля́ть ву́ха**» (plural). When you feel unwell overall, use the fixed dative chunk «**Мені́ пога́но**». Finally, always ask for medicine in the pharmacy **від** (from/against) your specific symptom, and know whether it is **за реце́птом** or **без реце́пту**.
