## Діало́ги (Dialogues)

Communication is about connection. Up until now, you have learned how to identify objects, describe what things are like, and talk about your preferences. Now, it is time to bring your Ukrainian to life through action. You are going to learn how to talk about what you do, what your friends do, and how to ask others about their daily activities.

Let us start by looking at a very common, everyday conversation. Imagine two friends meeting and catching up on their current activities. Pay close attention to how the action words change depending on who is speaking.

<div class="dialogue">


**Антон:** Приві́т! Що ти ро́биш? *(Hi! What are you doing?)*


**Марія:** Я чита́ю кни́гу. А ти? *(I am reading a book. And you?)*


**Антон:** Я слу́хаю му́зику. *(I am listening to music.)*


**Марія:** А що ро́бить Оле́на? *(And what is Olena doing?)*


**Антон:** Вона́ готу́є вече́рю. *(She is cooking dinner.)*


</div>



In this short exchange, we see the pattern established in *Ukrainian Lessons Podcast Episode 22*. The core question is **Що ти робиш?** (What are you doing?). Notice how the verb **робити** (to do) changes. When asking "you", it is **робиш**. When asking about "Olena" (she), it becomes **робить**. Important: **робити** belongs to a different verb group (Group II), which you will learn in Module 17. For now, just memorize **робиш** and **робить** as fixed phrases — do not try to apply the Group I pattern to this verb.

Similarly, when Maria talks about herself, she says **Я читаю** (I read / I am reading). When Anton talks about himself, he says **Я слухаю** (I listen / I am listening). The ending of the verb clearly signals who is performing the action. You do not always even need the pronoun **я** (I) because the verb ending tells you everything!

Let us look at another natural dialogue, this time in a slightly more professional or academic setting.

<div class="dialogue">


**Максим:** Де ти працю́єш? *(Where do you work?)*


**Анна:** Я працю́ю в о́фісі. А ти? *(I work in an office. And you?)*


**Максим:** Я не працюю, я навча́юся. *(I don't work, I study.)*


**Анна:** Ти зна́єш украї́нську? *(Do you know Ukrainian?)*


**Максим:** Так, я вивча́ю! *(Yes, I am learning!)*


</div>



Here, the verbs give us critical information about a person's life. **Де ти працюєш?** means "Where do you work?". Anna replies with **Я працюю** (I work). Максим introduces a new action, **я навчаюся** (I study), and confirms **я вивчаю** (I am learning).

These dialogues show verbs in their natural habitat. In Ukrainian, verbs are highly dynamic. They adjust their endings to perfectly match the subject of the sentence. This might feel like a lot of moving parts at first, but it follows a beautiful, predictable rhythm.

## Пе́рша дієвідмі́на (Group I Verbs)

Now that you have seen these verbs in action, it is time to understand the mechanics behind them. In Ukrainian grammar, verbs are organized into groups based on how their endings change. This process of changing verb endings is called conjugation.

According to standard Ukrainian pedagogy, such as the *Варзацька Grade 4* textbook (page 129) and *Захарійчук Grade 4* (page 110), verbs in the present tense (**теперішній час**) change their endings according to the person (I, you, he/she) and number (singular or plural). The present tense shows actions that are happening right now or actions that happen regularly.

Most verbs you will encounter early on belong to what we call **Перша дієвідміна** (Group I). These verbs typically have an infinitive (dictionary form) ending in **-ати**, **-увати**, or **-яти**.

To conjugate a Group I verb, you take the infinitive, drop the final **-ти** to find the stem, and then add the correct personal ending. Let us look at the complete pattern using the verb **читати** (to read).

* Я **читаю** (I read)
* Ти **читаєш** (You read)
* Він/вона/воно́ **читає** (He/she/it reads)
* Ми **читаємо** (We read)
* Ви **читаєте** (You all read / You formal read)
* Вони́ **читають** (They read)

The core pattern for Group I is adding these specific endings to the stem: **-ю**, **-єш**, **-є**, **-ємо**, **-єте**, **-ють**. Once you memorize this melodic sequence, you can conjugate hundreds of Ukrainian verbs that follow this exact pattern. Later, you will meet verbs with small stem changes, but the personal endings will always feel familiar.

Let us apply this exact same pattern to six essential Group I verbs that you will use every single day.

1. **читати** (to read)
   * я **читаю**
   * ти **читаєш**
   * він/вона **читає**

2. **знати** (to know)
   * я **знаю**
   * ти **знаєш**
   * він/вона **знає**

3. **слухати** (to listen)
   * я **слухаю**
   * ти **слухаєш**
   * він/вона **слухає**

4. **гуляти** (to walk / to stroll)
   * я **гуляю**
   * ти **гуляєш**
   * він/вона **гуляє**

5. **працювати** (to work)
   This verb has a special trick. For verbs ending in **-увати**, the **-ува-** portion transforms into **-у-** before adding the endings.
   * я **працюю**
   * ти **працюєш**
   * він/вона **працює**

6. **готувати** (to cook)
   This verb follows the exact same transformation rule as **працювати**. The **-ува-** becomes **-у-**.
   * я **готую**
   * ти **готуєш**
   * він/вона **готує**

There is a beautiful rhythm to these words. Try saying them aloud: *знаю, знаєш, знає*. You will feel how the sounds flow naturally from one to the next. The **-ю** ending always points back to "me", while the **-ш** sound at the end of **-єш** is the signature of "you".

## Я, ти, він/вона (Persons)

While it is important to understand the full conjugation table, including the plural forms (**ми**, **ви**, **вони**), we need to be practical. As a beginner, you do not need to master all six forms perfectly on day one.

Instead, we will focus our energy on the three most-used forms: **я** (I), **ти** (you singular/informal), and **він/вона** (he/she). In fact, these three singular forms cover about 90% of basic A1 conversations. When you meet someone new, you talk about yourself, you ask them questions, and you might mention a mutual friend or family member.

Let us lock in these three critical endings:
* **Я** always takes the **-ю** ending (e.g., **я читаю**).
* **Ти** always takes the **-єш** ending (e.g., **ти читаєш**).
* **Він/вона** always takes the **-є** ending (e.g., **він читає**).

Now we can start building real sentences by combining these verbs with the vocabulary you already know. In previous modules, you learned nouns and adjectives. Now we can put them into action.

* **Я читаю нову́ книгу.** (I am reading a new book.)
* **Ти знаєш цю пі́сню?** (Do you know this song?)
* **Вона слухає українську музику.** (She is listening to Ukrainian music.)
* **Я працюю, а він гуляє.** (I am working, and he is walking.)
* **Ти готуєш смачну́ вечерю.** (You are cooking a tasty dinner.)

You might notice something interesting happening to the nouns at the end of these sentences. The word for book is **книга**, but in the sentence it becomes **книгу**. The word for song is **пісня**, but it becomes **пісню**.

This happens because these objects are receiving the action of the verb. In Ukrainian, words change their endings to show their role in the sentence. For now, do not worry about the complex grammar rules behind this. Just learn these combinations as solid blocks or chunks of language. When you read a book, you say **читаю книгу**. When you listen to music, you say **слухаю музику**. Treating them as fixed phrases is the fastest way to sound natural.

Let us look at how you can use the verb **малювати** (to draw) and **грати** (to play), which both follow the Group I pattern perfectly.

* **Я малю́ю карти́ну.** (I am drawing a picture.)
* **Він гра́є у футбо́л.** (He is playing football.)

Whenever you want to talk about your daily routine, you will reach for these forms. Whether you are working (**я працюю**), studying (**я вивчаю**), or just walking in the park (**я гуляю**), the **-ю** ending is your best friend.

## Підсумок — Summary

With the First Conjugation (**Перша дієвідміна**) in your toolkit, you are no longer limited to pointing at things or describing them. You can now express action, routine, and daily life — the core of real conversation.

Let us review the core engine of Group I verbs. When a verb's dictionary form ends in **-ати** (like **читати** or **знати**), it follows a clear, consistent pattern in the present tense. You simply remove the **-ти** and attach the personal endings.

The pattern you must memorize is:
* я **-ю**
* ти **-єш**
* він/вона **-є**
* ми **-ємо**
* ви **-єте**
* вони **-ють**

This single pattern unlocks a huge portion of the Ukrainian language. It works flawlessly for **читати** (to read), **знати** (to know), **слухати** (to listen), and **гуляти** (to walk). It also works for verbs ending in **-увати** like **працювати** (to work) and **готувати** (to cook), with the small adjustment that **-ува-** becomes **-у-** before you add the endings (**працюю**, **готуєш**).

As a beginner, your primary mission is to automate the singular forms. When talking about yourself, instinctively reach for the **-ю** ending. When asking someone a question directly, use the **-єш** ending. When gossiping about a third person, the **-є** ending is what you need.

Test yourself right now with these three challenges:

1. Can you conjugate **слухати** for all three persons? You should immediately think: **я слухаю**, **ти слухаєш**, **він слухає**.
2. Now try **гуляти**: **я гуляю**, **ти гуляєш**, **вона гуляє**. Did you get all three?
3. Say what you do every day — for example: **Я читаю книгу. Я слухаю музику. Я працюю.**
4. Now ask someone else: **Що ти робиш? Де ти працюєш? Ти знаєш українську?**

If you can produce these forms without hesitation, you are ready for Module 17, where you will meet **Друга дієвідміна** (Group II) — verbs like **робити** that follow a different, but equally logical, pattern.
