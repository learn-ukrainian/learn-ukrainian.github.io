## Що ми зна́ємо? (What Do We Know?)

Imagine you arrive in Kyiv. You need to read a train schedule, agree on a time to meet a friend for coffee, and decide whether to wear a warm coat or take an umbrella. A few modules ago, this would have been completely impossible. Now, you have the exact vocabulary and grammatical structures to handle these everyday situations. 

This checkpoint brings together everything from the **Time and Nature** phase. You now have the tools to organize your day, schedule meetings, and talk about the world outside. Language is fundamentally about connection, and coordinating time and place is how we connect with others. You have learned how to structure sentences using simple present tense verbs, but more importantly, you have learned the vocabulary that gives those verbs context. A verb like "I work" is much more useful when you can add "on Monday" or "from nine to five." 

Let us review the core questions you can now answer confidently.

- **Котра годи́на?** (What time is it?)
- **О котрі́й?** (At what time?)
- **Який сього́дні день?** (What day is it today?)
- **Яка сьогодні пого́да?** (What is the weather like today?)
- **Коли твій день наро́дження?** (When is your birthday?)
- **Що ти ро́биш у субо́ту?** (What are you doing on Saturday?)
- **Як ча́сто ти гуля́єш?** (How often do you walk?)

These questions form the absolute foundation of daily conversations. Whether you are buying a bus ticket, arranging a coffee date, or just making small talk about the rain, these structures are essential. You will use them every single day in a Ukrainian-speaking environment.

Before we move on to new topics like navigating a city and asking for directions, we need to make sure these calendar and time chunks are automatic. You do not want to think about grammar when someone asks you for the time. You want to respond naturally and immediately with **О десятій** (At ten) or **У вівторок** (On Tuesday). Review your notes, practice the dialogues out loud, and make these phrases part of your active vocabulary.

## Чита́ння (Reading Practice)

Let us see how all these pieces fit together in a complete story. Read this short text about a typical week. Notice how the time chunks, days, and sequence words connect the ideas into a smooth narrative. 

> Мій ти́ждень ду́же акти́вний і ціка́вий. *(My week is very active and interesting.)*
> У понеді́лок я працю́ю з дев'я́тої до п'я́тої. *(On Monday I work from nine to five.)*
> Споча́тку я п'ю ка́ву. *(First I drink coffee.)*
> По́тім я чита́ю докуме́нти. *(Then I read documents.)*
> У вівторок я вивча́ю украї́нську мо́ву. *(On Tuesday I study the Ukrainian language.)*
> Урок почина́ється о сьо́мій ве́чора. *(The lesson starts at seven in the evening.)*
> Мої́ вихідні́ за́вжди́ дуже ціка́ві. *(My weekends are always very interesting.)*
> Ме́ні подо́бається о́сінь. *(I like autumn.)*
> Восени́ надво́рі те́пло і со́нячно. *(In autumn it is warm and sunny outside.)*
> Я часто гуля́ю в парку. *(I often walk in the park.)*
> Взи́мку надворі дуже хо́лодно. *(In winter it is very cold outside.)*
> Часто іде́ сніг. *(Often it snows.)*
> Взимку я ходжу́ в кіно́. *(In winter I go to the cinema.)*
> Наре́шті в неді́лю я про́сто відпочива́ю. *(Finally on Sunday I just rest.)*

This text uses simple sentences, but it communicates a massive amount of information. The secret is the vocabulary of sequence and frequency. When you tell a story, you cannot just list random facts. You need to guide the listener through time. Words like **спочатку** (first), **потім** (then), and **нарешті** (finally) give your story structure. They show the exact order of events clearly.

Words like **завжди** (always) and **часто** (often) add crucial detail about your habits. They transform a basic statement into a personal description of your lifestyle. Notice how the prepositions **у** and **в** are used constantly: **у понеділок** (on Monday), **влітку** (in summer). These small words are the glue that holds the Ukrainian sentence together.

## Грама́тика (Grammar Summary)

To talk about time and nature accurately, you need to remember a few key grammar patterns. In Ukrainian, prepositions change the endings of the words that follow them. You do not need to memorize complex grammatical tables or case names yet. Instead, learn these as fixed vocabulary chunks. When you learn the word for Friday, learn the phrase "on Friday" at the exact same time.

### 1. Telling Time
When asking for the current time, use the question **Котра година?** (What time is it?). The answer is a simple ordinal number. When asking *at what time* an event happens, use **О котрій?** (At what time?). The answer requires the preposition **о** or **об** and a different ending. This distinction is critical for clear communication.

- **Зараз п'ята година.** (It is five o'clock now.)
- **Зустріч о п'ятій.** (The meeting is at five.)
- **Зараз одина́дцята година.** (It is eleven o'clock now.)
- **Обід об одина́дцятій.** (Lunch is at eleven.)
- **Зараз пе́рша година.** (It is one o'clock now.)
- **Урок о пе́ршій.** (The lesson is at one.)

### 2. Days of the Week
To say that something happens on a specific day, use the preposition **у** or **в**. The days of the week change their endings slightly. Learn these as single units of meaning so you can recall them instantly.

- **понеділок** → **у понеділок** (on Monday)
- **вівторок** → **у вівторок** (on Tuesday)
- **середа** → **в середу** (on Wednesday)
- **субота** → **в суботу** (on Saturday)
- **неділя** → **в неділю** (on Sunday)

### 3. Months and Seasons
Months also use the preposition **у** or **в**, but they take the locative case ending **-і**. Seasons, however, are single adverbs that answer the question **коли?** (when?). They do not need a preposition at all.

- **січень** → **у січні** (in January)
- **травень** → **у травні** (in May)
- **серпень** → **у серпні** (in August)
- **зима** → **взимку** (in winter)
- **весна** → **навесні** (in spring)
- **літо** → **влітку** (in summer)
- **осінь** → **восени** (in autumn)

### 4. Weather
Weather descriptions often use short, impersonal adverbs without a subject like the English "it". You simply state the condition directly. For rain and snow, Ukrainian uses the active verb **іде** (goes).

- **Сьогодні холодно.** (It is cold today.)
- **Надворі дуже тепло.** (It is very warm outside.)
- **Зараз спекотно.** (It is hot now.)
- **Сьогодні іде дощ.** (It is raining today.)
- **Взимку іде сніг.** (It snows in winter.)

### 5. Sequence and Frequency
When you tell a story, sequence words organize your ideas. Frequency words describe your habits. They make your Ukrainian sound much more natural and mature.

- **Спочатку я працюю.** (First I work.)
- **Потім я відпочиваю.** (Then I rest.)
- **Нарешті я вдо́ма.** (Finally I am at home.)
- **Я завжди читаю.** (I always read.)
- **Вона рі́дко гуля́є.** (She rarely walks.)
- **Я ніколи не запі́знююся.** (I never am late.)

## Діало́г (Connected Dialogue)

Now let us look at a real-world situation. Two friends are planning a weekend outing. They need to discuss the day, the time, and the weather to make a decision. This is not just a grammar exercise; it is exactly how you will interact with friends in Ukraine. Read the dialogue and notice how naturally the questions and answers flow back and forth. 

<div class="dialogue">


**Максим:** Яка за́втра погода? *(What is the weather like tomorrow?)*


**Анна:** Тепло і сонячно. *(Warm and sunny.)*


**Максим:** Чудо́во! Ході́мо в парк! О котрій? *(Great! Let's go to the park! At what time?)*


**Анна:** О десятій ранку. *(At ten in the morning.)*


**Максим:** До́бре! Я часто гуляю в суботу. *(Good! I often walk on Saturday.)*


**Анна:** А потім ходімо в кіно! *(And then let's go to the cinema!)*


**Максим:** О п'ятій? *(At five?)*


**Анна:** Так! *(Yes!)*


</div>


In this conversation, Maksym initiates the plan by asking about tomorrow. He checks the weather because they want to go to the park. The phrase **Ходімо в парк!** (Let's go to the park!) is a very common and friendly way to suggest an activity. They coordinate the exact time using the chunk **О десятій ранку** (At ten in the morning). Notice how they add **ранку** (of the morning) to be precise, just like saying "AM".

Anna suggests a second activity using the sequence word **потім** (then). They confirm the time for the movie using **О п'ятій** (At five). 

Notice how much can be achieved with very few words. When you know the correct grammatical chunks, you do not need to build long, complicated sentences. The chunk **О десятій ранку** stands alone as a complete, perfectly natural answer. You do not need to say "We will meet at ten in the morning." This is a key principle of speaking naturally. Native speakers rely heavily on context and short, accurate phrases. This dialogue demonstrates how you combine vocabulary from different lessons to achieve a real communicative goal.

## Підсумок — Summary

You have reached a major milestone in your language journey. The A1.4 phase is now complete, and your ability to communicate in Ukrainian has expanded significantly. Think back to your very first lessons. You are no longer limited to simple greetings and identifying objects. You can now place events in time, organize your schedule, and describe the conditions around you. 

Let us review exactly what you can do now.

First, you can talk about schedules with precise details. You know how to ask **Котра година?** (What time is it?) and understand the answer. You can schedule meetings, classes, and appointments using the crucial chunk **о котрій** (at what time). You can confidently list the days of the week, the twelve months of the year, and the four seasons. You know that events happen **у понеділок** (on Monday) or **взимку** (in winter). This allows you to plan both your immediate week and your long-term goals.

Second, you can describe the environment. You can look outside and confidently say **іде дощ** (it is raining) or **сьогодні тепло** (it is warm today). This is essential for planning daily activities, deciding what to wear, and making natural small talk with neighbors and colleagues.

Finally, you can tell a coherent, logical story about your day or week. You can link actions together using words like **спочатку** (first) and **потім** (then). You can explain how often you do your hobbies using frequency words like **завжди** (always) or **іноді** (sometimes). Your sentences are no longer isolated; they connect to form a narrative.
