# Food, Drinks & Shopping

## Розминка: Що ми їмо та п'ємо?

### Eating and Drinking: Our Daily Routine
In our previous lessons, we learned how to handle numbers and money. Now, it is time to use those skills for something very practical: shopping for food! Every day, we engage in the simple but essential acts of eating and drinking. In Ukraine, food is not just fuel; it is a way to show hospitality and connect with others. When you visit someone's home, you will often hear questions about what you would like to have. Starting with these basic habits helps us build the foundation for any social interaction. We will focus on the simple things we consume at home or buy at a small local store. Let’s look at how we describe these daily actions in Ukrainian.

In this section, we use simple phrases to describe what is on our table. We talk about «хліб» — [xlʲib] (bread), «молоко» — [mɔ.lɔ.ˈkɔ] (milk), and «вода» — [ʋɔ.ˈda] (water). These are the building blocks of a typical day. Ukrainian families value fresh ingredients, and you will see people buying these items every morning.

_Приклади:_
- **Ми їмо хліб.** — [mɪ ˈjis.mɔ xlʲib] — We eat bread.
- **Ти п'єш воду.** — [tɪ pjeʃ ʋɔ.ˈdu] — You drink water.
- **Вона їсть яблуко.** — [ʋɔ.ˈna jistʲ ˈjab.lu.kɔ] — She eats an apple.

### The Action Verbs: To Eat, To Drink, and To Love
To talk about our food habits, we need three primary verbs: «їсти» (to eat), «пити» (to drink), and «любити» (to love). In Ukrainian, these verbs belong to different groups, and their endings change based on the person. The verb «їсти» — [ˈjis.tɪ] is unique because it has very short forms. The verb «пити» — [ˈpɪ.tɪ] changes its stem slightly when we conjugate it. Finally, «любити» — [lʲu.ˈbɪ.tɪ] is essential for expressing what we enjoy. Notice how the letter «л» — [lʲ] appears in the "I" form of «любити». This is a common pattern for many Ukrainian verbs.

| Person | **їсти** (to eat) | **пити** (to drink) | **любити** (to love) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Я** (I) | **їм** — [jim] | **п'ю** — [pju] | **люблю** — [lʲu.ˈblʲu] |
| **Ти** (You) | **їси** — [ji.ˈsɪ] | **п'єш** — [pjeʃ] | **любиш** — [ˈlʲu.bɪʃ] |
| **Він/Вона** (He/She) | **їсть** — [jistʲ] | **п'є** — [pje] | **любить** — [ˈlʲu.bɪtʲ] |
| **Ми** (We) | **їмо** — [ji.ˈmɔ] | **п'ємо** — [pje.ˈmɔ] | **любимо** — [ˈlʲu.bɪ.mɔ] |
| **Ви** (You pl.) | **їсте** — [ji.ˈstɛ] | **п'єте** — [pje.ˈtɛ] | **любите** — [ˈlʲu.bɪ.tɛ] |
| **Вони** (They) | **їдять** — [ji.ˈdʲatʲ] | **п'ють** — [pjutʲ] | **люблять** — [ˈlʲu.blʲatʲ] |

Using these verbs is straightforward. You simply place the verb after the person and follow it with the food or drink. We keep our sentences short and clear.

### Expressing Preferences: My Favorite Drinks
When we use the verb «любити» (to love) to talk about drinks, the words for those drinks might change their endings. This happens because the drink is the object of our action. This is called the **Accusative case**. For words ending in «-а», like «кава» — [ˈka.ʋa] (coffee) or «вода» — [ʋɔ.ˈda] (water), the ending changes to «-у». However, words like «сік» — [sʲik] (juice) or «чай» — [tʃaj] (tea) stay exactly the same. We use these patterns to tell our friends what we want to drink.

[!tip]
In Ukrainian, when you love a feminine word (ending in **-а**), it changes to **-у**.
- **кава** (coffee) → Я люблю **каву**.
- **вода** (water) → Я люблю **воду**.
Masculine drinks like **сік** (juice) or **чай** (tea) do not change their endings!

_Приклади:_
- **Я люблю каву.** — [ja lʲu.ˈblʲu ˈka.ʋu] — I love coffee.
- **Ми не любимо сік.** — [mɪ nɛ ˈlʲu.bɪ.mɔ sʲik] — We do not love juice.
- **Він п'є тепле молоко.** — [ʋin pje ˈtɛ.plɛ mɔ.lɔ.ˈkɔ] — He drinks warm milk.

Let's look at a quick conversation you might have at a neighbor's house:
— **Ти любиш чай?** (Do you love tea?)
— **Ні, я люблю каву.** (No, I love coffee.)
— **Будь ласка, кава і цукор.** (Please, coffee and sugar.)
## Презентація: Продукти та покупки

### Смачні овочі та фрукти
After a nice coffee with sugar at your neighbor's house, you might need to go and buy some more food. Ukrainians love fresh produce! **Ви йдете на ринок.** (You go to the market.) **Ви бачите багато продуктів.** (You see many products.) It is easy to find the basics for a good dinner or a healthy snack. **Овочі та фрукти корисні.** (Vegetables and fruits are healthy.) **Це дуже смачно.** (This is very tasty.)

**Овочі** — [ˈɔwɔtʃʲi] (vegetables) are essential for Ukrainian cooking.
**картопля** — [kɐrˈtɔplʲɐ] (potato)
**помідор** — [pomiˈdɔr] (tomato)

**Фрукти** — [ˈfrʊktɪ] (fruits) are great for a quick energy boost.
**яблуко** — [ˈjɑblʊkɔ] (apple)
**банан** — [bɐˈnɑn] (banana)

In a sentence, these words stay simple. **Тут є свіжі овочі.** (There are fresh vegetables here.)
*   **Це велика картопля.** (This is a big potato.)
*   **Ось червоний помідор.** (Here is a red tomato.)
*   **Я люблю солодке яблуко.** (I love a sweet apple.)
*   **Ви купуєте яблуко та банан.** (You buy an apple and a banana.)

Notice how we describe them. We use the **Називний відмінок** (Nominative case) when we simply name the items on the shelf. **Це смачний фрукт.** (This is a tasty fruit.)

### М'ясо та молочні продукти
Now let’s move to the colder sections of the store. Protein and dairy are very important for the Ukrainian diet. **Там ви купуєте м’ясо та молоко.** (There you buy meat and milk.) You will often find dedicated counters for these. **Тут є молоко та йогурт.** (There is milk and yogurt here.)

**М'ясо** — [ˈmɲɑsɔ] (meat) comes in many forms.
**курка** — [ˈkʊrkɐ] (chicken)
**свинина** — [swɪˈnɪnɐ] (pork)
**риба** — [ˈrɪbɐ] (fish)

**Молочні продукти** — [mɔˈlɔtʃnʲi prɔˈdʊktɪ] (dairy products) are a staple in every home.
**молоко** — [mɔlɔˈkɔ] (milk)
**сир** — [sɪr] (cheese/cottage cheese)
**йогурт** — [ˈjɔɦʊrt] (yogurt)
**яйце** — [jɐjˈtsɛ] (egg)

**Це свіже м'ясо.** (This is fresh meat.) **Риба — це теж продукт.** (Fish is also a product.)
*   **Це біла курка.** (This is white chicken.)
*   **Там є холодне молоко.** (There is cold milk there.)
*   **Я люблю смачний сир.** (I love tasty cheese.)
*   **Ось одне свіже яйце.** (Here is one fresh egg.)

**Молочні продукти смачні.** (Dairy products are tasty.) In Ukraine, **сир** can mean both hard cheese and soft cottage cheese. Usually, the context tells you which one is on the table!

### Одиниці виміру та упаковка
When we shop, we don't just point. We need to say how much we want! **Ми говоримо: один кілограм або один літр.** (We say: one kilogram or one liter.) This makes shopping much easier and more precise. **Я хочу один літр.** (I want one liter.)

**Одиниці виміру** — [ɔdɪˈnɪtsʲi wɪˈmʲirʊ] (units of measurement) help us weigh our food.
**кілограм** — [kʲilɔˈɦrɑm] (kilogram)
**літр** — [lʲitr] (liter)

**Упаковка** — [ʊpɐˈkɔwkɐ] (packaging) tells us how items are sold.
**пляшка** — [ˈplʲɑʃkɐ] (bottle)
**пачка** — [ˈpɑtʃkɐ] (pack)
**батон** — [bɐˈtɔn] (loaf/long white bread)

*   **Ось один кілограм цукру.** (Here is one kilogram of sugar.)
*   **Це одна пляшка води.** (This is one bottle of water.)
*   **Будь ласка, одна пачка масла.** (Please, one pack of butter.)
*   **Він купує один батон.** (He buys one loaf.)

> [!context]
> In Ukraine, we use the metric system. **Ми купуємо молоко в літрах.** (We buy milk in liters.) **Ми купуємо картоплю в кілограмах.** (We buy potatoes in kilograms.) For bread, we often ask for a **батон** or a **хліб**. A **батон** — [bɐˈtɔn] is usually white, long bread.

### Ціни та гривні
Now it is time to talk about money! To find out the price, we use a simple phrase. **Ви питаєте: скільки це коштує?** (You ask: how much does this cost?)

**Скільки коштує?** — [ˈskʲilʲkɪ ˈkɔʃtʊje] (How much does it cost?)

The currency in Ukraine is the **гривня** — [ˈɦrɪwnʲɐ].
*   **Скільки коштує хліб?** (How much does the bread cost?)
*   **Скільки коштує молоко?** (How much does the milk cost?)
*   **Це коштує десять гривень.** (This costs ten hryvnias.)

| Ukrainian | IPA | English |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Скільки коштує? | [ˈskʲilʲkɪ ˈkɔʃtʊje] | How much does it cost? |
| гривня | [ˈɦrɪwnʲɐ] | hryvnia |
| ціна | [tsʲiˈnɑ] | price |

**Тут висока ціна.** (The price is high here.) Always look for the price tag! It usually has the price per kilogram or per pack. **Це дешевий помідор.** (This is a cheap tomato.)

### Граматика: Що ви купуєте?
When we take an action, like buying, we use a special grammar form. This is the **Знахідний відмінок** (Accusative case). **Це наш прямий додаток.** (This is our direct object.)

For many masculine words like **хліб** or **сік**, the word does not change.
*   **Я купую хліб.** (I buy bread.)
*   **Ти купуєш сік.** (You buy juice.)
*   **Ми купуємо цукор.** (We buy sugar.)

**купувати** — [kʊpʊˈwɑtɪ] (to buy)
**я купую** — [jɑ kʊˈpʊjʊ] (I buy)

When you are the "subject" and the bread is the "object", the bread stays the same. **Сьогодні я купую сир.** (Today I buy cheese.)
*   **Він купує батон.** (He buys a loaf.)
*   **Вона купує сир.** (She buys cheese.)
*   **Вони купують м'ясо.** (They buy meat.)

**Ми купуємо продукти.** (We buy products.) Using this case correctly helps people understand exactly what you are doing in the shop!
## Практика: У магазині та культурні традиції

### Складання списку покупок
When you prepare to go to the store, it is helpful to have a **список покупок** (shopping list). In Ukrainian, we often think about what we have and what we need. When we talk about a specific quantity of an item, like a «кілограм» (kilogram) or a «пляшка» (bottle), we use the **Genitive case** for the food itself. This is because we are saying «a kilogram *of* apples» or «a bottle *of* water». For masculine and neuter nouns, the ending often changes to **-а** or **-я**, while feminine nouns change to **-и** or **-і**.

Let's look at how we build these phrases:
- **кілограм яблук** — [kiloˈɦram ˈjabluk] (a kilogram of apples)
- **пляшка води** — [ˈplʲaʃka woˈdɪ] (a bottle of water)
- **пачка цукру** — [ˈpatʃka ˈtsukru] (a pack of sugar)

Notice that for sugar, we use **цукру** — [ˈtsukru]. Using these correct endings helps you sound like a natural shopper!

**Приклади:**
- Я купую **кілограм яблук**. (I buy a kilogram of apples.)
- Ти купуєш **літр молока**. (You buy a liter of milk.)
- Ми маємо **пачку цукру**. (We have a pack of sugar.)

### Діалог: У продуктовому магазині
When you are finally at the store, you might need to speak with the **продавець** (shopkeeper). Standard phrases make the process smooth and polite. You can start by asking for what you need or asking about the price. Remember, in Ukraine, we pay in **гривні** — [ˈɦrɪwnʲi] (hryvnias).

Here is a simple dialogue you might hear at a local shop:
- **Покупець:** Добрий день! Скільки коштує це молоко? (Buyer: Good day! How much does this milk cost?)
- **Продавець:** Добрий день! Воно коштує тридцять гривень. (Seller: Good day! It costs thirty hryvnias.)
- **Покупець:** Добре. Я купую пляшку молока і батон хліба. (Buyer: Good. I am buying a bottle of milk and a loaf of bread.)
- **Продавець:** Будь ласка. Це сорок гривень. (Seller: Here you go. That is forty hryvnias.)

Using these phrases helps you practice both your numbers and your new food vocabulary in a real-world setting.

[!tip]
In Ukrainian shops, it is common to say «Будь ласка» — [budʲ ˈlaska] when giving money or receiving a product. It translates to «Please», but in this context, it works like «Here you go».

### Українські традиції: Хліб на столі
In Ukraine, **хліб** — [xlʲib] (bread) is much more than just food. It is a symbol of life, hospitality, and home. There is an old saying: «Хліб — усьому голова» (Bread is the head of everything), meaning it is the most important thing on the table. When guests arrive, they are traditionally greeted with «хліб і сіль» (bread and salt) on a beautiful embroidered towel called a «рушник».

Different types of bread are used for different occasions:
- **Батон** — [baˈtɔn]: A white, oblong loaf common for daily sandwiches.
- **Паляниця** — [palʲɐˈnɪtsʲɐ]: A traditional round hearth bread, now a famous symbol of Ukrainian identity.
- **Чорний хліб** — [ˈtʃɔrnɪj xlʲib]: Dark rye bread, perfect for eating with a bowl of hot borscht.

[!culture]
Ukrainian borscht is never complete without bread. Most people eat it with rye bread and sometimes «пампушки» — [pamˈpuʃkɪ], which are small garlic rolls. This combination is a true taste of Ukraine!

### Готуємо вечерю
Now that we have our ingredients, what can we make? A typical Ukrainian dinner is simple but hearty. We use **картопля** — [karˈtɔplʲa] (potatoes) as a base for many dishes. You might buy a **кілограм картоплі** (a kilogram of potatoes) and some **м'ясо** — [ˈmjasɔ] (meat) to make a delicious roast.

Think about what is in your bag:
- **сир** — [sɪr] (cheese)
- **яйце** — [jajˈtsɛ] (egg)
- **курка** — [ˈkurka] (chicken)

With these items, you can prepare a wonderful meal for your family or friends. Learning these words allows you to follow simple recipes and understand what goes into famous dishes like «вареники» — [waˈrɛnɪkɪ].

**Приклади:**
- Мама готує **м'ясо**. (Mom is cooking meat.)
- Тато купує **сир**. (Dad is buying cheese.)
- Я люблю **свіжі яйця**. (I love fresh eggs.)

## Summary
У цьому модулі ми навчилися купувати їжу. Тепер ви знаєте:
1.  Назви продуктів: **хліб, молоко, м'ясо, овочі, фрукти**.
2.  Дієслова: **їсти, пити, любити**.
3.  Знахідний відмінок для напоїв: **Я люблю каву** (не *кава*), але **Я люблю чай**.
4.  Одиниці виміру: **кілограм, літр, пляшка, пачка**.
5.  Як запитати ціну та купити продукти в магазині.

Смачного! (Bon appetit!)
