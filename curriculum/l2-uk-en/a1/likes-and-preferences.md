# Likes and Preferences

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> У цій темі ми вивчаємо важливі слова. Ми говоримо: я люблю, я хочу.
> In this topic, we learn important words. We say: I love, I want.

## Мені подобається (I like)

In English, the verb «to like» works exactly like most other action verbs. You say «I like coffee.» The person is the active subject, and the coffee is the direct object. In Ukrainian, expressing that you like something works backward. You do not perform a direct action on the object. Instead, the object is pleasing to you. We use the phrase **мені подобається**. Learning this structure early will help you understand how the Ukrainian language builds sentences differently from English.

The word **мені** translates literally as «to me.» It is a set phrase that you can just memorize as a single block right now. Similarly, the word **тобі** means «to you.» We use these specific words when we talk about feelings, impressions, and personal preferences.

Because the sentence is inverted, the person is simply the receiver of the feeling. The thing that is liked is the actual grammatical subject of the sentence.

Because the thing you like is the subject, the verb must perfectly agree with it. If you like one single thing, you use the singular form of the verb.

**Мені подобається кава.**
I like coffee.

**Мені подобається музика.**
I like music.

**Тобі подобається кава.**
You like coffee.

If you like multiple things, the verb must change to match a plural subject. The verb becomes **подобаються**. Notice the small change at the very end of the word. English does not change the verb «like» for plural objects, so this is a new concept for you.

**Мені подобаються книги.**
I like books.

**Тобі подобаються фільми.**
You like films.

You can also use this helpful structure with action verbs. If you want to say that you like doing an activity, you use the infinitive form of the action verb. The infinitive is the basic dictionary form of a verb, which almost always ends in the letters -ти. When you use an infinitive, you always use the singular verb form.

**Мені подобається читати.**
I like to read.

**Мені подобається їсти.**
I like to eat.

**Тобі подобається читати.**
You like to read.

> [!warning]
> Це дуже важливе правило. Ми ніколи не використовуємо слово «я» тут.
> This is a very important rule. We never use the word «я» here.
> Many beginners try to use the word «я» (I) when they talk about what they like. Remember that the feeling happens «to you». Always start your sentence with **мені** or **тобі** when using this verb.

Ця структура дуже цікава. Вона базова для української мови. Ми вчимо її зараз. Вона дуже важлива. Ми часто говоримо про музику, книги, фільми і спорт.
This structure is very interesting. It is basic for the Ukrainian language. We learn it now. It is very important. We often talk about music, books, films, and sports.
Закінчення слів змінюються. Це цікаво знати і легко вивчити.
Word endings change. It is interesting to know this and easy to learn.

## Я люблю (I love)

Sometimes, simply liking something is not enough. When you have a strong passion or deep affection, you need a stronger word. For this, we use the verb **любити** (to love).

This is a regular Class II verb. Let us look at how it changes in the present tense. 
Це дуже популярне слово в Україні. Ми використовуємо його кожного дня.
This is a very popular word in Ukraine. We use it every day.

**Я люблю**
I love

**Ти любиш**
You love

**Він любить**
He loves

Unlike the previous phrase, this verb works exactly like English. The person is the active subject, and the thing you love is the direct object. Because the thing is the direct object, it must take the Accusative case. For many feminine nouns that end in -а, this means the ending changes to -у.

**Я люблю каву.**
I love coffee.

**Я люблю музику.**
I love music.

**Ти любиш маму.**
You love mom.

Notice how **кава** became **каву**, and **музика** became **музику**. This small change shows that the word is receiving the action of your love. You are the subject performing the action.

You can also use this verb with other actions. Just add the infinitive form of the second verb.

**Я люблю читати.**
I love to read.

**Ти любиш їсти.**
You love to eat.

**Я люблю піти.**
I love to go.

**Я дуже люблю читати цікаву книгу.**
I really love to read an interesting book.

**Ми любимо слухати музику.**
We love to listen to music.

**Він любить смачну каву.**
He loves tasty coffee.

There is an important difference in meaning between our two new phrases. They are not perfectly interchangeable.

> [!culture]
> Українці використовують обидва слова. Але вони мають різні значення.
> Ukrainians use both words. But they have different meanings.
> The word **подобається** describes an aesthetic appeal or a state of finding something pleasant. You might use it for an interesting book or a good lecture. The word **люблю** describes a strong active enjoyment, a deep emotional connection, or a permanent passion. Use **подобається** when you are tasting a new dish for the first time and you find it pleasant. Use **люблю** when talking about your absolute favorite meal that you could eat every single day.

**Мені подобається цікава книга.**
I find the interesting book appealing.

**Я люблю цікаву книгу.**
I love the interesting book.

Це сильне слово. Воно показує ваші емоції.
This is a strong word. It shows your emotions.

## Я хочу (I want)

Another very common way to express your personal preferences is to state what you want. We use the irregular verb **хотіти** (to want). It is slightly irregular, so you will need to memorize its present tense forms carefully. 
Ми дуже часто використовуємо це слово. В магазині ми хочемо купити хліб. В кафе ми хочемо їсти смачний борщ.
We use this word very often. In the shop, we want to buy bread. In the cafe, we want to eat tasty borsch.

**Я хочу**
I want

**Ти хочеш**
You want

**Ми хочемо**
We want

**Ви хочете**
You want (plural or polite)

Just like the verb for loving, you can use this verb with an action. You simply pair the conjugated form with an infinitive verb. You do not need to change the infinitive verb at all.

**Я хочу їсти.**
I want to eat.

**Я хочу піти.**
I want to go.

**Ти хочеш читати.**
You want to read.

You can also want specific objects. When you want an object, that object takes the Accusative case, just as we saw before. Feminine nouns ending in -а change their ending to -у.

**Я хочу каву.**
I want coffee.

**Я хочу смачний борщ.**
I want tasty borsch.

**Ти хочеш музику.**
You want music.

**Ми хочемо смачний чай.**
We want tasty tea.

**Він хоче читати книгу.**
He wants to read a book.

**Вона хоче піти в кафе.**
She wants to go to a cafe.

Remember that the previous phrase **мені подобається** does not use the Accusative case because the object is the grammatical subject. This is a very important grammatical difference between the verbs.

> [!note]
> Це слово звучить дуже прямо. Але це нормально в Україні.
> This word sounds very direct. But this is normal in Ukraine.
> In English, saying «I want» can sometimes sound rude or demanding. People often say «I would like» instead. In Ukrainian daily life, using **Я хочу** is completely polite and natural. You do not need to worry about sounding impolite when you order food or express a desire.

Це дуже корисне слово. Ми використовуємо його часто.
This is a very useful word. We use it often.
Воно допомагає в кафе.
It helps in a cafe.

## Порівняння (Comparing Likes)

Once you can express your own preferences, you will want to ask other people about theirs. Learning how to ask questions allows you to compare likes and build natural conversations.
Це дуже цікаво. Ми часто запитуємо друзів.
This is very interesting. We often ask friends.

You can start by asking direct questions using our new verbs. The sentence structure remains exactly the same as a normal statement. You simply change your intonation to make it sound like a question.

**Тобі подобається музика?**
Do you like music?

**Ти любиш читати?**
Do you love to read?

**Ви хочете каву?**
Do you want coffee?

If you have already stated your own preference, you can use a shorter question to ask for the other person's opinion. We use the conjunction **а**, which means «and» or «but» when contrasting two things.

**А ти?**
And you?



Let us look at two short dialogues to see how this works in real conversations about food and free time.

**Діалог перший**
Dialogue one

— Мені подобається кава. А тобі?
— I like coffee. And you?
— А я люблю смачний борщ.
— And I love tasty borsch.
— Ти хочеш їсти?
— Do you want to eat?
— Так, я хочу борщ.
— Yes, I want borsch.

**Діалог другий**
Dialogue two

— Ти любиш читати?
— Do you love to read?
— Так, я люблю читати. А ти?
— Yes, I love to read. And you?
— Мені подобається музика.
— I like music.

**Діалог третій**
Dialogue three

— Ти хочеш каву?
— Do you want coffee?
— Ні, я не хочу каву. Мені подобається чай.
— No, I do not want coffee. I like tea.
— А я люблю каву.
— And I love coffee.
— Ти любиш музику?
— Do you love music?
— Так, я дуже люблю музику. А ти?
— Yes, I really love music. And you?
— І я люблю.

> [!tip]
> Маленьке слово «а» дуже корисне. Воно створює м'який контраст.
> The small word «а» is very useful. It creates a soft contrast.
> You can use it to smoothly bounce the conversation back and forth between speakers.
