# Reflexive Verbs (-ся)

## Розминка: Повернення дії на себе

Уявіть: ви дивитеся у дзеркало (Imagine: you look into a mirror). You see yourself and you begin your daily routine. In Ukrainian, when we talk about actions that we do to ourselves, we use a special tool called **зворотні дієслова** (reflexive verbs). Think of these verbs as a «boomerang» — the action starts with you, travels out, and then comes right back to you!

### The Magic Mirror of -ся
In English, we often use words like «myself» or «yourself» to show that an action returns to the subject. For example, you say «I wash myself.» In Ukrainian, we don't need a separate word like «myself» for every person. Instead, we use a tiny, powerful suffix: **-ся**. This suffix is like a little mirror attached to the end of the verb.

When you see a verb ending in **-ся**, you know the subject is performing the action on their own body or mind. Let's look at the verb **мити** (to wash). If you wash your car, you use the plain verb. But if you wash your own face, you use the reflexive form **митися**. It is a simple shift that changes everything!

*   **мити** — [ˈmɪtɪ] — to wash (something)
*   **митися** — [ˈmɪtɪsʲɐ] — to wash oneself

### A Piece of History in Your Pocket
You might wonder where this mysterious **-ся** comes from. It actually has a very logical history! Hundreds of years ago, Ukrainians used a separate word: **себе** [sɛˈbɛ]. This word means «self.» Over a long time, people spoke faster and faster, and **себе** eventually shrank down to just **-ся** and stuck to the end of the verb.

It is like a fossil of an ancient pronoun that is now part of the verb itself. Because it used to be a separate word, it always stays at the very end, even after we conjugate the verb for different people like «I,» «you,» or «they.» Understanding this «self» connection makes it much easier to remember why we use it for our morning habits!

> [!fact]
> Historically, the reflexive suffix was mobile and could move around the sentence. Today, in modern Ukrainian, it is strictly fixed to the end of the verb. You never have to guess where to put it!

### Doing vs. Self-Doing
To really master this, we need to see the difference between a **перехідне дієслово** (transitive verb) and a **зворотне дієслово** (reflexive verb). A transitive verb moves the action to an object. Я одягаю дитину. Я роблю дію для когось іншого. Але я одягаюся. Дія повертається.

In our daily lives, we use these «self-doing» verbs constantly. We wake up, we wash up, we dress up, and we even laugh! Notice that some verbs, like **сміятися** (to laugh), are always reflexive in Ukrainian because you can't really «laugh someone else» — the feeling stays inside you.

*   Я **одягаю** ляльку. (I dress a doll.)
*   Я **одягаюся** швидко. (I dress myself quickly.)
*   Він **миє** вікно. (He washes a window.)
*   Він **миється** зранку. (He washes himself in the morning.)
## Презентація: Правила вживання та відмінювання

### The Golden Rule: -ся vs. -сь
While the suffix **-ся** [sʲɐ] is the default «face» of reflexive verbs, it actually has a twin: **-сь** [sʲ]. Choosing between them is like choosing between a light coat and a heavy jacket — it all depends on what comes before it! The rule is purely phonetic, designed to keep the language flowing smoothly without awkward clusters of sounds.

If the verb form ends in a **приголосний** (consonant), we use the full suffix **-ся**. If it ends in a **голосний** (vowel), we use the shorter **-сь**. This «vowel-vowel avoidance» is a common feature in Ukrainian that makes your speech sound much more natural and rhythmic.

| Ending Type | Suffix | Example Verb | IPA |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Consonant** | **-ся** | вмиваються | [ʋmɪˈʋɑjutʲsʲɐ] |
| **Vowel** | **-сь** | вмиваюся | [ʋmɪˈʋɑjusʲ] |

*   Я **вмиваюся** зранку. (I wash myself in the morning.)
*   Ми **вмиваємося** разом. (We wash ourselves together.)
*   Вони **вмиваються** швидко. (They wash themselves quickly.)

> [!tip]
> In very formal writing or poetry, you might occasionally see **-ся** used after a vowel. However, for everyday speaking and standard modern Ukrainian, always follow the «Vowel likes -сь, Consonant likes -ся» rule to sound like a local!

### Conjugating in the Present Tense
Conjugating a **зворотне дієслово** (reflexive verb) might look intimidating at first, but here is a secret: you already know how to do it! You simply conjugate the base verb as you normally would, and then «glue» the correct suffix onto the very end. The suffix always sits outside the conjugation, like a little tail that never falls off.

Let's look at **вмиватися** (to wash oneself). Notice how the base verb **вмивати** changes its ending for each person, and then we add either **-ся** or **-сь** depending on whether that ending is a vowel or a consonant. Pay close attention to the **наголос** (stress) in the table below, as it stays on the same syllable throughout the pattern.

| Person | Base + Ending | Reflexive Suffix | Full Form |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Я** (I) | вмива-ю | **-сь** | **вмиваюся** |
| **Ти** (You sing.) | вмива-єш | **-ся** | **вмиваєшся** |
| **Він/Вона** (He/She) | вмива-ється | **-ся** | **вмивається** |
| **Ми** (We) | вмива-ємо | **-сь** | **вмиваємося** |
| **Ви** (You plur.) | вмива-єте | **-сь** | **вмиваєтеся** |
| **Вони** (They) | вмива-ють | **-ся** | **вмиваються** |

*   Ти **вмиваєшся** холодною водою. (You wash yourself with cold water.)
*   Вона **вмивається** милом. (She washes herself with soap.)
*   Ви **вмиваєтеся** дуже довго! (You are washing yourselves for a very long time!)

### Three Types of Reflexive Meanings
Не всі зворотні дієслова працюють однаково (Not all reflexive verbs work the same). We can group them into three categories. First, **власне-зворотні** (true reflexive), where you do the action to yourself. Наприклад, **вмиватися** або **одягатися**.

The second group consists of **взаємно-зворотні** (reciprocal) verbs. These describe an action that two or more people do to each other. You can't «greet yourself» or «meet yourself» in this sense; you need a partner! Common examples are **вітатися** (to greet each other) and **зустрічатися** (to meet each other). Finally, there are **лексикалізовані** (lexicalized) verbs. These are «reflexive by nature» — they always have the suffix, and their meaning doesn't necessarily imply «self.»

*   **одягатися** — [odʲɐˈɦɑtɪsʲɐ] — to dress oneself (True Reflexive)
*   **вітатися** — [ʋiˈtɑtɪsʲɐ] — to greet each other (Reciprocal)
*   **сміятися** — [smʲiˈjɑtɪsʲɐ] — to laugh (Lexicalized)

**Зверніть увагу:** (Pay attention:) Even though **сміятися** or **дивитися** end in **-ся**, we don't translate them as «laughing oneself.» Такі дієслова просто так створені (Such verbs are just created that way).

*   Він завжди **сміється**. (He always laughs.)
*   Ми **вітаємося** кожен день. (We greet each other every day.)
*   Я **дивлюся** цікаве кіно. (I am watching an interesting movie.)

### High-Frequency Verbs for Daily Life
To really feel comfortable in a Ukrainian-speaking environment, you should memorize a few «power verbs» that appear in almost every conversation. One of the most important is **називатися** (to be called/named). We use it for objects, titles, or sometimes even for ourselves in formal introductions.

> [!tip]
> While you can say **Як ви називаєтеся?** (What are you called?), it is more common to use **Як вас звати?**. Reserve **називатися** mostly for things, like **Це місто називається Київ** (This city is called Kyiv).

Another essential verb is **вчитися** (to study/learn). Unlike the plain verb for «to teach,» **вчитися** shows that the learning process is happening within you. You will also often hear **повертатися** (to return), whether someone is coming back home or returning to a topic in a conversation. Using these correctly will make your A1 Ukrainian sound much more sophisticated and polished.

*   Як **називається** це місто? (What is this city called?)
*   Я **вчуся** кожного дня. (I study every day.)
*   Він **повертається** додому пізно. (He returns home late.)

**називатися** — [nɐzɪˈʋɑtɪsʲɐ]
**вчитися** — [ˈʋtʃɪtɪsʲɐ]
**повертатися** — [poʋerˈtɑtɪsʲɐ]
## Практика: Відмінювання та вправи

Just as **сміятися** — [sʲmʲiˈjɑtɪsʲɐ] (to laugh) or **подобатися** — [poˈdɔbɐtɪsʲɐ] (to please) describe internal states, other reflexive verbs describe actions we perform on ourselves. To master these **дієслова** (verbs), we must focus on how the **суфікс** (suffix) behaves during **відмінювання** (conjugation). When we change the person, the suffix always stays at the very end. Think of it as a permanent attachment that travels with the verb throughout the sentence.

### Відмінювання: Практика з «я», «ти», «він»
The process of **відмінювання** (conjugation) for reflexive verbs follows the standard patterns you already know. You first conjugate the base **дієслово** (verb) according to the person, and then you attach the reflexive part. For example, look at the **дієслово** «одягатися» — [odʲɐˈɦɑtɪsʲɐ] (to dress oneself). Even when the verb ending changes for «ти» or «він», the suffix remains firmly attached. This **правило** (rule) is the key to accuracy in your **мовлення** (speech).

**Приклад** (Example):
- **Я одягаюся.** — I am dressing myself.
- **Ти одягаєшся.** — You are dressing yourself.
- **Він одягається.** — He is dressing himself.
- **Вона одягається.** — She is dressing herself.
- **Ми одягаємося.** — We are dressing ourselves.

Note how the stem conjugates normally like any Class I verb, and then we simply attach the reflexive part based on the vowel or consonant rule we learned earlier.

### Пари дієслів: Перехідні та зворотні
One of the most powerful features of the Ukrainian language is how a small suffix can transform the entire focus of a sentence. We call the verbs without the suffix **перехідні дієслова** (transitive verbs) because the action "crosses over" to someone else. When we add the **зворотний суфікс** (reflexive suffix), the action returns to the subject. This changes the **дієслово** (verb) from an outward force to an inward one (**дія на себе** — action on oneself).

Compare these pairs to see the difference in **значення** (meaning):
- **Вона вмиває дитину.** — She is washing the child.
- **Вона вмивається.** — [ʋmɪˈʋɑjɛtsʲɐ] — She is washing herself.
- **Я одягаю дитину.** — I am dressing the child.
- **Я одягаюся.** — I am dressing myself.
- **Я дивлюся фільм.** — [dɪˈʋlʲusʲɐ] — I am watching a movie.
- **Я займаюся спортом.** — [ja zɐjˈmɑjusʲ spɔrtɔm] — I am doing sports / I am engaged in sports.

In the first **речення** (sentence), the action is directed outward. In the second, the subject and the object are the same person. The verb **дивитися** — [dɪˈʋɪtɪsʲɐ] (to watch) is always reflexive because the act of looking is considered an internal sensory experience.

### Вибір суфікса: -ся чи -сь?
As we have seen, the choice between **-ся** and **-сь** depends on the sound that comes right before it. A consonant likes **-ся**, while a vowel prefers the shorter **-сь**. This **фонетичне правило** (phonetic rule) creates a rhythmic pattern within a single conjugation that helps the language flow smoothly. When you conjugate (**відмінюєте**), always check the last letter of your base verb form.

Let's look at the verb **вчитися** — [ˈʋtʃɪtɪsʲɐ] (to study/learn):
- **Я вчуся.** — I study (vowel + **-сь**).
- **Ми вчимося.** — We study (vowel + **-сь**).
- **Вони вчаться.** — They study (consonant + **-ся**).
- **Ти вчишся.** — You study (consonant + **-ся**).

[!observe] Soft Buzzing Sounds
The written cluster **-ться** sounds like a long soft **[ts:a]**. Similarly, the combination **-шся** in conversational speech sounds like a soft **[s:a]**. Listen carefully to these sounds to make your speech sound more natural and authentic.

This phonetic switch ensures that two vowels don't clash. Always listen to the ending before you add the reflexive part.

### Знайомство: «Називатися» та «знайомитися»
Now we can use these tools to build a simple **діалог** (dialogue). The verb **називатися** — [nɐzɪˈwɑtɪsʲɐ] (to be called) is perfect for objects or groups, while **знайомитися** — [znɐˈjɔmɪtɪsʲɐ] (to get acquainted) is essential for social life. These verbs help us navigate new environments (**нове середовище**) and meet new people in a polite and clear way (**ввічливо та ясно**).

**Речення** (Sentences):
- **Як називається це місто?** — What is this city called?
- **Ми знайомимося.** — We are getting acquainted.
- **Вони знайомляться.** — They are getting acquainted.
- **Я знайомлюся.** — I am getting acquainted.

[!warning] Common Suffix Loss
Learners often forget to keep the suffix when they conjugate. Remember: the **-ся** is part of the verb's identity. Without it, the meaning changes or becomes incomplete.
- Incorrect: *Я вмиваю* (I wash...)
- Correct: **Я вмиваюся** (I wash myself)
This is a very common mistake because many English equivalents don't use the word "self."
## Культурний аспект та особливості вимови

This loyalty of the **-ся** suffix is not just a grammar rule; it also shapes the beautiful sounds of the Ukrainian language. **Українська мова — це співуча мова** (Ukrainian is a singing language). In your journey with **зворотні дієслова** (reflexive verbs), you will notice a small gap between how words look on the page and how they sound in conversation. This is where the music of our language truly begins to shine.

### The Secret of Pronouncing -ться and -шся
In Ukrainian, some letter combinations melt together to create unique, soft sounds that are easier to say quickly. When you see the ending **-ться** in the 3rd person (like in **він вмивається**), it might look like three separate letters. However, we pronounce this whole cluster as one long, soft «ts» sound: **[tsʲ:a]**. It sounds almost like a gentle, prolonged buzz at the end of the word. Similarly, the 2nd person ending **-шся** (as in **ти вмиваєшся**) is pronounced as a long, soft «s» sound: **[sʲ:a]**. Mastering these two sounds is the key to sounding like a natural speaker rather than a student reading from a list. **Це секрет гарної вимови** (This is the secret of good pronunciation). When you speak, let these sounds flow together without a pause or a hard stop.

Examples:
- **Він вмивається.** — [ʋʲin ʋmɪˈʋajɛtsʲ:a] (He is washing.)
- **Ти вмиваєшся.** — [tɪ ʋmɪˈʋajɛsʲ:a] (You are washing.)
- **Вони вчаться.** — [ʋoˈnɪ ˈʋtʃatsʲ:a] (They are studying.)
- **Вона одягається.** — [ʋoˈna odʲeˈɦajɛtsʲ:a] (She is dressing.)

*Usage Note:* Do not try to pronounce the «t» or «sh» sounds separately in these endings. Let the sounds blend into that long, soft finish for a natural rhythm!

### A Handy Mnemonic: Vowels and Consonants
To help you choose between the two forms of the suffix, **-ся** and **-сь**, I have a simple trick for you. **Це дуже просте правило** (This is a very simple rule). Think of it as a friendly match between letters where everyone wants to find the best partner! Vowels love the short form **-сь**, while consonants prefer the fuller **-ся**. This rule exists to make the language easier to speak without too many sounds clashing together. If a verb form ends in a vowel, we add **-сь**. If it ends in a consonant, we use **-ся**. **Використовуйте це правило кожний день** (Use this rule every day). It creates that smooth, rolling sound that makes our language so pleasant to the ear.

**Mnemonic Table: Choosing the Suffix**

| Ending Type | Example Ending | Suffix to Use | Example Verb |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Vowel** | ...ю, ...о, ...е | **-сь** | Я вмиваю**сь** |
| **Consonant** | ...ш, ...ть | **-ся** | Ти вмиваєш**ся** |

Examples:
- **Я одягаюсь.** — [ja odʲeˈɦajusʲ] (I am dressing myself.)
- **Ти одягаєшся.** — [tɪ odʲeˈɦajɛsʲ:a] (You are dressing yourself.)
- **Ми вчимось.** — [mɪ ʋtʃɪˈmɔsʲ] (We are studying.)
- **Ви вмиваєтесь.** — [ʋɪ ʋmɪˈʋajɛtɛsʲ] (You [plural] are washing.)

### A Big Step for Your Self-Expression
Reaching the point where you can use reflexive verbs is a significant milestone in your studies. These verbs are the building blocks of daily life and **український етикет** (Ukrainian etiquette). They allow you to describe your morning routine and your important activities. **Ви вчитеся дуже добре** (You are studying very well). When you use verbs like **називатися** (to be called) or **знайомитися** (to get acquainted), you are showing deep respect for our culture. **Це шлях до нових друзів** (This is the path to new friends). It is the bridge between being a visitor and becoming a true friend in Ukraine!

Examples:
- **Як ви називаєтесь?** — [jak ʋɪ nɐzɪˈʋajɛtɛsʲ] (What are you called?)
- **Я називаюсь Андрій.** — [ja nɐzɪˈʋajusʲ] (I am called Andriy.)
- **Ми знайомимось.** — [mɪ znɐˈjɔmɪmɔsʲ] (We are getting acquainted.)
- **Я повертаюсь.** — [ja poʋɛrˈtajusʲ] (I am returning.)

[!culture]
**The Language of Community**
In Ukraine, using the correct reflexive form is part of good etiquette. When you say **знайомитися**, you are signaling a desire for a friendly connection. It is the language of community and warm hospitality!

### Домашній затишок та етикет
Reflexive verbs help you navigate the world with confidence and grace. From the early morning when you wake up and say **я вмиваюсь**, to the afternoon when you meet a new neighbor and say **я називаюсь**, these words are your keys to the rhythm of Ukrainian life. They are not just linguistic tools; they are reflections of how we see ourselves and our relationships. **Це ваш успіх** (This is your success) on the path to fluency! By paying attention to these small phonetic details and cultural nuances, you show that you care about the beauty of the language. Keep practicing these soft endings in your daily routine. You will soon feel like a true part of our community. Remember, every time you use a reflexive verb correctly, you are bringing yourself closer to the heart of Ukraine!
