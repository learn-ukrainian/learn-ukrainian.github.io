# This Is / I Am

## Вступ

### The "Missing Verb" Effect
Have you ever noticed that Ukrainian sentences seem shorter than English ones? This is not an accident. In English, you cannot make a sentence without the verb "to be" (am, is, are). You must say "I **am** a student" or "This **is** a book". But Ukrainian works differently in the present tense. We simply skip this verb.

Linguists call this the "zero copula". This means there is nothing between the subject (who?) and the predicate (what are they?). We just place two words side by side. Imagine that the word "is" is a ghost. We know it exists logically, but we do not say it or write it.

### Formula: Subject + Description
To build such a sentence, you need only two elements: **who** (subject) and **description**. You do not need to conjugate a verb. Just imagine a blank space where "is" or "are" would be in English.

Look at this simple logic:
*   English: I **[am]** a student.
*   Ukrainian: Я **[___]** студент.

> [!tip]
> **Don't Look for "Is"!**
> Beginners often try to insert the word **є** [jɛ] (is/are). For example, "Я є студент". While grammatically possible, it sounds unnatural. In 99% of cases, Ukrainians simply say: "Я студент".

### Examples in Action
Let's see how this looks in real life. Imagine you are meeting new neighbors. You simply point at a person or object and name them. The lack of a verb lets you speak faster.

Here are the basic models we will use:

*   **Я студент.** [ja stuˈdɛnt] — (I [am] a student.)
*   **Це стіл.** [t͡sɛ sʲtʲil] — (This [is] a table.)
*   **Він українець.** [ʋin ukraˈjinɛtʲsʲ] — (He [is] a Ukrainian.)
*   **Вона вчителька.** [ʋɔˈnɑ ˈʋt͡ʃɪtɛlʲka] — (She [is] a teacher.)

As you can see, the meaning is clear without the verb.

## Презентація

### Personal Pronouns: I, You, We, They

Welcome back! As your neighbor, I want you to feel confident when speaking about yourself. Imagine we met outside. The first thing we need is words for people.

Let's look at these small but powerful words.

**Я** [jɑ] — **I**.
It is short, like an exhale. You are the center of your universe.
*   **Я** [jɑ] — (I)

**Ти** [tɪ] — **you** (singular, informal).
You use this with a friend, child, or family member.
*   **Ти** [tɪ] — (you, informal)

**Ми** [mɪ] — **we**.
You and I, we are together.
*   **Ми** [mɪ] — (we)

**Ви** [ʋɪ] — **you** (plural OR formal).
Use this for groups or for one person you respect or don't know well.
*   **Ви** [ʋɪ] — (you, plural/formal)

**Вони** [ʋɔˈnɪ] — **they**.
People who are not with us.
*   **Вони** [ʋɔˈnɪ] — (they)

Let's use this in context. Imagine pointing at yourself and me.

> **Я** тут. (I [am] here.)
> **Ти** там. (You [are] there.)
> **Ми** друзі. (We [are] friends.)

See? It's simple. The main thing is the sound. Ukrainian **«и»** [ɪ] sounds deeper than English "i" in "bit".

### He, She, It: Gender Matters

Now, let's move to the most interesting part. Remember we said Ukrainian sees the world through gender? When we speak about the third person (he, she, it), we must know the gender.

**Він** [ʋʲin] — **he**.
Used for men and boys. Note the soft [nʲ] at the end.
*   **Він** студент. (He [is] a student.)

**Вона** [ʋɔˈnɑ] — **she**.
Used for women and girls. Stress is on the second syllable: vo-NA.
*   **Вона** студентка. (She [is] a student.)

**Воно** [ʋɔˈnɔ] — **it**.
Used for things with no distinct gender, or small creatures.
*   **Воно** — (it)

> [!myth-buster]
> **Myth:** "Воно" (It) is used for all inanimate objects.
> **Reality:** No! In Ukrainian, a table is **Він**, and a lamp is **Вона**. The word "Воно" is reserved only for neuter words (ending in -o or -e).

So, when you look at your friend Ivan, you think: "Він". When you look at Maria, you think: "Вона". But what about objects?

### The "It" Trap: Objects Have Personality

This is where English speakers often stumble. In English, a table is "it". In Ukrainian, objects have gender. We use **Він** and **Вона** for them too.

Let's look at examples from my home.

Here is my **стіл** (table).
*   Це стіл. **Він** тут. (This [is] a table. It [He] [is] here.)
    We don't say "Воно". We say "Він". For a Ukrainian, a table is masculine.

Here is my **кава** (coffee).
*   Це кава. **Вона** там. (This [is] coffee. It [She] [is] there.)
    Coffee is feminine.

And here is a **вікно** (window).
*   Це вікно. **Воно** велике. (This [is] a window. It [is] big.)
    Here we finally use "Воно", because "вікно" ends in -o.

**Tip from your neighbor:**
*   Ends in consonant (**телефон**, **паспорт**) $\rightarrow$ **Він** (Masculine).
*   Ends in -а (**карта**, **піца**) $\rightarrow$ **Вона** (Feminine).
*   Ends in -о (**місто**, **авто**) $\rightarrow$ **Воно** (Neuter).

### Magic Word "Це": Universal Pointer

Now I will give you the most useful word for survival. This word is **Це** [t͡sɛ].

In English, you say "This is...", "That is...", "It is...", "These are...".
In Ukrainian, you just say **Це**.

This word does not change. It works for everything.
**Це** [t͡sɛ] — (This is / It is / These are)

Look how it works:

*   **Це** Іван. (This is Ivan.)
*   **Це** Марія. (This is Mariya.)
*   **Це** я. (It is me.)
*   **Це** ми. (This is us.)
*   **Це** студенти. (These are students.)

See? One small word replaces many English structures. You don't need "is" or "are".

Imagine pointing at things in an airport:
*   **Це** паспорт. (This [is] a passport.)
*   **Це** валіза. (This [is] a suitcase.)

Pronunciation is simple: [t͡sɛ]. The sound [t͡s] is like in "ca**ts**".

### Ty or Vy? Social "Airbag"

Addressing people is a social code. We have two words for "you": **Ти** and **Ви**.

**Ви** [ʋɪ] (Formal/Plural) — your "safety airbag".
Use **Ви** when:
1.  Speaking to a stranger.
2.  Addressing an older person.
3.  In a formal setting.
4.  Talking to a group.

**Ти** [tɪ] (Informal) — a sign of closeness.
Use **Ти** only when:
1.  Chatting with a friend or family member.
2.  Speaking to a child.
3.  The person invited you to use "ty".

> [!culture]
> **The Switch**
> Switching from "Ви" to "Ти" is a ritual. Usually, the older person suggests: "Перейдімо на «ти»?" (Let's switch to 'ty'?). This means the distance is gone. Until then, keep the distance. It is respect, not coldness.

### No-Verb Formula: Subject + Predicate

We know the "Missing Verb". Let's look at the formula.

**Subject** + **Predicate**.
No "am", "is", "are" between them.

Examples:

1.  **Я студент.** [jɑ stuˈdɛnt]
    *   (I [am a] student.)

2.  **Він українець.** [ʋʲin ukrɑˈjinet͡sʲ]
    *   (He [is a] Ukrainian.)

3.  **Ми тут.** [mɪ tut]
    *   (We [are] here.)

4.  **Вони там.** [ʋɔˈnɪ tɑm]
    *   (They [are] there.)

Do not say "Я є студент". Just put two words together.

Practice these pairs:
*   Я — Макс.
*   Ти — Олена.
*   Вона — мама.
*   Він — тато.

### Negation: The Power of "Не"

What if you want to say something is *not* true?
Use the word **Не** [nɛ].
Place it **before** the word you negate.

Formula: **Subject** + **Не** + **Predicate**.

*   **Я не студент.** [jɑ nɛ stuˈdɛnt]
    (I [am] not [a] student.)

*   **Він не тут.** [ʋʲin nɛ tut]
    (He [is] not here.)

*   **Це не стіл.** [t͡sɛ nɛ stʲil]
    (This [is] not [a] table.)

Notice the word order. **Не** acts as a barrier.
English: "is not". Ukrainian: just **не**.

*   Він **не** українець. (He [is] not Ukrainian.)
*   Вона **не** вдома. (She [is] not at home.)

Pronounce it short: [nɛ].

## Практика

Now let's practice real situations. Imagine we are sitting on a bench discussing what we see.

### Names and Pronouns

In conversation, we replace names with pronouns.
Remember: verb **є** disappears.

1.  **Іван — студент.** $\rightarrow$ **Він студент.**
2.  **Марія — вчителька.** $\rightarrow$ **Вона вчителька.**
3.  **Іван і Марія — українці.** $\rightarrow$ **Вони українці.**

> [!note]
> **Notice**
> In English, "I am" becomes "He is". In Ukrainian, nothing changes because there is **no verb**!
> *   Я [___] тут.
> *   Він [___] тут.
> This makes it easy for beginners.

### Objects have Character (He or She?)

Remember, a table is a "guy". Never say "it" for an object's location.

*   **Де телефон?** (Where is the phone?) — **Він тут.** (He is here.)
*   **Де лампа?** (Where is the lamp?) — **Вона там.** (She is there.)
*   **Де вікно?** (Where is the window?) — **Воно тут.** (It is here.)

Check the ending of the word.

### Who did we meet?

Who gets **«Ти»**, and who gets **«Ви»**?

| Who is this? | Which pronoun? | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Friend | **Ти** [tɪ] | Ти студент? |
| Boss | **Ви** [ʋɪ] | Ви тут? |
| Child | **Ти** [tɪ] | Ти хто? |
| Stranger | **Ви** [ʋɪ] | Хто Ви? |

Rule: If you didn't drink coffee in the kitchen together — start with **«Ви»**.

## Продукція

### Introducing Yourself

To introduce yourself, use **Я** [ja] and your name.
No verbs!

*   **Я Том.** — [ja tɔm] — I am Tom.
*   **Я студент.** — [ja stuˈdɛnt] — I am a student.
*   **Я українець.** — [ja ukrɑˈjinɛtʲsʲ] — I am a Ukrainian (male).
*   **Я українка.** — [ja ukrɑˈjinkɑ] — I am a Ukrainian (female).

> [!fact]
> **Simple is Beautiful**
> In English: "I *am*", "He *is*". In Ukrainian: just point and name.

### Naming the World

Use **Це** [t͡sɛ] to point at things.

*   **Це стіл.** — [t͡sɛ sʲtʲil] — This is a table.
*   **Це телефон.** — [t͡sɛ tɛlɛˈfɔn] — This is a phone.
*   **Це Анна.** — [t͡sɛ ˈɑnːɑ] — This is Anna.

To ask questions, add **Хто** [xtɔ] (who) or **Що** [ʃtɔ] (what):
*   **Хто це? Це вчитель.** — [xtɔ t͡sɛ? t͡sɛ ˈʋt͡ʃɪtɛlʲ] — Who is this? This is a teacher.

### We and They

Same rule: **Pronoun + Role**.

*   **Ми студенти.** — [mɪ stuˈdɛntɪ] — We are students.
*   **Вони туристи.** — [ʋɔˈnɪ tuˈrɪstɪ] — They are tourists.
*   **Ви друзі.** — [ʋɪ ˈdruzʲi] — You are friends.

## Культурний контекст

### "Ви" — Your Safety Airbag

In Ukrainian culture, distance matters. **Ви** is a formal suit. **Ти** is a t-shirt.
Do not say "ти" to strangers.

Golden Rule: If in doubt, choose **Ви**. Use it with elders, strangers, waiters, doctors. Even if they are your age. It shows respect.

> [!warning] Age Matters
> Never say "ти" to an older person unless they offer it. It is a strict taboo.

### Ritual of Switching

Switching to **ти** is a social moment. Usually, the older person offers it.

Example:
*   **Oleh:** Перейдімо на «ти»? (Let's switch to 'ty'?)
*   **Iryna:** З радістю! (With pleasure!)

### Modern Rules

The world is changing. In IT companies or among youth, **ти** is common immediately.
But in a bank or university, keep the hierarchy.

*   **Bank:** Ви наш клієнт. (Formal)
*   **Party:** Ти класний друг. (Informal)
