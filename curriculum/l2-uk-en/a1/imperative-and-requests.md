## Наказовий спосіб

You have done an amazing job learning how to describe the world around you. Ви вже вмієте описувати світ. You know how to talk about facts, describe your daily routines, and share your plans for the future. Ви знаєте факти та плани на майбутнє. You have also learned how to express your wishes and obligations using verbs like **хотіти** and **мусити**. Ви також навчилися висловлювати бажання та обов'язки. Now, we are taking a practical step forward into interactive communication. Тепер ми робимо крок до спілкування. We are exploring the imperative mood. Ми вивчаємо наказовий спосіб.

What is the imperative mood? Simply put, it is the form of the verb we use when we want to tell someone to do something. Що таке наказовий спосіб? Це форма дієслова для наказів. We use it for giving commands, making requests, offering advice, or giving instructions. Ми використовуємо його для наказів та прохань. When a teacher tells you to read a text, or when a friend tells you to look at a beautiful sunset, they are using the imperative mood. Коли вчитель каже «Читай!», це наказовий спосіб.

In Ukrainian, we have specific rules for forming these commands. В українській мові є правила творення наказів. At your current level, we will focus entirely on the second person forms: you (singular) and you (plural or polite). На вашому рівні ми зосередимося на другій особі: ти та ви. To form the imperative, you generally start with the infinitive, which is the dictionary form of the verb ending in **-ти**. Для творення наказового способу ми беремо інфінітив. You remove that **-ти** ending to find the stem of the verb, and then you add a specific imperative suffix. Ви прибираєте закінчення **-ти** та додаєте суфікс. The exact pattern depends heavily on the conjugation type of the verb and whether the stem ends in a vowel or a consonant.

Let us look at the difference between the informal and formal forms. Розглянемо різницю між неофіційними та офіційними формами. As you already know, Ukrainian has two ways to say "you": the informal **ти** and the formal or plural **ви**. Ви вже знаєте, що в українській мові є «ти» та «ви». When you form an imperative, you must match this distinction. Коли ви утворюєте наказ, ви повинні враховувати цю різницю. For example, if you want to tell a close friend to read, you use the **ти** form: **читай**. Наприклад, форма «ти» — «Читай». However, if you are speaking to a group of people, or to someone you address formally, you use the **ви** form: **читайте**. Але форма «ви» — «Читайте». Notice how we added **-те**? Similarly, for the verb to write, you say **пиши** to a friend, but **пишіть** to your teacher. Для дієслова «писати» форма «ти» — «пиши», а форма «ви» — «пишіть». For the verb to speak, it is **говори** informally, and **говоріть** formally. Для «говорити» це «говори» (ти) та «говоріть» (ви). The **ви** form usually adds **-те** or **-іть** to the base imperative, adding a layer of politeness and plurality. Форма «ви» додає закінчення **-те** або **-іть**.

> [!tip] Infinitives vs. Imperatives
> A common error among learners is confusing the imperative mood with the infinitive form. Помилка учнів — плутати наказовий спосіб та інфінітив. Because English simply uses the base form of the verb for commands (like "Read!"), English speakers often try to use the Ukrainian infinitive **читати** when giving a command. But in Ukrainian, **Читати!** used as a command feels like an impersonal public sign or a strict rule. It translates closer to "Reading is to be done!" or "One must read!" On the other hand, **Читай!** is a direct, personal command meant for a specific person. Context always determines which form to use, but in everyday conversations, you will almost always use the personal imperative forms. Вдома ви кажете: «Читай!», а не «Читати!».

## Вісім обов'язкових дієслів

Now that you understand the basic mechanics, let us focus on the most important verbs you need right now. Тепер зосередимося на найважливіших дієсловах. According to the Ukrainian State Standard, there are eight required verbs that you must master at this stage. Є вісім обов'язкових дієслів на цьому рівні. These are the absolute core of daily classroom and everyday interaction. Це важливі дієслова для щоденного спілкування. Let us explore them one by one.

The eight essential verbs are (Вісім основних дієслів):

* **читати** → **читай** (read!) / **читайте** (read, formal/plural!)
* **писати** → **пиши** (write!) / **пишіть** (write, formal/plural!)
* **сказати** → **скажи** (say!) / **скажіть** (say, formal/plural!)
* **дати** → **дай** (give!) / **дайте** (give, formal/plural!)
* **іти** → **іди** (go!) / **ідіть** (go, formal/plural!)
* **слухати** → **слухай** (listen!) / **слухайте** (listen, formal/plural!)
* **дивитися** → **дивись** (look!) / **дивіться** (look, formal/plural!)
* **стояти** → **стій** (halt!) / **стійте** (halt, formal/plural!)

You will hear these verbs constantly in a classroom context. When your teacher gives instructions, they will use the formal/plural forms. 

* Читайте текст. — Read the text.
* Пишіть у зошиті. — Write in your notebook.
* Слухайте уважно. — Listen carefully.
* Дивіться на дошку. — Look at the board.

Notice that while some of these follow a very predictable pattern, others have irregular stems that you simply must memorize. For example, the verb **дати** drops its vowel in the stem to become **дай**. You cannot say it any other way. Another very important change happens with the perfective verb **сказати**. It features a consonant mutation where the **к** changes to a **ж**, resulting in **скажи**. Finally, the verb **стояти** undergoes a dramatic stem vowel change to become **стій**, which is often used as an urgent safety command meaning «halt!» or «stop!» Memorize these set forms carefully. 

When you encounter reflexive verbs like **дивитися**, notice that the **-ся** ending drops entirely in the informal imperative, leaving just **дивись**. 

## Ввічливе прохання

When you give commands in Ukrainian, they can sometimes sound quite direct or even harsh to English speakers. To soften your commands and turn them into polite requests, you need to use specific words and phrases. Let us explore how to make your imperatives polite.

The most universal and important softener is **будь ласка**, which means «please». Whenever you ask for something, you should include this phrase. For example, if you are at a market or a café, you will use it with the verb **дати**:

* Дайте, будь ласка. — Please give.

One of the great things about **будь ласка** is its position flexibility. It can appear at the beginning, in the middle, or at the end of a sentence. However, it is most common and natural to place it directly after the verb. You will find it very useful with other important service verbs. For instance, **показати** (to show) becomes **покажи** or **покажіть**. **Допомогти** (to help) becomes **допоможи** or **допоможіть**. **Взяти** (to take) is irregular and becomes **візьми** or **візьміть**. **Чекати** (to wait) becomes **чекай** or **чекайте**.

* Покажіть, будь ласка. — Please show.
* Візьміть, будь ласка. — Please take.
* Зачекайте хвилинку. — Wait a minute.

For more formal situations, such as official environments or formal service contexts, you can use the phrase **прошу вас** followed by the infinitive verb. This translates to «I ask you to...» and is more formal than simply adding **будь ласка**. 

* Прошу вас сісти. — Please sit down.

Finally, there is an indirect request pattern that is the most polite option available to you right now: **Чи не могли б ви...?** This translates to «Could you...?» and gives you a preview of the conditional mood. You can learn it as a fixed phrase for now. 

* Чи не могли б ви повторити? — Could you repeat?

## Заборони (Prohibitions)

So far, we have talked about telling people what to do. But how do you tell someone what *not* to do? Expressing prohibitions in Ukrainian is surprisingly straightforward once you know the positive imperative.

To form a negative imperative, you simply use the negative particle **не** followed immediately by the imperative form of the verb. It is the exact same formation as the positive command, just with **не** placed in front of it. 

* Не кури! — Do not smoke!
* Не біжи! — Do not run!
* Не чіпай! — Do not touch!

The rule is consistent and easy to apply. Whether you are speaking to one person or a group, just add **не** to the correct imperative form.

It is important to preview a connection to our future lessons on prohibitions and signs. There is a distinct difference between a personal prohibition and a public sign prohibition. A personal prohibition, like **Не чіпай!**, is directed at a specific person or people in the moment. However, on public signs, you will usually see the infinitive form used instead. A sign in a museum will say **Не торкатися!** (Do not touch!). Both phrases mean «do not touch», but they differ significantly in their register. The personal imperative is for active conversation, while the infinitive is for general, impersonal rules.

## Практика (Practice)

Now it is time to practice what you have learned! Command drills are an excellent way to train your brain to produce the correct imperative form instinctively. Imagine your teacher gives you a situation, and you must respond with the appropriate command. Let us focus on the required verbs in varied contexts. 

Imagine you want a friend to look at a funny picture on your phone. You would say:
> — Дивись! (Look!)

Now, imagine you need a group of tourists to go straight ahead. You would say:
> — Ідіть! (Go!)

What if you need a taxi driver to stop the car immediately?
> — Стійте! (Stop!)

If you want your classmate to read the next paragraph?
> — Читай! (Read!)

You should also practice politeness escalation, upgrading direct commands to polite requests. Let us take the verb **дати** (to give). Look at how the request changes:

1. **Дай!** (Give!)
2. **Дайте, будь ласка.** (Please give.)
3. **Чи не могли б ви дати?** (Could you give?)

Try this escalation practice yourself with the verb **допомогти** (to help). Start with **Допоможіть!**, add **будь ласка**, and then try the indirect pattern. Training yourself to automatically reach for polite forms will make your spoken Ukrainian much more natural and respectful.
