## Вітання (Greetings)

When you start learning a new language, the first thing you need is a reliable way to say hello. In Ukrainian, greetings are not just about acknowledging someone—they are traditionally a way of wishing people well, health, and a good day. Let's look at the essential words you need to make a great first impression. 

The most common informal greeting is **приві́т** (hello). This is the universal casual hello, but it has strict rules. You only use **приві́т** with your friends, your peers, or children. You should never use it with strangers, teachers, or shop assistants.

For formal situations, Ukrainian uses time-based greetings. In the morning, until about noon, you say **до́брий ра́нок** (good morning). From noon until about six in the evening, the standard greeting is **до́брий день** (good afternoon). In the evening, after six, you say **до́брий ве́чір** (good evening). These are used with strangers, elders, and in any professional setting. When you are unsure of the social context, matching the greeting to the time of day is always the right choice. In fact, using **до́брий день** is considered the safest default greeting if you do not know the person.

When it is time to part ways, the formal goodbye is **до поба́чення**. Literally, this means "until seeing." If you are speaking informally, remember that casual farewells will be covered in later modules, though you can always wave. Another very warm and common farewell is **на все до́бре** (all the best), which works perfectly in both formal and semi-formal contexts. You can also use **здра́стуйте** as an alternative formal hello, though it is slightly old-fashioned. If you are receiving guests, remember that a warm smile is often the best welcome.

* **приві́т** — hello (informal)
* **до́брий ра́нок** — good morning
* **до́брий день** — good afternoon
* **до́брий ве́чір** — good evening
* **до поба́чення** — goodbye (formal)
* **на все до́бре** — all the best

## Ти і Ви (T-V distinction)

Like many European languages, Ukrainian has two different words for "you." Choosing the right one is incredibly important for showing respect. This concept is sometimes called the T-V distinction, and it is strictly observed in Ukrainian culture. Using the wrong form can accidentally cause offense, so let's break it down.

The word **ти** is the singular informal "you." You use **ти** when you are speaking with exactly one person whom you know very well. This includes your close friends, your family members, children, and peers of your exact age in casual settings. It creates a feeling of closeness and intimacy.

The word **ви**, on the other hand, has two jobs. First, it is the plural "you." Anytime you are addressing two or more people, you always use **ви**, regardless of who they are. Second, **ви** is the singular formal "you." You use this when speaking to one person whom you respect or do not know well. This includes strangers, teachers, elders, bosses, or any professional contact. 

* **ти** — you (singular informal)
* **ви** — you (plural) OR you (singular formal)

The safety rule is simple: always start with **ви** until you are explicitly invited to switch. Using **ти** with a stranger or an elder is considered rude and disrespectful in Ukrainian culture. This boundary is more strictly observed than in many Western European cultures. When someone wants to become less formal, they will suggest the transition.

> [!tip]
> Remember that the transition to **ти** is a big step! You should wait for a Ukrainian speaker to explicitly invite you to use the informal register.

When this happens, it means you have made a friend, and you can comfortably start using the informal register!

## Ввічливість (Politeness)

Good manners are universal, but the specific words we use carry different shades of meaning. Let's look at the foundational expressions of politeness in Ukrainian. These are the words that will help you navigate daily life smoothly, from ordering in a cafe to bumping into someone on the street. 

The universal expression of gratitude is **дякую** (thank you). You will use this word constantly. If you want to show deeper appreciation, you can intensify it. You can say **ду́же дякую** (thank you very much) or **щи́ро дякую** (sincerely thank you). Both are beautiful and highly appreciated. 

The magic word that goes hand-in-hand with gratitude is **будь ла́ска** (please / you're welcome). Notice that it is written as two separate words. It has a dual function. You use it when you are requesting something, and you also use it as the standard polite response when someone thanks you. 

* **дякую** — thank you
* **ду́же дякую** — thank you very much
* **щи́ро дякую** — sincerely thank you
* **будь ла́ска** — please / you're welcome

When you need to get someone's attention or apologize, you have a few options. The formal way to say excuse me is **ви́бачте**. You use this to get attention from a stranger or to apologize for a minor inconvenience. The informal version is **ви́бач**. If you have genuinely inconvenienced someone, a more formal and sincere apology is **перепро́шую** (I apologize). 

Here are some typical usage contexts:
* Ordering at a cafe: **Будь ла́ска, ка́ву.** (A coffee, please.)
* Bumping into someone: **Ви́бачте!** (Excuse me!)
* Receiving a gift: **Ду́же дякую!** (Thank you very much!)

## Звертання (Addressing People)

When you address someone directly in Ukrainian, the form of their name or title changes. This is called the vocative case. For now, do not worry about the grammar mechanics. Just memorize these forms as fixed phrases—we will cover the full vocative rules later. 

When calling family members, the endings change softly. You will use forms like **ма́мо** (mom), **тату** (dad), **бабу́сю** (grandma), and **діду́сю** (grandpa). When addressing a good friend, you say **дру́же** (friend). 

For formal address, you use specific titles. The word for Mr. is **па́не**, and the word for Ms. is **па́ні**. Notice how the name also changes to match the formal address. 

* **До́брий день, па́не Пе́тре!** (Good afternoon, Mr. Petro!)
* **До́брий день, па́ні Окса́но!** (Good afternoon, Ms. Oksana!)

The key pattern is quite consistent. Most feminine names and titles end in **-о** in direct address, such as **ма́мо**, **Окса́но**, or **па́ні Іро**. Most masculine names and titles end in **-е** or **-у**, such as **дру́же**, **тату**, or **па́не Пе́тре**.

## Знайомство (Introductions)

Meeting new people is exciting! Now that you know how to greet people and be polite, let's learn how to ask for names and introduce yourself. The Ukrainian State Standard highlights specific formulas for these basic interactions. 

To ask someone's name without using complex verbs, you can simply ask "Who are you?". Remember the T-V distinction we discussed earlier? It applies here too. Asking names requires choosing the right level of formality.

* Formal: **Хто ви?** (Who are you?)
* Informal: **Хто ти?** (Who are you?)

When responding, the standard self-introduction formula is simple and consistent. You say **Я...** (I am...) followed by your name. Once you have exchanged names, it is polite to express your pleasure at meeting them. You can say **ду́же приє́мно** (very pleasant).

Let's look at the difference between formal and informal introductions. 

**Meeting a professor (Formal):**
* **До́брий день.** (Good afternoon.)
* **Хто ви?** (Who are you?)
* **Я...** (I am...)
* **Ду́же приє́мно.** (Very pleasant.)

**Meeting a classmate (Informal):**
* **Приві́т!** (Hello!)
* **Хто ти?** (Who are you?)
* **Я...** (I am...)
* **Приє́мно!** (Pleasant!)

## Діалоги (Dialogues)

Let's put everything together! Reading dialogues helps you see how these phrases flow in real conversations. Pay attention to the formality levels in each scenario. 

**Dialogue 1 — Meeting someone for the first time (formal):**
Here is a full greeting, introduction, and farewell sequence using formal language.
> — **До́брий день!** (Good afternoon!)
> — **До́брий день! Хто ви?** (Good afternoon! Who are you?)
> — **Я А́нна. А ви?** (I am Anna. And you?)
> — **Я Оле́г. Ду́же приє́мно.** (I am Oleh. Very pleasant.)
> — **На все до́бре!** (All the best!)
> — **До поба́чення!** (Goodbye!)

**Dialogue 2 — Greeting a friend (informal):**
This uses casual register for a quick check-in.
> — **Приві́т, ма́мо!** (Hi, mom!)
> — **Приві́т! Як спра́ви?** (Hi! How are things?)
> — **До́бре, дякую. На все до́бре!** (Good, thank you. All the best!)
> — **На все до́бре!** (All the best!)

**Dialogue 3 — Asking for something politely:**
Using polite expressions to get attention and request an item.
> — **Ви́бачте!** (Excuse me!)
> — **Так?** (Yes?)
> — **Будь ла́ска, ка́ву і сік.** (Coffee and juice, please.)
> — **Будь ла́ска.** (Here you go / You're welcome.)
> — **Дякую!** (Thank you!)

**Dialogue 4 — Thanking and saying goodbye:**
Combining gratitude with a warm farewell.
> — **Ду́же дякую, па́не Пе́тре!** (Thank you very much, Mr. Petro!)
> — **Будь ла́ска! На все до́бре!** (You're welcome! All the best!)
> — **До поба́чення!** (Goodbye!)
