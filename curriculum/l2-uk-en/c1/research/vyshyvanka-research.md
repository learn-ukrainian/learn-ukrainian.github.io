## Cultural Hooks

1. **Решетилівська вишивка = UNESCO-nominated intangible heritage.** The "білим по білому" (white-on-white) technique from Решетилівка, Poltava region, combines up to 5–7 distinct embroidery techniques in a single garment and is inscribed on Ukraine's National Intangible Cultural Heritage register. (Source: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Решетилівська_вишивка)

2. **Борщівська вишивка: black-thread mourning legend.** The distinctive black-thread embroidery of the Borshchiv region (Ternopil oblast) is documented as connected to a 17th-century vow by women whose husbands died in Ottoman raids — the colour black signifying perpetual grief and remembrance. Inscribed on the National Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2020. (Source: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Борщівська_вишивка)

3. **День вишиванки origin.** The Vsesvitniy Den Vyshyvanky is observed on the **third Thursday of May** each year. It started as a student initiative at Chernivtsi National University (2006) and has since grown into a global diaspora solidarity event. It is explicitly non-state and non-religious — anyone can participate by wearing a vyshyvanka. (Source: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Всесвітній_день_вишиванки)

## Common Learner Errors

1. **вишиванка vs. вишивка conflation** → **вишиванка** = the embroidered shirt (garment); **вишивка** = the embroidery process, ornament, or decorative technique. Learners (especially heritage speakers influenced by colloquial usage) apply "вишиванка" to any embroidered object. The plan correctly flags this as a priority lexical distinction.

2. **Verb collocations for wearing:** надягати вишиванку (to put on a vyshyvanka) vs. вдягатися у вишиванку (to dress in a vyshyvanka) vs. носити вишиванку (to wear, habitual). English speakers default to generic "одягати" for all three, collapsing aspect and semantic nuance. The error "надівати вишиванку на сукню" (mentioned in plan §5) is a calque from Russian надевать на платье.

3. **Топонім gender agreement:** Поділля (neuter, ІІ declension in -я) → *Поділля славне своїм…*; Полтавщина (feminine) → *Полтавщина славна своєю…*; Гуцульщина (feminine) → *Гуцульщина відома своїми…*. English speakers fail to track gender across regional ethnonyms ending in -щина vs. neuter -я forms.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **Wikipedia caution:** The Ukrainian Wikipedia summary uses "народна руська сорочка" — "руська" here is the historical Ruthenian ethnonym (not "Russian"), referring to pre-modern Rus' cultural heritage. Content should use "українська народна сорочка" or "традиційна вишиванка" and contextualize "руська" historically if cited at all. Do not import the Wikipedia framing uncritically.
- **Low-frequency terms need scaffolding:** мережка (0.06 IPM), низинка (0.01 IPM), and пристріт require first-encounter glossing in the text body before appearing in activities — do not assume passive recognition.
- **"Генетичний код" metaphor** is a modern publicistic cliché in Ukrainian embroidery discourse; the content should both use it (it's culturally accurate) and critically interrogate it in the later "Практикум" section — this serves the C1 analytical register.
- **Decolonization frame:** вишиванка discourse was actively suppressed in Soviet/Russian imperial framing as "малоросійське" costume. The module should present вишиванка as a complete semiotic system, not an exotic folk curiosity — the "encoded destiny" subtitle signals this correctly.
- **Activity language-testing criterion (Rule 10a):** Regional identification exercises must require learners to read and process Ukrainian descriptions — e.g., "На основі тексту визначте, якому регіону відповідає техніка, описана в другому абзаці" — not free-recall "Name the region known for white-on-white."
