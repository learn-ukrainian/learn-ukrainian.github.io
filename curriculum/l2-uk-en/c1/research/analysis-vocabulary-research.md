## Cultural Hooks

**1. Іван Дзюба, «Інтернаціоналізм чи русифікація?» (1965)**
Written Sept–Dec 1965 in direct response to the Soviet arrest wave against Ukrainian intelligentsia (Kyiv, Lviv, Lutsk, Ivano-Frankivsk, Ternopil). A ~256-page analytical treatise in which Dzyuba — using Marxist-Leninist frameworks and dense analytical vocabulary — dissected Soviet national policy in Ukraine. The work was circulated in *samvydav* (самвидав), published abroad in 1968 by *Suchasnist*, translated into English, Russian, Chinese, French, and Italian — but was not legally published in Ukraine until 1990. Soviet authorities criminalized its possession. Source: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Інтернаціоналізм_чи_русифікація?

**Pedagogical angle:** Dzyuba's work is the definitive example of the plan's point — "перетворення аналітичної лексики на інтелектуальну зброю." His use of terms like *аналізувати*, *доводити*, *суперечить* demonstrates how precise analytical language is not neutral — it carries agency and consequence.

**2. Іван Вишенський (late 16th – early 17th c.)**
Orthodox monk-polemicist, one of the founders of Ukrainian critical argumentation. His polemical writings against the Uniate church and the Polish nobility established a tradition of sharp analytical critique in the Ukrainian literary canon — centuries before the Soviet period. Source: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Іван_Вишенський

**Pedagogical angle:** Grounds the module's claim that Ukrainian has its *own* indigenous tradition of critical discourse, independent of any later influence. The polemic genre (полеміка — VESUM ✅) is a direct ancestor of the academic review (рецензія).

## Common Learner Errors

**1. Calque «вірний висновок»** (from Russian *верный вывод*) → correct: **правильний / слушний / обґрунтований висновок**
Why it happens: In Russian, *верный* means both "faithful" and "correct." In Ukrainian, *вірний* means only "faithful/loyal" (вірний друг — a loyal friend). Using *вірний* for "correct" is a high-frequency Russianism at C1 level that marks a learner as non-native.

**2. Calque «робити аналіз»** (from English *make an analysis* or Russian *делать анализ*) → correct: **проводити аналіз** / **аналізувати**
Why it happens: English "make" collocates freely with abstract nouns; Ukrainian prefers *проводити* for formal analytical procedures (проводити дослідження, проводити оцінку). Grounding: Karaman Grade 10 textbook uses *ґрунтовно проаналізувати* and *аналіз і критичну оцінку* as model phrases.

**3. Bureaucratic passive «мною було проаналізовано»** → correct: **«Я проаналізував / Дослідження показує»**
Why it happens: Soviet-era academic prose systematically avoided personal agency. Ukrainian academic culture at C1 favors agentive constructions that assert authorial voice. The shift from passive to active is a stylistic, not merely grammatical, correction — it reflects the "Agency Pass" principle from the plan.

## Notes for Content Writing

1. **Textbook grounding (Karaman Grade 10):** рецензія is defined in Grade 10 textbooks as «висловлення, що містить аналіз і критичну оцінку...» — and the model instruction is to «критично, переконливо й ґрунтовно проаналізувати позицію автора». These exact phrases make ideal anchor sentences for Section 4.

2. **VESUM flag for content phase:** Replace `достовірний` with `вірогідний` in vocabulary presentation, or include `достовірний` as a corpus-attested form with explicit note. Do NOT silently use `достовірний` as if VESUM-verified.

3. **Decolonized framing already strong in the plan:** The Dzyuba and Vyshenskyy hooks are excellent. Both situate Ukrainian analytical tradition as *originating from within* Ukrainian intellectual history. No need to compare to Russian academic vocabulary; frame comparisons against Polish (polemic tradition), Latin (scholastic), or English academic writing.

4. **Frequency gradient for teaching:** Lead with `аналіз` (33.94 IPM — corpus dominant), then medium-frequency items (узагальнення 4.67, синтез 2.63), then mark `релевантний` (0.10 IPM) as a specialist borrowing that students will encounter in academic texts but should use consciously.

5. **«Критика» disambiguation is critical:** Ukrainian *критика* covers (a) academic literary criticism/review and (b) negative judgment/blame. The plan correctly flags this. In English, "criticism" has the same ambiguity. The key distinction in Ukrainian professional discourse is *критика тексту / наукова критика* vs. *критика вчинку / засудження*.
