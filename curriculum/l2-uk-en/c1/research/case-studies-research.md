## Cultural Hooks

1. **Суржик vs. dialect** — Wikipedia (uk): "Суржик — елементи двох або кількох мов, об'єднані штучно, без дотримання норм літературної мови." The distinction between суржик (unregulated mixing, primarily Ukrainian-Russian) and authentic regional dialect (governed by historical phonological rules) is a live sociolinguistic debate in Ukraine. Гуцульський говір is confirmed as "один з архаїчних говорів галицько-буковинської групи південно-західного наріччя" — an archaic, not a corrupted, dialect. Lexemes like *файний*, *ґаздиня* are historical borrowings (from Polish/German) that entered the Carpathian dialect organically — categorically different from суржик.

2. **Закон про забезпечення функціонування державної мови (2019)** — Wikipedia search returned no cached article, but this is the commonly known Мовний закон (Law 2704-VIII, signed April 2019, in force July 2019). Key case study data: the law mandates Ukrainian in public service, media, and education. It generated genuine policy debate about balancing state-building and minority language rights (Hungarian community in Zakarpattia, Russian speakers). This tension is the analytical core of Case 1 — learners must weigh arguments without reducing to binary positions.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **приймати рішення → ухвалювати рішення** — The Russianism *приймати* (cf. Russian *принимать решение*) is the most common written calque at C1. Ukrainian standard is *ухвалювати/ухвалити рішення*. GRAC confirms *ухвалювати + рішення* as the overwhelmingly dominant collocation. The module's Практикум directly addresses this — content must make the rule explicit with real examples from official Ukrainian documents.

2. **Суржик ≠ dialectalism** — Learners often conflate surzyk with regional Ukrainian dialects. The error: labelling Carpathian dialect words (*файний*, *ґаздиня*, *маржина*) as "нечиста мова." Content must foreground that dialects have internal normative consistency; суржик lacks that consistency. Case 3 is built around this exact misconception.

3. **Passive over-use: "Ситуацію було проаналізовано"** — Passive participial constructions are heavily transferred from academic-register Russian (and overused by advanced learners aiming for "formality"). Ukrainian professional standard increasingly prefers agent-explicit active constructions ("Ми проаналізували ситуацію") or nominalized forms ("аналіз показав"). The Практикум section directly targets this — content must give side-by-side rewrites and explain *why* active voice signals stronger мовна агентність.

## Notes for Content Writing

- **No кейс-метод textbook coverage found in RAG** — Ukrainian school textbooks don't treat "case study methodology" as a named pedagogical unit. Phase B must define the кейс-метод explicitly within the module (not assume prior exposure). The content outline's вступ section is correctly front-loaded.
- **Rhetoric textbooks (Glazova Gr.10, Zabolotnyi Gr.11)** contain relevant material on public speaking analysis (contact with audience, speech structure critique). Quoted principles can anchor Case 4's rhetoric analysis section.
- **джинса** is confirmed in VESUM but absent from most RAG textbook content — it's a live media-literacy term used in journalism pedagogy, not school curriculum. The content should define it clearly and give concrete Ukrainian examples (marked advertorial content).
- **Decolonized framing**: all four cases concern Ukrainian institutional reality (Ukrainian law, Ukrainian media, Ukrainian dialects, Ukrainian political speeches). No Russian comparisons needed or appropriate. Frame суржик analysis as Ukrainian sociolinguistic phenomenon with its own history — Soviet-era language policies created conditions for суржик spread; this is historical context, not a comparison to Russian norms.
- **Word budget note**: The plan allocates 400+800+700+800+700+400+200 = 4000 words. Phase B should target 5000–5500 (1.25–1.375× overshoot) to comfortably clear the 4000-word audit gate after editing passes.
