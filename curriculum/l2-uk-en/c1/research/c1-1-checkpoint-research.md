## Cultural Hooks

1. **Києво-Могилянська академія (1632–1817)**: Founded by Metropolitan Petro Mohyla as Kyevo-Bratska Kolegiya; received academy status in the latter half of the 17th century; became one of the largest and most distinguished educational centers in Eastern Europe (Wikipedia: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Києво-Могилянська_академія). This is the foundational anchor for Ukrainian academic tradition — predates many Western European equivalents in quality and scope.

2. **Наукове товариство імені Шевченка (est. 1873, Lviv)**: Described as "багатопрофільна академія наук українського народу" and "рушійна сила формування та розвитку української науки кінця XIX — першої половини XX ст." (Wikipedia: uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/Наукове_товариство_імені_Шевченка). Published in Ukrainian when Tsarist Russia banned it (Ems Decree 1876) — making it a concrete example of academic resistance to cultural suppression. The NTSh operated from Austro-Hungarian Galicia precisely because Ukrainian scholarly publishing was illegal in the Russian Empire.

## Common Learner Errors

1. **«приймати участь» → «брати участь»** — Direct calque of Russian *принимать участие*. Ukrainian requires *брати*; *приймати* governs objects like *рішення*, *присягу*, not *участь*. Extremely common even in advanced L2 learners.

2. **«рахувати» (для думки) → «вважати»** — Confusion from Russian *считать* (polysemic: count + consider). Ukrainian *рахувати* = to count/calculate only. Opinion = *вважати, думати, гадати*. Example error: «Я рахую, що ця гіпотеза…» → «Я вважаю, що ця гіпотеза…»

3. **«дана проблема» → «ця/зазначена/розглядувана проблема»** — Soviet chancellery calque of Russian *данный* used as a demonstrative. Ukrainian academic prose uses *цей, зазначений, розглядуваний, порушений*. The form *розглядуваний* (VESUM confirmed) is specifically academic register.

4. **Passive voice overuse** — L2 writers (especially those with English academic writing backgrounds) overuse passive constructions (*було зроблено висновок*). Ukrainian academic style prefers active voice (*ми дійшли висновку*) or impersonal -но/-то forms (*зроблено висновок*) — the latter is Ukrainian-native and not a Russianism.

## Notes for Content Writing

1. **Checkpoint framing**: Content must be a *synthesis* module, not a teaching module. Phase B should open by explicitly naming the 14 C1.1 modules being consolidated, then work through them systematically. No new grammar concepts should be introduced.

2. **Номіналізація treatment**: Given GRAC frequency of 0.01 ipm, never assume the learner encountered this term. Phase B must model it as *process* (читати → читання, аналізувати → аналіз), not just name it.

3. **NTSh decolonization angle**: The fact that Ukrainian scholarly publishing was banned in the Russian Empire (1876 Ems Decree) while the NTSh thrived in Galicia is pedagogically powerful — it frames Ukrainian academic language as *resistant* and *chosen*, not derivative. Use this to anchor the cultural section.

4. **Self-assessment rubric**: The plan's Section 5 (Усна презентація та самооцінка, 1100 words) is the largest section. It should include a concrete CEFR C1 descriptor checklist that learners can apply to their own production, tied to the module's vocabulary: *компетенція*, *критерій*, *самооцінка*.

5. **Hedging markers list**: For Section 3, compile a productive table: *ймовірно, можна припустити, видається доцільним, є підстави вважати, варто зазначити, слід наголосити* — with register labels (нейтральний/піднесений). Phase B should include fill-in exercises from this list.

6. **-но/-то vs passive**: Section 2 transformation practice should contrast three parallel constructions: active (*ми встановили*), -но/-то impersonal (*встановлено*), periphrastic passive (*було встановлено*) — showing which registers prefer which.
