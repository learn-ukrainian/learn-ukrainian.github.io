## Cultural Hooks
1. **The Dissident Lexicographer**: Svyatoslav Karavansky compiled his groundbreaking "Practical Dictionary of Synonyms of the Ukrainian Language" while serving a 31-year sentence in Soviet labor camps. His work actively preserved the authentic, un-Russified vocabulary of the 1929 "Kharkiv" orthography, demonstrating that precision in language is intrinsically linked to cultural survival.
2. **Record-breaking Synonymy**: The Ukrainian word «бити» (to beat) has over 45 documented synonyms (лупцювати, гамселити, періщити, батожити, etc.), highlighting the immense lexical wealth of the language and the critical need for precise selection based on intensity and context.

## Common Learner Errors
1. **Кальки (Calques)**: *приймати участь* → **брати участь** — Direct literal translation of the Russian idiom "принимать участие".
2. **False Friends (Хибні друзі)**: Using *неділя* to mean a week (like Russian "неделя") → **тиждень** is a week, while **неділя** specifically means Sunday.
3. **Pleonasms (Плеоназми)**: *моя особиста думка* → **моя думка** — The possessive pronoun already inherently implies that it is personal, making "особиста" redundant.

## Notes for Content Writing
- **Decolonized framing**: Treat Ukrainian synonymy as an independent, structurally rich system. Highlight Karavansky's work as a defense of the language's natural precision against artificial Soviet policies of language convergence. Never use Russian as a baseline; frame Russianisms strictly as external interference.
- **Immersion**: B2 modules require 100% Ukrainian immersion. No English scaffolding should be used in the prose.
- **Precision**: Emphasize the semantic distinction between similar words (e.g., *вважати* vs *рахувати*) to correct persistent L1/Russian interference systematically.
