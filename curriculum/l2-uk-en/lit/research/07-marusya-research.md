## Key Facts (Verified from Sources)

- **Publication:** 1834 in almanac «Утренняя звезда» (Kharkiv)
- **French translation (CORRECTED):** The 1854 Paris French translation was of «Сердешна Оксана», NOT «Маруся». Source: Ukrainian Wikipedia on Kvitka-Osnovyanenko: "У 1854 в Парижі опубліковано французькою мовою «Сердешну Оксану»." The Wikipedia article on «Маруся» (повість) mentions NO European translations of Маруся specifically. Kvitka's works were also translated into Polish, Bulgarian, Czech, and other languages.
- **Маруся's role:** Created Kvitka's reputation as serious prose writer, which made later translations (including Serdeshna Oksana in Paris) possible. Маруся's domestic reception was the foundation for international recognition.
- **Narrator:** Грицько Основ'яненко (Kvitka's literary alter ego)
- **Setting:** Idealized Slobozhanshchyna village
- **Plot:** Marusya + Vasyl love → Naum refuses due to rekrutchyna → Vasyl leaves for earnings → Marusya falls ill and dies → Vasyl enters monastery → dies within a year
- **Kvitka's stated purpose:** "Написав я 'Марусю', щоб довести одному панові, що мовою нашою можна розчулити того, хто не вірить" (letter to P. Smorodsky, 1841)
- **Franko quote:** "Квітка — це фундамент. Гоголь — це купол"
- **Polevoy debate:** Russian critic denied Ukrainian as literary language; Marusya as creative response (covered in LIT-06, referenced but not repeated in LIT-07)

## Content Decisions

1. **No biography re-telling:** LIT-06 covers Kvitka's life comprehensively. LIT-07 goes straight into text analysis.
2. **No sentimentalism re-explanation:** LIT-05 covers European sentimentalism context (Sterne, Richardson, Rousseau, Goethe, Karamzin). LIT-07 assumes this knowledge.
3. **Deep text analysis:** Focus on close reading of plot, characters, ethnography, language — the "what" and "how" rather than background.
4. **Kvitka vs Gogol section:** Goes deeper than LIT-06's brief mention, covering language choice as political act and cost of each path.
5. **Expanded themes:** Philosophy of pokora (Skovoroda connection), feminist re-reading, Naum's utopian village as political statement, Grits'ko narrator technique, psychological parallelism, Slobozhanshchyna dialectisms as literary asset.
