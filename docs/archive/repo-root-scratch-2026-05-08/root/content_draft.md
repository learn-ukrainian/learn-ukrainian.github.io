# Imperative and Requests

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Learning how to ask people to do things is an essential skill for everyday life. You will learn how to give direct commands, make polite requests, and understand simple instructions in Ukrainian. This helps you communicate clearly with friends, service workers, and strangers.

## Наказовий спосіб (Imperative Mood)

> — **Ти читаєш?** — Are you reading?
> — **Так, я читаю.** — Yes, I am reading.
> — **Читай тут!** — Read here!
> 
> — **Ви читаєте?** — Are you reading?
> — **Так, ми читаємо.** — Yes, we are reading.
> — **Читайте тут!** — Read here!

When you need to tell someone to do something, you use the imperative mood. In English, you use the base form of the verb, like "read" or "listen." In Ukrainian, the imperative mood has specific endings depending on who you are talking to. If you are speaking to one person informally, you use the **ти** form. If you are speaking to multiple people, or to one person formally, you use the **ви** form. 

To create the imperative command for a single person, you typically drop the **-ти** ending from the infinitive verb and use the command stem. This stem is your base for the **ти** command. When you want to be formal or address a group of people, you simply take that informal command and add the suffix **-те** or **-іть** to the end. The choice between these endings depends on the specific verb, but the pattern of adding a suffix for the plural or formal command is very consistent.

| Infinitive | **ти**-command | **ви**-command |
|---|---|---|
| **читати** | **читай** | **читайте** |
| **писати** | **пиши** | **пишіть** |
| **чекати** | **чекай** | **чекайте** |

> [!tip] Watch out for infinitives!
> A very common mistake for learners is using the full infinitive form when giving a personal command. For example, saying ~~**Читати!**~~ when you want someone to read right now. The infinitive **читати** is mostly used on public signs or as a general rule, not as a direct spoken instruction to a specific person. Always use the proper command forms like **читай** or **читайте** when speaking directly to someone.

- **Читай слова!** — Read the words!
- **Пишіть слова!** — Write the words!
- **Чекайте там!** — Wait there!
- **Чекай тут!** — Wait here!

By mastering these simple endings, you will be able to direct people confidently. Remember to always match the level of formality to the situation, using the appropriate plural or polite endings when necessary. This distinction is crucial for showing proper respect in Ukrainian society.

## Вісім обов'язкових дієслів (Eight Required Verbs)

> — **Слухайте дуже уважно.** — Listen very carefully.
> — **Читайте цей текст.** — Read this text.
> — **Пишіть тут.** — Write here.
> — **Дивіться на ці слова.** — Look at these words.
> — **Ідіть до нас.** — Go to us.

There are eight essential command verbs that you will hear constantly, especially in classroom settings, during lessons, or in daily instructions. These are the core verbs you need to recognize and use right away. They cover the most basic actions like reading, writing, listening, and moving. Let us look at the forms of these important verbs. 

| Infinitive | **ти**-command | **ви**-command |
|---|---|---|
| **читати** | **читай** | **читайте** |
| **писати** | **пиши** | **пишіть** |
| **слухати** | **слухай** | **слухайте** |
| **дивитися** | **дивись** | **дивіться** |
| **іти** | **іди** | **ідіть** |

Some of the most important verbs have irregular command forms. You cannot simply drop the **-ти** ending to find the command. Instead, you must memorize the special command stems as set phrases. This is especially true for verbs like **дати** (to give), **сказати** (to say), and **стояти** (to stand). The stem changes completely, but the rule for making it formal or plural remains exactly the same: you still add **-те** or **-іть** to the informal command.

| Infinitive | **ти**-command | **ви**-command |
|---|---|---|
| **дати** | **дай** | **дайте** |
| **сказати** | **скажи** | **скажіть** |
| **стояти** | **стій** | **стійте** |

> [!warning] Irregular Stems
> Notice how **сказати** changes the letter **к** to **ж** in the command form **скажи**. The verb **дати** becomes the very short **дай**, not ~~**дайи**~~. The verb **стояти** changes its vowel from **о** to **і**, becoming **стій**. You must memorize these exact forms because they are incredibly common in daily life. 

- **Скажіть це слово.** — Say this word.
- **Дайте цю книгу.** — Give this book.
- **Стій там!** — Stand there!
- **Дивись тут!** — Look here!
- **Слухайте нас!** — Listen to us!

Whenever you enter a class, the teacher will use the **ви** form to address all the students. If you are talking to a friend, you will use the **ти** form. Practice these eight verbs until you can recognize them instantly without thinking about the infinitive form.

## Ввічливе прохання (Polite Requests)

> — **Дай це.** — Give this.
> — **Дайте, будь ласка, це.** — Please give this.
> — **Скажіть, будь ласка, де він.** — Please tell, where he is.
> — **Прошу вас читати.** — I ask you to read.

Ukrainians value politeness, especially when giving commands. A direct command can sound a bit harsh if you do not soften it. The universal way to soften any command and turn it into a polite request is by adding the phrase **будь ласка**, which translates to "please." You can place this magical phrase almost anywhere in the sentence. It can go at the very beginning, in the middle, or at the end. The most common position is right after the verb.

- **Дайте, будь ласка, час.** — Please give time.
- **Покажіть, будь ласка, це.** — Please show this.
- **Будь ласка, допоможіть.** — Please help.
- **Зачекайте, будь ласка.** — Please wait.

If you are in a more official situation, such as an office or an upscale service environment, you might hear the phrase **Прошу вас** combined with the infinitive verb. This translates to "I ask you to" and sounds quite formal and respectful. It is a great alternative when you want to show maximum respect to a stranger or a professional.

| Direct Command | Polite Request | Formal Request |
|---|---|---|
| **Слухайте!** | **Слухайте, будь ласка.** | **Прошу вас слухати.** |
| **Пишіть!** | **Пишіть, будь ласка.** | **Прошу вас писати.** |
| **Чекайте!** | **Чекайте, будь ласка.** | **Прошу вас чекати.** |

> [!culture] The Magic of Politeness
> The phrase **будь ласка** literally translates to "be kind." It is an essential tool in your Ukrainian vocabulary. No matter how direct your command might be grammatically, simply dropping this phrase into the sentence will instantly make you sound polite, respectful, and culturally aware.

Sometimes you might also hear an indirect request, like "Could you please...". This is an advanced pattern that uses the conditional mood, which you will study much later. But it is good to recognize it when people speak to you in a very polite manner. For now, stick to adding "please" to your commands to achieve the perfect balance of clarity and politeness.

## Заборони (Prohibitions)

> — **Не пиши тут!** — Do not write here!
> — **Не дивись туди!** — Do not look there!
> — **Не слухайте їх!** — Do not listen to them!
> — **Не ідіть туди.** — Do not go there.

Sometimes you need to tell someone not to do something. Forming a negative command, or a prohibition, is incredibly simple in Ukrainian. You just place the negative word **не** directly before the positive command form. You do not need to change the verb ending at all. The same rules for formal and informal speech apply exactly as they do for positive commands.

| Positive Command | Negative Command |
|---|---|
| **Читайте!** | **Не читайте!** |
| **Стій!** | **Не стій!** |
| **Чекай!** | **Не чекай!** |

- **Не чекайте нас.** — Do not wait for us.
- **Не дайте це.** — Do not give this.
- **Не дивіться там.** — Do not look there.

You might notice that public signs often use the infinitive form with the negative word, rather than the personal command forms you are learning here. A public sign might display the infinitive to indicate a general rule for everyone, rather than a spoken command to you personally. However, when you are speaking directly to a person, you must use the correct personal command forms.

## Практика і підсумок (Summary and Practice)

> — **Скажіть, будь ласка, де вони?** — Please tell, where are they?
> — **Вони там. Ідіть туди!** — They are there. Go there!
> — **Дуже дякую.** — Thank you very much.
> — **Будь ласка. Не чекайте тут.** — You are welcome. Do not wait here.

You have learned how to form commands for the second person singular and plural. You drop the infinitive ending to find the command stem, and then you add the appropriate suffix for formal or group situations. You also learned the eight essential command verbs that you will use every single day. Most importantly, you know how to turn any direct instruction into a polite and culturally appropriate request by adding the universal politeness marker.

- **How do you change an informal command to a formal command?** — You add the suffix **-те** or **-іть** to the end of the informal command word.
- **What are the command forms of the irregular verbs?** — The irregular verbs have special stems. For example, the command for giving is **дай**, the command for saying is **скажи**, and the command for standing is **стій**.
- **How do you make a command negative?** — You simply place the word **не** directly in front of the command verb.
- **Where can you place the politeness marker in a sentence?** — You can put it at the beginning, the middle, or the end, but right after the verb is the most common and natural position.
