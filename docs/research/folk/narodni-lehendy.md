## 1. Definition, Classification, And Boundaries

In this module, `народна легенда` means a short oral prose narrative that speaks
as if it preserves a remembered truth, yet allows the extraordinary: miracle,
metamorphosis, sacred intervention, heroic hyperbole, demonological encounter,
or an explanation of why a place, star, plant, custom, or name is as it is. A
legend is not simply "a story with fantasy." Its distinctive contract is the
combination of a truth claim and a wonder claim. The narrator may not be able to
prove the event historically, but the story points to a witness: a hill, a
river, a church, a ruined wall, a family memory, a local name, a saint's feast, a
known hero, or a still-visible feature of the landscape.

The term itself carries a written-Christian history before it becomes a broad
folk-prose term. ЕСУМ derives `легенда` through German or French from Medieval
Latin `legenda`, glossed as "те, що повинно бути прочитане", originally
liturgical readings. The Ukrainian literary encyclopedia likewise begins from
the medieval hagiographic sense: `verify_quote(author="Колектив")` confirmed the
line "ЛЕГЕНДА (від лат." with score `1.0`, work
`wave8-ukr-lit-entsyklopediia`, chunk `fc2291b5_c4278`. That origin matters
because Ukrainian folk legend is not only pre-Christian survival. It is also
what happened when saints' lives, apocrypha, church readings, local shrines,
older agrarian cosmology, and historical memory entered one oral field.

For classification, use two layers at once. The first is the scholarly layer in
the encyclopedia: mythological, apocryphal, onomatological, and historical
legends (`fc2291b5_c4278`). The second is the pedagogical layer in school
materials: mythological, biblical, and heroic (`5-klas-ukrlit-avramenko-2022_s0006`).
These are not contradictions; they are different grids. A biblical legend in a
schoolbook may be hagiographic or apocryphal in a scholarly catalogue. A
toponymic legend may also be etiological, because it explains the origin of a
name. A historical legend may contain mythological machinery, as in hero cycles
where a warrior becomes invisible, speaks with the earth, or defeats an
impossible enemy. The module should teach this as a map, not as a rigid taxonomy.

The most important boundaries:

- `Легенда` vs `переказ`. A `переказ` is an oral narrative about historical
  events and heroes. The Zabolotnyi textbook explicitly says that `перекази`
  are close to legends but have almost no fantastic elements and are more
  plausible, an expression of historical memory
  (`5-klas-ukrlit-zabolotnyi-2022_s0023`). Грінченко gives `переказ` as
  "передача на словах" and "преданіе", with examples about historical
  traditions. The boundary is porous: an Ivan Sirko story called a `переказ` in
  school materials may include shapeshifting or character-magic and therefore
  behave like a heroic legend. The learner module should mark such cases as
  border forms, not force them into one box.

- `Легенда` vs `міф`. A myth explains the structure and origin of the world,
  gods, spirits, and cosmic order. School sources define myth as a story about
  the origin of the universe, natural phenomena, gods, spirits, and fantastic
  beings (`5-klas-ukrlit-zabolotnyi-2022_s0009`;
  `5-klas-ukrlit-avramenko-2022_s0306`). A legend may preserve mythic material,
  but it localizes it: this star cluster, this lake, this city, this saint, this
  hill, this heroic person. For a C1+ learner, the crucial distinction is scale:
  myth tends toward cosmology; legend tends toward exemplary event plus proof in
  a name, place, or memory.

- `Легенда` vs `казка`. A tale foregrounds invention and compositional freedom:
  a teller may reshape geography, sequence, and character names according to
  tale logic. A legend has less freedom because its truth posture depends on
  named persons, named localities, or a durable explanation. The Zabolotnyi
  textbook notes that, although legends can contain fantastic elements, the
  narrator cannot freely rearrange facts, time, geographical names, or heroes as
  in a tale (`5-klas-ukrlit-zabolotnyi-2022_s0009`). This is the cleanest
  classroom boundary.

- `Легенда` vs `билиця` / `бувальщина`. These are witness-like "this happened"
  forms. ЕСУМ connects `бувальщина` with `бути` / `бувати`, and Грінченко
  defines `бувальщина` as `былое, быль`. The famous Shevchenko line was
  `verify_quote`-confirmed: "Бо се не казка, а билиця," score `0.9796`, author
  `Шевченко Т.`, work `ukrlib-shevchenko`, chunks `3a9666f7_c0000`,
  `d1b5c8a6_c0132`. A `билиця` can be demonological, moral, or anecdotal without
  becoming a full legend. A legend normally has a stronger explanatory or
  exemplary frame.

The five module-facing subtypes should be taught with overlap:

1. Etiological legends explain origins: stars, plants, animals, weather, fire,
   death, mountains, rivers, customs, or moral facts of the world.
2. Hagiographic, biblical, and apocryphal legends place Christian figures,
   saints, icons, Mary, Christ, angels, the devil, or noncanonical biblical
   elaborations into local oral storytelling.
3. Historical and heroic legends preserve community evaluation of persons and
   events: Kyiv and the Mongols, Zaporozhians, Bohdan Khmelnytskyi, Ivan Sirko,
   Ivan Bohun, Semen Palii, Ustym Karmeliuk, Oleksa Dovbush, Taras Shevchenko.
4. Demonological legends narrate encounters with rusalky, mavky, chorty,
   didky, lisovyky, vodyanyky, witches, revenants, treasures, whirlwinds, or
   restless dead. They are often closer to `билиця` in performance but can
   become legends when attached to a place, moral order, or origin explanation.
5. Toponymic / onomatological legends explain names: Kyiv, Pereiaslav,
   Chernihiv, Velykyi Luh, Dnipro, Desna, Shatske Lake, Zmiievi Valy, local
   wells, fields, hills, and village names. They preserve place memory even when
   their etymology is not scientific etymology.

## 2. Origins And Historical Context

Ukrainian folk legend is a layered genre. Its oldest recoverable layers include
mythological explanation, place-memory, and lower demonology; its medieval layers
include hagiography, apocrypha, saints' miracles, and Christian moral narrative;
its early modern and modern layers include Cossack history, opryshky, serfdom,
imperial oppression, local resistance, and the transformation of writers and
national figures into folk heroes. The genre does not have one "birth date."
Most texts were written down in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, but they
often preserve older narrative motifs.

The written-Christian channel is explicit. The encyclopedia says that in
medieval Europe the term first referred to saints' lives read in church on the
saint's day, and then spread to religious-didactic and fantastic stories. It also
notes that from the time of Kyivan Rus there were apocryphal narratives about
Adam, Moses, the creation of the world, Solomon's judgments, and related biblical
material (`fc2291b5_c4278`). Krypiakevych's discussion of apocrypha calls them
expanded narrative matter on biblical themes: they arose and circulated with
Christianity, filled gaps left by canonical Scripture, moved between oral and
written circulation, and were sometimes banned, which only increased popular
interest (`48346587_c0300`). For the learner module, this means "apocryphal" is
not an insult and not the same as "false story" in everyday speech. It marks a
textual and oral zone outside the biblical canon that deeply affected popular
religious imagination.

The Christian-pagan "dual-faith" layer must be handled carefully. Hrushevsky
describes how Christianity, as it moved outside cultural centers, adapted to
older cults and views, producing the mixture called `двоєвіря` in old writing.
His examples include old feast cycles nominally adapted to Christian feasts,
older views of nature filled with supernatural powers, and transfers of traits:
Perun to Saint Elijah, Dazhbog to Saint George, Volos to Saint Vlasii
(`c44f1207_c1264`). The module should not present this as a simple formula
"pagan god became saint." The safer C1+ formulation is that saint narratives
often absorbed older functions, fears, and seasonal logics. Folk legend is one
of the places where that absorption becomes narratively visible.

Another origin channel is place-based historical memory. The encyclopedia lists
historical legends about the Mongol period, Zaporozhians, Khmelnytskyi, Sirko,
Bohun, Palii, Morozenko, Karmeliuk, Dovbush, Chalii, Shevchenko, and others
(`fc2291b5_c4278`). These are not all equally old, not all equally well
documented, and not all equally historical. Their shared function is that they
express community judgment: who defended the people, who betrayed them, who
mocked power, who escaped prison, who avenged injustice, who was remembered as
"ours" when official narratives ignored or distorted the community's view.

The nineteenth-century recording context shaped what we can now study.
Collectors and editors often worked under imperial and colonial labels:
`малорусский`, `южнорусский`, `Западно-Русский край`. Those titles must be cited
accurately as bibliographic artifacts, not repeated as identity categories. A
decolonized module says: the sources were often printed in imperial languages
and categories, but the material is Ukrainian oral tradition recorded from
Ukrainian-speaking communities in specific regions.

Key printed and institutional milestones:

- Mykhailo Drahomanov's `Малорусские народные предания и рассказы` (Kyiv, 1876)
  is listed by the encyclopedia as a core edition for legends and narratives
  (`fc2291b5_c4279`; `feaa5fa7_c0648`). Drahomanov also wrote on Ukrainian
  folk legends and apocrypha in exile (`2971c499_c0049` from the earlier search
  pass).

- Pavlo Chubynskyi's `Труды этнографическо-статистической экспедиции в
  Западно-Русский край` gathered massive ethnographic material in 1872-1878 and
  is cited in language and folklore reading lists (`0988b006_c0348`). For this
  dossier, cite it as historical source context, not as a modern identity label.

- Volodymyr Hnatiuk's `Галицько-руські народні легенди`, volumes 1-2 in
  `Етнографічний збірник` 12-13, Lviv, 1902-1903, is one of the central legend
  editions (`fc2291b5_c4279`, `fc2291b5_c1457`, `fc2291b5_c2346`).

- Borys Hrinchenko's `Этнографические материалы, собранные в Черниговской и
  соседних с ней губерниях` (1895, 1896, 1899) and `З уст народу` (1900) are
  repeatedly cited as ethnographic materials (`0988b006_c0348`,
  `feaa5fa7_c0648`). His dictionary also helps distinguish older Ukrainian
  genre vocabulary from modern calques.

The strongest historical claim we can make is therefore not "this legend is
ancient" in a vague romantic sense. The stronger claim is: Ukrainian folk
legends preserve layered oral memory whose recorded witnesses connect medieval
apocryphal writing, early modern religious storytelling, nineteenth-century
ethnography, regional demonology, local toponymy, and modern national memory.

## 3. Poetics, Form, And Technique

A legend is usually compact. Its energy goes into the turn from ordinary
situation to extraordinary proof: drought becomes constellation, betrayal
becomes lost city gate, social injustice becomes mountain origin, saintly sign
becomes military protection, a demonological warning becomes the reason people
avoid a place after dusk. The plot does not need the full architecture of a
fairy tale. It needs an encounter, a marvel, and a durable sign.

The most concise scholarly poetics line is in the encyclopedia:
`verify_quote(author="Колектив")` confirmed "Сюжетною домінантою Л. є чудо" with
score `1.0`, work `wave8-ukr-lit-entsyklopediia`, chunk `fc2291b5_c4279`. The
same entry names fantasy, metamorphosis, and hyperbole as characteristic
features. In practical teaching terms:

- `чудо` is the pivot, not decoration. The miraculous event proves the moral or
  sacred order of the story.
- `метаморфоза` turns humans into plants, birds, stones, stars, or landscape
  features. The encyclopedia gives typical examples: a girl becomes a poplar, a
  widow a gull, a daughter-in-law a cuckoo, a man a sycamore, oak, or stone
  (`fc2291b5_c4278`).
- `гіпербола` enlarges a historical person into a heroic figure: impossible
  strength, invisibility, supernatural endurance, prophetic speech, or victory
  over a monstrous enemy.
- `локальний доказ` closes the story: "that is why the place is called...", "the
  stars are still visible", "the walls still stand", "people still avoid the
  well", "the plant still burns but does not burn up", "the mountain has that
  shape."

Hrushevsky is useful for the mythological legend because he attends to affect,
not only plot. `verify_quote(author="Грушевський М.")` confirmed "Для
міфологічної легенди як жанру..." with score `1.0`, work
`wave12-hrushevsky-iu-odnotom`, chunk `da46aa92_c0537`, and duplicate
`wave4-hrushevsky-istlit`, chunk `5e7696fa_c0532`. The line names artistic
parallelism aimed at an emotional, sympathetic reaction. In module language:
legend often asks the listener to feel kinship between human suffering and
natural form. A tree, bird, lake, star, or stone is not just scenery; it is a
transformed memory.

Formally, Ukrainian legends tend to use:

- A loose truth marker: "кажуть", "за переказом", "давно це було", "старі люди
  розповідали", "у нашому селі", "коло того місця", "ще й тепер видно".
- A vague or local time: "колись", "за татар", "за панщини", "коли запорожці
  ходили", "як Бог творив світ".
- Concrete place deixis: the named city, river, valley, church, well, grave,
  forest, mountain, or ruin often matters more than exact chronology.
- A tight cast: saint, poor person, widow, child, Cossack, oppressor, lord,
  demon, animal helper, old woman, local hero, stranger, or enemy army.
- A closing explanation: the story is complete when it explains a name, custom,
  landscape form, taboo, or moral truth.

The narrator's stance is also a technique. Legends often avoid the confident
fictional voice of tales. They speak through mediated knowledge: someone saw,
someone heard, elders said, the place proves it. This lets the story remain both
believable and flexible. The teller can vary detail, but the core sign remains.

Demonological legend adds a different poetics: suspense, night setting, taboo,
and witness intensity. The `Енциклопедія українознавства` notes that stories
about the chort are among the most widespread in Ukrainian verbal art and that
the figure may appear as threatening or comic, sometimes even tricked and put to
human service (`feaa5fa7_c0574`). This double register is important. Ukrainian
demonology is not uniformly gothic. It ranges from fear to mockery, from danger
to practical cunning.

Toponymic legend uses what linguistics calls folk etymology. The literary
encyclopedia defines folk etymology as a speaker's reinterpretation of an opaque
word by associating it with familiar sounds and meanings; it adds that folk
etymology underlies many hydronymic and toponymic legends, with examples such as
Chortomlyk, Tomakivka, and Lepetykha (`fc2291b5_c5415`). The module should teach
this explicitly: a toponymic legend is cultural evidence, not automatically
scientific etymology. It tells us how people made a place meaningful.

`Михайлик і Золоті ворота` is the best quote-confirmed primary-like sample

Ukrainian folk `переказ` close to heroic legend and gives it "as it remained in

Why it matters: the legend is both historical and eschatological. It links the
Mongol threat, Kyiv's Golden Gate, communal betrayal, heroic departure, and a
future restoration. The hero's removal of the gate is not plausible chronicle
history; it is a narrative way to say that the city's honor can be lost through
bad communal counsel and restored only when memory speaks rightly. For a C1+
learner, this is an ideal boundary case: the source calls it a `переказ`, the
encyclopedia lists it under historical legends, and the plot behaves like legend
because it depends on marvel and prophetic return.

The `Коваль / Кожум'яка / Змій / Змієві вали` cycle is not quoted here as a
folk primary text, but Popovych provides a compact scholarly witness:

## 5. Practice, Performance, And Social Function

Legends are not only texts. They are a mode of telling that activates place,
memory, and evaluation. A legend about a lake changes how a listener sees the
lake; a legend about a city gate changes how a listener imagines the city's
loss; a demonological tale changes how a child or traveler behaves near water,
forest, graveyard, mill, crossroads, or abandoned building; a heroic legend
changes how a community speaks about justice and resistance.

Performance contexts vary:

- Family and village telling: elders explain why a place has its name, why
  people avoid a ravine, why a hill is sacred, why a tree or well matters.
- Seasonal and ritual contexts: hagiographic and apocryphal legends cluster
  around saints' days, church feasts, household piety, and folk-Christian
  calendars.
- Work and travel contexts: stories about mills, forests, wells, treasures,
  roads, and dangerous places warn listeners and encode practical caution.
- School and print contexts: modern learners often meet legends in grade 5
  literature units, where genre boundaries are simplified but useful.
- Heritage tourism and local museums: legends become a way to narrate castles,
  mounds, city gates, mountain lakes, old churches, and battle sites.

The belief stance is crucial. The teller does not always demand literal belief,
but the story is told as more than entertainment. It asks for respect toward a
place, a dead person, a saint, a hero, or a danger. In demonological `билиці`,
the teller may insist on witness truth: "it happened to my neighbor." In
toponymic legends, the proof is the name. In historical legends, the proof may
be collective memory: "people still say." In apocryphal legends, the proof is a
popular moral or theological order rather than church canon.

For learners, performance should be modeled as restrained oral prose, not
dramatic fairy-tale acting. The voice can be calm, intimate, and place-specific.
Important discourse markers: `кажуть`, `за переказом`, `старі люди
розповідали`, `тому й`, `відтоді`, `і досі`, `не смій`, `не ходи`, `так і
назвали`. Learners should practice the difference between "I am inventing a
tale" and "I am transmitting a local memory."

Legends also organize moral categories:

- Mercy: `Про Зоряний Віз` turns giving water into stars.
- Loyalty and betrayal: `Михайлик і Золоті ворота` makes bad communal counsel
  visible in the loss of the gate.
- Justice against oppression: Dovbush, Karmeliuk, and related heroes are
  remembered as protectors of the poor, even when historical documents are more
  complex.
- Sacred protection: `Неопалима купина` uses a biblical sign to narrate the
  defense of homeland.
- Boundary respect: demonological legends teach when not to mock, go, dig,
  swim, whistle, or trespass.
- Place belonging: toponymic legends say that a community's landscape is not
  empty space; it is readable memory.

## 6. Collectors, Editions, And Researchers

Mykhailo Drahomanov is indispensable for nineteenth-century Ukrainian folk
prose. The encyclopedia lists `Малорусские народные предания и рассказы` (Kyiv,
1876) among key editions for legends and `перекази` (`fc2291b5_c4279`), and
the `Енциклопедія українознавства` lists it under folk prose and demonological
stories (`feaa5fa7_c0648`). Drahomanov's title uses the imperial-era
`малорусские`; the module should cite the title but frame the material as
Ukrainian.

Pavlo Chubynskyi matters because his expedition created one of the largest
nineteenth-century ethnographic corpora. Ohiienko's language-history reading
recommendations list Chubynskyi's seven-volume `Труды
этнографическо-статистической экспедиции в Западно-русский край` alongside
Kуліш, Drahomanov/Antonovych, Nomys, Rudchenko, Hrinchenko, and others
(`0988b006_c0348`). Again, the title's colonial geography is a historical fact
of publication, not a category the module should reproduce uncritically.

Volodymyr Hnatiuk is the central named collector/editor for this topic. The
Ukrainian literary encyclopedia says he initiated systematic scholarly
publication of Ukrainian folklore and ethnographic materials, including
`Галицько-руські народні легенди` in two volumes, 1902-1903
(`fc2291b5_c1457`). The `Етнографічний збірник` article notes that Hnatiuk's
legend volumes, opришky narratives, demonology materials, and other collections
appeared in a high-scholarly apparatus series of the Shevchenko Scientific
Society (`fc2291b5_c2346`). For the module, Hnatiuk is not just a name in a
bibliography; he models how regional variants, informants, genres, and apparatus
matter.

Borys Hrinchenko matters in two ways. First, his ethnographic materials from
Chernihiv and neighboring provinces are repeatedly cited in folklore reading
lists (`0988b006_c0348`, `feaa5fa7_c0648`). Second, his dictionary gives
pre-Soviet evidence for genre vocabulary: `переказ`, `бувальщина`, `билиця`.
This helps keep the module from importing unnatural register or Russian-pattern
terms.

module cites the layer it is using.

## 7. Cultural And Anti-Colonial Role

The carrying-identity-under-oppression thesis is strongest when concrete. Folk
legends carried Ukrainian identity under oppression in at least five ways.

First, they preserved place-memory. Toponymic legends taught people to read the
land in Ukrainian narrative terms: a river has a story, a hill has a loss, a
city gate has a promise, a lake has a moral, a field has a name. This mattered
under imperial renaming and administrative abstraction. The `Енциклопедія
українознавства` notes both colonizing/artificial names in the South, such as
Odesa, Kherson, Sevastopol, and others, and folk-etymological transformations
such as Drohobych under the influence of a "Second Bull" legend
(`feaa5fa7_c0852`). A toponymic legend may be linguistically wrong and still be
culturally powerful, because it resists the idea that the land is nameless
imperial space.

Second, legends preserved a moral history of heroes outside official archives.
The encyclopedia's list of legend figures - Zaporozhians, Khmelnytskyi, Sirko,
Bohun, Palii, Morozenko, Karmeliuk, Dovbush, Chalii, Shevchenko - is a map of
popular evaluation (`fc2291b5_c4278`). Some were Cossack leaders, some rebels,
some outlaws, some writers turned folk figures. The point is not that every
legend is historically exact. The point is that communities narrated who
defended them, who tricked power, who embodied courage, who suffered with the
people, and who could be imagined as returning, escaping, or remaining alive in
memory.

Third, legends kept Ukrainian language and local idiom active in domains where
imperial languages dominated print, school, church administration, and state
documents. Ohiienko explicitly recommends reading ethnographic materials for
deeper language knowledge (`0988b006_c0348`). Folklore preserved syntax,
address, rural vocabulary, taboo terms, place names, dialect features, and oral
formulae that did not depend on state approval.

Fourth, apocryphal and hagiographic legends localized sacred history. Popular
Christian imagination could tell about creation, saints, Mary, death, the devil,
and justice in a language and symbolic world close to the community. This was
not simply resistance to church authority; it was a way of making cosmic and
Christian history intelligible in local categories. Because some apocryphal
legends were noncanonical and sometimes persecuted by the church
(`fc2291b5_c4278`; `48346587_c0300`), they also preserved a popular theological
voice beyond official doctrine.

Fifth, collectors and institutions turned dispersed oral memory into archives.
The Shevchenko Scientific Society's `Етнографічний збірник` printed Hnatiuk's
legend and demonology materials with scholarly apparatus (`fc2291b5_c2346`).
Drahomanov's 1876 collection, Chubynskyi's expedition, Hrinchenko's materials,
and Franko's apocryphal editions made folk prose harder to erase. The archive
itself became anti-colonial cultural infrastructure.

This anti-colonial role must not be inflated into simple propaganda. Legends
can preserve prejudice, local rivalry, class fantasy, confessional tension, and
gendered fear. They can also romanticize violence or turn complex persons into
icons. The decolonized approach is not "believe every legend"; it is "read the
legend as evidence of Ukrainian memory-making under unequal power."

## 8. Bridges To Literature, Art, And High Culture

The encyclopedia says legend motifs were widely used by Kvitka-Osnovianenko,
Shevchenko, Rudanskyi, Franko, Lesia Ukrainka, Rylskyi, Sosiura, Malyshko,
Zahul, Lina Kostenko, and others (`fc2291b5_c4279`). This is the module's main
bridge from folk prose to high culture.

Shevchenko is central in both directions. He collected folk songs, dumy,
stories, `перекази`, proverbs, and legends (`d77902e2_c2140`), used folk
historical memory in his writing, and then became the subject of folk legends
himself. The Shevchenko dictionary lists studies and editions such as Krymskyi's
`Народні легенди про Шевченка`, Yaremenko's work on Shevchenko in folk
`перекази` and legends, Sokolov's `Народні легенди про Шевченка`, and the 1963
volume `Народ і Шевченко. Легенди, перекази, пісні...` (`d77902e2_c2312`).
This feedback loop is crucial: a poet shaped by folklore becomes a folk figure.

Lesia Ukrainka is the bridge for demonological imagination. `Лісова пісня` does
not simply copy folk beings; it transforms Volyn/Polissia legend, belief, and
lower mythology into modern drama. Krypiakevych's discussion names Vodyanyk,
Water Rusalkа, Lisovyk, Perelesnyk, Poterchata, Kuts, Field Rusalka, and Mavka
as figures through which the play poetizes forest, water, marsh, field, and
human freedom (`48346587_c0601`). For the module, this bridge lets learners see
why demonological legend is not marginal: it helped produce one of the central
works of Ukrainian modern literature.

Franko matters through apocrypha, legend scholarship, and literary processing.
His `Апокрифи і легенди з українських рукописів` made noncanonical religious
narrative available as part of Ukrainian literary history. The dossier should
not treat apocrypha as mere "superstition"; in high culture, they become a
serious archive of imagination, ethics, and medieval textual circulation.

Natalena Koroleva's `Легенди старокиївські` offers a literary reworking of
ancient Kyiv legend material (`fc2291b5_c3739` from the earlier grounding pass).
It is not folk primary text, but it is useful for showing how the legend frame
can become historical fiction and spiritual prose.

Visual art can use demonological and heroic motifs. Butovych's 1924 Leipzig
woodcuts `Українські духи` are noted as deeply engaged with Ukrainian
demonology (`48346587_c0868` from the earlier grounding pass). The module can
use this as a visual bridge: not a cartoon monster gallery, but an artist's
attempt to give graphic form to beings remembered in folk speech.

The high-culture bridge should always preserve directionality:

- Folk legend gives literature motifs, plots, images, discourse markers, and
  moral conflicts.
- Literature gives legend new prestige, new audience, and sometimes new
  variants.
- A literary retelling is not automatically a primary folk text.
- A school anthology version is not automatically a collector edition.
- A painting or drama may be a brilliant interpretation and still not a source
  for reconstructing "ancient belief."

## 9. Decolonization, NPOV, And Source Disagreement

This topic carries several predictable risks. The dossier resolves them as
follows.

Avoid Russocentric framing. Do not call Ukrainian legends "south Russian" or
"Little Russian" except inside exact historical titles. Do not treat Kyivan Rus
as a precursor of modern Russia in a way that erases Ukrainian cultural
continuity. Do not take imperial publication geography (`Западно-Русский край`)
as an identity label. When citing Drahomanov, Chubynskyi, Hrinchenko, or older
editions with imperial titles, say explicitly that these are bibliographic
titles produced under historical conditions.

Avoid romantic-nationalist overclaim. A legend is not a document unless
independently corroborated. It may preserve a historical memory, but it may also
compress, moralize, relocate, or invent. `Михайлик і Золоті ворота` is powerful
Kyiv memory; it is not a literal proof that the gate was carried to
Constantinople. Dovbush and Karmeliuk legends express popular justice; they do
not replace historical research. Sirko shapeshifting belongs to heroic legend
logic; it is not military biography.

Avoid pantheon inflation. School materials sometimes list ancient gods and
beings, including figures whose modern interpretation is contested. The module
may mention that school sources introduce Perun, Veles, Yarilo, Berehynia,
rusalky, mavky, lisovyky, vodyanyky, domovyky, and witches
(`5-klas-ukrlit-zabolotnyi-2022_s0009`), but it should not build an invented
fixed "Ukrainian pantheon" from modern popular lists. The safer evidence-based
statement is that lower mythology and place-based spirits are strongly attested
in ethnographic materials, while higher pre-Christian religion is harder to
reconstruct.

Do not flatten `двоєвір'я`. It is not proof of a pure pagan survival hidden
under Christianity, and it is not proof that folk Christianity is defective.
Hrushevsky's evidence supports a mixed cultural process: old calendar, nature,
and supernatural views persisted and interacted with Christian feasts, saints,
and rites (`c44f1207_c1264`). The learner module should use `нашарування`,
`переплетення`, `локалізація`, and `переосмислення`, not crude replacement
formulae.

Treat folk etymology honestly. The literary encyclopedia says folk etymology
underlies many hydronymic and toponymic legends (`fc2291b5_c5415`), and the
toponymy article notes Drohobych / `Другий Бич` as folk-etymological influence
(`feaa5fa7_c0852`). This is cultural data, not linguistic proof. The module can
say "легенда пояснює назву", not "так насправді походить назва", unless ЕСУМ or
toponymic scholarship confirms it.

Respect regional variation. Hnatiuk's Galician-Ruthenian legend volumes,
Podillia materials, Carpathian legends, Hutsul demonology, Kyiv historical
cycles, and Black Sea / southern toponyms do not form one uniform corpus.
Learners should encounter Ukrainian legend as a polycentric tradition: Galicia,
Podillia, Polissia, Hutsulshchyna, central Ukraine, the Dnipro region,
Slobozhanshchyna, southern steppe, Crimea, and diaspora contexts may preserve
different motifs and names.

Resolve source conflicts by hierarchy. Collector editions, scholarly
encyclopedias, etymological dictionaries, historical dictionaries, and textbook
corpus evidence outrank unsourced web claims. Wikipedia-only extraordinary
claims should be flagged, not asserted. School textbooks are useful for current
pedagogical classification and common examples, but their excerpt wording is not
automatically quote-safe. This is why the dossier marks unverified school legend
text as paraphrase-only.

Performance sequence for a legend-retelling activity:

1. Name the place/person/object: river, lake, gate, saint, hero, plant, mountain,
   star cluster, well, forest, or ruin.
2. Establish the truth posture: `кажуть`, `за переказом`, `старі люди
   розповідали`, `і досі видно`.
3. Present the crisis: drought, enemy, injustice, taboo violation, demon
   encounter, impossible task, sacred test.
4. Turn to the marvel: miracle, transformation, supernatural helper, saintly
   sign, heroic hyperbole, chort tricked, earth opening, star-making.
