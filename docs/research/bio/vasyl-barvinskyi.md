## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Барвінський Василь Олександрович.
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** В. Б.; Vasyl Barvinskyi; Vasyl Barvinsky.
- **Born:** 1888-02-20 | Ternopil | Austria-Hungary. ESU gives 20.02.1888, Ternopil.
- **Died:** 1963-06-09 | Lviv | Ukrainian SSR, USSR. ESU gives 09.06.1963, Lviv.
- **Family / education key facts:** Son of Oleksandr and Yevheniia Barvinskyi; brother of Bohdan and Oleksandr; father of Ivan-Sebastian. Studied piano in Lviv and composition in Prague; became a composer, pianist, musicologist, critic, teacher, and organizer of Ukrainian musical life. ESU gives doctor of art history in 1940 and honorary doctor of the Ukrainian University in Prague in 1938.

## 2. Oppression mechanism

- **What happened:** Arrest, forced confession/context of Soviet ideological purge, ten-year camp exile with wife, banning and destruction of manuscripts.
- **When:** ESU states that he was arrested on 1948-01-29 and exiled with his wife for ten years to Mordovian camps; he returned to Lviv in 1958.
- **By whom:** Soviet security and judicial apparatus; exact agency and case file number in retrieved sources: source not found. The mechanism belongs to the Stalinist postwar campaign against Ukrainian cultural institutions in western Ukraine.
- **Document references:** ESU provides the arrest date and camp term. Retrieved sources did not expose a stable case number, indictment text, or rehabilitation document number.
- **Mechanism specifics:** Barvinskyi had rebuilt and led professional Ukrainian music education in Lviv. After the Soviet return to western Ukraine, his role as rector, composer, and public Ukrainian cultural authority made him vulnerable. The 1948 arrest removed him and his wife Nataliia Puluj-Barvinska from Lviv cultural life. Sources also document destruction of manuscripts: later accounts describe orders to burn his scores and the long posthumous work of recovery. The damage was not only biographical; it erased repertoire, pedagogy, and institutional memory.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** Printed and copied works, student memory, diaspora preservation, and recovered manuscripts survived. ESU and modern music scholarship emphasize the loss or delayed recovery of many scores, including the long-lost piano concerto copy found in Argentina in 1993.

The post-camp evidence is pedagogically useful because it shows recovery rather than simple closure. Dyka's article reproduces a 1963 letter in which Barvinskyi, ill and only a few months from death, still asks for school concert programs and sends music to the school. That letter does not erase the camp term; it shows what Soviet repression failed to destroy: his habit of thinking institutionally about Ukrainian musical education.

## 3. Major works

- `1908` — *8 прелюдій*, piano cycle; ESU notes its role in bringing impressionist features into Ukrainian piano music.
- `1911` — *Українська рапсодія*, symphonic work.
- `1911` — Piano Trio, chamber work.
- `1912` — String Quartet, chamber work.
- `1915` — Piano Sextet, chamber work and part of his instrumental-modernizing project.
- `1926` — Cello Sonata, chamber repertoire.
- `1935` — Second String Quartet.
- `1937` — Piano Concerto; manuscript lost/suppressed, copy found in Argentina in 1993 per later music sources.
- `1953-1963` — Piano Quintet and late works after camp return; part of the reconstruction period.
- `Various` — carol and folk-song arrangements, music criticism, and pedagogical miniatures.

## 4. Primary-source quotes

- "світлі моменти з нашої історії музики" — quoted from Barvinskyi's published musical-literary heritage in Nina Dyka's LNMA article. Pedagogically, this short phrase shows his historical optimism after repeated cultural loss.
- "щоб не думали, що я перестав цікавитися школою" — letter to Oleksandr Tyshchenko, 1963-02-28, reproduced in the same LNMA article. It shows the teacher's continued institutional care only months before his death.

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** Musical-critical, pedagogical, and epistolary; public prose is formal but not opaque.
- **CEFR readiness for full reading:** B2/C1 for letters; C1 for music criticism.
- **Lexicon notes:** Music terms: *прелюдія*, *квартет*, *секстет*, *соната*, *гармонічна мова*, *камерно-інструментальний жанр*.
- **Stylistic features:** Polite epistolary formulas, careful institutional memory, and national-cultural vocabulary without sloganizing.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** The size of the destroyed corpus; the relation between his Prague training and Ukrainian modernism; how to reconstruct works from copies and late testimony.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** He is often summarized as "composer whose manuscripts were burned"; that is true but incomplete. His pedagogical, critical, and institutional roles are equally important.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them:** Soviet framings treated western Ukrainian cultural institutions as nationalist contamination; modern Russocentric music histories often fold him into a vague Soviet regional canon and omit the arrest.
- **Other-perspective considerations:** His life crosses Habsburg, Polish, Soviet, Czech, and diaspora networks. Do not reduce that complexity to a single anti-Soviet martyr arc.

Another point for writers is genre balance. ESU identifies chamber, symphonic, piano, vocal, and pedagogical work; a lesson that uses only the story of destroyed scores risks turning music into biography alone. Phase 2 should pair the repression mechanism with listening or score-reading tasks so learners see the composer's formal choices, not only the state's violence.
