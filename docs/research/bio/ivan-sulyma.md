## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Іван Сулима. ЕІУ's biography title is
  `СУЛИМА Іван`; IEU uses `Ivan Sulyma` inside the Sulyma-family entry. Use
  `Ivan Sulyma` for English metadata. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T1: IEU Sulyma]
- **Patronymic / fuller form:** `Іван Михайлович Сулима` is source-supported in
  the ЕІУ family article, where the founder of the later Sulyma line is named
  as Hetman Ivan Mykhailovych Sulyma. The main ЕІУ biography does not put the
  patronymic in the title, so treat it as an alias/metadata variant rather than
  mandatory canonical display. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma family; T1: ЕІУ Sulyma]
- **Aliases / spelling variants:** Іван Сулима; Іван Михайлович Сулима; Ivan
  Sulyma; Ivan Sulima; Sulyma, Ivan; older OCR/source spellings such as
  `Судима` or `Сулим` in quoted material. Do not normalize source spellings
  inside quotation notes without a comment. [T1/T2/T4]
- **Born / died:** birth year unknown; died in **1635**. Some popular pages give
  exact birth years or **12 December 1635** for execution, but the inspected
  Tier 1 baseline only supports unknown birth and 1635 death. Use exact day
  only after checking the source edition behind the claim. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma;
  T3/T5]
- **Origin / status:** ЕІУ describes him as a petty noble from Chernihiv region
  who served for a long time as estate manager for the Zolkiewski and
  Daniłowicz magnate households near Boryspil. This makes him a frontier
  estate/service figure before his Zaporizhian career, not simply a
  spontaneous folk avenger. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma]
- **Move into Cossack life:** ЕІУ says magnate oppression pushed him into
  Cossack activity. Phrase this carefully: the source gives a social-pressure
  explanation, but the exact personal break, motives, and timing remain
  source-thin. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T4: Shcherbak 1994 lead]
- **Office / constituency:** elected as kosh otaman at the Zaporizhian Sich and
  first mentioned as Cossack hetman in **1628**. The safer teaching formula is
  "leader of unregistered / Zaporizhian Cossacks," because his authority came
  through volatile Sich and campaign councils rather than a stable state office.
  [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T4: Shcherbak 1994 lead]
- **Campaign profile before 1635:** ЕІУ says he took part in many campaigns
  against Ottoman and Tatar forces. IEU related entries and Shcherbak's
  specialist portrait connect him with Black Sea / Crimean frontier warfare,
  but exact campaign lists require source-edition recheck before classroom use.
  [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T1: IEU Kiliia/Moldavia leads; T4]
- **Papal-medal tradition:** Shcherbak's historical portrait, citing Albrecht
  Radziwill's diary, reports a papal-gift tradition connected with freeing or
  delivering captives. Because the inspected Tier 1 Sulyma article does not
  include this episode, keep it as a source-sensitive tradition and recheck
  Radziwill before using it as a lesson fact. [T4: Shcherbak 1994; T3/T5
  caution]
- **Kodak context:** Kodak was built in summer 1635 on the right bank of the
  Dnipro opposite the Kodak rapid to isolate the Zaporizhian Sich from the
  Ukrainian lands under Commonwealth control. ЕІУ names Beauplan as the
  engineering lead and a garrison of about 200 German mercenaries under Jean
  Marion. [T1: ЕІУ Kodak]
- **1635 uprising:** in summer 1635 Sulyma led a Zaporizhian movement from the
  Sich onto the volost. On the night of **11-12 August 1635** by ЕІУ Sulyma,
  Cossacks stormed Kodak and destroyed the garrison. Kovalets warns that the
  exact capture date is source-dependent and best bounded to the first half of
  August unless a module is quoting a specific edition. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T1:
  ЕІУ Kodak; T4: Kovalets 2013]
- **After Kodak:** ЕІУ says the rebels moved up the Dnipro toward Kremenchuk and
  Kryliv and sent universals calling the Ukrainian population to struggle
  against Commonwealth noble oppression. Kovalets reconstructs broader plans
  for returning into Ukraine but marks much of the next phase as uncertain and
  dependent on sparse, contradictory reports. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T4: Kovalets
  2013]
- **Suppression:** royal commissioners Adam Kysil and Lukasz Zolkiewski
  organized registered Cossacks against the rebels. ЕІУ presents a months-long
  defense on a Dnipro island, followed by registered Cossacks capturing Sulyma
  and handing him to the authorities. Kovalets argues the fortified-camp
  geography and the sequence after Kodak must be reconstructed cautiously. [T1:
  ЕІУ Sulyma; T4: Kovalets 2013; T2: Lviv Chronicle]
- **Execution:** Sulyma and several companions were taken to Warsaw and executed
  as enemies of the Commonwealth. The Lviv Chronicle says four were executed
  and two spared through diplomatic intercession; treat the exact count and
  intercession narrative as source-edition material. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T2: Lviv
  Chronicle]
- **Family/descendant legacy:** IEU and ЕІУ family entries trace later Sulyma
  descendants in the Cossack-starshyna / Left-Bank elite line, especially sons
  Stepan and Fedir. Use this for legacy and archival context, not as proof that
  the 1635 revolt was an elite-family project. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma family; T1: IEU
  Sulyma]

   display title.

   module checks Lazarevsky, Motyzhyn/Sulyma archive material, or another
   specialist source directly.

5. **Papal recognition:** source-sensitive and plausible as a tradition through

8. **Betrayal/capture:** Lviv Chronicle language makes betrayal vivid, but that

## 2. Oppression / appropriation mechanism

**What happened:** Sulyma's biography is useful because one life exposes several
early-seventeenth-century mechanisms at once: frontier service, magnate estate
pressure, Cossack maritime warfare, register restriction, infrastructure
control, commissioner diplomacy, and exemplary punishment. Kodak was not just a
fortress in a battle story. ЕІУ Kodak says it was built to isolate the
Zaporizhian Sich from the Ukrainian lands under Commonwealth authority. Its
location near the Dnipro rapids made movement, communication, and return from
the Sich/sea corridor politically visible and militarily vulnerable. [T1: ЕІУ
Sulyma; T1: ЕІУ Kodak]

**By whom:** Commonwealth military and administrative power in the Kodak
construction and punishment layer; magnate estate structures in the early
biography layer; commissioners Adam Kysil and Lukasz Zolkiewski in the
suppression layer; registered Cossack officers and units in the enforcement
layer; unregistered Zaporizhians and expelled-from-register Cossacks in the
mobilization layer. Sulyma also had agency: he commanded violent maritime raids,
accepted Cossack leadership, destroyed a garrison, issued or sponsored
universals, and chose armed confrontation.

**Document references:** Kurukove/register context; the 1635 sejm measure
against Cossack "disorder" as discussed in Shcherbak/Kovalets leads; ЕІУ Kodak
on fortress purpose and garrison; Lviv Chronicle on the assault and capture
tradition; Kovalets 2013 on source fragments from Muscovite questioning and
Commonwealth reports; Radziwill diary as a lead for trial/execution memory.
[T1: ЕІУ Kodak; T2: Lviv Chronicle; T4: Kovalets 2013; T4: Shcherbak 1994]

**Mechanism specifics:** Kodak attempted to make Cossack mobility governable.
It stood between the Sich and the volost world, where Cossacks, peasants,
townspeople, estate managers, magnates, commissioners, and registered officers
all had different stakes. Destroying Kodak challenged that infrastructure, but
Sulyma's force could not convert the shock into durable control. Kovalets
emphasizes resistance from registered Cossacks, rumor, sparse evidence, and the
probable retreat toward a fortified Zaporizhian position. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T4:
Kovalets 2013]

**What survived / what was distorted:** what survived is a strong memory of
Kodak as an anti-coercion event and Sulyma as a martyr executed in Warsaw. What
gets distorted is the simplicity of the story. Imperial/Commonwealth labels can
reduce the revolt to criminal disorder; Soviet inheritance can flatten it into
class conflict; patriotic memory can make Sulyma a pure proto-national hero;
adventure prose can turn him into a flawless sea warrior. A decolonized reading
keeps the harder structure: constrained frontier society, coercive
infrastructure, Cossack agency, factional enforcement, violence on all sides,
and later memory-making.

## 3. Major works / legacy

Sulyma left no literary corpus; "works" means offices, campaigns, documents,
military actions, family archive afterlife, and memory.

- `before 1615` — early life remains source-thin. The safest baseline is
  petty-noble Chernihiv-region origin and family connection to Mykhailo Sulyma,
  with exact birth data left open. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T1: ЕІУ Sulyma family]
- `1615 and after` — served in estate-management roles around Boryspil /
  Pereiaslav holdings connected with Zolkiewski and Daniłowicz magnate
  households. Use as evidence of a service/estate background before his
  Zaporizhian career. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T4: Shcherbak 1994 lead]
- `1620s` — became active on Zaporizhia, was elected kosh otaman, and built a
  reputation in campaigns against Ottoman and Tatar frontier targets. Specific
  campaign narratives need source-edition confirmation. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma]
- `before 1621, source-sensitive` — papal-medal / captive-galley tradition.
  Treat as an advanced source-criticism case: if true, it links Ottoman galley
  captivity, Christian-captive diplomacy, and elite recognition; if unverified,
  it shows how memory magnifies a Cossack sea commander. [T4: Shcherbak 1994;
  T3/T5]
- `1628` — first mentioned as Cossack hetman by ЕІУ; Shcherbak's portrait
  points to a 29 December 1628 letter form as a research lead. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma;
  T4: Shcherbak 1994]
- `1629` — likely connected with the Crimea/Perekop campaign environment that
  also involved Hryhorii Chornyi and Taras Fedorovych. Teach as a comparison
  point for contested leadership and campaign councils, not as a settled
  single-commander triumph. [T4: Shcherbak 1994; Existing dossier:
  `docs/research/bio/taras-fedorovych-triasylo.md`]
- `early 1630s` — continued as a prominent Zaporizhian sea-campaign leader.
  IEU related entries name Sulyma in connection with Kiliia/Moldavia-region
  Ottoman-frontier raiding; Shcherbak/Kovalets provide further Azov/Black Sea
  leads. [T1: IEU Kiliia/Moldavia leads; T4]
- `summer 1635` — returned to the center of source attention around Kodak,
  with a Cossack council and command of unregistered / Zaporizhian forces.
  [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T4: Kovalets 2013; T2: Lviv Chronicle]
- `11-12 August 1635 by ЕІУ` — storming and destruction of Kodak. ЕІУ Kodak
  presents Kodak as a newly built fortification with a garrison under Jean
  Marion; the Lviv Chronicle gives a vivid but partisan source-language
  account. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T1: ЕІУ Kodak; T2: Lviv Chronicle]
- `August-September 1635` — movement toward Kremenchuk/Kryliv and attempted
  wider mobilization. Kovalets reconstructs possible plans for a broader
  uprising, while warning that the evidence after Kodak is fragmentary. [T1:
  ЕІУ Sulyma; T4: Kovalets 2013]
- `autumn 1635` — pressure from commissioners and registered Cossacks; retreat
  or defense around a fortified Dnipro/Zaporizhian position; eventual capture
  or hand-over of leaders. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T2: Lviv Chronicle; T4: Kovalets
  2013]
- `late 1635` — execution in Warsaw with several companions. Teach as
  exemplary punishment designed to discipline Cossack revolt and Black Sea
  raiding, not as inevitable legal justice. [T1: ЕІУ Sulyma; T2: Lviv
  Chronicle; T4: Shcherbak 1994]
- `1637-1638 afterlife` — IEU Pavliuk says Pavlo But/Pavliuk participated in
  the 1635 Sulyma rebellion before leading the 1637 revolt. Sulyma therefore
  belongs in a sequence of post-1630 Cossack pressure, not an isolated anecdote.
  [T1: IEU Pavliuk; T1: IEU Wladyslaw IV Vasa]
- `later family archive` — the Sulyma family became a Cossack-starshyna /
  Left-Bank elite line; IEU notes that part of the family archive was published
  by Oleksander Lazarevsky as *Sulimovskii arkhiv*. [T1: IEU Sulyma]
- `1913 and later literary memory` — Andrian Kashchenko's *Nad Kodatskym
  porohom* made Sulyma a popular historical-fiction figure. Use it in LIT-HIST
  as memory-making, not as a documentary biography. [Existing dossier:
  `docs/research/bio/andrian-kashchenko.md`; Existing plan:
  `curriculum/l2-uk-en/plans/lit-hist-fic/andrian-kashchenko-cossack-myth.yaml`]

## 6. Contested points

- **Canonical name and patronymic:** use `Ivan Sulyma` / `Іван Сулима` as the
  canonical form; keep `Іван Михайлович Сулима` in aliases because the ЕІУ
  family article supports it.
- **Birth year and birthplace:** exact data are not secure from the inspected
  Tier 1 baseline. Avoid opening a module with a precise birth scene.
- **Status:** petty-noble origin and estate-management service matter. They
  prevent a simple class-war biography, but they do not make him merely an
  elite client either.
- **Magnate oppression:** ЕІУ uses this as a causal phrase. A lesson should
  name estate pressure and frontier opportunity without inventing a personal
  grievance story.
- **Office/title:** `hetman` can mean a Cossack campaign leader or unregistered
  Cossack leader in this context. Do not map it onto later Hetmanate state
  offices.
- **Campaign fame:** Black Sea / Crimean / Ottoman-frontier campaigns are core
  to Sulyma's profile, but detailed lists and papal-medal claims need a
  checked specialist or source edition before becoming quiz facts.
- **Papal / royal recognition:** treat the papal-medal tradition as an
  interesting source problem; do not use it to sanitize raiding, captivity, or
  Ottoman-frontier violence.
- **Kodak violence:** the garrison was destroyed. Do not make the episode a
  clean liberation scene; teach infrastructure coercion and insurgent violence
  together.
- **Registered Cossacks:** do not flatten registered Cossacks into traitors.
  They were inside a coercive register/pay/service order and were used by
  commissioners against unregistered forces.
- **Betrayal narrative:** the Lviv Chronicle's betrayal language is important,
  but the dossier should keep it as source language rather than neutral
  narrator judgment.
- **Execution and martyrdom:** Warsaw execution becomes martyr memory, but the
  legal-punishment frame in Commonwealth sources and heroic memory in later
  Ukrainian prose both need source criticism.
- **Kashchenko memory:** *Nad Kodatskym porohom* is useful for historical
  fiction and Soviet-era suppression of Cossack myth, not for factual campaign
  reconstruction.
