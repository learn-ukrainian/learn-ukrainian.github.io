## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Філарет Михайлович Колесса.
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Філарет Колесса; Filaret Kolessa. Russian-imperial
  transliterations must not appear in body text except in the forbidden-forms
  field in §8.
- **Born:** 17 July 1871 | село Татарське (now Піщани, Стрийський район),
  Львівщина | then Austria-Hungary [T1: ЕІУ] [T3: uk.wikipedia].
- **Died:** 3 March 1947 | Львів | buried in the family vault at the Lychakiv
  cemetery (поле № 78) [T3: uk.wikipedia].
- **Family / education key facts:** Brother of the scholars Олександр and Іван
  Колесса; father of the composer Микола Колесса. Studied at the Vienna
  university (1891–1892), attending Anton Bruckner's harmony lectures, and
  graduated from the Lviv university (1896); doctorate in philology, Vienna,
  1918 [T3: uk.wikipedia]. Core roles: ethnographer, folklorist, composer,
  musicologist, literary scholar — founder of Ukrainian ethnographic
  musicology; full member of the НТШ (1909) and the ВУАН (1929) [T1: ЕІУ].

## 2. Oppression mechanism

Not Applicable as a **Russian/Soviet** repression-primary biography — Kolessa was
not arrested, exiled, or executed. The mechanism to name honestly is the Soviet
co-optation and constraint of his final years, framed per the expansion-memo
guidance on Soviet-era cultural figures.

- **What happened:** After the 1939 Soviet annexation of Western Ukraine,
  Kolessa was absorbed into Soviet institutions and public roles while continuing
  Ukrainian folklore scholarship under ideological constraint.
- **When (specific dates):** elected to the People's Assembly of Western Ukraine
  (October 1939); from 1939 professor at the Lviv university; director of the
  state ethnography museum in Львів (from 1940); head of the Lviv branch of the
  Institute of Art Studies, Folklore and Ethnography of the Academy of Sciences
  of the Ukrainian SSR (from 1940); deputy of the USSR Supreme Soviet (1947)
  [T3: uk.wikipedia].
- **By whom (specific agency):** the Soviet state administration of occupied
  Western Ukraine and the Academy of Sciences of the Ukrainian SSR.
- **Document references:** his Soviet-era institutional appointments and the
  wartime edited volume «Фольклор Вітчизняної війни» (1945) are the documentary
  anchors of the co-optation; cite these directly rather than implying either
  martyrdom or willing service.
- **Mechanism specifics:** the honest frame is constraint, not persecution — a
  Galician scholar whose «Марш українських стрільців» (1915, Sich Riflemen) and
  NTSh-era national-music work sat uneasily with the new Soviet order, and who
  preserved Ukrainian folklore scholarship inside Soviet structures. Do not
  invent repression; do not present the Soviet roles as a success story.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** his phonograph recordings of the
  kobzar–lirnyk duma repertoire and his folklore corpus survived and are
  foundational; the manuscript «Історія української етнографії» remained
  unpublished at his death.

## 3. Major works

- `1905` — «Огляд українсько-руської народної поезії» (Львів, Просвіта) [T3].
- `1906`–`1907` — «Ритміка українських народних пісень» (ЗНТШ) [T3].
- `1910`, `1913` — «Мелодії українських народних дум», vols XIII–XIV — phonograph
  transcriptions of the kobzar–lirnyk duma repertoire, fruit of the 1908
  Naddniprianshchyna expedition with Опанас Сластьон, Леся Українка, and Климент
  Квітка [T3].
- `1920` — «Українські народні думи», first complete edition with study and
  notes (Львів, Просвіта) [T3].
- `1928` — «Українська народна пісня у найновішій фазі свого розвитку» [T3].
- `1936`–`1937` — «Балада про дочку-пташку в слов'янській народній поезії» [T3].
- `1938` — «Українська усна словесність» (645 pp.), the first Ukrainian folklore
  textbook and chrestomathy [T3].
- Composer of choral works on texts of Шевченко, Федькович, Маковей, and Олесь,
  and of «Марш українських стрільців» (1915); collections «Наша дума» (1902) and
  regional song arrangements [T3].

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** academic musicological and folkloristic prose, plus the dialectal
  folk-song texts and duma recitative he transcribed.
- **CEFR readiness for full reading:** C1 for the scholarly prose; B2 for glossed
  duma and folk-song excerpts (with the heightened recitative register noted).
- **Lexicon notes:** ethnomusicological terminology (ритміка, речитатив,
  наверствування); dialectism in the recorded songs needs gloss.
- **Stylistic features:** combined text-and-melody analysis, genre-and-rhythm
  formulae, comparative-Slavic framing kept Ukrainian-centred.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** Kolessa's stance on "migratory"
  folklore plots — he criticised Drahomanov, Sumtsov, and Franko for
  overstating foreign influence and aligned with Hrushevsky's emphasis on the
  degree of nationalisation of wandering motifs — remains a live methodological
  debate; scholars also weigh how to read his Soviet-era institutional roles.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** popular memory sometimes attaches
  the contested claim of a mass execution of kobzars at a 1930s congress to the
  duma tradition Kolessa documented. That claim is not securely documented and
  must NOT be asserted as fact in the module — present the kobzar–lirnyk
  tradition and the Soviet pressure on it carefully and sourced, without
  fabricated repression.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them (if applicable):** Russian
  framing minimises the distinctness of the Ukrainian duma tradition and of
  Ukrainian folk music; Kolessa's transcriptions and his "uniqueness of Ukrainian
  oral literature" argument are a primary counter, which the module should
  foreground.
- **Polish / Jewish / other-perspective considerations (if applicable):** his
  comparative Slavic studies (e.g. the bird-daughter ballad across East and West
  Slavic traditions) should be framed as establishing Ukrainian specificity, not
  as subsuming Ukrainian folklore into a pan-Slavic whole.
