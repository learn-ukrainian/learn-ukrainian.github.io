## 1. Identity / chronology

- **Canonical UA:** `Христина Данилівна Алчевська`; birth name `Христина Данилівна Журавльова`.
- **Canonical EN:** `Khrystyna Alchevska`; use `Khrystyna Danilivna Alchevska` when distinguishing her from her daughter.
- **Core identity:** Ukrainian pedagogue, adult-education organizer, public education figure, bibliographer, publicist, memoirist, philanthropist, Kharkiv Hromada-linked civic actor, and founder / long-time leader of the Kharkiv women's Sunday school.
- **Pseudonym:** EIU, UBE, NIBU, and IEU connect her 1860s correspondence with Alexander Herzen's `Kolokol` to the pseudonym `Українка` / `Ukrainka`.
- **Birth:** ESU, IEU, UBE, NMIU, and NIBU support 16 April 1841, Borzna, Chernihiv gubernia, now Chernihiv oblast. UBE gives old/new style `04(16).04.1841`; use 16 April 1841 in low-context copy unless calendar style is being taught.
- **Death:** IEU gives 15 August 1920, Kharkiv; UBE gives old/new style `02(15).08.1920`; use 15 August 1920 in low-context copy.
- **Family background:** EIU / UBE / NIBU identify her father Danylo Zhuravliov / Zhuravlov as a county-school teacher in Borzna. NMIU and the source pack note the recurring narrative that her father opposed girls' formal education, so she learned largely by self-education.
- **Marriage:** In 1862 she married Oleksii Alchevskyi, a banker, industrialist, philanthropist, and civic patron. Teach this as a partnership and resource network, not as a reason to make her biography derivative of his.
- **Children and identity risk:** The Alchevsky family included composer Hryhorii, singer Ivan, pedagogue / critic Mykola, teacher Hanna, biologist Dmytro, and poet Khrystia / Khrystyna Oleksiivna Alchevska. The daughter is a separate BIO candidate risk: do not attribute the daughter's RUP activism, modernist poetry, translations, or 1931 death to the mother.
- **Kharkiv women's Sunday school:** ESU / IEU / UBE / NIBU agree that Alchevska founded a women's Sunday school in 1862; IEU and EIU say it was officially recognized / opened in 1870 and operated for about fifty years, until 1919.
- **Scale and method:** EIU says the school involved more than 100 volunteer teachers and, in 1896, received a purpose-built building funded by Alchevska at 50,000 rubles. UBE says the school educated 17,000 illiterate people across its activity.
- **Major works:** `Что читать народу?` (three volumes, 1884-1906), `Книга взрослых` (three volumes, 1899-1917), and `Передуманное и пережитое` (1912) are the key title anchors. EIU says Alchevska wrote 1,150 annotations for `Что читать народу?`; UBE foregrounds the larger bibliographic and reader-interest methodology.
- **Recognition:** ESU / EIU / IEU / UBE note international exhibition awards and her vice-presidential role in the International League of Education. Use this as evidence of pedagogical reach, not as the center of the learner narrative.

## 2. Political pressure mechanism

- **Empire as school-language regime:** Alchevska worked under a state that treated Ukrainian-language schooling, Sunday schools, and civic education as politically suspect. The pressure mechanism is not only police surveillance; it is the imperial restriction of what language and texts could count as legal education.
- **Sunday-school suspicion:** The early 1860s crackdown on Sunday schools pushed Ukrainian and democratic educational work into private homes, tolerated gray zones, and official licensing struggles. Alchevska's 1862 school and 1870 recognition should be taught as institutional survival under surveillance.
- **Valuev / Ems context:** NIBU explicitly connects early use of Kulish's primer and Ukrainian topics with later restrictions. The Valuev Circular and Ems Ukaz are the wider language-policy background for why official materials and print titles often had to appear in Russian.
- **Kharkiv Hromada network:** NMIU, NIBU, textbooks, and the path helper connect Alchevska to Kharkiv Hromada and civic-education circles. This lets modules show that adult literacy was part of Ukrainian self-organization, not neutral philanthropy alone.
- **Russian-language compromise:** `Что читать народу?`, `Книга взрослых`, and the official school curriculum are Russian-language or imperial-print-context titles. Treat the language as a constraint and strategic compromise; do not infer anti-Ukrainian identity from publication language.
- **Hrinchenko rift:** The Oleksiivka / Hrinchenko conflict is a useful advanced anchor. Borys Hrinchenko pressed for Ukrainian-language teaching; Alchevska prioritized keeping schools open under inspectors. Present this as a real strategic dispute inside Ukrainian education, not as a morality play.
- **Shevchenko memory under restriction:** EIU and the source pack record that the Alchevskys installed an early private Shevchenko bust / monument in their Kharkiv garden in 1899 / 1900. This is a strong decolonial anchor because private space became a way to bypass public monument restrictions.
- **Class and patronage pressure:** The school relied on private wealth, philanthropy, volunteer women teachers, elite scholarly allies, and Alchevsky family resources. That made it possible, but also made it a paternalistic popular-education project rather than purely grassroots peasant self-organization.
- **Soviet simplification risk:** Soviet-friendly summaries could celebrate her as a democratic educator while muting Ukrainian-language and Hromada contexts; anti-Soviet retellings can flatten her Russian-language compromises. Keep both the achievement and the constraints.

## 3. Major events / historical footprint

- **1841 Borzna birth and self-education:** A girl excluded from formal education becoming an adult-education organizer is the cleanest learner hook, but it should be kept source-sensitive because later memoir narratives can sharpen family conflict.
- **1860s Herzen correspondence:** The pseudonym `Українка` and a letter in `Kolokol` connect her early formation to democratic publicism and women entering public print, while not making her a party politician.
- **1862 Kharkiv founding moment:** The women's Sunday school began in the same year as her marriage and Kharkiv relocation. It links domestic space, civic networks, and women's literacy.
- **1870 official recognition:** Passing from informal school to officially permitted school lets modules teach licenses, inspectors, curriculum, and the legal language of empire.
- **1879 Oleksiivka school:** The rural school on the Alchevsky estate, where Borys Hrinchenko later taught, connects her Kharkiv work with village schooling, Ukrainian-language disputes, and the future author of the Ukrainian dictionary.
- **1884-1906 `Что читать народу?`:** The three-volume guide turned reader choice, annotations, and suitability of texts for new readers into a method. It is a better pedagogical anchor than a generic claim that she "loved education."
- **1896 school building:** EIU's 50,000-ruble purpose-built building detail gives a concrete infrastructure scene: adult education needed rooms, schedules, teachers, heating, libraries, and money.
- **1899 / 1900 Shevchenko bust:** The private Kharkiv Shevchenko memorial links biography to memory politics, censorship, and the Shevchenko cult outside official monuments.
- **1899-1917 `Книга взрослых`:** The adult manual's 40 editions make it a measurable impact anchor for adult literacy and reading pedagogy.
- **1912 memoir and jubilee publications:** `Передуманное и пережитое` and anniversary materials help production teams use her as both a subject and a source witness.
- **1919 school closure / end of long project:** The school operated through late imperial collapse and revolutionary disruption, ending around 1919. Do not imply uninterrupted normality through war and revolution.
- **1920 death in Kharkiv:** The death date closes the mother's biography. It must not be confused with the daughter's 1931 death or with family tragedies later in 1920.

## 4. Pedagogical anchors

- **A classroom under empire:** Put adult women learners, volunteer teachers, official inspectors, language restrictions, and reading materials in one scene.
- **A book list as politics:** `Що читати народові?` / `Что читать народу?` can teach that choosing books for new readers is a political and pedagogical act.
- **Adult education vocabulary:** `недільна школа`, `дорослі учні`, `грамотність`, `самоосвіта`, `читанка`, `бібліографічний покажчик`, `анотація`, `народна освіта`, `вчителька-доброволиця`.
- **Women's education anchor:** Her biography belongs beside Sofiia Rusova, Nataliya Kobrynska, Milena Rudnytska, Olena Pchilka, and Mariia Voiakovska as a way to show women building institutions before, during, and after 1917.
- **Kharkiv map anchor:** Borzna, Kharkiv, Oleksiivka, and the Alchevsky estate create a compact map from Chernihiv region to Sloboda Ukraine and the Donbas borderland.
- **Hromada / language-policy bridge:** Pair her Sunday school with Hromady, Valuev / Ems, and later language-policy surfaces so learners see how school and language politics fit together.
- **Hrinchenko comparison:** Use Borys Hrinchenko as a debate partner: one Ukrainian education project can contain strategic disagreements over whether compromise preserves infrastructure or reproduces imperial language power.
- **Shevchenko visual anchor:** The private Shevchenko bust lets teachers connect image, memory, censorship, and domestic / public space.
- **Anti-hagiography anchor:** Ask what private philanthropy can and cannot solve when the state controls language, curriculum, and inspection.

## 6. Risks / open questions

- **Mother/daughter conflation:** This is the highest production risk. The daughter Khrystia / Khrystyna Oleksiivna Alchevska (1882-1931) was a poet and translator; the mother Khrystyna Danilivna Alchevska (1841-1920) was the educator and school organizer.
- **UINP tag mismatch:** The source-pack helper found a UINP tag/index issue where Maria Hrushevska / Voiakovska material appears under a Khrystyna Alchevska tag. Do not use "First Lady of the UNR," Maupassant / Zola translation, or autonomy-statute details for this subject.
- **Birth/death date notation:** Core sources agree on 1841-1920 but vary in old/new style presentation. Use 16 April 1841 and 15 August 1920 unless teaching calendar conversion.
- **School founding chronology:** 1862 informal founding and 1870 official recognition both matter. Do not reduce the school to only one date without explaining informal versus licensed status.
- **Ukrainian-language teaching claims:** Sources support Ukrainian language, folk song, Shevchenko, Kulish primer, and Ukrainian history elements, but the official curriculum operated under imperial restrictions. Use source labels for exact language-of-instruction claims.
- **Oleksiivka / Hrinchenko conflict:** The rift is valuable but can become a one-sided indictment. Use it to show competing strategies under repression; do not make Hrinchenko the sole "true Ukrainian" and Alchevska the sole compromiser.
- **Oleksii Alchevskyi death:** Some summaries mention suicide or suspicious death after financial crisis. This is relevant family context only; avoid sensational detail unless the module verifies it from a dedicated source.
- **Scale claims:** `17,000 learners`, `100 volunteer teachers`, `600-700 students`, `50,000 rubles`, and `40 editions` are useful, but source-label them because different articles count periods and institutions differently.
- **Shevchenko monument date:** EIU and media notes support 1899 / 1900 variants for the private Kharkiv bust. Use `1899/1900` or source-label exact year.
- **Commons metadata:** The Commons category has useful files but sometimes misleading imperial or "Russian-Ukrainian" metadata. Use file pages and independent identity checks, not category labels alone.
