# Лесь Танюк — Research Dossier

Canonical slug: `les-taniuk`

## 1. Identity and research scope

Лесь (Леонід) Степанович Танюк (8 July 1938 — 18 March 2016) was a Ukrainian theatre and film director, organizer of the Kyiv Club of Creative Youth, cultural-memory activist, translator, scholar, writer, and later parliamentarian. The Encyclopedia of the History of Ukraine identifies him as a director, art historian, poet, translator, publicist, teacher, civic, cultural, and state figure [S1]. KHPG's profile, written by Танюк in June 1998 and later updated with death date, identifies him as a director, organizer of the Club of Creative Youth, art historian, translator, and political figure [S2].

There is a retrieved source conflict over birthplace: EIU says he was born in Zhukyn, now Vyshhorod district, Kyiv oblast [S1], while the KHPG profile says Kyiv [S2]. This dossier records the conflict and does not resolve it beyond source attribution.

## 2. Chronology

KHPG says his childhood was spent with his parents in a concentration camp in Germany; after return the family was not allowed to live in Kyiv and moved to Lutsk [S2]. EIU records that after school in Lutsk he worked as a foundry moulder, studied in a cultural-education technical school, and became an actor in the Volyn regional music-drama theatre [S1]. In 1963 he graduated from the directing faculty of the Kyiv Karpenko-Kary Theatre Institute, from Marian Krushelnytskyi's course [S1, S2].

From 1959-1960 Танюк was one of the founders and first president of the Kyiv Club of Creative Youth [S2]. KHPG says the club had literary, theatre, cinema, fine-art, architecture, music, jazz, and other sections, and that its participants included Ivan Svitlychnyi, Alla Horska, Ivan Drach, Viktor Zaretskyi, Viacheslav Chornovil, Liudmyla Semykina, Iryna Zhylenko, Serhii Paradzhanov, Yevhen Sverstiuk, Yurii Badzio, Vasyl Stus, Vasyl Symonenko, and many others [S2]. The KHPG glossary entry confirms that the theatre director Les Taniuk was elected president and that the club became a center of young Ukrainian intelligentsia [S3].

The club organized evenings for Shevchenko, Franko, Lesia Ukrainka, Les Kurbas, Mykola Kulish, and contemporary poetry, and regularly discussed repressed writers and artists of the Executed Renaissance [S2]. In 1962, according to KHPG, a group under Taniuk's leadership gathered material on repressed Ukrainian intellectuals in the Solovky context, and a commission with Taniuk, Alla Horska, and Vasyl Symonenko checked evidence of mass burials near Brovary/Bykivnia [S2]. EIU states that persecution by local authorities forced him to leave for Moscow, where he worked in theatres from 1965 to 1986 [S1].

## 3. Significance for the bio track

Танюк belongs in Block E as the institution-builder of шістдесятники cultural life. His significance is not an Article 62 prison sentence, but infrastructure: clubs, readings, theatre productions, memory investigations, and later parliamentary/cultural-policy work. A lesson can show that dissent often begins as organizing space, naming forbidden predecessors, and protecting memory [S1, S2, S3].

The KTYM material is unusually rich for teaching networks. KHPG names sections, participants, events, and topics: literature, theatre, cinema, visual art, architecture, music, jazz, evenings for Ukrainian classics, readings on repressed authors, and trips to document monuments and burial sites [S2, S3]. This lets students build a network diagram from source evidence rather than from a teacher-provided list. It also shows why the authorities could experience cultural programming as political threat.

## 4. Pressure mechanisms and source limits

The pressure mechanism was administrative and professional rather than a retrieved criminal sentence. EIU states that persecution from local authorities led him to Moscow [S1]. KHPG describes the club's openly anti-totalitarian/protest spirit and the growing tension around it [S2]. The KHPG glossary says the club was closed in 1964 after official ideological attacks [S3]. Arrest, court sentence, or camp term for Танюк: source not found in retrieved materials.

Primary-source status: the KHPG profile is explicitly attributed to a text written by Taniuk in June 1998 [S2]. Direct classroom quotation should be kept short; the most useful paraphrased evidence is his account of KTYM membership, cultural evenings, and Bykivnia investigation [S2].

The birthplace conflict should remain visible. EIU's Zhukyn entry and KHPG's Kyiv entry are both retrieved sources [S1, S2]. The curriculum should not silently harmonize them. A small sidebar can explain that biographical dictionaries sometimes differ, and that exact civil-registration evidence is needed before choosing one as definitive.

## 5. Works and public actions

Core actions include founding/leading KTYM, staging Brecht and Kulish, organizing evenings for Ukrainian cultural memory, documenting threatened churches and monuments, investigating Solovky and Bykivnia memory, and later leading theatre/cultural institutions [S1, S2, S3]. EIU records that from 1992 he headed the Ukrainian theatre workers' union and that he served as a people's deputy in the first through fifth convocations, including leadership roles in parliamentary culture committees [S1].

For theatre/literature integration, pair Танюк with Les Kurbas and Mykola Kulish modules. EIU says he created an author studio at the club and staged plays by Ivan Drach, Mykola Kulish, and Bertolt Brecht [S1]. KHPG says evenings for Kurbas and Kulish drew strong attention [S2]. This gives a direct bridge from biography into drama history and the recovery of suppressed modernism.

## 8. Pedagogical notes

Use a "build the network" activity: students map KTYM sections, names, and actions. Then ask which actions were artistic, which were historical-memory work, and which crossed into open political challenge.

A second activity can contrast Танюк with imprisoned figures in this batch. Students should identify that no arrest or camp sentence was retrieved for him, then explain why his dossier still belongs in a dissidents block: the sourced answer is KTYM leadership, anti-totalitarian cultural programming, Bykivnia/Solovky memory work, and administrative/professional pressure [S1, S2, S3].

That contrast keeps the batch from flattening every dissident biography into the same prison narrative.
