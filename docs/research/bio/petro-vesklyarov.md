## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Петро Юхимович Вескляров. The canonical Ukrainian spelling is the one used by the **Енциклопедія Сучасної України** entry and Ukrainian Wikipedia. The user-supplied form `Весклярів` was not found as the canonical nominative form in the checked sources and is not used as authority here. [T1: ЕСУ — Вескляров Петро Юхимович; T3: Ukrainian Wikipedia via `query_wikipedia`]
- **Public persona / alias:** Дід Панас, the on-screen and radio storyteller associated with the children's programme `На добраніч, діти` and the broader evening-tale format. [T1; T2: Суспільне; T2: Укрінформ]
- **Born:** 28 May old style / **10 June 1911** | містечко / місто **Тальне**, then Kyiv guberniya / Uman povit in imperial administrative geography; now Cherkasy oblast, Ukraine. ESU gives the old-style/new-style date and modern Talne. [T1]
- **Died:** **5 January 1994** | Kyiv. ESU gives Kyiv as place of death; Ukrainian Wikipedia and regional sources say his ashes are in the columbarium of Baikove cemetery. [T1; T3; T4: Вісті Черкащини]
- **Professional status:** Actor of theatre and film; **Заслужений артист УРСР** (1973); member of the Union of Cinematographers of Ukraine from 1962. [T1]
- **Career anchors:** Cherkasy travelling workers' and peasants' theatre, 1932-1940; Volyn Ukrainian music-drama theatre named for Taras Shevchenko in Lutsk, 1946-1959; Kyiv Dovzhenko film studio, 1959-1982; Ukrainian television, where ESU dates his `На добраніч, діти` work as 1964-1986. [T1]
- **Birth-name / family-name caution:** Journalism and local-memory sources report variants such as Пінхас / Пінхус Хаїмович Вексляр / Вескляр and describe `Вескляров` as the wartime or professional form. ESU does not carry that birth-name layer, so the dossier uses it only as a reported, cross-source tradition, not as a Tier 1 legal claim. [T2: Укрінформ; T4: Вісті Черкащини]

The most stable identity statement is therefore simple: the curriculum figure is **Петро Юхимович Вескляров**, the Ukrainian actor and television storyteller widely remembered as **Дід Панас**. The dossier should not normalize the previous misspelled slug, and it should not replace the attested surname `Вескляров` with `Весклярів`.

## 2. Oppression mechanism

This is not a clean "dissident martyr" biography. Вескляров worked inside Soviet institutions, accepted a Soviet honorific title, and became famous through state radio and television. The decolonial question is narrower and more interesting: how could a state-controlled Soviet media system both use and distrust an intensely Ukrainian public persona?

- **Nazi occupation and wartime identity danger:** Ukrainian Wikipedia and Укрінформ report that he was attached to a military theatre in 1941, was caught in the German encirclement, passed through a filtration camp, and escaped. Укрінформ adds that he changed or stabilized the surname `Вескляров` in that context and that hiding Jewish origin was a survival matter; this claim is plausible and cross-reported, but it is not in ESU, so it must be taught with source-tier caution. [T2: Укрінформ; T3]
- **Soviet suspicion after occupation:** Укрінформ reports that, after returning from the occupied zone, "competent organs" pushed him away from Kyiv, which helps explain the 1946-1959 Lutsk period. Again, ESU confirms the Lutsk employment dates but not the coercive motive, so the motive is a Tier 2 reported explanation rather than a settled archival finding. [T1; T2: Укрінформ]
- **Ukrainian language and costume as soft conflict:** Multiple post-Soviet sources say his personal embroidered shirts and Ukrainian speech irritated television authorities and led to accusations of nationalism. Суспільне writes that his shirt and Ukrainian-language tales drew attacks during anti-nationalist campaigns; Вісті Черкащини describes attempts by Moscow-oriented management to replace the national costume; Укрінформ says viewers' letters forced management to abandon replacement attempts. These reports are mutually reinforcing, but they still belong to media-memory sources rather than a recovered party file. [T2: Суспільне; T2: Укрінформ; T4: Вісті Черкащини]
- **Late removal from air:** Sources disagree over how to explain the end of the television career. ESU gives 1964-1986 for the programme; other sources place his disappearance or forced retirement in 1988. Вечірній Київ says he began forgetting text and holding long pauses, then was dismissed. Укрінформ frames the November 1988 end as a shift toward commercialized television standards. The dossier should present the end as a combination of age, changed media policy, and institutional disposability rather than as a single proven punitive act. [T1; T2: Укрінформ; T4: Вечірній Київ]

The oppression mechanism, then, is not a prison file. It is a layered system of **survival under occupation**, **Soviet suspicion of anyone touched by occupied territory**, and **cultural policing of visible Ukrainianness**. His achievement was not that he openly defeated the state; it was that, within a narrow permitted children's slot, he made spoken Ukrainian feel intimate, competent, warm, and daily.

## 3. Major works

- `1932-1940` — actor in the Cherkasy travelling workers' and peasants' theatre; later club leadership at the Hrebinka railway station is reported in journalistic biographies. [T1; T2: Укрінформ]
- `1946-1959` — actor at the Volyn Ukrainian music-drama theatre named for Taras Shevchenko in Lutsk. ESU lists this as a central employment period. Reported stage roles include Mykola Zadorozhnyi in Franko's `Украдене щастя`, the Commander in Lesia Ukrainka's `Камінний господар`, and the Governor in Gogol's `Ревізор`. [T1]
- `1959-1982` — actor at the Kyiv Dovzhenko film studio. ESU lists roles in `Іванна`, `Олекса Довбуш`, `Лісова пісня`, `Гадюка`, `Падав іній`, and `Циган`; other sources expand the count, but ESU is the safer filmography anchor. [T1]
- `1962` — according to Ukrainian Wikipedia and journalistic sources, began appearing as Дід Панас on Ukrainian radio evening tales. [T2: Укрінформ; T3]
- `1964-1986` — according to ESU, hosted `На добраніч, діти` on Ukrainian television as Дід Панас. Public-memory sources often round this to "24 years" or extend the public persona's end to 1988; the dossier should preserve the date disagreement. [T1; T2: Суспільне; T2: Урядовий кур'єр]
- `legacy` — only fragments and later transferred materials survive. Суспільне and Укрінформ emphasize that live broadcasts were often not recorded, making his most famous "texts" unusually dependent on memory, colleagues' testimony, and a small set of recovered clips. [T2: Суспільне; T2: Укрінформ]

The practical curriculum value lies less in a single authored work and more in **performance practice**: address, pacing, memory, improvisation, child-directed register, and the conversion of a domestic storytelling ritual into a mass-media ritual.

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** warm child-directed spoken Ukrainian, with ritual greeting/closing, diminutives, direct address, and a rural-domestic visual frame. The register is not "high literature"; it is a curated oral style designed to sound like a trusted elder.
- **CEFR readiness for full reading/listening:** B1-B2 for reconstructed short programme formulas; C1 for media-history discussion about censorship, memory, testimony, and contested oral evidence.
- **Lexicon notes:** казкар, телеведучий, вишиванка, малята, прямий ефір, телесуфлер, фільтраційний табір, амплуа, глядацькі листи. `verify_words` found `казкар`, `вишиванка`, `малятка`, `малята`, and `телеведучий`; it did not find proper-name forms `Вескляров` or `Весклярів`, which is expected for names and not evidence against the person.
- **Stylistic features:** use of vocative-like address to children, steady pacing, memory-based narration, formulaic openings and closings, and improvisational additions that colleagues remembered as both charm and risk.

For learners, the point is that "simple" speech can be culturally dense. Дід Панас spoke to children, but the cultural work was adult: he normalized Ukrainian as the language of comfort, bedtime, humor, and public trust in a media environment where Russian had institutional dominance.

## 6. Contested points

- **Birth name and ethnicity:** ESU identifies the public actor as Петро Юхимович Вескляров and does not discuss a birth name. Ukrainian Wikipedia, Укрінформ, and local articles give variants around Пінхас / Пінхус and Вексляр / Вескляр, linking them to Jewish origin and wartime survival. The dossier can report this as a strong secondary-source tradition but should not overstate legal-document certainty without an archival record.
- **Dates of the TV persona:** ESU says 1964-1986 for `На добраніч, діти`; public-memory accounts often say he worked into 1988 or was removed in 1988. The safest formulation is: ESU documents the core television period as 1964-1986; media accounts describe his public-era ending around 1988.
- **The obscene phrase:** Not verified. Treat as a folklore/meme case study: how a society attaches rebellious desire to a beloved, mild public persona. Do not use it to build a factual biography.
- **Hagiography risk:** He was not outside Soviet institutions. He made his career in Soviet theatre, film, radio, and television. A truthful decolonized reading does not turn him into a secret dissident; it shows how a state-approved role could still carry nontrivial Ukrainian cultural continuity.
- **Anti-hagiography in private life:** Some sources present loneliness, poverty, family distance, or institutional neglect in his last years. These points are plausible but unevenly documented; use them to resist sentimental myth, not to invent a melodrama.
- **Russian/Soviet appropriation frame:** His official title was Soviet, and his workplace was Ukrainian republican television. The decolonial issue is not a simple Russia-vs-Ukraine ownership fight; it is how a Ukrainian-language ritual survived inside a Soviet media machine that simultaneously promoted "friendship of peoples" and policed Ukrainian distinctiveness.
