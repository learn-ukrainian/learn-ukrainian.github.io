# Юрій Шухевич — Research Dossier

Canonical slug: `yurii-shukhevych`

## 1. Identity and research scope

Юрій-Богдан Романович Шухевич (28 March 1933, Ohliadiv/Ohladiv, Lviv region — 22 November 2022, Munich) was a Ukrainian dissident, political prisoner, civic and political figure, member of the Ukrainian Helsinki Group milieu, and later member of parliament. This dossier treats him as a Block E dissident and long-term political prisoner, not as an OUN/UPA military figure. KHPG frames his imprisonment as punishment for being the son of Roman Shukhevych and calls him "almost 40 years in captivity" [S1]. Rada and CHESNO provide official/public records for his 2014-2019 parliamentary role [S2, S3].

## 2. Chronology

KHPG records that in 1945 his mother and grandmother were arrested as members of Roman Shukhevych's family, while Yurii and his five-year-old sister were placed first in a Chornobyl children's home and then in Stalino/Donetsk [S1]. In 1948, after he returned to Donbas to try to take his sister, he was arrested and later sentenced to ten years for "connection with the underground" [S1]. His father died on 5 March 1950; KHPG states that Yurii was brought from prison to identify the body and saw his father for the last time [S1].

KHPG says he was released in 1956, then returned to complete the term after a protest by the USSR prosecutor; in 1958, without release to freedom, he received another ten-year sentence for anti-Soviet agitation and propaganda [S1]. He served in Mordovian camps and was released in 1968 without the right to live in Ukraine for five years, so he settled in Nalchik and worked as an electrician [S1]. There he met and associated with шістдесятники and for a time lived in Nina Strokata's apartment [S1].

He was arrested again in 1972, with KHPG giving 14 February and noting other sources give 26 March [S1]. The accusation centered on an unfinished article, `Роздуми вголос`, about prospects for the Ukrainian national liberation movement [S1]. He was sentenced as a particularly dangerous recidivist to ten years of special-regime camps and five years of exile; after a letter to the UN, he received an additional 1973 sentence of ten years in prison and five years exile [S1]. In February 1979 he was announced as a member of the Ukrainian Helsinki Group among prisoners and exiles; KHPG notes he later said this was not agreed with him beforehand [S1].

In independent Ukraine he became a public political figure. The official Rada profile records him as a people's deputy of the eighth convocation from 27 November 2014 to 29 August 2019, elected on the Radical Party list, and as deputy chair of the committee for veterans and persons with disabilities [S2]. CHESNO records the same convocation and notes his death in November 2022 [S3].

## 3. Significance for the bio track

Шухевич's dissident biography helps students distinguish inherited collective punishment from later conscious political action. His first punishment came when he was a minor and son of a wanted insurgent commander; his later texts, refusals, and rights activity placed him in the dissident field in his own right [S1]. The dossier must prevent a shortcut in which Yurii's life is absorbed into Roman Shukhevych or UPA military history. For this batch, the central theme is Soviet hostage-family logic and long-term political imprisonment.

A useful module title would be "Punished as a son, acting as a dissident." It captures the two phases without collapsing them. The KHPG source documents the family basis of the first arrest and children's-home separation, while also documenting the later `Роздуми вголос`, UN letter, and UHG context [S1]. The Rada and CHESNO sources then show that his public life continued into independent Ukraine, but those later party affiliations should be taught as afterlife/legacy rather than the core of the Block E dossier [S2, S3].

## 4. Persecution and pressure mechanisms

The mechanisms include family-member punishment, children's-home separation, repeated sentencing, ban from living in Ukraine, special-regime camp terms, prison terms, exile, pressure to collaborate or repent, and health damage in prison [S1]. KHPG records that he learned from imprisoned professors, philosophers, writers, scientists, and engineers, a detail useful for showing prisons as coerced intellectual spaces [S1].

Duration language requires care. KHPG says "майже 40 років у неволі" [S1]. Other summaries count prison/camp/exile differently; exact methodology for the "40 years" figure is source not found. Use "approximately forty years in captivity" only when citing KHPG's wording, and distinguish it from narrower camp-year counts.

The 1972 arrest date also needs caution. KHPG gives 14 February and notes that other sources give 26 March [S1]. This is a good place to model honest uncertainty: the lesson can say "retrieved sources vary" and move on, because the fact of the arrest, prosecution, and sentence is well sourced even if the exact arrest date remains unresolved in this pack.

## 5. Works and public actions

Retrieved sources name the article `Роздуми вголос`, the letter to the UN, and his later parliamentary work [S1, S2]. Text of `Роздуми вголос` was not retrieved; KHPG states that a copy reached Lviv and was lost, so direct quotation is source not found [S1]. A classroom activity can focus on why an unfinished analytical article was treated as criminal evidence.

The Rada profile is useful for a short legacy box: people's deputy, eighth convocation, 2014-2019; deputy chair of a committee related to veterans and persons with disabilities [S2]. CHESNO adds a civic-monitoring profile and death notice [S3]. These sources should not replace KHPG for Soviet-era repression; they serve different evidentiary purposes.

## 8. Pedagogical notes

Frame the first lesson question as: "What does it mean to punish a child for a parent's identity?" Then separate that from later dissident choices. Use source conflict explicitly: date of 1972 arrest varies in retrieved KHPG wording; exact resolution source not found.

A second question can ask students to classify each period: children's home and first sentence; post-1958 camps; Nalchik exile-from-Ukraine period; 1972-1980s imprisonment/exile; independent Ukraine politics. This structure prevents a one-sentence biography from hiding the repeated nature of Soviet coercion.
