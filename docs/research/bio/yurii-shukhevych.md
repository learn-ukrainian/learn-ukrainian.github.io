# Юрій Шухевич — Research Dossier

Canonical slug: `yurii-shukhevych`

## 1. Identity and research scope

Юрій-Богдан Романович Шухевич (28 March 1933, Ohliadiv/Ohladiv, Lviv region — 22 November 2022, Munich) was a Ukrainian dissident, political prisoner, civic and political figure, member of the Ukrainian Helsinki Group milieu, organizer and head of UNA-UNSO in independent Ukraine, and later member of parliament. This dossier treats his Soviet imprisonment as a Block E dissident and long-term political-prisoner history without concealing his later ultra-right political affiliation. KHPG frames his imprisonment as punishment for being the son of Roman Shukhevych and calls him "almost 40 years in captivity" [S1]. Rada provides the official record for his 2014-2019 parliamentary role [S2]; Ukrainian Wikipedia supplies a separately attributed overview of the contested post-1991 political record [S3].

## 2. Chronology

KHPG records that in 1945 his mother and grandmother were arrested as members of Roman Shukhevych's family, while Yurii and his five-year-old sister were placed first in a Chornobyl children's home and then in Stalino/Donetsk [S1]. Ukrainian Wikipedia instead dates the first arrest/exile episode to 1944, says he was exiled with his mother, and places their separation and his transfer to a Donbas children's home in 1946 [S3]. The packet does not silently choose between those incompatible sequences: a module must attribute each account and seek the underlying archival record before asserting a single day-by-day chronology. In 1948, after he returned to Donbas to try to take his sister, he was arrested and later sentenced to ten years for "connection with the underground" [S1]. His father died on 5 March 1950; KHPG states that Yurii was brought from prison to identify the body and saw his father for the last time [S1].

KHPG says he was released in 1956, then returned to complete the term after a protest by the USSR prosecutor; in 1958, without release to freedom, he received another ten-year sentence for anti-Soviet agitation and propaganda [S1]. He served in Mordovian camps and was released in 1968 without the right to live in Ukraine for five years, so he settled in Nalchik and worked as an electrician [S1]. There he met and associated with шістдесятники and for a time lived in Nina Strokata's apartment [S1].

He was arrested again in 1972, with KHPG giving 14 February and noting other sources give 26 March [S1]. The accusation centered on an unfinished article, `Роздуми вголос`, about prospects for the Ukrainian national liberation movement [S1]. KHPG describes a ten-year special-regime term plus five years of exile, whereas Ukrainian Wikipedia reports nine years plus five for the 1972 proceeding; both distinguish the additional 1973 sentence of ten years in prison and five years of exile [S1, S3]. The one-year discrepancy is unresolved in this packet and must remain attributed. In February 1979 he was announced as a member of the Ukrainian Helsinki Group among prisoners and exiles; KHPG notes he later said this was not agreed with him beforehand [S1]. Ukrainian Wikipedia reports that he completely lost his sight in Soviet prisons and, after the 1988 end of exile, entered a home for people with disabilities [S3].

In independent Ukraine he became a public political figure. Ukrainian Wikipedia describes him as an ultra-right political figure, records that he organized and headed UNA-UNSO in 1990-1994 and again headed it in 2006-2014, and notes the 2006 Hero of Ukraine award [S3]. The official Rada profile records him as a people's deputy of the eighth convocation from 27 November 2014 to 29 August 2019, elected on the Radical Party list, and as deputy chair of the committee for veterans and persons with disabilities [S2]. These later affiliations and honors require explicit critical treatment; they neither excuse Soviet persecution nor permit the catalog to sanitize his post-1991 politics.

## 3. Significance for the bio track

Шухевич's dissident biography helps students distinguish inherited collective punishment from later conscious political action. His first punishment came when he was a minor and son of a wanted insurgent commander; his later texts, refusals, and rights activity placed him in the dissident field in his own right [S1]. The dossier must prevent a shortcut in which Yurii's life is absorbed into Roman Shukhevych or UPA military history. For this batch, the central theme is Soviet hostage-family logic and long-term political imprisonment.

A useful module title would be "Punished as a son, acting as a dissident." It captures the first two phases without collapsing them. The KHPG source documents the family basis of the first arrest and children's-home separation, while also documenting the later `Роздуми вголос`, UN letter, and UHG context [S1]. Rada and Ukrainian Wikipedia show that his public life continued into independent Ukraine [S2, S3]. Those later affiliations require a dedicated, critical section: they are not the core of the Soviet-repression dossier, but they cannot be reduced to a neutral "legacy" label.

## 4. Persecution and pressure mechanisms

The mechanisms include family-member punishment, children's-home separation, repeated sentencing, ban from living in Ukraine, special-regime camp terms, prison terms, exile, pressure to collaborate or repent, and the complete loss of sight that Ukrainian Wikipedia attributes to Soviet imprisonment [S1, S3]. KHPG records that he learned from imprisoned professors, philosophers, writers, scientists, and engineers, a detail useful for showing prisons as coerced intellectual spaces [S1].

Duration language requires care. KHPG says "майже 40 років у неволі" [S1]. Other summaries count prison/camp/exile differently; exact methodology for the "40 years" figure is source not found. Use "approximately forty years in captivity" only when citing KHPG's wording, and distinguish it from narrower camp-year counts.

The 1972 arrest date also needs caution. KHPG gives 14 February and notes that other sources give 26 March [S1]. This is a good place to model honest uncertainty: the lesson can say "retrieved sources vary" and move on, because the fact of the arrest, prosecution, and sentence is well sourced even if the exact arrest date remains unresolved in this pack.

## 5. Works and public actions

Retrieved sources name the article `Роздуми вголос`, the letter to the UN, and his later parliamentary work [S1, S2]. Text of `Роздуми вголос` was not retrieved; KHPG states that a copy reached Lviv and was lost, so direct quotation is source not found [S1]. A classroom activity can focus on why an unfinished analytical article was treated as criminal evidence.

The Rada profile is useful for a short legacy box: people's deputy, eighth convocation, 2014-2019; deputy chair of a committee related to veterans and persons with disabilities [S2]. Ukrainian Wikipedia adds the UNA-UNSO leadership, ultra-right descriptor, Hero of Ukraine award, blindness, and death record [S3]. It should be used as a signposted encyclopedic source whose own citations require checking, not as a replacement for KHPG on Soviet-era repression or for Rada on the parliamentary mandate.

## 8. Pedagogical notes

Frame the first lesson question as: "What does it mean to punish a child for a parent's identity?" Then separate that from later dissident choices. Use source conflict explicitly: date of 1972 arrest varies in retrieved KHPG wording; exact resolution source not found.

A second question can ask students to classify each period: children's home and first sentence; post-1958 camps; Nalchik exile-from-Ukraine period; 1972-1980s imprisonment/exile; and independent-Ukraine leadership in UNA-UNSO, the Hero of Ukraine award, and the Radical Party parliamentary mandate. This structure prevents a one-sentence biography from hiding either the repeated nature of Soviet coercion or the politically charged later record [S2, S3].
