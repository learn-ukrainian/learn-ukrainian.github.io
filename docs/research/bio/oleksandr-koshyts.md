## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Кошиць Олександр Антонович.
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Oleksandr Koshyts; Alexander Koshetz in diaspora/English contexts.
- **Born:** 1875-09-12 | Romashky, Kaniv county, Kyiv gubernia | Russian Empire. ESU gives 12.09.1875, Romashky.
- **Died:** 1944-09-21 | Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada. ESU gives 21.09.1944.
- **Family / education key facts:** From a priestly family; educated at Bohuslav theological school, Kyiv theological seminary, and Kyiv theological academy; studied at Mykola Lysenko's music school. ESU identifies him as choral conductor, composer, ethnographer, teacher, and civic music figure.

## 2. Oppression mechanism

- **What happened:** Forced political emigration after the defeat of the Ukrainian Revolution; Soviet erasure/containment of a UPR cultural diplomat; inability to return safely to Soviet Ukraine.
- **When:** The Ukrainian Republican Capella was created by UPR law on 1919-01-24 and toured abroad beginning in 1919. After Bolshevik consolidation, Koshyts remained abroad and died in Canada in 1944.
- **By whom:** Bolshevik/Soviet regime and the broader Russian imperial-Soviet denial of Ukrainian statehood. A personal Soviet criminal case against Koshyts was not found in retrieved sources.
- **Document references:** ESU documents the 1919-1920 European tour and later North American work; documentary sources on the Capella law are cited in Ukrainian cultural-diplomacy accounts. Soviet case number: source not found.
- **Mechanism specifics:** Koshyts's repression was exile through political defeat. His art was enlisted by the UPR as cultural diplomacy: the Capella presented Ukrainian song as evidence that Ukraine was not a Russian regional variant. Soviet rule made this biography difficult to teach openly at home, because the UPR mission and diaspora church/choral work contradicted the Soviet narrative of Ukrainian dependence.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** The tour archive, memoir *З піснею через світ*, church music, and diaspora choral institutions survived. What was severed was direct institutional continuity between Koshyts's Kyiv work and Soviet Ukrainian musical education.

This dossier should avoid claiming a personal arrest or execution. The sourced oppression mechanism is political exile and erasure: the state that sent the Capella abroad disappeared, while the Soviet state that occupied Ukraine treated UPR cultural diplomacy as hostile history. The Diasporiana memoir is therefore not just a colorful source; it is a preservation vessel for the very narrative that Soviet institutions could not safely publish.

## 3. Major works

- `1903-1905` — Kuban and choral folklore collecting, later used in arrangements.
- `1912-1916` — choral leadership at Mykola Sadovskyi's theater, Kyiv.
- `1919` — Artistic direction of the Ukrainian Republican Capella's European tour.
- `1922` — First liturgy cycle abroad, part of his church-music corpus.
- `1930` — Liturgy and church works, diaspora composition.
- `1935-1938` — later liturgies and sacred cycles; ESU lists five liturgies and about 100 sacred works.
- `1952` — *З піснею через світ*, memoir/diary publication in Canada, posthumous in relation to earlier events.

## 4. Primary-source quotes

- "показувати світові надзвичайну красу нашої пісні" — *З піснею через світ*, Diasporiana PDF, p. 109 in the scanned text. This is the clearest pedagogical statement of the Capella's cultural-diplomatic meaning.
- "Я був наче у сні" — quotation from Koshyts's memoir circulated in the Ukrainian press memory article on the Winnipeg centenary of the Ukrainian National Choir. It conveys the emotional register of representing Ukrainian song abroad.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** How to evaluate the Capella as art versus diplomacy; how much of the global success of "Shchedryk" belongs to Leontovych's composition, Koshyts's conducting, and the UPR cultural mission; how to teach UPR cultural diplomacy without hagiography.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** He is often remembered only through "Shchedryk" and the Capella. His sacred music, ethnography, theater work, and memoirs deserve separate treatment.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them:** Russocentric narratives frame Ukrainian choral music as regional folklore and erase the UPR state mission. Koshyts's biography directly refutes that erasure.
- **Other-perspective considerations:** The tour moved through European and American venues with different racial and colonial listening contexts; curriculum should not flatten all foreign reception into one triumphalist story.

The Capella also raises a source-method point. Reviews, memoir, and state documents answer different questions: reviews show reception; the UPR law shows state intent; the memoir shows internal strain and self-understanding. A good module should keep those source types separate so learners do not mistake later national memory for the whole event.

One more contested issue is language of exile. Koshyts was not simply a migrant conductor; he became a diaspora institution-builder because return to Soviet Ukraine would have meant submitting the UPR story to the state's censorship. The dossier should therefore use "forced political emigration" rather than neutral "lived abroad" when explaining the post-1919 arc.
