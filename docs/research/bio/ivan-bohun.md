## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Іван Богун. ЕІУ and IEU do not require a stable patronymic in the article title. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Іван Богун; Ivan Bohun; Bohun, Ivan; Іван Федорович Богун / Іван Теодорович Богун in some modern and lower-tier contexts; `Bohun-Fedorovych` in scholarship around the name problem; `Ivan Bogun` in older or simplified transliteration. Treat patronymic and birth-name claims as source-sensitive.
- **Born:** unknown. Lower-tier and commemorative sources often give **ca. 1618**, but ЕІУ gives unknown birth year. Use "born at an unknown date, often placed around 1618 in later summaries" unless a module verifies a stronger specialist source. [T1: ЕІУ; T3/T2]
- **Birthplace / origin:** uncertain. ЕІУ says he probably came from Ukrainian petty nobility; IEU also calls him a descendant of Ukrainian petty nobility. Avoid fixed birthplace claims such as Bratslav unless the lesson verifies a specialist source. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- **Died:** **17 February 1664** by ЕІУ; IEU gives February 1664. He was executed near **Novhorod-Siverskyi** after suspicion of contacts with Left-Bank Cossack and Muscovite forces during the 1663-1664 campaign. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- **Offices / command roles:** Kalnyk / Vinnytsia colonel in **1650, 1651, 1653-1657** by ЕІУ; IEU gives colonel of Kalnyk (Vinnytsia) **1649-1658**. He also appears in later contexts around the Pavoloch regiment under Right-Bank hetman Pavlo Teteria. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- **Military reputation:** important Khmelnytsky-era commander in Bratslav/Vinnytsia-region warfare, Berestechko retreat memory, Vinnytsia 1651, Monastyryshche 1653, and later Ruin-era factional politics. [T1: ЕІУ; T1 event entries]
- **Political stance:** standard references present him as opposing both Commonwealth control and a Muscovite orientation; ЕІУ says he warned against the alliance with the tsar and refused to swear loyalty. This should be framed as a source tradition with strong reference support, not as a simplified modern-national slogan. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]

**Source disagreement / correction note (flagged, not smoothed):**

1. **Birth date/place:** do not present ca. 1618 or a precise birthplace as Tier 1 facts. The safest formulation is "birth unknown; probably from Ukrainian petty-noble background."
2. **Patronymic / birth-name:** `Федорович` and `Теодорович` appear in scholarship and newer bibliographic materials. Use `Іван Богун` as the project canonical form and track patronymic variants in aliases.
3. **Kalnyk/Vinnytsia dates:** ЕІУ and IEU differ slightly on office span. Present him as Kalnyk/Vinnytsia colonel in the early 1650s, with exact ranges attributed to source.
4. **Berestechko command:** Bohun was one of several acting commanders named in the crisis. Teach his bridge/retreat role without turning him into the sole savior of the army.
5. **Pereiaslav oath:** ЕІУ supports refusal and warnings; modules should avoid inventing speeches, exact motives, or direct quotations unless verified in source editions.
6. **Vyhovskyi relationship:** Bohun supported Vyhovskyi against some early opposition, then opposed Hadiach and helped organize resistance. Do not flatten this into steady loyalty or simple betrayal.
7. **Execution:** suspicion of contacts with Left-Bank forces and Muscovites is the standard reference frame; the exact responsibility of Teteria or Commonwealth commanders should remain source-cautious.
8. **Memory:** folk, school, military, and visual memory magnify Bohun as an unbroken warrior icon. The dossier should keep that memory visible while separating it from evidentiary biography.

## 2. Oppression / appropriation mechanism

**What happened:** Bohun's career sits inside the Khmelnytsky-era struggle to build and defend Cossack institutions while neighboring powers and internal factions tried to absorb, redirect, or break them. He became famous because his military role was concrete: Vinnytsia, Monastyryshche, Berestechko, Bratslav/Uman/Ochmatativ contexts, and later civil conflict after Bohdan Khmelnytskyy's death. He also became politically legible because he resisted multiple dependency projects rather than fitting one stable foreign-alignment label. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]

**By whom:** Commonwealth military and administrative power in the Bratslav/Vinnytsia, imprisonment, and 1664 execution layers; Muscovite/tsarist power in the Pereiaslav-oath pressure and later Left-Bank military frame; Crimean and Ottoman-frontier pressures in campaign contexts; Cossack starshyna factions in the Vyhovskyi, Yurii Khmelnytskyi, and Teteria-era layers. Bohun also had agency and responsibility: he commanded troops in violent campaigns and helped deepen factional struggle after 1657.

**Document references:** the Zboriv treaty context where he appears among Chyhyryn-regiment officers; Berestechko campaign accounts; Pereiaslav oath and article traditions; the Hadiach treaty controversy; records around the 1659 anti-Vyhovskyi uprising; Marienburg/Malbork imprisonment notices; and 1664 execution reports. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: ЕІУ Bohun uprising; T1: IEU]

**Mechanism specifics:** imperial simplification can make Bohun either a useful frontier fighter or a rebel whose anti-control stance proves disorder. Popular nationalist simplification can make him a flawless independence prophet. Both flatten him. A decolonized BIO reading keeps the harder pattern: he resisted subordination to outside centers, but his choices unfolded through early-modern war, shifting alliances, captive-taking, town destruction, and civil conflict within the Cossack polity.

**What survived / what was distorted:** what survived is a strong Ukrainian memory of military skill, refusal, and tactical ingenuity. What was distorted is the evidentiary boundary. Later art, literature, textbooks, and commemorative practice often convert uncertain birth, attributed speeches, and battlefield legend into settled biography. The curriculum should teach why Bohun matters without using him as an all-purpose heroic emblem.

## 3. Major works / legacy

Bohun left no literary corpus; "works" means offices, campaigns, decisions, and memory traditions.

- `early 1640s` — ЕІУ places him in Cossack-Don activity against Ottoman/Tatar frontier threats; newer specialist work discusses early episodes, but modules should treat the details as research leads unless directly inspected. [T1: ЕІУ; T4 lead]
- `1648` — fought in the Bratslav region and distinguished himself in the Pyliavtsi campaign according to IEU. [T1: IEU]
- `1649` — mentioned in the Zboriv treaty context among officers of a Chyhyryn-regiment company according to ЕІУ. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `1650` — appointed Kalnyk colonel by ЕІУ; this anchors his Vinnytsia/Bratslav regional command role. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `1-10 March 1651` — led the Vinnytsia defense against forces under Marcin Kalinowski's command; ЕІУ gives the defensive works, ice-trap maneuver, sortie, head wound, relief action, and strategic effect on the Bratslav campaign. [T1: ЕІУ Vinnytsia defenses]
- `28 June-10 July 1651` — Berestechko: after Islam-Girei III withdrew and Bohdan Khmelnytskyy was detained, acting commanders included Fedir Dzhalalii, Matvii Hladkyi, and Ivan Bohun. Under Bohun's direction, preparations for crossings over the Pliashivka began; the retreat became chaotic and costly, but the main forces and much artillery escaped encirclement. [T1: ЕІУ Berestechko]
- `1652-1653` — continued as a major field commander in renewed fighting; ЕІУ Bohun entry names Bratslav, Uman, Oхmatativ, Zhvanets, and Moldova-campaign contexts. Use exact tactical detail only where event entries support it. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `10-11 April 1653` — Monastyryshche defense: Bohun concentrated forces there to delay Stefan Czarniecki's advance until Khmelnytskyy's arrival; ЕІУ credits a hidden-exit cavalry strike in the rear with causing panic and retreat. [T1: ЕІУ Monastyryshche defense]
- `1654` — opposed the Pereiaslav course and refused to swear loyalty to the tsar according to ЕІУ; IEU similarly frames him as opposed to a Muscovite orientation. Treat as a strong source-supported stance, but not as permission to invent modern language for his motives. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- `1654-1657` — operated around Bratslav, Uman, Oхmatativ, and against Tatar cavalry raiding forces according to ЕІУ. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `1658` — initially supported Ivan Vyhovskyi against anti-hetman opposition, then rejected the Hadiach settlement and turned against Vyhovskyi's Commonwealth-oriented policy. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: ЕІУ Bohun uprising]
- `1659` — with Ivan Sirko and other forces, helped organize anti-Vyhovskyi resistance on both banks of the Dnipro. ЕІУ's separate entry calls this a mass armed uprising and places it inside the civil-war crisis after 1657. [T1: ЕІУ Bohun uprising]
- `1659-1660` — IEU says Bohun opposed Yurii Khmelnytskyi's Pereiaslav course; this belongs in a broader lesson on how treaty constraints, factional politics, and bank division hardened after Bohdan's death. [T1: IEU]
- `1662` — captured by Commonwealth authorities and imprisoned in Marienburg / Malbork fortress after prolonged pursuit. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- `1663` — released for the campaign into Left-Bank Ukraine and connected with the Pavoloch regiment under Right-Bank hetman Pavlo Teteria; the release was instrumental, tied to his military usefulness and popularity. [T1: IEU; T1: ЕІУ]
- `February 1664` — arrested and executed near Novhorod-Siverskyi after suspicion of contacts with Left-Bank Cossack and Muscovite forces during the campaign. Keep accusation language as accusation language. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- `later memory` — celebrated in folk tradition, especially the historical duma "Іван Богун," and in visual/literary memory. This memory is pedagogically useful only if students distinguish source evidence, folklore, and later nation-building commemoration. [T1: ЕІУ; T2 bibliography]

## 6. Contested points

- **Birth and name:** a lesson should not open with a confident birthplace, exact birthday, or fixed patronymic. Start with the verifiable office and campaign record.
- **Warrior icon vs political actor:** Bohun was not only a battlefield figure. Pereiaslav, Hadiach, Vyhovskyi, Yurii Khmelnytskyi, Teteria, imprisonment, and execution make him a political actor in a collapsing and contested Cossack state.
- **Berestechko memory:** his role was crucial, but the retreat was also costly and chaotic. Do not let the "army saved" formula erase casualties, command disorganization, or the broader consequences of the defeat.
- **Crimean alliance context:** Berestechko narratives often use betrayal language. Teach the geopolitical interests of the Crimean Khanate and the risks of alliance politics rather than ethnicizing blame.
- **Vinnytsia/Monastyryshche tactics:** ЕІУ supports vivid tactical episodes, but later retellings can sharpen them into legend. Use source-linked details and avoid embellishment.
- **Pereiaslav refusal:** treat the refusal as source-supported but do not create direct speeches or modern ideological language for Bohun.
- **Vyhovskyi and Hadiach:** Bohun's shift from supporting Vyhovskyi against opposition to fighting him after Hadiach is a core complexity. It demonstrates that "loyalty" and "independence" are not simple categories in the Ruin.
- **Execution responsibility:** Bohun was executed after suspicion of contacts with Left-Bank forces and Muscovites. Claims that one rival personally engineered the death may be plausible in scholarship, but they need direct support before classroom use.
- **Violence:** Bohun's campaigns occurred in a war of sieges, reprisals, raids, and civilian vulnerability. A decolonized dossier should not turn resistance into sanitized adventure.
- **Folk and national memory:** dumas, portraits, school prose, military naming, and local commemoration made Bohun durable. That recovery counters erasure, but it can also produce hagiography.
- **Image authenticity:** familiar portraits and battle images are later representations. Caption as later visual memory unless metadata proves life-era provenance.
