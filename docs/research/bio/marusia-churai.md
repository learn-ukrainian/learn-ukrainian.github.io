## 1. Identity and historical status

The learner-facing canonical form is **Маруся Чурай** / **Marusia Churai**. `Маруся Чураївна` is a later name form. A patronymic, exact birth/death dates, family tree, appearance, trial, sentence, pardon, pilgrimage, cause of death, and monastic life must not be presented as established facts.

No inspected source supplies a verified contemporary record of a historical Marusia Churai. The strongest safe description is **напівлегендарна постать української культурної пам'яті**. The module is therefore a biography of a legend: it follows how a name, a set of attributed songs, and a narrative accumulated over time.

## 2. What the legend says—and what it does not prove

The familiar narrative places Churai in seventeenth-century Poltava, associates her with Hordii Churai, Hryts, Ivan Iskra, a poisoning, a Poltava trial, and a pardon by Bohdan Khmelnytsky. These elements belong to retellings and literary reconstructions. They can be summarized only with repeated labels such as `за легендою`, `у пізнішому переказі`, and `у літературній версії`.

The often reproduced pardon text is not treated as a chancery document. No archive reference, shelfmark, image of an original, or reliable critical edition was verified. Its rhetorical vividness is precisely why it is dangerous in a source-critical lesson.

## 3. Song attribution

The safe rule is absolute: **tradition attributes; the dossier does not certify authorship**. `Ой не ходи, Грицю`, `Віють вітри`, `Засвіт встали козаченьки` and other titles may be named as works culturally associated with Churai, but never as her proven compositions.

This discipline protects both truth and Ukrainian folklore. Oral songs circulate in variants, move among performers, and may acquire individual authors only in later memory. Refusing a false signature does not devalue the tradition; it makes collective transmission visible.

## 4. Nineteenth-century codification

Romantic-era writing turned a flexible song-and-name complex into a detailed personal biography. Plokhy traces how an alleged Cossack surname, literary mystification, and later prose combined to produce a compelling story with dates, parents, trial, and death. The 1839 Russian-language imperial novella is relevant because it claimed historical grounding and helped stabilize that story.

Its imperial label must appear only in quotation marks and within analysis. It reduced Ukrainian culture to a provincial “Little Russian” category and compared the heroine through an imported classical analogy. It is not acceptable learner-facing nomenclature.

The evidence does not justify the opposite simplification—that Russian literature invented Ukrainian song. Ukrainian oral culture existed on its own terms. The defensible claim is narrower: imperial literature personalized, classified, and circulated one version of a Ukrainian legend while claiming more historical authority than the sources support.

## 5. Ukrainian literary reception

Ukrainian writers repeatedly transformed the song plot and Churai image. The reception includes Romantic poetry, drama, opera/operetta, prose by Olha Kobylianska, and Lina Kostenko's historical novel in verse. Each work has its own author, genre, date, and ideological moment.

Kostenko's `Маруся Чурай`, first published in 1979, is the most influential modern reinterpretation. The novel is not a primary source about a seventeenth-century person. It uses the legend to think about art, betrayal, war, community, and historical memory during a period of Soviet pressure on Ukrainian culture.

## 6. Russian-imperial and Soviet propaganda risks

Russian-imperial discourse used `малоросійський` to subordinate Ukrainian culture within an empire. The dossier never repeats that adjective as a neutral geographic synonym. When a historical title requires it, the text marks it as an imperial label and explains the power relation.

Soviet popularization could recode Churai into an official patriotic folk heroine and turn the intimate tragedy into a simplified political parable. Such recoding may reveal Soviet memory policy, but it cannot establish seventeenth-century facts.

There is also a post-imperial risk: answering propaganda with an equally unsupported heroic biography. Decolonization does not require a fabricated archive. It requires Ukrainian agency, accurate naming, source hierarchy, and the freedom to say `ми не знаємо`.

## 7. Poltava and public memory

Poltava is central to the legend's geography and contemporary commemoration. Streets, cultural events, the singing field, the 2000 Ukrainian postage stamp, and the 2006 monument demonstrate the image's civic life. They prove remembrance, not historicity.

The preferred visual is `File:The Monument to Marusia Churai.jpg`, photographed by Vladyslav Lesyuk in 2019 and licensed CC BY-SA 4.0. Its caption must say `сучасний пам'ятник` and `не історичний портрет`. A second candidate is the 2000 postage stamp on Commons under `PD-UA-exempt`; it likewise represents cultural memory rather than appearance.

## 8. Pedagogical anchors

1. **Evidence labels:** learners tag claims as document, legend, traditional attribution, literary reception, propaganda, or commemoration.
2. **Rhetorical ordering:** learners inspect a popular article that tells the complete story before admitting documents are absent.
3. **Attribution lab:** learners rewrite `Чурай написала` as `традиція приписує Чурай` and explain the epistemic change.
4. **Imperial vocabulary:** learners contextualize the historical `малоросійський` label without normalizing it.
5. **Cross-track bridge:** learners distinguish this BIO legend study from the two LIT modules about Kostenko's authored novel.

## 9. Open questions and blockers

- No documentary biography was established.
- No historical likeness exists; only later representations may be used.
- Individual song authorship remains unproved.
- The precise route from oral tradition to each nineteenth-century text needs edition-level work before quoting.
- Existing LIT materials contain inconsistent publication-date statements; the BIO plan uses the 1979 date supported by ESU/UINP and does not silently copy the inconsistency.
- Direct quotations from songs, alleged decrees, or literary works require separate text and rights verification before module build.

## 10. Build guardrails

- Never open with exact dates as if they were a verified lifespan.
- Never call the monument or stamp a portrait.
- Never quote the alleged pardon as a historical document.
- Never use Russian imperial or Soviet retellings as load-bearing biography.
- Never say an imperial writer created the Ukrainian legend or songs.
- Never say Kostenko restored a provable historical truth; call the novel an authored literary reinterpretation.
- Always keep the formula `традиція приписує` next to song titles.
- Preserve maximum Ukrainian immersion and source-critical C1 pedagogy.
