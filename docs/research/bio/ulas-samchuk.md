## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Улас Олексійович Самчук.
- **Project English canonical:** Ulas Samchuk.
- **Name variants / aliases:** ESU lists pseudonyms and cryptonyms including Данильченко, В. Данильчук, Перебендя, А. Офіренко, Іван Влодко, Ольга Волинянка, Кай, Ф. Грак, У. С., М. П., П. Б., Б. П., В. P., М. К., Ф. Н. IEU gives scholarly transliteration `Samčuk`; keep `Samchuk` in learner-facing English and quarantine `Samčuk` to catalog notes.
- **Born:** ESU gives 7 February old style / 20 February new style 1905, Derman, Dubno county, Volhynia gubernia, now Rivne region. IEU, EHIU, VUE, and UINP use 20 February 1905.
- **Died / burial:** ESU and IEU give 9 July 1987, Toronto; ESU says buried in Oakville, Ontario, Canada. EHIU says St Volodymyr cemetery in the Toronto area. Treat Oakville / Toronto-area cemetery as the learner-safe wording unless a burial-source citation is added.
- **Education:** ESU and EHIU: Kremenets Ukrainian private gymnasium, free-auditor study at Breslau / Wroclaw University in 1928-1929, Ukrainian Free University in Prague in 1929-1931 under Stepan Smal-Stotsky. ESU notes he did not complete higher education.
- **Languages:** ESU says he knew Polish, Czech, German, Russian, and French. Do not turn this into cultural assimilation; it is part of the multilingual interwar and exile life of a Ukrainian writer.
- **Political / civic path:** ESU and EHIU place him in Ukrainian civic work, UVO / OUN circles from the late 1920s, Carpathian Ukraine journalism in 1938-1939, OUN(M) expeditionary-group return in 1941, `Volyn` newspaper editorship in Rivne, DP-camp Germany in 1944-1948, MUR leadership from 1945, Toronto emigration in 1948, `Slovo` writers' association, Shevchenko monument committee, and UNR-in-exile cultural office.
- **Core identity for BIO:** writer, journalist, publicist, civic and political-cultural organizer whose life crosses Volhynia, Prague, Carpathian Ukraine, occupied Rivne, DP Germany, and Canada.

## 2. Oppression / pressure mechanism

- **Load-bearing frame:** Samchuk is not a Soviet security-service biography. Do not invent an NKVD, MGB, or KGB arrest case. The pressure story is exile, border regimes, anti-Ukrainian censorship and silencing, wartime occupation constraints, DP displacement, and diaspora cultural work after return became impossible.
- **Interwar minority and exile pressure:** ESU / EHIU show a young Volhynian Ukrainian writer moving through Polish-ruled western Ukrainian institutions, deserting Polish military service in 1927, then building literary and political-cultural life in Germany and Prague. Teach this as a borderland Ukrainian life under states that did not give Ukrainian culture sovereign security.
- **Carpathian Ukraine and Hungarian imprisonment:** ESU says he took part in Carpathian Ukraine civic / election work and was imprisoned by Hungarians in the Voryuloposh camp after its defeat. This is a real pressure node and should not be replaced by a generic "war years" summary.
- **Occupation-era press constraints:** ESU / EHIU identify Samchuk as founder / editor of `Volyn` in Rivne after arriving with an OUN(M) expeditionary group. UINP adds the crucial caution: legal Ukrainian publications under Nazi occupation were required to follow Third Reich rhetoric, including anti-Jewish rhetoric, and points readers toward scholarship on that press context. Future lessons must neither hide this nor flatten Samchuk into a single-label moral verdict without textual evidence.
- **DP and permanent exile:** IEU and EHIU: he fled / moved to Germany in 1944, lived in displaced-persons conditions, headed MUR, then emigrated to Canada in 1948. The pressure is a generation forced to make Ukrainian literature outside Soviet Ukraine.
- **Soviet silencing / post-1991 return:** Samchuk's Holodomor and diaspora works were outside the Soviet canon. His BIO lesson should make clear why a writer central to Ukrainian famine memory and diaspora prose was available to many Ukrainian readers only after independence-era recovery.

## 3. Major works / contributions

- **`Волинь` trilogy (1932-1937):** IEU calls Samchuk's broad-canvas prose rooted in realist tradition; EHIU says `Волинь` builds a collective image of a young Ukrainian person trying to locate Ukraine in the world and imagine cultural / state formation.
- **`Марія` (1934):** ESU, NBU, and UINP describe it as the first Ukrainian literary work / first fiction work about forced collectivization and the Holodomor. Teach with source precision: "institutional sources describe it as first," not "everyone agrees without qualification."
- **`Гори говорять!` (1934):** ESU and IEU identify the novel with Hutsul struggle and the Carpathian / Transcarpathian Ukrainian question.
- **`Кулак` (1937):** ESU frames it as a social-psychological novel about remaking national consciousness and a former peasant mastering the Ukrainian city. Be careful: `куркуль` is a Soviet class-smear term elsewhere; Samchuk's title is part of his own interwar argument and needs historical explanation.
- **`Юність Василя Шеремети` (1946-1947):** autobiographical school-youth novel, useful for a humanizing BIO lesson about adolescence, self-education, and ambition.
- **`Ост` trilogy (1948, 1957, 1982):** IEU / EHIU: a long chronicle of Ukrainian people through revolution, Soviet rule, forced labor / wartime displacement, and postwar emigration.
- **UPA / war novels and public memory:** `Чого не гоїть огонь` (1959) and wartime memoirs require explicit contextual framing; use them for advanced learners only.
- **Memoir cycle:** ESU / EHIU list `П'ять по дванадцятій`, `На білому коні`, `На коні вороному`, `Планета Ді-Пі`. EHIU treats these memoirs as a valuable source complex for modern Ukrainian history, especially UVO / OUN, Carpathian Ukraine, World War II, and western Ukrainian diaspora.
- **MUR leadership:** IEU's MUR entry says Samchuk headed MUR through its existence; it gathered Ukrainian writers scattered by war and tried to create a forum for literary debate in DP Europe.
- **Diaspora institution-building:** `Slovo`, the Shevchenko monument committee in Washington, Ukrainian diaspora / UNR-in-exile cultural activity, and Canadian Ukrainian civic life make him a BIO bridge from literature to institution-building.

- **`Марія` dedication:** "Матерям, що загинули..." Source: NBU record of the 1934 Lviv edition and school-text witnesses. Pedagogical use: the novel names the dead before it builds argument; students should hear testimony before evaluating literary status.

## 6. Contested points

- **Birth date:** Use 20 February 1905 in learner title / timeline. If discussing archival dates, note ESU's 7 February old style / 20 February new style.
- **"Ukrainian Homer" epithet:** UINP reports foreign scholars used this reception label, and it is common in popular memory. Treat it as reception rhetoric, not a neutral scholarly conclusion.
- **Nobel wording:** ESU says his candidacy was advanced in 1980 but he did not receive the prize. Do not claim "Nobel nominee" as a verified official short-list fact unless a Nobel archive / nomination source is checked.
- **`Volyn` editorship dates:** IEU says he edited `Volyn` in 1941-1943; EHIU narrows his role to 1941-1942 in the statement about wartime national consciousness. Keep date range source-specific if building a timeline.
- **Occupation-era journalism:** This is the main quality risk. UINP's own calendar article flags that legal Ukrainian publications under Nazi occupation were bound by Third Reich rhetoric, including toward Jews. Future content must surface this as a hard historical problem and avoid both apology and anachronistic simplification.
- **OUN affiliation:** ESU / EHIU identify Samchuk's UVO / OUN / OUN(M) cultural-political work. State it directly. Do not use vague "patriotic circles" language that hides organization names, and do not let organization labels replace close reading of texts.
- **`Марія` as first Holodomor fiction:** ESU, NBU, and UINP support the "first" framing. Use it with attribution because "first" can depend on genre boundaries, publication history, and later canon recovery.
