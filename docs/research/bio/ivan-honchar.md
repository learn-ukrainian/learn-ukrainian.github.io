## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Гончар Іван Макарович.
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Ivan Honchar; Ivan Makarovych Honchar.
- **Born:** 1911-01-14 Old Style / 1911-01-27 New Style | Lypianka, Chyhyryn county, Kyiv gubernia | Russian Empire. ESU gives 14(27).01.1911.
- **Died:** 1993-06-18 | Kyiv | Ukraine. ESU gives 18.06.1993, Kyiv.
- **Family / education key facts:** Sculptor, painter, graphic artist, ethnographer, collector; father of Petro Honchar. ESU records Shevchenko State Prize in 1989 and People's Artist of Ukraine in 1991. KPI and the Ivan Honchar Museum state that his collected materials became the basis for the state museum founded in 1993.

## 2. Oppression mechanism

- **What happened:** KGB surveillance, pressure on visitors, unofficial suppression of a private Ukrainian cultural museum, and long refusal to recognize the collection publicly.
- **When:** The private "home museum" began in 1959, according to the official museum history. Surveillance and visitor risk are described for the Soviet decades, especially the post-thaw environment. Exact KGB file dates: source not found.
- **By whom:** Soviet KGB and cultural-ideological authorities; official museum history names the KGB's watch over the Novonavodnytska home.
- **Document references:** The official museum history says the house was watched by the "невсипне око" of the KGB and that visitors risked persecution/repression. A declassified file citation was not retrieved.
- **Mechanism specifics:** Honchar's cultural act was to collect and display Ukrainian folk art as a full national world, not as Soviet decorative ethnography. The official museum page says that from the mid-1950s he worked against the totalitarian regime and that his house-workshop became a "Ноїв ковчег" of Ukrainian culture. The home museum attracted artists, writers, and students; the risk fell not only on Honchar but on visitors, because entering that house meant encountering a non-Soviet picture of Ukrainian continuity.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** The collection survived and became the National Center of Folk Culture "Ivan Honchar Museum". What was destroyed or delayed was decades of public institutional support and safe access; the collection had to live as a watched private space.

This is a useful case for teaching "soft" repression precisely. The official museum history says visitors could risk persecution, and KPI describes the 18-volume *Україна та українці* album work. The mechanism is therefore not a single court date but a sustained climate: surveillance, visitor intimidation, and denial of public legitimacy to a collection that later became a national institution.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** How to separate ethnographic preservation from later nationalist romanticization; how to catalog the albums and visitor books as sources; how to document KGB pressure from declassified files.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** He can be flattened into "collector of folk things". The stronger point is that he created an alternative Ukrainian public sphere inside a watched apartment.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them:** Soviet/Russian narratives often treat folk culture as harmless ornament while suppressing its national-political force. Honchar's home museum shows why the regime feared "ornament" when it carried memory.
- **Other-perspective considerations:** The collection should be taught with regional diversity and without making "folk" equal to a single central-Ukrainian image.

There is also a museum-ethics question: Phase 2 should avoid presenting collected objects as anonymous decor. Where possible, lessons should name region, maker, function, and collection context. That keeps Honchar's preservation work connected to communities rather than turning it into a heroic collector-only story.
