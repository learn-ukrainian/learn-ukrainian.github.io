# Опанас Заливаха — Research Dossier

Canonical slug: `opanas-zalyvakha`

## 1. Identity and research scope

Опанас Іванович Заливаха (26 November 1925, Husynka, Kharkiv oblast — 24 April 2007, Ivano-Frankivsk) was a Ukrainian painter, graphic artist, sculptor, ceramicist, шістдесятник, and former political prisoner. ESU records him as a painter and lists the Shevchenko Prize, Vasyl Stus Prize, Honored Artist of Ukraine title, and membership in the National Union of Artists of Ukraine [S1]. KHPG identifies him as an artist, active member of the Club of Creative Youth, and major figure of Ukrainian culture [S2].

## 2. Chronology

KHPG records that his family moved to the Far East in 1933 while escaping famine, and that he later studied art in Irkutsk, Samarkand, and Leningrad [S2]. ESU states that he graduated from the Repin Institute of Painting, Sculpture and Architecture in Leningrad in 1960 [S1]. KHPG adds that he was expelled earlier for political nonconformity, then returned after Stalin's death [S2].

In 1957, practice in Kosiv gave him close contact with Ukrainian language, ethnography, and spiritual life; KHPG quotes him as saying that he was not a political figure but tried to be Ukrainian and know Ukrainian history and art [S2]. After work in Tyumen, he moved to Ukraine in December 1961 and joined the Ivano-Frankivsk artists' society [S2]. His May 1962 personal exhibition was closed after a week; ESU and KHPG both connect his early career to official ideological pressure [S1, S2].

Заливаха often visited Kyiv's Club of Creative Youth and worked in the same artistic milieu as Alla Horska, Liudmyla Semykina, Halyna Sevruk, and Halyna Zubchenko [S2, S4]. In 1964 he co-created the stained-glass work `Шевченко. Мати`; a textbook source retrieved by MCP records that the commission called it "ideologically hostile" and that the university administration destroyed it [S4]. He was arrested in 1965 and sentenced to five years of strict-regime camps for anti-Soviet agitation and propaganda [S1]. He served in a Mordovian camp and returned to Ivano-Frankivsk in 1970 [S1].

## 3. Significance for the bio track

Заливаха is a key visual-art counterpart to better-known literary шістдесятники. His biography lets learners see that cultural resistance was not limited to poems or samvydav essays: monumental art, stained glass, ex libris, portraiture, and exhibition practice could also become political evidence. He belongs in Block E because the state response escalated from exhibition closure to destruction of a public artwork and imprisonment [S1, S2, S4].

The lesson should stress that his Ukrainian turn was cultural before it was prosecutorial. KHPG links the Kosiv experience to his study of Ukrainian language, ethnography, spiritual life, books, history, and national art [S2]. That makes him useful for discussing how identity formation could occur through craft, place, and artistic networks, not only through party programs. It also links his biography naturally to Alla Horska: both used visual form to make Ukrainian memory public, and both encountered state violence against that public memory [S2, S4].

## 4. Persecution and pressure mechanisms

The first pressure mechanism was professional gatekeeping: KHPG says his 1962 Ivano-Frankivsk exhibition was closed because officials saw no "bright optimistic heroes" of the communist future [S2]. The second was public destruction: `Шевченко. Мати`, created for Kyiv University in 1964, was destroyed after being labelled ideologically hostile [S4]. The third was criminal prosecution: ESU states that he was arrested in August 1965 and sentenced to five years in strict-regime camps for anti-Soviet propaganda and agitation [S1].

KHPG and ESU both document the camp setting; ESU specifies Mordovian camp No. 11 and notes that he secretly made ex libris, greeting cards, sketches, posters with Shevchenko, and a portrait of the Latvian poet-prisoner Knuts Skujenieks [S1]. Exact archival case number: source not found.

The stained-glass episode should be taught as source-backed, not legendary. The textbook chunk names the artists, the location in Kyiv University's red building, the Shevchenko quotation in the composition, the commission's "ideologically hostile" judgment, and the destruction by the university administration [S4]. KHPG and ESU then show how the same artist entered the prison system in the next wave [S1, S2]. This sequencing lets students see the continuity between symbolic censorship and criminal prosecution.

## 5. Works and public actions

Works and media include painting, graphics, sculpture, ceramics, stained glass, mosaics, portraits, ex libris, and book design [S1]. For a lesson, the core works are `Шевченко. Мати`, the camp-era ex libris and Shevchenko posters, and later portraits of Ukrainian cultural figures [S1, S4]. KHPG's concise self-description, that he simply tried to be Ukrainian, is a useful primary-source anchor [S2].

A document set could combine three object types: a description of the destroyed stained glass, an ESU list of camp-made graphic works, and KHPG's biographical explanation of his path into Ukrainian culture. Students can then answer how an image becomes "evidence" under a censorial regime. Avoid asking them to infer the full appearance of destroyed works from memory alone; where reproductions are missing, say source not found.

## 8. Pedagogical notes

Ask students to distinguish three state actions: closing an exhibition, destroying a public artwork, and criminal sentencing. This avoids a single generic "persecuted artist" label and shows escalation. Vocabulary can include `вітраж`, `експозиція`, `табір суворого режиму`, `самвидав`, and `ідейно ворожий`.

For assessment, require one sentence that names the source for each claim. Example: "The 1962 exhibition closure comes from KHPG; the 1965 sentence comes from ESU; the destruction of `Шевченко. Мати` comes from the textbook/MCP source." This keeps learners from repeating later commemorative summaries without evidence.

For language practice, his dossier supports contrastive verbs of state action: `закрити виставку`, `знищити вітраж`, `заарештувати`, `засудити`, `конфіскувати`, `повернутися`. Students can build a sourced timeline in Ukrainian, then mark which verbs describe administrative pressure and which describe criminal punishment. The distinction is central to the dossier's factual accuracy.

Do not let the final activity skip the art itself.
