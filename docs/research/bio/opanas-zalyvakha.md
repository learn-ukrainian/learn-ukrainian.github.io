## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Опанас Іванович Заливаха. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ biography]
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Панас is used as a familiar form in the recorded interview. Canonical English metadata: Opanas Zalyvakha. Russified `Афанасий Заливаха` is forbidden in body text and appears only in §8 as an error form.
- **Born:** 26 November 1925 | Гусинка, Куп'янський район, Харківщина | Українська СРР, СРСР. ЕСУ and ХПГ agree on the date and place. In the 1999 interview, Заливаха cautions that officials assigned the exact date only after his family reached the Far East, so the day and month are standard biographical metadata but not a surviving birth-record claim. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ biography and interview]
- **Died:** 24 April 2007 | Івано-Франківськ | independent Ukraine. Buried at Дем'янів Лаз. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ biography]
- **Family / education key facts:** His family escaped the Holodomor by leaving Харківщина for the Far East in 1933. He trained in Іркутськ, Самарканд, and at the Рєпін Institute in Leningrad; after an earlier expulsion for political nonconformity, he completed the institute in 1960. A 1957 practice placement in Косів became the decisive encounter with Ukrainian language, ethnography, and visual culture. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ biography; T2: УІНП]

**Source disagreement note:** ЕСУ dates the arrest to August 1965; the ХПГ biography places it in September 1965. Both identify the same first-wave repression and five-year strict-regime sentence. The dossier preserves the month-level disagreement and uses `August–September 1965` where a single month is not necessary.

## 2. Oppression mechanism

- **What happened:** professional censorship, destruction of a public artwork, КДБ surveillance, arrest, a closed political trial, five years in strict-regime camps, confiscation of camp art, and post-release employment restrictions.
- **When:** his Івано-Франківськ exhibition was closed after one week in May 1962; the stained-glass work `Шевченко. Мати` was destroyed in 1964; he was arrested in August–September 1965; a closed court sentenced him in March 1966; he returned to Івано-Франківськ in 1970. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ biography; T2: УІНП]
- **By whom:** party cultural bodies closed the exhibition and ordered the stained glass destroyed. The ХПГ biography records active КДБ surveillance after the stained-glass episode. Soviet investigators and the closed court prosecuted him under Article 62 part 1 of the Criminal Code of the Ukrainian SSR. The exact arresting КДБ unit was not identified in the retrieved sources.
- **Document references:** Article 62 part 1, `антирадянська агітація і пропаганда`; closed hearing in March 1966; five years in strict-regime camps. No stable archival case number was located in this research pass. That absence is recorded as a gap, not filled by inference. [T2: ХПГ biography]
- **Mechanism specifics:** the May 1962 exhibition was closed because officials rejected the absence of sanctioned optimistic heroes. In 1964 Заливаха co-created `Шевченко. Мати` with Алла Горська, Людмила Семикіна, Галина Севрук, and Галина Зубченко; the university work was destroyed before public acceptance under party pressure. УІНП connects the 1965 arrest to his transport of samvydav and other prohibited Ukrainian literature. Together these records show escalation from aesthetic discipline to surveillance and criminal punishment rather than a generic story of an artist who was merely "unpopular" with officials. [T2: ХПГ; T2: УІНП]
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** in the Mordovian camps he first was forbidden to draw, then gained limited access to paper and paint after prisoner protests. ХПГ reports that searches confiscated works and that the administration said more than 200 were destroyed. ЕСУ records surviving knowledge of ex libris, cards, sketches, Shevchenko posters, and a portrait of Latvian poet-prisoner Кнут Скуєнієкс. The stained glass itself was destroyed; later descriptions cannot substitute for the lost object. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ]

## 3. Major works

- `1963` — *Борітеся — поборете*, mosaic; an early Ukrainian historical composition. [T1: ЕСУ]
- `1964` — *Шевченко. Мати*, stained glass for Kyiv University, co-created with Горська, Семикіна, Севрук, and Зубченко; destroyed by the institution under party pressure. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ; T2: УІНП]
- `1964` — *Чи буде суд? Чи буде кара?*, linocut; Shevchenko-centered graphic work. [T1: ЕСУ]
- `1965–1970` — camp ex libris, greeting cards, sketches, Shevchenko posters, and the portrait of Кнут Скуєнієкс; an incompletely preserved corpus created under prohibition. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: ХПГ]
- `1969` — *Козак Мамай*, wood, metal, and tempera; made during imprisonment according to the ЕСУ chronology. [T1: ЕСУ]
- `1970` — *Доля*, philosophical painting completed around the year of his return. [T1: ЕСУ]
- `1982` — *Привид комунізму*, a direct visual indictment of the totalitarian regime. [T1: ЕСУ]
- `1997–1998` — *33-й рік* and *Голодомор*, late works returning to the catastrophe his family survived. [T1: ЕСУ; T2: УІНП for the family history]
- `2003` — book design for Василь Стус's *Палімпсести*, linking his visual practice to dissident literary memory. [T1: ЕСУ]

## 4. Primary-source quotes (≥2 required)

**Quote 1 — the question that redirected his identity:**

> «Я задумався: хто я є?»

Source: Опанас Заливаха, interview with Василь Овсієнко, recorded 20 September 1999, ХПГ transcript, passage on his Рєпін Institute years. The sentence is pedagogically useful because the interview immediately grounds identity in an encounter with national artistic traditions, not in an inherited slogan. [T2: ХПГ interview]

**Quote 2 — entering Ukrainian cultural life:**

> «Так я ставав на українські виміри громадського й культурного життя.»

Source: the same 1999 ХПГ interview, passage on reading with Іван Світличний and working in Алла Горська's studio. The line connects deliberate language learning, reading, and artistic community to civic formation. [T2: ХПГ interview]

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** colloquial oral history joined to visual-art and political vocabulary.
- **CEFR readiness for full reading:** B2 for selected interview passages; C1 for long sections where chronology, remembered dialogue, and aesthetic argument interleave.
- **Lexicon notes:** pre-teach `вітраж`, `мозаїка`, `екслібрис`, `художня рада`, `самвидав`, `суворий режим`, and `ідейно ворожий`. The last phrase must be marked as a Soviet institutional label, not the narrator's evaluation.
- **Stylistic features:** first-person recollection, embedded dialogue, concrete visual detail, self-correction, and movement between personal memory and institutional critique. Заливаха learned and consciously practised Ukrainian as an adult; the interview itself is evidence of that language biography.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated or unresolved:** the exact arrest month differs between ЕСУ (August) and ХПГ (September). No archival case identifier or complete inventory of confiscated camp works was retrieved. The dossier therefore avoids a false day-level precision and does not convert the reported `more than 200` destroyed works into a catalogued total.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** the stained-glass episode can eclipse both the 1962 exhibition closure and the later camp graphics. Treating all three mechanisms separately shows how professional censorship, destruction, and imprisonment escalated. His biography must also retain the art after 1970 rather than end with release from camp.
- **Where Soviet/Russian framing attacks him:** Soviet institutions treated Ukrainian color, subject matter, language, and artistic networks as evidence of political disloyalty. Their labels are evidence of repression, not neutral art criticism. A Russocentric retelling also obscures the Holodomor displacement and his deliberate recovery of Ukrainian language and visual tradition.
- **Other-perspective considerations:** his training took place in Russian Soviet institutions, but institutional location does not define the cultural meaning of his mature work. The dossier should neither erase that training nor let it become a claim of Russian cultural ownership. His association with Latvian poet Кнут Скуєнієкс also offers a comparative, non-Russian account of cultural resistance in Soviet camps.
