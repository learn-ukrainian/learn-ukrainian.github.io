- **Family / education key facts:** Born into an impoverished noble family of a
  retired officer; early schooling at home, then the Second Kyiv Gymnasium and

  hromada around the journal *Основа* and met Taras Shevchenko, Mykola
  Kostomarov, and Panteleimon Kulish [T3: uk.wikipedia]. Core roles: Ukrainian
  ethnographer, folklorist, dialectologist, civic organiser, jurist, poet, and
  author of the Ukrainian anthem text [T1: ЕІУ].

## 2. Oppression mechanism

This is a repression-primary biography; the mechanism is twice-documented
imperial administrative exile and expulsion aimed at the Ukrainian movement.

- **What happened:** Administrative exile under police supervision (1862), then
  a second expulsion from Ukraine under the Ems Ukaz (1876).
- **When (specific dates):** On 17 January 1863 the chief of gendarmes, Prince
  Vasyl Dolgorukov, ordered Chubynskyi exiled to Arkhangelsk губернія; he
  remained in the north roughly seven years until a return permit in 1869. In
  1876 he was again expelled from Kyiv and barred from residence in the
  Ukrainian and capital provinces [T3: uk.wikipedia].
- **By whom (specific Russian/Soviet/RF agency):** The Imperial Russian
  gendarme corps (Третій відділ) and the Ministry of Internal Affairs; the
  1876 measure flowed directly from the Ems Ukaz signed by Alexander II.
- **Document references:** The 1863 exile order names the pretext "за вредное
  влияние на умы простолюдинов" (for harmful influence on the minds of common
  people); the Ems Ukaz of 1876 explicitly listed Drahomanov and Chubynskyi as
  dangerous agitators to be removed [T3: uk.wikipedia; T1: ЕІУ]. Quote the
  imperial citation as evidence of the mechanism, not as endorsement of its
  framing.
- **Mechanism specifics:** Chubynskyi was not imprisoned or executed; the
  imperial state used internal banishment and residence bans to break the
  Ukrainian cultural movement by removing its organisers from Ukraine. During
  the Arkhangelsk years he was forced to channel his statistical and
  ethnographic talent into surveys of the Russian north, an instructive example
  of how empire extracted colonised expertise even while suppressing its
  national use.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** The anthem text «Ще не вмерла
  Україна» and the seven-volume *Праці етнографічно-статистичної експедиції*
  survived and became foundational; the South-Western Branch of the geographic
  society he led was shut down after the Ems Ukaz.

## 3. Major works

- `1862` — «Ще не вмерла Україна», poem; later the text of the Ukrainian
  national anthem (music by Mykhailo Verbytskyi) [T3].
- `1869` — *Очерк народных юридических обычаев и понятий в Малороссии*, legal
  ethnography, Petersburg [T3].
- `1871` — *Сопілка Павлуся*, poetry collection [T3].
- `1872`–`1878` — *Праці етнографічно-статистичної експедиції в
  Південно-Західний край*, 7 volumes in 9 books — legends, riddles, proverbs,
  tales, the folk calendar, ritual and everyday songs, customary courts; the
  largest nineteenth-century corpus of Ukrainian folklore [T1: ЕІУ] [T3].
- `1875` — gold medal at the Paris international exhibition for the
  ethnographic-statistical work [T3].
- Contributed the dialect basis for the 1871 map of "південноруські наріччя"
  compiled with Kostiantyn Mykhalchuk, charting the territory of the Ukrainian
  language [T3].

Note: the seven-volume expedition corpus and the anthem are the load-bearing
pedagogical anchors; the Arkhangelsk-era Russian-north surveys should be named
as forced-labour-of-talent context, not as the interpretive centre.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** the precise textual history of
  «Ще не вмерла Україна» — first-line wording, the relationship of Chubynskyi's
  poem to earlier Slavic anthem models, and which stanzas belong to the
  standardised state anthem — is an active editorial question, not a settled
  recitation. Modern scholarship also weighs how much of the great expedition
  corpus reflects Chubynskyi's own fieldwork versus collaborative collection.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** popular memory flattens Chubynskyi
  to "the man who wrote the anthem," eclipsing the ethnographer who assembled
  the foundational corpus of Ukrainian folk culture and the civic organiser
  twice exiled for it. The module should lead with the full scholar-activist,
  with the anthem as one outcome of a life of Ukrainian cultural infrastructure.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them (if applicable):** Russian
  framing recasts the imperial-era persecution as benign administrative routine
  and treats his Arkhangelsk surveys as proof of loyal service to Russian
  science. Counter this by naming the exile and the Ems Ukaz as deliberate
  suppression of the Ukrainian movement, using the imperial documents
  themselves as evidence.
- **Polish / Jewish / other-perspective considerations (if applicable):** the
  seventh expedition volume surveyed Poles, Jews, Germans, Czechs, Hungarians,
  and Roma of the region; nineteenth-century ethnographic categories there are
  dated and must be framed historically, never reproduced as description.
