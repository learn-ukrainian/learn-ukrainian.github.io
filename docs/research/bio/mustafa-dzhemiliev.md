## 2. Oppression mechanism

- **What happened:** Childhood deportation; repeated Soviet arrests and imprisonment; hunger strikes; Russian occupation ban from Crimea after 2014.
- **When:** 1944 deportation of family; first conviction in May 1966 per Ukrainian history textbook MCP result; repeated convictions through Soviet decades; 303-day hunger strike after accusation of slandering the Soviet system, per the Mission of the President in Crimea; Russian occupation authorities banned him from entering Crimea in 2014.
- **By whom:** NKVD/state deportation apparatus in 1944; Soviet KGB/courts in the dissident period; Russian occupation authorities/FSB-linked administration after 2014.
- **Document references:** Retrieved official sources did not provide all Soviet case numbers. The official Crimean Mission page identifies the repeated fabricated cases and about fifteen years in prisons; the textbook MCP result identifies the Tashkent process with Illya Gabay. Specific Soviet case IDs: source not found.
- **Mechanism specifics:** The oppression began before conscious political activity: his family was deported with the Crimean Tatar people in 1944. As a young adult he collected testimony on deportation deaths and abuses, joined the underground Union of Crimean Tatar Youth in 1959, and was placed under KGB surveillance. The Mission page says he was expelled from an institute for an "anti-Soviet" and "nationalist" manuscript on Crimean Tatar history, then imprisoned after refusing Soviet military service. His long hunger strike made him an international human-rights case. After the 2014 Russian occupation of Crimea, the ban from the peninsula renewed the old Soviet method: the occupier again tried to sever a Crimean Tatar leader from Crimea.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** His political authority survived prisons and exile; the Mejlis and Crimean Tatar national movement remain central to Ukrainian deoccupation discourse. The Soviet and Russian systems destroyed family homes, community continuity, and decades of residence rights.

For Phase 2, keep two levels separate. The 1944 deportation was collective state violence against the Crimean Tatar people; the later arrests were targeted retaliation against a public organizer. Dzhemiliev's biography links the two, but the dossier should not make the deportation merely a childhood anecdote or make the dissident trials appear separate from the right of return.

- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them:** Russian occupation propaganda labels Mejlis leaders as extremists and treats Crimea as permanently Russian. This dossier must frame 2014 as Russian aggression and occupation, not as a neutral territorial dispute.
- **Other-perspective considerations:** Crimean Tatar, Ukrainian, Jewish, Russian dissident, and international human-rights networks all appear in the evidence. Keep Crimean Tatar agency primary.
