## 2. Oppression mechanism

This is a repression-primary biography; the mechanism is documented imperial
administrative exile, manuscript seizure, and a later Soviet publication ban.

- **What happened:** Administrative exile without trial (1863), permanent
  confiscation of manuscripts during a police search (1871), and a Soviet-era
  ban on his works (from the late 1920s).
- **When (specific dates):** In 1863, accused of spreading "малоросійська
  пропаганда," Konyskyi was exiled to Vologda without investigation or trial; he
  then lived abroad and under police supervision in Єлисаветград, Бобринець, and
  Катеринослав until the surveillance was lifted in 1872. In 1871 a police
  search seized and permanently lost the bulk of what he had written and
  collected [T3: uk.wikipedia] [T1: ЕІУ].
- **By whom (specific Russian/Soviet/RF agency):** the Imperial Russian police
  and Ministry of Internal Affairs (1863, 1871); the Soviet censorship apparatus
  (from the late 1920s).
- **Document references:** the 1863 exile order on the "малоросійська
  пропаганда" charge and the 1871 search-and-seizure are the anchors; the works
  lost in 1871 included a novel, three narrative poems («Семен Палій», «Козак
  Розум», «На засланні»), about 20,000 words gathered for a Ukrainian
  dictionary, and a large proverb collection [T3: uk.wikipedia].
- **Mechanism specifics:** the empire used exile, surveillance, and the physical
  destruction of unpublished Ukrainian-language scholarship rather than
  execution; the 1871 seizure is a precise example of how imperial policing
  erased Ukrainian lexicographic and literary work before it could circulate.
  After the late 1920s the Soviet state banned his works (except a few poems) and
  labelled him a "націоналіст"; a partial rehabilitation came only in the 1980s.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** the two-volume Shevchenko biography,
  «Молитва за Україну», the «Вік» publishing legacy, and much fiction survived;
  the 1871 dictionary material, the proverb collection, and several manuscripts
  were lost forever.

## 3. Major works

- `1862`–`1882` — Ukrainian primers and readers for Sunday schools:
  «Українські прописи» (1862), «Арифметика, або щотниця» (1863), «Граматка,
  або перша читанка» (1882) [T3].
- `1875` — повість «Семен Жук і його родичі» [T3].
- `1885` — повість «Юрій Горовенко»; the article «Український націоналізм»
  earlier (1875) [T3].
- `1885` — «Молитва за Україну» («Боже великий, єдиний»), text set to music by
  Микола Лисенко — a durable Ukrainian spiritual-civic hymn [T3].
- `1898`–`1901` — «Тарас Шевченко-Грушівський: Хроніка його життя», 2 vols
  (Львів) — the first substantial scholarly biography of Shevchenko, praised by
  Франко and Кримський [T3] [T1: ЕІУ].
- Founder of the «Вік» publishing house in Kyiv (over 100 Ukrainian-language
  books in 15 years) and editor of early «Записки НТШ» [T3].

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** mixed — clear nineteenth-century literary Ukrainian prose,
  educational primer language, and solemn civic-hymn diction.
- **CEFR readiness for full reading:** B1/B2 for «Молитва за Україну» and the
  primers; C1 for the Shevchenko biography and the exile history.
- **Lexicon notes:** "теорія малих діл" and civic-education vocabulary; some
  archaic forms in the primers need modern gloss.
- **Stylistic features:** didactic clarity (he wrote for Sunday schools),
  liberal civic moralism, and the elevated register of the hymn.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** critics give a restrained
  assessment of Konyskyi's poetry and fiction while ranking very highly his
  organisational legacy — the NTSh transformation, the «Вік» publishing house,
  and the Shevchenko biography; the balance between Konyskyi the modest writer
  and Konyskyi the institution-builder is the central interpretive question.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** popular memory remembers him
  narrowly as "author of «Молитва за Україну»," eclipsing the civic organiser
  who helped found the НТШ and secured Hrushevsky's Lviv chair. Lead with the
  infrastructure-builder, with the hymn as one outcome.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them (if applicable):** Soviet
  literary scholarship branded Konyskyi a "націоналіст" and banned his work;
  modern Russian framing inherits this, minimising the 1863 exile and 1871
  seizure as routine. Counter by naming the specific repressions and the Soviet
  ban using the documentary record.
- **Polish / Jewish / other-perspective considerations (if applicable):** his
  engagement with the Ukrainian–Polish question in Galicia is a documented theme
  in recent scholarship (Левченко) and can be surfaced as nuance rather than
  forced into a perspective category.
