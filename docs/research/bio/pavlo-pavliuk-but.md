## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Павло Михнович Бут (Павлюк). ЕІУ's biography
  title is `БУТ (ПАВЛЮК) ПАВЛО МИХНОВИЧ`; IEU gives `Pavliuk, Pavlo` and lists
  Pavlo But / Pavlo Mikhnovych as alternate forms. Use `Pavlo Pavliuk / But`
  for English discovery metadata and `Павло Михнович Бут (Павлюк)` for
  Ukrainian source notes. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T1: IEU Pavliuk]
- **Aliases / spelling variants:** Павло Бут; Павло Михнович Бут; Павлюк;
  Павло Павлюк; Pavlo Pavliuk; Pavlo But; Pavlo Mikhnovych; Pavliuk, Pavlo;
  Pavljuk in older scholarly transliteration. Do not treat `Павлюк` as a
  separate person from `Бут`; do track both because search surfaces differ.
  [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- **Born / died:** birth year unknown. ЕІУ gives death in **1638** and the
  uprising entry places execution at the beginning of 1638; IEU gives **19
  April 1638 in Warsaw**. Use the exact day only with attribution until the
  source edition behind the date is checked directly. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk;
  T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU Pavliuk]
- **Origin / early life:** the strongest inspected Tier 1 baseline does not
  securely give birthplace or family background. Later narrative layers often
  place him in the Chyhyryn Cossack world, but this should be treated as a
  source lead, not an opening hard fact. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T4:
  *Історія України в особах* lead]
- **Early career:** ЕІУ says he took part for many years at the beginning of
  the seventeenth century in Cossack campaigns against Ottoman and Tatar
  targets. IEU adds that in early 1637 he led several thousand Cossacks on the
  side of Crimean khan Inayet Girei against an Ottoman appointee. [T1: ЕІУ
  But/Pavliuk; T1: IEU Pavliuk]
- **1635 Kodak / Sulyma link:** during Ivan Sulyma's 1635 anti-Kodak action,
  Pavliuk/But was captured by royal commissioners with Sulyma and sent to
  Warsaw. ЕІУ says there are different versions of why his death sentence was
  canceled and suggests the authorities may have wanted to use an influential
  Cossack later. IEU more simply says he participated in Sulyma's rebellion.
  [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T1: IEU Pavliuk; Existing dossier:
  `docs/research/bio/ivan-sulyma.md`]
- **1637 leadership:** ЕІУ's uprising entry says that in May 1637 Pavlo But
  (Pavliuk) was elected Cossack hetman at the Bazavluk Sich and sent universals
  onto the volost calling people to join the uprising for Cossack rights and
  privileges. ЕІУ biography says simply that in 1637 he was elected at the Sich
  and led the uprising. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk]
- **Universal / mobilization language:** ЕІУ says the universals appealed to
  Cossack rights and privileges and also used anti-Catholic and anti-Union
  slogans. IEU says after taking Korsun in August Pavliuk issued a universal
  exhorting the Ukrainian people to rise. Treat exact date, place, and wording
  as source-edition questions. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU Pavliuk; T2/T4:
  Hrushevsky]
- **Registered-Cossack conflict:** the movement targeted the registered
  leadership that remained loyal to Commonwealth authority. ЕІУ describes the
  arrest of senior registered officers under Sava Kononovych in Pereiaslav and
  the spread of revolt after Cherkasy. Teach this as internal Cossack conflict
  under register pressure, not as "all Cossacks" against one outside enemy.
  [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU Registered Cossacks; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- **Geography / sequence:** Kryliv, Pereiaslav, Cherkasy, Korsun, Mosny,
  Kumeyky, Chyhyryn, and Borovytsia appear across the source layer. Use a
  cautious sequence: Sich election and universals; movement onto the volost;
  pressure on registered officers and towns; delayed consolidation; defeat at
  Kumeyky; retreat/capitulation at Borovytsia. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: ЕІУ
  Kumeyky; T1: IEU Pavliuk; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- **Kumeyky:** the decisive battle took place on **16 (6) December 1637** near
  Kumeyky, close to Cherkasy. ЕІУ gives variable estimates for both sides and
  emphasizes failed external support, incomplete volost backing, and the
  inability to concentrate forces. The Cossack wagon camp was broken, losses
  were heavy, and Pavliuk retreated toward Borovytsia. [T1: ЕІУ Kumeyky; T1:
  IEU Kumeiky]
- **Borovytsia:** after Kumeyky, the rebel remnants withdrew south of Cherkasy
  to Borovytsia. Surrounded and without external support, they capitulated.
  IEU Kumeiky says Commonwealth forces surrounded the insurgents at Borovytsia
  on 20 December; Hrushevsky gives 10 (20) December for the encirclement and
  later the Borovytsia council/declaration. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU
  Kumeiky; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- **Capture / hand-over:** ЕІУ says P. But was seized by soldiers and sent to
  Warsaw; IEU says he was handed over and executed despite Adam Kysil's promise
  to the contrary. Hrushevsky preserves the tension between Potocki's demand
  for leaders and Kysil's guarantee. Do not smooth this into either clean
  battlefield capture or clean voluntary surrender. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU
  Pavliuk; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- **Execution:** Павлюк/But was taken to Warsaw and executed. ЕІУ uprising
  specifies quartering at the beginning of 1638; IEU gives 19 April 1638. Use
  "executed in Warsaw in 1638" as the safe classroom baseline unless a lesson
  is explicitly about date-source comparison. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU
  Pavliuk]
- **Khmelnytsky adjacency:** ЕІУ says the Borovytsia oath/declaration was
  signed by military clerk Bohdan Khmelnytsky. This is a high-value connection
  for later biography, but it should not be turned into a claim that Pavliuk
  directly caused Khmelnytsky's later success. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T2/T4:
  Hrushevsky]
- **Aftermath:** the 1638 continuation under Yakiv Ostrianyn, Dmytro Hunia,
  Karpo Skydan, and others belongs to the same pressure cycle. IEU Registered
  Cossacks and the 1638 ordinance source show that after suppression the
  register was reduced, Cossack election and court rights were curtailed, and
  Cossacks outside the register were pushed toward dependency. [T1: IEU
  Registered Cossacks; T2: Ordinance 1638]

**Source disagreement / correction note (flagged, not smoothed):**

1. **Name order:** `Бут (Павлюк) Павло Михнович` is the ЕІУ title; learners
   will often search `Павло Павлюк`. Keep both and explain that `Павлюк` is
   the familiar revolt-memory name.
2. **Birthplace / family:** do not open with a precise Chyhyryn family scene
   unless the module cites a checked specialist source. The inspected Tier 1
   baseline is source-thin.
3. **Exact execution date:** IEU's 19 April 1638 and ЕІУ's early-1638 wording
   should be treated as an attributed disagreement until an original or
   specialist source edition is checked.
4. **Office/title:** `hetman` here means a volatile Sich / insurgent Cossack
   leadership role, not a stable later-state office.
5. **Korsun / Kryliv / Cherkasy sequence:** sources compress or emphasize
   different places. Use event functions rather than overconfident route maps.
6. **Registered officers:** Sava Kononovych and the registered leadership are
   not a timeless "traitor" category. Their choices belong to a coercive
   register/pay/service order.
7. **Violence:** Pavliuk's side mobilized against coercion, but also executed
   or handed over opponents in the registered-Cossack layer. A dossier must not
   erase insurgent violence.
8. **Kysil's promise:** IEU and Hrushevsky make the violated-promise issue
   important. Teach it as a source problem and coercive-politics problem, not
   merely as sentimental betrayal.
9. **Class-war shorthand:** older Soviet-style explanations can call the revolt
   anti-feudal. Use that as historiographic vocabulary, not as the full
   explanation.
10. **Modern-nationalist shorthand:** Pavliuk is not a ready-made modern
    nationalist prototype. The biography is about early-modern Cossack status,
    faith rhetoric, military service, register politics, and coercion.

## 2. Oppression / appropriation mechanism

**What happened:** Pavliuk/But's biography is useful because it shows the
post-Kodak escalation from one fortress revolt into a larger register and
status crisis. After the 1635 Kodak affair and the execution of Sulyma, the
Commonwealth still needed Cossack frontier service but sought to discipline
unregistered and semi-registered military communities. Pavliuk survived the
1635 punishment layer, returned as an influential Cossack, seized or defended
artillery as a symbol of Cossack military autonomy, and then led a revolt whose
language joined Cossack rights, status, faith rhetoric, and armed resistance.
[T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU Registered Cossacks]

**By whom:** Commonwealth military and administrative power in the register,
commissioner, and Warsaw-punishment layers; magnate and estate power in the
volost pressure layer; registered-Cossack officers in the enforcement and
compromise layer; unregistered / Zaporizhian Cossacks, townspeople, and
peasants in the mobilization layer. Pavliuk had agency: he joined violent
frontier campaigns, took part in the 1635 Sulyma revolt, accepted insurgent
leadership, issued or sponsored universals, targeted registered opponents, and
made military choices that failed at Kumeyky.

**Document references:** the 1635 Kodak/Sulyma punishment context; Kurukove /
Pereiaslav register background; Pavliuk's universals and letters as preserved
through Hrushevsky/source traditions; Potocki's and Kysil's negotiation layer
at Borovytsia; the Borovytsia declaration signed by Bohdan Khmelnytsky as
military clerk; and the 1638 ordinance governing the registered Host after the
defeats. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T1: ЕІУ Kumeyky; T1: IEU Registered Cossacks;
T2/T4: Hrushevsky; T2: Ordinance 1638]

**Mechanism specifics:** the core mechanism is controlled recognition. A narrow
register created a boundary between paid, privileged, supervised Cossacks and
those who claimed Cossack status without state recognition. Pavliuk's revolt
attacked that boundary by calling people into Cossack service and by removing
registered leaders viewed as collaborators. The state response attacked the
same boundary from the other side: battlefield suppression, leader hand-over,
execution, removal of elected insignia, appointment/control of officers, and
later restrictions on who could become or remain a Cossack. [T1: IEU
Registered Cossacks; T2: Ordinance 1638]

**What survived / what was distorted:** what survived is the memory of a
short, intense uprising, the broken promise / execution story, and the lesson
that post-1630 Cossack pressure did not disappear after each defeat. What gets
distorted is the simplicity of the figure. Commonwealth and Polish-source
language can reduce the movement to rebellion and disorder; Soviet inheritance
can flatten it into class struggle; patriotic memory can make Pavliuk a pure
folk avenger; some popular Cossack prose can make him a flawless precursor to
later victories. A decolonized reading keeps the harder structure: early-modern
status conflict, frontier service, confessional rhetoric, coercive law,
insurgent violence, failed military strategy, and later memory-making.

## 3. Major works / legacy

Pavliuk/But left no literary corpus; "works" means offices, campaigns,
universals, military actions, capitulation documents, and memory afterlife.

- `unknown-before 1630` — early life is source-thin. Use unknown birth and
  uncertain origin as the baseline. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T1: IEU Pavliuk]
- `early 17th century` — participated for many years in Cossack campaigns
  against Ottoman and Tatar forces. Exact campaign itineraries require
  specialist-source checks. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk]
- `1630 source-sensitive` — later narrative layers connect him with the Taras
  Fedorovych / Triasylo environment and with Massliv Stav-era Cossack politics.
  Treat this as a research lead unless the lesson checks the source directly.
  [T4: *Історія України в особах* lead; Existing dossier:
  `docs/research/bio/taras-fedorovych-triasylo.md`]
- `1635` — participated in Ivan Sulyma's revolt that destroyed Kodak; was
  captured with Sulyma and sent to Warsaw, but unlike Sulyma survived the
  punishment layer. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T1: IEU Pavliuk; Existing dossier:
  `docs/research/bio/ivan-sulyma.md`]
- `early 1637` — led several thousand Cossacks in a Crimean-khan campaign
  against an Ottoman-backed opponent, according to IEU. Use to complicate a
  single anti-Commonwealth biography: his frontier world included Crimean and
  Ottoman politics too. [T1: IEU Pavliuk]
- `spring-summer 1637` — elected at the Sich as Cossack hetman / leader of the
  insurgent side; issued or sponsored universals calling people to the movement
  in the name of Cossack rights, privileges, and faith. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1:
  ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- `1637` — pressure against registered leadership: Pereiaslav registered
  officers were arrested, Sava Kononovych's leadership was overturned, and the
  revolt spread into Podniprovia and Left-Bank areas. [T1: ЕІУ uprising; T2/T4:
  Hrushevsky]
- `late 1637` — attempted to draw broader forces and possible external support
  while Potocki's forces moved into the region. ЕІУ Kumeyky emphasizes that the
  insurgents failed to obtain effective international support, failed to win
  enough volost backing, and failed to concentrate forces. [T1: ЕІУ Kumeyky]
- `16 (6) December 1637` — Kumeyky: Pavliuk's force attacked with a wagon camp
  but was routed after the camp was immobilized and broken. This is a
  military-judgment lesson, not only a martyr prelude. [T1: ЕІУ Kumeyky; T2/T4:
  Hrushevsky]
- `after Kumeyky` — Pavliuk, Skydan, and other officers left the camp or
  withdrew; Dmytro Hunia led remnants out at night toward Borovytsia. Use this
  to avoid a single-commander narrative. [T1: ЕІУ Kumeyky; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- `20 December 1637 N.S., source-sensitive` — Borovytsia encirclement and
  negotiation. Adam Kysil's guarantee, Potocki's demand for leaders, and the
  hand-over of Pavliuk belong together as a lesson on coercive negotiation.
  [T1: IEU Kumeiky; T1: IEU Pavliuk; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- `24 December 1637 N.S., source-sensitive` — Borovytsia council/declaration
  and removal or surrender of insignia; Bohdan Khmelnytsky signed in the name
  of the Host as military clerk in the Hrushevsky / ЕІУ tradition. [T1: ЕІУ
  uprising; T2/T4: Hrushevsky]
- `1638` — Warsaw execution. Use "executed in Warsaw in 1638" unless teaching
  the exact-date disagreement. [T1: ЕІУ But/Pavliuk; T1: ЕІУ uprising; T1: IEU
  Pavliuk]
- `1638 afterlife` — suppression was followed by the Ostrianyn/Hunia/Skydan
  continuation and by the 1638 ordinance curtailing elected Cossack rights and
  hardening register control. [T1: IEU Kumeiky; T1: IEU Registered Cossacks;
  T2: Ordinance 1638]
- `nineteenth-twentieth-century memory` — Pavliuk appears in literary and
  historical-memory lists of Cossack revolt leaders. Use these as memory
  afterlife, not as evidence for the seventeenth-century biography. [Existing
  dossier: `docs/research/bio/taras-shevchenko.md`; T3 navigation only]

## 6. Contested points

- **Name and slug:** keep `pavlo-pavliuk-but` because learners search both
  Pavliuk and But. In prose, lead with both names at first mention.
- **Patronymic:** `Михнович` is supported by ЕІУ and IEU, but not every
  learning surface needs the full patronymic in the title.
- **Origin:** Chyhyryn-origin statements circulate in narrative scholarship and
  popular retellings. Use only with attribution unless a module checks the
  cited source.
- **1635 survival:** ЕІУ notes different versions of why his death sentence was
  canceled. Do not invent a single rescue story.
- **Crimean campaign:** IEU's early-1637 Inayet Girei note is important
  because it resists a single-axis biography. It should not be inflated into a
  foreign-policy program.
- **Election date:** May / June / summer phrasing varies across sources. Use
  "elected at the Sich in 1637" unless date precision is necessary.
- **Universal wording:** strong slogans around Cossack rights and faith are
  source-supported, but exact classroom quotation needs source-edition
  checking.
- **Registered-Cossack conflict:** registered officers could be coercive
  instruments, compromise brokers, and Cossacks under pressure at the same
  time. Avoid one-word moral labels.
- **Kumeyky numbers:** troop and casualty estimates vary widely. Teach the
  battle's outcome and tactical problem before numbers.
- **Borovytsia:** capture, surrender, hand-over, and violated guarantee are not
  identical. Explain the source layer instead of choosing a neat verb.
- **Execution:** Warsaw execution is secure; exact date and method details
  should be attributed. ЕІУ uprising says quartered; IEU gives 19 April 1638.
- **Khmelnytsky link:** the Borovytsia signature matters, but it is not proof
  of a direct Pavliuk-to-Khmelnytsky blueprint.
- **Later memory:** literary lists and school-memory summaries are useful for
  afterlife, not for reconstructing motives or route maps.
