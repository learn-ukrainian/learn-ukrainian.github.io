## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Ольга Юліанівна Кобилянська. [T1: IEU — "Kobylianska, Olha"; T3: uk.wikipedia — "О́льга Юліа́нівна Кобиля́нська"]
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** none of note. Russian/Soviet-imperial transliteration (forbidden in body text, §8 only): Ольга Юлиановна Кобылянская / "Olga Kobylyanskaya."
- **Born:** **27 November 1863** | **Гура-Гумора** (Gura Humorului), Буковина | Австро-Угорщина (now Gura Humorului, Romania). [T1: IEU — "b 27 November 1863 in Gura Humorului, Bukovyna"; corpus T3: Огієнко — "27.XI.1863"; corpus T3: Крип'якевич — "1863 р. в містечку Гурагомор на Буковині"]
- **Died:** **21 March 1942** (aged 78) | **Чернівці** | then under Romanian (Axis) occupation | the Romanian authorities forbade public funeral rites — only family attended. [T1: IEU — "d 21 March 1942 in Chernivtsi"; T3: uk.wikipedia — "21 березня 1942 року 78-річна Ольга Кобилянська померла… румунська влада заборонила прилюдні похоронні обряди"]
- **Family / education key facts:** Born into a **Germanised civil-servant family** (урядницька знімчена родина); largely **self-educated and very widely read**. Her **first literary language was German** — she wrote *novellen* in German **from ~1880** — and she **taught herself literary Ukrainian**, turning to it under the influence of the feminist **Наталія Кобринська** and her close friend **Софія Окуневська**, the **first Ukrainian woman doctor in Austria-Hungary**, who introduced her to Ukrainian letters and feminist ideas. Lifelong friend and ally of **Леся Українка**. [T1: IEU — "first novellen written in German, beginning in 1880"; T3: uk.wikipedia — Okunevska "першої української жінки-лікарки в Австро-Угорщині"; corpus T3: Крип'якевич — "По-українськи почала писати під впливом Наталі Кобринської та Софії Окуневської"]

**Source disagreement note (flagged, not smoothed):** Most sources give birth **1863** (IEU, Крип'якевич, Огієнко all agree); one **ЕУ** entry in the corpus erroneously prints "**(1865 — 1942)**" — a known slip, corrected here to **1863**. The novel **«Земля»** is dated **1901/1902** by most sources (T3 uk.wikipedia: "1901"; IEU: "1902"); the same corpus ЕУ entry prints "**(1910)**" (likely a later edition) — the established date is **1901–1902**. Work *writing vs publication* also diverges: IEU gives «Людина» **1891** and «Царівна» **1895** (composition), while the **publication** dates are commonly **1894** and **1896** respectively — do not conflate the two events (#M-4).

## 2. Oppression mechanism

This is the load-bearing section. Kobylianska's case is unusual and must be told honestly: in her final years she was **caught between two totalitarian occupiers**, and the documentary record does **not** support a simple "heroic victim" or a simple "collaborator" story.

**(a) Soviet co-optation (1940–41) — named instruments, but coercion NOT documented in the sources fetched.** After the **USSR annexed Northern Bukovyna in June 1940**, the Soviet apparatus **enlisted the prestige of the gravely ill, ~76-year-old writer**: she was **awarded an honorary certificate (почесна грамота)**, **admitted to the Soviet Writers' Union**, and a **party-rhetoric greeting welcoming the arrival of Soviet power in Bukovyna was published under her name in the almanac «Вільна Буковина»** — uk.wikipedia frames it as written by her («…**написала** „партійне" за риторикою вітання… для альманаху «Вільна Буковина»»), though whether the gravely ill ~76-year-old composed it freely, under pressure, or had it attached to her name is contested (see honesty flag). [T3: uk.wikipedia — "У 1940 році… Кобилянську було нагороджено почесною грамотою"; "…для чого написала «партійне» за риторикою вітання… для альманаху «Вільна Буковина»"]
  **Honesty flag (#M-4, brief ⚠):** the brief described a "**forced-signature episode**," but the fetched sources document the *award and the propaganda-greeting-under-her-name* **without documenting coercion**. Whether a dying, mostly German-formed elderly writer composed that text freely, under pressure, or had it attributed to her is **contested and not settled here** — the dossier states what is documented (her name was used for Soviet propaganda) and explicitly flags the "forced" element as **unverified**, rather than asserting either heroism or collaboration.

**(b) Romanian/Axis persecution (1941–42) — documented order.** After Romania (an Axis state) re-took Bukovyna in 1941, the **Romanian government ordered the writer handed over to a military court** — a step **not carried out** before her death. On her death (21 March 1942) the **Romanian authorities banned a public funeral**. [T3: uk.wikipedia — "Румунський уряд дав розпорядження передати письменницю військовому суду"; public funeral forbidden]

**(c) Posthumous Soviet-canon reduction.** The Soviet canon then claimed her selectively: it foregrounded **«Земля»** as **realist anti-bourgeois/anti-kulak peasant tragedy** and muted the **Nietzschean-feminist-modernist** core of **«Людина»**, **«Царівна»** and **«Valse mélancolique»** — the "**аристократизм духу**," the independent New Woman, the German-modernist formation that did not fit the socialist-realist template.

**What survived / what was distorted:** her texts survived and were reprinted (Soviet collected works 1927–29, 1962–63, etc.), but her **feminism and modernism were trimmed**, and the genuinely tragic complexity of her 1940–42 entrapment between Soviet and Romanian power was smoothed in both directions.

## 3. Major works

(Composition vs publication dates distinguished where they diverge, per §1.)

- `written ~1891, publ. 1894` — **«Людина»** ("A Person"), dedicated to the feminist cause; a New-Woman story. [T1: IEU — comp. 1891]
- `written ~1895, publ. 1896` — **«Царівна»** ("The Princess"), her **diary-form psychological novel** of a young woman's struggle for independence (§4). [T1: IEU — comp. 1895; T3/ЕУ — publ. 1896]
- `1898` — **«Valse mélancolique»**, a landmark novella on independent women, art and friendship — among the earliest such in Ukrainian. [corpus T3: Крип'якевич — "найкращою є «Valse melancholique»"; T3: ЕУ]
- `1901–1902` — **«Земля»** ("Land"), her most famous novel — a fratricide over land (based on a real case in the village Димка), fusing realism with mystic "сила землі." [T1: IEU — 1902; T3: uk.wikipedia — 1901]
- `1909` — **«В неділю рано зілля копала»**, an ethnographic-romantic tragedy of love after a folk song, set in the Carpathians. [T1: IEU; T3: ЕУ]
- later — **«Ніоба»** (1907), **«Через кладку»** (1912), **«За ситуаціями»** (1914), **«Апостол черні»** (1926). [T3: ЕУ work-list]

**Reduction note:** the Soviet canon privileged «Земля»'s social realism over the feminist-modernist novellas (§2c, §6).

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** literary Ukrainian **acquired as effectively a learned language** (after German and the local Bukovyna dialect) — psychological, lyrical, early-modernist prose with a Nietzschean colouring and an "аристократизм духу." Огієнко's verdict: she "**вибилась уже понад говірковий регіональний рівень**" — i.e. rose above dialect to standard literary prose comparable to Нечуй-Левицький. Some Germanisms and Bukovyna features remain as texture.
- **CEFR readiness for full reading:** her psychological novels and novellas sit at **B2–C1**; «Земля»'s peasant-realist surface is approachable at **B2**, its mystical layer at **C1**.
- **Lexicon notes (pre-teach):** письменниця-модерністка, феміністка, новела, жіноцтво, аристократизм, знімчений — all VESUM-valid (verified). The German-modernist conceptual vocabulary is the distinctive layer.
- **Stylistic features:** diary/interior monologue; the "любовний поєдинок" plot fused with a heroine's fight for independence; landscape as psychological mirror; the fusion in «Земля» of social realism and mystic fatalism.
- **Pedagogical hook:** a writer who reached literary Ukrainian as a *chosen, learned* language — an unusually direct inspiration for L2 learners.

## 6. Contested points

- **Feminist-modernist vs Soviet "realist."** Modern scholarship (Тамара Гундорова on her neo-Romantic tendencies; Соломія Павличко's modernism studies) recovers the **feminist early-modernist** Kobylianska against the Soviet "критик буржуазного села" frame (§2c). [T4: Гундорова; T4: Павличко — referenced]
- **The 1940–42 entrapment (handle honestly — the dossier's hardest point).** Between Soviet co-optation (award, Writers'-Union membership, a greeting under her name) and a Romanian order to court-martial her, the elderly, ailing writer's *own agency* in 1940–41 is **genuinely unclear in the record** (§2a). Resist both the Soviet "she welcomed liberation" gloss and any reflexive "pure victim/pure collaborator" claim.
- **The attributed private quote.** The line **«Якось проживемо, тільки б більшовики не прийшли»** is widely repeated as her private remark; it is **[UNVERIFIED — attributed]**, not sourced to a verified document in this pass. It captures a humanly plausible anti-Soviet apprehension that sits in *tension* with the pro-Soviet greeting published under her name (§2a) — which is exactly why the period must be taught as contested, not resolved. **Do not present this quote as established.**
- **Russian/Soviet disinformation.** Soviet and Russian framings alternately claim her as a Soviet-welcoming writer or minimise her Ukrainian-national feminism; her self-chosen turn *to* Ukrainian (from German) under Kobrynska/Okunevska refutes the reduction.
- **Birth-year and «Земля»-date slips** (1865 vs 1863; 1910 vs 1901–02) are encyclopedia errors, not real mysteries (§1).
