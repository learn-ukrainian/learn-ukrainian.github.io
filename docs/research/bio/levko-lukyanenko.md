## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Лук'яненко Левко Григорович. ESU, EIU, IEU, and KHPG all identify the same dissident, lawyer, writer, political prisoner, and later Ukrainian state figure. [T1: ESU; T1: EIU; T1: IEU; T2: KHPG]
- **Project display / English canonical:** Levko Lukyanenko for the existing BIO plan slug; Levko Lukianenko as the BGN/PCGN-style transliteration used by IEU and the earlier Phase 1 dossier. Keep both as search aliases. [T1: IEU; local allocation note]
- **Born:** 24 August 1928, Khrypivka, Chernihiv region, Ukrainian SSR, per IEU, KHPG, UINP, and common Ukrainian public-memory sources. [T1: IEU; T2: KHPG; T2: UINP]
- **Died:** 7 July 2018, Kyiv / Kyiv region context. ESU gives Kyiv; IEU gives Khotiv, Kyiv oblast. Treat the date as stable and the immediate place as a source variant unless a death certificate is consulted. [T1: ESU; T1: IEU]
- **Education / early path:** served in the Soviet Army from 1944 to 1953; graduated from the law faculty of Moscow University in 1958; assigned to party-propaganda work in western Ukraine, where he moved toward organized legalist resistance. [T1: EIU; T1: IEU]
- **Core public roles:** organizer of the Ukrainian Workers' and Peasants' Union (УРСС), founding member of the Ukrainian Helsinki Group, later head of the Ukrainian Helsinki Association / Ukrainian Republican Party, people's deputy, first Ukrainian ambassador to Canada, co-author of the 1990 Declaration of State Sovereignty, and author / co-drafter of the 1991 Act of Declaration of Independence. [T1: EIU; T1: IEU; T2: KHPG]

**Source disagreement note (birth year):** ESU and EIU print **1927**, while IEU, KHPG, UINP, the local project plan, and most memorial sources print **1928**. LH explains the 1927 form as a wartime document-year change connected with his 1944 mobilization while still underage. This dossier uses **1928** as the learner-facing project date and retains **1927** as a registry/source variant, not a separate identity.

## 2. Oppression mechanism

- **What happened:** Soviet prosecution for a legalist independence program, death sentence later commuted to 15 years, political-prisoner camp and prison terms, second prosecution after joining the Ukrainian Helsinki Group, special-regime imprisonment, and internal exile.
- **When:** first arrest on 21 January 1961; Lviv oblast court death sentence in May 1961; commutation after 72 days to 15 years; release in January 1976; UHG entry on 9 November 1976; second arrest on 12 December 1977; Chernihiv oblast court sentence on 17-20 June 1978; exile in Tomsk oblast from December 1987; pardon/release on 30 November 1988; return to Ukraine in early 1989. [T1: IEU; T2: KHPG]
- **By whom:** KGB / Soviet security apparatus, Lviv oblast court, Supreme Court of the USSR, Chernihiv oblast court, Mordovian and Perm camp administrations, Vladimir prison administration, and exile authorities in Tomsk oblast. [T1: EIU; T1: IEU; T2: KHPG]
- **Document references:** KHPG identifies the 1961 conviction under **Art. 56 part 1 and Art. 64 of the Criminal Code of the Ukrainian SSR**; IEU gives Articles **56-1 and 64**. KHPG identifies the 1978 conviction under **Art. 62 part 2 of the Criminal Code of the Ukrainian SSR**; IEU records Article **62-1**. Treat the hyphenated IEU forms as source notation for the same Soviet criminal-code provisions named by KHPG, but do not build fine-grained legal exercises until the Ukrainian SSR Criminal Code text or court file is checked directly. A stable archival case-file signature was not retrieved in this pass. [T1: IEU; T2: KHPG]
- **Regime terms:** Soviet accusations such as "зрада Батьківщини", "антирадянська агітація і пропаганда", "сепаратизм", and "особливо небезпечний рецидивіст" are evidence of the regime's criminalizing vocabulary. They must be quoted as Soviet charges, not adopted as neutral description.

The load-bearing point is that Lukyanenko's first case was not a vague "anti-Soviet" posture. The УРСС program argued for Ukraine's exit from the USSR by referendum and by appeal to rights formally printed in Soviet constitutional language and international law. That legal argument exposed the gap between Soviet constitutional fiction and police-state practice. ESU and EIU both emphasize the strategy of leaving Moscow's rule through legal and constitutional means; KHPG gives the narrower meeting, arrest, and court chronology. [T1: ESU; T1: EIU; T2: KHPG]

After the first fifteen-year term he did not move into private silence. He signed Ukrainian Helsinki Group documents, wrote appeals and samvydav, defended persecuted artist Petro Ruban, and addressed the Belgrade follow-up meeting on discrimination against Ukrainians. The second case punished that rights-documentation work and converted him into an "especially dangerous recidivist." This is the chronology to preserve: dissident legalism in 1959-1961, prison resistance, UHG rights work in 1976-1977, then the 1978 sentence and exile. [T1: EIU; T1: IEU; T2: KHPG]

**What survived / what was destroyed:** roughly a quarter century of life was consumed by prison, camps, special-regime confinement, and exile; sources often round the total to 26-27 years. What survived were the memoirs, letters, samvydav texts, UHG documentation, and the legal-political idea that an apparently symbolic constitutional right could become a real independence claim in 1991.

## 3. Major works / contributions

- `1959-1960` — draft program of the Ukrainian Workers' and Peasants' Union, legal-political independence program; the first prosecution's central evidence. [T1: EIU; T2: KHPG]
- `1969` — appeal with Mykhailo Horyn and Ivan Kandyba to the UN Human Rights Commission about chemical abuse of prisoners. [T1: ESU]
- `1977` — "Рік свободи," published abroad in *Suchasnist* / transmitted through Radio Liberty channels; UHG-era reflection on his year after release. [T1: ESU; T2: KHPG]
- `1977` — "Зупиніть кривосуддя!" and related UHG/samvydav texts, rights-defense publicistics. [T1: EIU; T2: KHPG]
- `1989` — *Що далі?*, written in exile / published by UGS circles, a transition-era programmatic brochure. [T2: KHPG]
- `1991` — *Сповідь у камері смертників*, memoir of death-row imprisonment and political formation. [T1: EIU; T2: KHPG]
- `1991` — *Вірую в Бога і в Україну* and *За Україну, за її волю...*, early independence-era memoir/publicistic volumes. [T1: ESU; T2: KHPG]
- `1991` — Act of Declaration of Independence of Ukraine, composed in the parliamentary crisis of 23-24 August and adopted by the Verkhovna Rada. Treat as a state document with Lukyanenko/Sanduliak drafting attribution, not as a private text. [T1: IEU; T2: Rada; T5: OPORA]
- `1994` — *Не дам загинуть Україні!*, political writings, reflections, interviews, and letters. [T2: NBUV]
- `2005-2012` — *З часів неволі*, multi-volume prison memoir cycle. [T1: ESU; T2: KHPG]
- `2010` — *До історії Української гельсинської спілки*, history/memoir of the UHG-UHA transition. [T1: ESU; T2: KHPG]
- `2014-2015` — *Шлях до відродження*, 13-volume collected works; KNPU describes the set as a biographical-documentary collection covering political writing, memoirs, UHG history, Holodomor and Soviet-terror reflections, and late publicistics. [T2: KNPU]

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** legal-political publicistics, prison memoir, human-rights documentation, independence-era parliamentary language, and later polemical nationalist essay prose.
- **CEFR readiness:** B2 for the short Act text with vocabulary support; C1 for prison memoirs and UHG documents; C1-C2 for later publicistic and ideological essays because learners must track Soviet legal vocabulary, rights discourse, nationalist terminology, and post-1991 political polemic.
- **Core vocabulary:** `самовизначення`, `суверенітет`, `державність`, `правозахисник`, `самвидав`, `політв'язень`, `заслання`, `камера смертників`, `рецидивіст`, `кривосуддя`, `референдум`, `конституційне право виходу`.
- **Stylistic features:** the legal brief and the vow are the two poles: arguments built through statutes, rights, and international law, then memoiristic statements of oath, sacrifice, and national duty.
- **Classroom caution:** his text set is not one register. A lesson that quotes the Act should sound restrained and legal; a lesson that quotes death-row memoir should prepare affective context; a lesson using late publicistics needs a controversy note and minority-perspective check.

## 6. Contested points

- **Birth-year discrepancy:** 1928 is the project/public-memory date; 1927 appears in ESU/EIU and some official records. The discrepancy should be taught, if relevant, as a wartime-document / mobilization problem rather than as uncertainty about the person.
- **Author versus institution:** ESU and KHPG call him author of the Act; IEU and OPORA note drafting with other deputies, especially Leonid Sanduliak; the legal text was adopted by the Verkhovna Rada. The strongest classroom framing is: Lukyanenko was a principal drafter and symbolic author, while the Act's legal force came from parliamentary adoption and the 1 December 1991 referendum.
- **Martyr-only flattening:** death sentence and long imprisonment are central, but he was not only a suffering symbol. His distinctive contribution was legalist independence strategy: using Soviet constitutional language, international law, and human-rights documentation against the Soviet state.
- **Party-politics-only flattening:** after 1990 he led the Ukrainian Republican Party, ran for president, served in parliament, and became ambassador to Canada. Those facts belong in chronology, but they should not retroactively make the 1959-1988 dissident story a normal party career.
- **Late legacy debates:** some late publicistic positions require explicit classroom caution, but this pass did not retrieve a source strong enough to support a precise "MAUP ties" or "xenophobic statements" claim in the dossier voice. A decolonized lesson does not need hagiography: Soviet persecution was real, his independence role was foundational, and later polemical texts should be sourced and framed separately before classroom use.
- **Russian disinformation attack surface:** Soviet/Russian framing turns the УРСС and UHG into "separatism" or "extremism" and uses late controversies to smear the whole independence movement. The answer is source discipline: quote the Soviet charges as regime terms, document the legalist program, and separate later contested rhetoric from the 1961 and 1978 repression mechanisms.
- **Minority-perspective considerations:** UHG and broader dissident networks included Jewish, Crimean Tatar, Russian dissident, and international human-rights interlocutors. If a module uses Lukyanenko's late statements about nation, race, or migration, it needs a minority-perspective discussion frame and should not present polemic as a civic norm.
