## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Яким Семенович Сомко. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Яким Сомко; Сомченко; Самко; Самкович; Іоаким Сомко in older forms; Yakym Somko; Ioakym Somko. Forbidden body-text forms: Яким Самко as canonical spelling; Яким Сомко as a "pro-Moscow loyalist" without autonomy context; "martyr of the people" or "enemy of the people" as factual labels.
- **Born:** date unknown. ЕІУ does not give a birth year; IEU gives "b ?" in Pereiaslav. Lower-tier summaries often supply `1610s`; keep this as lower-tier unless a module verifies a source edition. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU; T3]
- **Birth / family origin:** from a wealthy burgher family of Pereiaslav. ЕІУ identifies him as the brother of Bohdan Khmelnytskyy's first wife Hanna; the Black Council ЕІУ entry also calls him Yurii Khmelnytskyy's maternal uncle. IEU's "first father-in-law" wording should be treated cautiously against this Ukrainian kinship evidence. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: ЕІУ Chorna Rada; T1: IEU]
- **Died:** **18/28 September 1663** | Borzna. ЕІУ gives `28(18).09.1663`; IEU gives 28 September 1663. Some lower-tier English summaries mistakenly surface 1664; do not use 1664 as the dossier date. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU; T3]
- **Family / education key facts:** in late 1647 sheltered Bohdan Khmelnytskyy's younger children when Khmelnytskyy went to the Sich; served in the Pereiaslav regimental milieu and Khmelnytskyy's broader circle; claims that he studied at the Kyiv-Mohyla school are disputed by later scholarship. [T1: ЕІУ; T4: Horobets]
- **Office / role:** Pereiaslav sotnyk in 1654; acting Pereiaslav colonel / Pereiaslav colonel in the 1659-1662 sequence; acting hetman of Left-Bank Ukraine, **1660-1663**; claimant/full-hetman-elect at Bykiv/Kozelets-style councils but not durably recognized by Moscow; executed rival of Ivan Briukhovetskyi after the 1663 Black Council. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU; T4: Horobets]

**Source disagreement / correction note (flagged, not smoothed):**

1. **Birth date and birthplace:** use "date unknown; Pereiaslav family origin" as the stable formulation. Do not upgrade `1610s` to a Tier 1 fact.
2. **Kinship:** treat Somko as Bohdan Khmelnytskyy's brother-in-law and Yurii Khmelnytskyy's uncle; flag IEU's "father-in-law" wording if teaching kinship directly.
3. **Office/titulature:** `гетьман наказний Лівобережної України` is the safest form for 1660-1663. `Full hetman` belongs only in the contested 1661/1662 election-recognition context.
4. **Death date:** write **18/28 September 1663** if calendar precision matters; otherwise "September 1663 in Borzna."
5. **Execution details:** the stable fact is arrest after the Black Council, military-court condemnation on treason accusations, and execution at Borzna. Avoid invented last words or novel scenes.

## 2. Oppression / appropriation mechanism

**What happened:** Somko tried to hold the Left Bank after Yurii Khmelnytskyy's Slobodyshche/Chudniv turn toward the Commonwealth. His project kept Muscovite protection but tried to preserve the Hetmanate's autonomous status, strengthen hetman authority, and limit unauthorized voivode/garrison and fiscal interference. Moscow did not recognize his contested election, treated him as unreliable, and then used the 1663 Black Council framework to secure a different outcome. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: ЕІУ Chorna Rada; T4: Horobets]

**By whom:** Moscow/tsarist power in the recognition, election-management, military-presence, arrest, and treason-trial layer; Briukhovetskyi and his coalition in the local-campaign layer; rival Left-Bank starshyna and Methodius/Fylymonovych-style ecclesiastical politics in the denunciation layer; Somko and his own starshyna circle in the elite-centralizing layer. No side should be erased.

**Document references:** the Slobodyshche/Chudniv settlement of 1660; the Pereiaslav/1659 "New Articles" background; the Bykiv/Kozelets election-recognition crisis of 1661-1662; the tsarist instruction and Gagin mission for the Nizhyn Black Council; later confiscation/exile decisions around Somko's circle. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: ЕІУ Chorna Rada; T4: Horobets]

**Mechanism specifics:** the imperial simplification is to present Moscow as neutral arbiter over a disorderly Ukrainian political field. The record is sharper: Moscow approval became a veto point, Moscow military/diplomatic presence shaped the council, and accusations of secret contacts with Yurii Khmelnytskyy, Pavlo Teteria, Poland, and Crimea were politically useful. The Ukrainian romantic simplification moves in the opposite direction and turns Somko into pure constitutional nobility. A decolonized BIO reading has to show the trap and the agency together: Somko defended autonomy against Moscow interference, but his model was also elite-centered, suspicious of Zaporozhian participation, and ready to strengthen hetman coercive power over colonels and rivals. [T1: ЕІУ Chorna Rada; T1: IEU; T4: Horobets]

**What survived / what was distorted:** Somko survived as the dignified losing candidate in Kulish's *Chorna rada*, but literary memory can make his political program cleaner than it was. The distortion to avoid is "good elite versus bad masses." The structural lesson is harder: real social grievances, starshyna privilege, Zaporozhian political claims, Moscow intervention, and factional denunciations interacted.

## 3. Major works / legacy

- `late 1647` — sheltered Bohdan Khmelnytskyy's younger children in Pereiaslav when Khmelnytskyy left for the Sich to begin the uprising. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `1654` — Pereiaslav sotnyk; visited the tsar's camp in Lithuania on Khmelnytskyy's assignment. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `1657-1659` — opposed Ivan Vyhovskyi after Yurii Khmelnytskyy's displacement; spent time on the Don according to ЕІУ; lower-source details around a 1659 election after Ivan Bezpalyi are explicitly source-sensitive. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `autumn 1659` — after Yurii Khmelnytskyy's election, acted in the Pereiaslav regimental administration while Tymish Tsetsiura was absent. [T1: ЕІУ; T4: Horobets]
- `second half of 1660` — as acting hetman, governed Left-Bank Ukraine during Yurii Khmelnytskyy's Volhynian campaign and rejected the Slobodyshche/Chudniv turn toward the Commonwealth. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: ЕІУ political-processes entry]
- `winter 1660-1661` — fought Right-Bank, Crown, and Crimean-aligned forces; consolidated several Left-Bank regiments around his command. [T1: ЕІУ; T4: Horobets]
- `28 April 1661` — Bykiv-area election effort near Nizhyn: Left-Bank representatives supported Somko, but Vasyl Zolotarenko, Methodius/Fylymonovych, and Moscow-recognition politics blocked stable confirmation. [T1: ЕІУ; T4: Horobets]
- `11/21 October-14/24 December 1661` — defended Pereiaslav against Right-Bank and Crimean pressure while maintaining contacts and negotiations in the fractured Hetmanate field. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `April 1662` — Kozelets council proclaimed him full hetman of Left-Bank Ukraine, but Moscow refused recognition because no official tsarist representative was present and Somko's reliability was doubted. [T1: ЕІУ; T4: Horobets]
- `summer 1662` — with Russian forces, defeated Yurii Khmelnytskyy's Right-Bank forces and pushed them back across the Dnipro. [T1: ЕІУ]
- `pre-June 1663` — before the Nizhyn council, tried to negotiate with Pavlo Teteria and the Crimean Khanate; opponents used such contacts to intensify treason accusations. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU]
- `27-28 June 1663 N.S. / 17-18 June O.S.` — Black Council near Nizhyn; Somko lost to Ivan Briukhovetskyi in a council shaped by tsarist instruction, military presence, social grievance, and factional mobilization. [T1: ЕІУ Chorna Rada; T1: IEU Chorna rada]
- `June-September 1663` — arrested under Gagin's authority, imprisoned with Vasyl Zolotarenko and supporters, condemned by military court on treason accusations. [T1: ЕІУ; T1: IEU; primary: Samovydets]
- `18/28 September 1663` — executed at Borzna; other supporters were exiled or subordinated to the new order. [T1: ЕІУ; primary: Samovydets]
- `1857 and after` — Panteleimon Kulish's *Chorna rada* made Somko a central literary figure and helped fix him in Ukrainian memory as a tragic, dignified rival to Briukhovetskyi. [T1: IEU; T1: IEU Chorna rada]

## 6. Contested points

- **Legalist martyr or elite claimant?** Somko had a more explicit autonomy program than many rivals and pushed back against voivodes and Moscow fiscal interference. He also represented affluent Cossack/starshyna interests and tried to strengthen hetman power over colonels. Do not make him a democrat by contrast with Briukhovetskyi.
- **Pro-Moscow or anti-Moscow?** Both labels flatten him. He wanted Muscovite protection under stricter vassal/autonomy terms, not unconditional incorporation. His later contacts with Right-Bank and Crimean actors were either practical statecraft, tactical hedging, or dangerous ambiguity depending on source perspective.
- **Kozelets legitimacy:** the 1662 council mattered, but Moscow did not recognize it. The lesson should teach the difference between local election, starshyna support, general council, and imperial confirmation.
- **Moscow as referee:** do not present the tsarist side as a neutral legitimacy mechanism. ЕІУ's Black Council entry makes the tsarist instruction and Gagin's military presence central to the process.
- **Briukhovetskyi versus Somko:** avoid a tidy contrast of corrupt populist versus pure statesman. Briukhovetskyi exploited real anti-starshyna resentment; Somko's side had real institutional arguments and real elite blind spots.
- **Zolotarenko and Methodius:** factional denunciations matter. Somko's fall was not simply Moscow versus one Ukrainian leader; intra-Left-Bank competition and church-political mediation shaped Moscow's view.
- **Execution:** do not add romantic last speeches, rescue scenes, or novel details to the fact section. Keep Kulish in literary memory unless the lesson is explicitly about fiction.
- **Kulish overlay:** *Chorna rada* is indispensable for memory, but it is not a source to reconstruct exact 1663 events. It turns political causality into character drama.
- **Image authenticity:** the known Commons portrait is a 19th-century/copy tradition with an unknown artist, not a life portrait. Use it only with a provenance caption.
- **Modern disinformation:** Russian narratives can use Somko as either failed separatist or proof of "natural" Moscow arbitration; Ukrainian memory can use him as a spotless martyr. The dossier should teach why both moves are too simple.
