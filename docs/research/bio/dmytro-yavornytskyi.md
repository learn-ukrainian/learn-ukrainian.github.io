## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Яворницький Дмитро Іванович.
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Дмитро Яворницький; early family/name form Єварницький / Evarnytskyi; Dmytro Yavornytskyi.
- **Born:** 1855-11-06 | Сонцівка, Харківська губернія, Russian Empire | now Borysivka area, Kharkiv oblast. NAS profile gives 06.11.1855; Ukrainian Wikipedia via MCP and museum sources confirm the same year and village.
- **Died:** 1940-08-05 | Dnipropetrovsk, Ukrainian SSR, USSR | cause not specified in retrieved Tier 1/2 sources.
- **Family / education key facts:** Son of a village deacon; trained at Kharkiv University; became a historian, archaeologist, ethnographer, folklorist, lexicographer, and museum builder. He directed the Katerynoslav/Dnipropetrovsk historical museum from 1902 until the Soviet dismissal in 1933, and was elected to NTSh in 1914 and VUAN in 1929. ## 2. Oppression mechanism

- **What happened:** Ideological dismissal, surveillance, professional destruction, and material deprivation.
- **When:** The decisive Soviet action was the 1933 campaign. MCP-retrieved Ukrainian Wikipedia, corroborated by the NAS profile, states that on 1933-08-31, after accusations of "український буржуазний націоналізм", the education commissar Volodymyr Zatonskyi removed him from the museum directorship.
- **By whom:** Soviet Ukrainian education and party apparatus; GPU surveillance is reported in the retrieved Wikipedia chunk. Specific GPU file ID: source not found.
- **Document references:** Retrieved sources name Zatonskyi's dismissal order of 1933-08-31 and the ideological accusation. A full archival call number for the dismissal order or surveillance file was not found.
- **Mechanism specifics:** The repression was not an execution or camp sentence but a targeted removal from the institution he had built. The MCP result states that the museum was labeled a nest of nationalist counterrevolutionary propaganda; the NAS profile states that in 1933 he was accused of "Ukrainian bourgeois nationalism", dismissed, and left without means of subsistence. The same MCP result says his academic pension was withheld for four months and that he had to sell possessions for food. This belongs pedagogically with Soviet anti-Ukrainian scholarly repression: the regime attacked the archive, the museum, and the historian's public authority, not only the person.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** His museum collections survived as a core of the present Dnipro historical museum; his work on Zaporizhian Cossack history survived in print and later reprint. What was damaged was institutional continuity: the museum was ideologically subordinated, and the living scholar was pushed out of the public role that made his research usable.

## 3. Major works

- `1888` — *Запорожье в остатках старины и преданиях народа*, historical-ethnographic study; one of the early monuments of his fieldwork method.
- `1892-1897` — *История запорожских козаков* / later Ukrainian *Історія запорозьких козаків*, 3 vols.; his central synthesis of Zaporizhian history.
- `1894` — *Вольности запорожских козаков*, source-based study of Cossack territorial and legal traditions.
- `1902` — museum leadership at Katerynoslav; not a book, but a major scholarly institution-building work.
- `1920` — *Словник української мови*, lexicographic work listed by NAS.
- `1928` — *Дніпрові пороги*, historical-geographic essay tied to the DniproHES-era rescue documentation.
- `1930s` — archival and museum cataloging; many materials remained unpublished or delayed after the 1933 purge.

## 4. Primary-source quotes

- "музей - це минуле, його історія" — letter to Viktor Hoshkevych quoted by the Dnipro museum article "Дмитро Яворницький: образ ученого в контексті минулого і сучасного". The line anchors why his dismissal was cultural violence, not a routine personnel change.
- "одні з найбагатших земель" — *Історія запорозьких козаків*, vol. 1, in the section on Zaporizhian lands, retrieved through the public text excerpt. The phrase is useful for learners because it shows his descriptive, territorial approach to Cossack history.

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** Scholarly-narrative, archival, ethnographic; older orthography and Russian-language publication contexts appear because many works were first printed under imperial conditions.
- **CEFR readiness for full reading:** C1 for primary prose; B2 excerpts are possible if vocabulary is pre-taught.
- **Lexicon notes:** Cossack institutional terms, geography of the Dnipro rapids, older ethnographic labels, and imperial administrative names.
- **Stylistic features:** Dense enumerations, concrete artifact vocabulary, and a museum-builder's habit of tying place, object, and oral memory.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** How to balance his romantic Cossack prose with his documentary contribution; how to handle Russian-language titles without Russifying his identity; how to evaluate his acceptance of some inherited Polish/Russian colonial descriptions of the Cossacks.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** He is often reduced to "the chronicler of the Sich", which hides his archaeology, museum work, lexicography, and rescue documentation before the flooding of the Dnipro rapids.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them:** Russian-imperial framing tends to treat him as a regional antiquarian of "South Russia"; the Soviet accusation of nationalism already shows why that framing is hostile to his Ukrainian subject.
- **Other-perspective considerations:** His Cossack studies intersect with Jewish, Polish, Crimean Tatar, and Ottoman histories; curriculum use should not turn Cossack freedom into a flat heroic myth.
