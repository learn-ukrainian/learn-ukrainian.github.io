## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name:** Михайло Михайлович Коцюбинський.
- **Pseudonym:** Захар Козуб.
- **Born:** 5 (17) September 1864 in Vinnytsia, Podilia Governorate, Russian Empire.
- **Died:** 12 (25) April 1913, aged 48; buried on Boldyna Hora in Chernihiv.
- **Education and early pressure:** studied in Bar and Sharhorod; financial hardship prevented
  completion of further formal education. Police classified him as politically unreliable and
  searched his home after detecting connections with the narodovoltsi milieu.
- **Public and professional activity:** worked as a teacher, served with the phylloxera commission
  in Bessarabia and Crimea during the 1890s, moved to Chernihiv in 1898, worked in zemstvo and
  statistical offices, and participated in Ukrainian national-cultural initiatives. ЕСУ records
  his membership in the Brotherhood of Tarasivtsi and censorship pressure on his journalism.
- **Family:** husband of Vira Deisha and father of Yurii, Oksana, Iryna, and Roman. Mykhailyna
  Kotsiubynska, literary scholar and active participant in the Sixtiers movement, was his niece.

**Unresolved death-place conflict:** ЕСУ and the Internet Encyclopedia of Ukraine give **Kyiv** as
the place of death and Chernihiv as the burial place. The refreshed Ukrainian Wikipedia article
gives **Chernihiv** as the place of death, and Chernihiv memorial sources describe his final return
home. A digitized Chernihiv church death-register file exists in the State Archive collection
(`ДАЧгО, ф. 679, оп. 10, спр. 1524`), but it was not transcribed in this pass. The dossier therefore
does **not** choose one place. Any learner-facing assertion must either cite the primary register or
state the disagreement.

## 2. Imperial pressure and later reception

### Documented during his lifetime

The strongest evidence is personal, not generic. Ukrainian Wikipedia records police surveillance
from 1882 and a 1883 search that confiscated manuscript copies of banned Shevchenko works. ЕСУ
records censorship obstacles at the newspaper *Volyn*, his involvement with the Brotherhood of
Tarasivtsi, and his subsequent Ukrainian cultural activity in Chernihiv. These facts are safer and
more specific than claiming that every publication was directly blocked by the Valuev Circular or
the Ems Decree.

### Reception after his death

Kotsiubynsky has been read through competing social-realist and modernist frameworks. The corpus
itself resists a simple binary: *Fata Morgana* examines village society and the upheavals of
1905–1907, while *Intermezzo* joins formal innovation to a final return toward people and their
suffering. A decolonized account should restore Ukrainian agency and modernist experimentation
without inventing a complete abandonment of social responsibility or an undocumented, uniform
Soviet suppression narrative.

### The Sixtiers connection

Parajanov's 1965 film based on *Тіні забутих предків* became the occasion for the documented public
protest by Ivan Dziuba, Vasyl Stus, and Viacheslav Chornovil at the Kyiv premiere on 4 September
1965. Mykhailyna Kotsiubynska was independently an active Sixtier and later a Shevchenko Prize
laureate. This is a useful history-of-reception connection, but it must not be presented as proof
that her uncle directly caused or ideologically directed her dissident work.

## 3. Major works

- `1902` — **«Цвіт яблуні»**, an innovative psychological text about a writer observing his own
  response to his child's death.
- `1903–1910` — **«Fata Morgana»**, a two-part social-psychological work about Ukrainian village
  life, land, labor, and the upheavals surrounding 1905–1907.
- `1908` — **«Intermezzo»**, an impressionist psychological novella about exhaustion, nature, and
  renewed readiness to hear human suffering.
- `1911` — **«Тіні забутих предків»**, a novella grounded in Hutsul life, language, landscape, and
  folklore; the basis of Parajanov's film.
- Other important prose includes **«На віру»**, **«Лялечка»**, **«Сміх»**, **«Persona grata»**,
  **«Він іде!»**, **«Подарунок на іменини»**, and **«Коні не винні»**.

The range matters pedagogically: these works show that social observation, psychological prose,
impressionist technique, and neoromantic or folkloric material coexist rather than replace one
another in a clean sequence.

## 5. Language and reading register

- **Register:** lyrical impressionism and social-psychological prose, with painterly attention to
  color, sound, movement, and interior perception.
- **«Тіні забутих предків»:** dense Hutsul vocabulary and cultural realia make unaided reading
  demanding; use a glossary and do not normalize dialect forms into standard Ukrainian.
- **«Intermezzo»:** appropriate for close reading of syntax, personification, sensory imagery, and
  the relation between private exhaustion and public responsibility.
- **«Fata Morgana»:** supports comparison of social position, land, labor, and competing hopes; a
  selected reading is more realistic than assigning the full work as a single seminar excerpt.
- **Personal correspondence:** the corpus contains Kotsiubynsky's Russian-language letters to
  Oleksandra Aplaksina (`59bbf782_c0002` and neighboring chunks). Use them as sensitive historical
  evidence, not as Ukrainian language models and not as license for sensational biography.

## 6. Contested points and prohibited shortcuts

- **Not “pure art versus the people.”** ЕСУ, the textbook corpus, *Fata Morgana*, and the ending of
  *Intermezzo* all contradict a simple abandonment-of-the-people thesis.
- **No unsupported critical attributions.** Do not claim that Serhii Yefremov accused Kotsiubynsky
  of being “detached from the people,” or that Mykola Voronyi called that detachment his greatest
  virtue, without exact primary citations.
- **No invented imperial-control story.** Meetings with Maksym Gorky on Capri are documented; a
  claim that Gorky attempted to “tame” Ukrainian literature was not verified and must be omitted.
- **Influence requires evidence.** Hamsun and Maeterlinck may be discussed as comparative contexts
  or documented reading influences only when a scholarly source is cited. “Dialogue with” them is
  too vague to function as a fact.
- **Death place remains disputed.** Do not infer place of death from place of burial.
- **Reception needs named scholarship.** Avoid claims about deliberate Soviet suppression unless
  a specific edition, curriculum, critic, or scholarly study is cited.
