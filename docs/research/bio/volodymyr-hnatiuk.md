## 1. Verified facts

- **Full name (UA, canonical):** Володимир Михайлович Гнатюк.
- **Pseudonyms / aliases:** Володимир Гнатюк; Volodymyr Hnatiuk. Russian-imperial
  transliterations must not appear in body text except in the forbidden-forms
  field in §8.
- **Born:** 9 May 1871 | село Велеснів, then Austria-Hungary | now Чортківський
  район, Тернопільська область, Україна [T1: ЕІУ] [T3: uk.wikipedia].
- **Died:** 6 October 1926 | Львів | after long illness; buried at the Lychakiv
  cemetery (поле № 5) [T3: uk.wikipedia].
- **Family / education key facts:** Educated in Basilian gymnasiums in Бучач and
  Станіслав, then the philosophy faculty of the Lviv university (Slavic
  philology), where he studied under Михайло Грушевський and Олександр Колесса
  [T3: uk.wikipedia]. Core roles: ethnographer, folklorist, linguist, literary
  scholar, translator, and the central organiser of NTSh ethnography. From 1899
  to his death he was the academic secretary of the НТШ; head of its Ethnographic
  Commission from 1916; corresponding member of the Petersburg Academy (1902);
  academician of the ВУАН (1924) [T1: ЕІУ] [T3: uk.wikipedia].

## 2. Oppression mechanism

Not Applicable as a **Russian/Soviet** repression-primary biography — Hnatiuk
lived and worked in Austrian (then Polish) Galicia and died in 1926 of long
illness. The relevant mechanism is the assimilationist denial his scholarship
fought, which must still be named specifically rather than left vague.

- **What happened:** No documented arrest, exile, or execution. The pressure on
  his life's work was the Russophile («москвофільство»), Hungarian, and Slovak
  denial of a distinct Ukrainian identity for the Lemkos, Transcarpathian
  Ruthenians, and Bačka Rusyns.
- **When (specific dates):** across his career (1896–1926); his polemical
  titles answer this denial directly — «Словаки чи русини» (1901) and «Чи
  закарпатські українці автохтони?» (1922).
- **By whom (specific agency):** assimilationist currents and administrations
  in Austria-Hungary and, later, the москвофільський press; this is a
  language-and-identity contest, not a Russian-Imperial police case.
- **Document references:** his own works «Русини Пряшівської єпархії і їх
  говори» (1900), «Словаки чи русини» (1901), and «Чи закарпатські українці
  автохтони?» (1922) are the documentary anchors of the contest.
- **Mechanism specifics:** the denial was the claim that Ukrainians at the
  western and southern edges of the ethnographic territory were "Slovaks,"
  "Hungarian Ruthenians," or "Russians"; Hnatiuk's field documentation refuted
  this by recording their language and lore as Ukrainian. Decolonial note: early
  in his career a москвофільська newspaper and another body lost or refused to
  print large folk-song collections he had submitted, an early example of how
  hostile gatekeeping erased Ukrainian fieldwork.
- **What survived / what was destroyed:** about 1000 published works and over
  1500 recorded folk texts survived (autographs in the Rylskyi Institute and the
  Shevchenko Institute of Literature); the early lost song collections did not.

## 3. Major works

- `1897`–`1911` — «Етнографічні матеріали з Угорської Русі», 6 vols — Lemko and
  Transcarpathian folk prose and song, including the unique 140-text repertoire
  of one village (Орябина) [T3].
- `1902` — «Угро-руські духовні вірші»; «Галицько-руські народні легенди»
  (2 vols, 1902–1903) [T3].
- `1905`–`1907` — «Коломийки», 3 vols [T3].
- `1914` — «Колядки і щедрівки», 2 vols [T3].
- `1916` — «Українські народні байки» (2 vols); «Національне відродження
  австро-угорських українців» [T3].
- `1922` — «Чи закарпатські українці автохтони?»; `1923` — «Гуцули»; `1926` —
  «Як поставав світ» [T3].
- Editor of about 60 volumes of the «Етнографічний Збірник» and «Матеріали до
  української етнології», and of the «Літературно-Науковий Вістник»; director
  of the Українська видавнича спілка [T1: ЕІУ] [T3].

## 5. Language register

- **Register:** academic folkloristic and editorial prose, plus the dialectal
  folk texts he recorded (Lemko, Hutsul, Transcarpathian).
- **CEFR readiness for full reading:** C1 for the scholarly apparatus; B2 for
  glossed folk-text excerpts (коломийки, колядки), with dialect support.
- **Lexicon notes:** heavy regional dialectism in the recorded texts; precise
  textological terminology in his prose.
- **Stylistic features:** rigorous transcription fidelity, genre classification,
  and comparative-Slavic framing kept Ukrainian-centred.

## 6. Contested points

- **What's debated in modern UA scholarship:** the full extent of Hnatiuk's
  ~1000-work corpus is still not fully studied; scholars weigh how to balance his
  enormous collecting achievement against the period's transcription conventions.
- **What gets simplified in popular memory:** popular memory reduces him to "a
  great collector," eclipsing the institution-builder who ran the NTSh
  ethnographic network, edited ~60 volumes, and directed Ukrainian publishing.
  Lead with the infrastructure and the autochthony argument, not a lone-collector
  image.
- **Where modern Russian disinformation attacks them (if applicable):** Russian
  and москвофільська framing claims the Lemkos and Transcarpathian Ukrainians as
  "Russians" / "Rusyns separate from Ukrainians"; Hnatiuk's fieldwork is a
  primary counter-argument and the module should foreground it.
- **Polish / Jewish / other-perspective considerations (if applicable):** the
  Slovak–Ukrainian and Hungarian-Ruthenian questions he engaged are sensitive
  cross-border identity issues; frame them as documentation of Ukrainian
  presence, not as ethnic claims against neighbours.
