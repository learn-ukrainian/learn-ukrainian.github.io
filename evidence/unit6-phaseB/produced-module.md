## Звуки і літери

The first rule is the anchor for everything in Ukrainian reading: **«Звуки ми чуємо й вимовляємо, а букви бачимо й пишемо»** (We hear and pronounce sounds, but we see and write letters). A **«звук»** (sound) is what your mouth says and your ear hears. A **«літера»** (letter), also called **«буква»** (letter), is the symbol your eyes see and your hand writes. In **«мама»** (mama), your ear hears the sound [м]; on the page, your eye sees **«М м»** (the letter M).

:::note
Keep the difference strict: sound belongs to speech; letter belongs to writing.
:::

Ukrainian has 33 letters, but 38 sounds. <!-- VERIFY: precise claim (33 letters; 38 sounds) --> That mismatch is normal. Some letters can stand for more than one sound in certain positions: **«я»** (letter ya), **«ю»** (letter yu), **«є»** (letter ye), and **«ї»** (letter yi). One sign, **«ь»** (soft sign), has no sound of its own; it only softens the consonant before it. This is why Ukrainian teachers say **«голосний звук»** (vowel sound), not “vowel letter.” A **«голосний»** (vowel) or **«приголосний»** (consonant) is a type of sound. Letters only mark sounds. На відміну від English, keep the answer simple at first: **«голосні»** (vowels) and **«приголосні»** (consonants) are the first **два** (two) sound groups; this is the basic **«відповідь»** (answer) to “what kind of sound is it?”

The Ukrainian **«алфавіт»** (alphabet), or **«абетка»** (alphabet), has 33 letters in this order: **А а, Б б, В в, Г г, Ґ ґ, Д д, Е е, Є є, Ж ж, З з, И и, І і, Ї ї, Й й, К к, Л л, М м, Н н, О о, П п, Р р, С с, Т т, У у, Ф ф, Х х, Ц ц, Ч ч, Ш ш, Щ щ, Ь ь, Ю ю, Я я**. <!-- VERIFY: precise claim (33 letters) --> First anchor words: **«ананас»** (pineapple), **«банан»** (banana), **«вода»** (water), **«гора»** (mountain), **«ґудзик»** (button), **«їжак»** (hedgehog), and **«день»** (day). Ukrainian spelling is mostly phonetic: once you know the sounds, most words can be read from what you see.

## Голосні звуки

A **«голосний»** (vowel) is a sound made only with voice. Air moves freely through the mouth, with no lips, teeth, or tongue blocking it. Українською: **«голосні»** (vowels) **вимовляються** (are pronounced) **без** (without) перешкоди; this is the main feature of **«голосних»** (vowel sounds). A first-grade Ukrainian textbook presents vowels as sounds you can hear in song, in a dark forest, in surprise, and in admiration: they are easy to pronounce and easy to sing. Try stretching them like a note: [а-а-а], [о-о-о], [у-у-у]. In **«мама»** (mama), **«око»** (eye), and **«урок»** (lesson), the open voiced parts are the vowels.

:::note
A **«звук»** (sound) is what you hear and say. A letter is what you see and write. Keep that difference clear.
:::

Ukrainian has six vowel sounds: [а], [о], [у], [е], [и], [і]. It has ten vowel letters: **А, О, У, Е, И, І, Я, Ю, Є, Ї** (vowel letters). The four extra letters **Я, Ю, Є, Ї** (special vowel letters) can mark two sounds in some positions. Module 2 will handle them fully. For now, remember this: every Ukrainian word has at least one vowel sound, and vowels are the heart of every syllable.

The pair [и] and [і] needs patient attention. For [і], make a small smile: lips spread, tongue high and forward. For [и], relax the smile: lips looser, jaw slightly lower. Practice the contrast in short pairs: **«дим»** (smoke) - **«дім»** (house), **«кит»** (whale) - **«кіт»** (cat), **«лис»** (fox) - **«ліс»** (forest), **«ти»** (you) - **«ті»** (those). The difference is small, but it changes meaning.

In sound models, Ukrainian schoolbooks often mark a vowel sound with [•]. Use it as a listening symbol. **«ма-ма»** (mama) has two [а] sounds: [•] [•]. **«мо-ло-ко»** (milk) has three clear [о] sounds: [•] [•] [•]. **«Уля»** (Ulia) begins with [у]. Ukrainian [о] stays clean even when unstressed: **«молоко»** (milk), not “malako.”

## Приголосні звуки

A **«приголосний»** (consonant) is a **«звук»** (sound) made with voice plus noise, or with noise only. Your lips, teeth, or tongue create a small obstacle for the air. Українською: перешкоду можуть робити **«губи»** (lips), teeth, or tongue; **він** (it, the sound) is a consonant because the air is not completely free. That is why consonants can buzz, whisper, whistle, or hiss. Vowels can be sung; a clean consonant cannot. Try holding [к], [п], [т]. They stop quickly in **«кіт»** (cat), **«тато»** (dad), and **«сон»** (sleep).

Ukrainian has 32 consonant sounds, but only 22 consonant letters. <!-- VERIFY: precise claim (32 consonant; 22 consonant) --> Some consonants form hard and soft pairs. Ukrainian school notation often marks a hard consonant with [–] and a soft consonant with [=]. This hard-soft contrast is not an English habit; treat it as a Slavic listening skill. In **англійській** (English), learners may miss this contrast at first, so listen for it **завжди** (always). In **«мама»** (mama), the model is [– • – •]. In **«ліс»** (forest), the first consonant is soft: [= • –].

:::note
A **«літера»** (letter) is what you see. A **«звук»** (sound) is what you say and hear. One letter may point to different sound behavior depending on its place in the word.
:::

For first recognition, use Anna Ohoiko’s videos with these consonant letters: **М, Н, С, К, Л, Р, Б, В, Д, П, Т, Г, Ґ, З, Ж, Ш, Х, Й, Ч, Щ, Ц, Ф** (consonant letters). Start with friendly anchor words: **«мама»** (mama), **«ніс»** (nose), **«сон»** (sleep), **«кіт»** (cat), **«лимон»** (lemon), **«рука»** (hand), **«дім»** (house), and **«тато»** (dad).

Watch four special points early. **«Г»** (letter he) is [ɦ], close to English h in “behind.” **«Ґ»** (letter ge) is [g], as in “go,” and appears in **«ґудзик»** (button). **«Щ»** (letter shcha) is always [шч]. **«Ь»** (soft sign) has no sound of its own; it softens the previous consonant, as in **«день»** (day). This soft sign is a **«знак»** (sign), not a vowel or consonant sound.

One first warning: final voiced consonants stay voiced in Ukrainian. Keep the contrast in **«гриб»** (mushroom) - **«грип»** (flu), and **«казка»** (fairy tale) - **«каска»** (helmet).

## Привіт!

> Вчитель: «Добрий день!» (Good afternoon!)
>
> Учні: «Добрий день!» (Good afternoon!)
>
> Вчитель: «Привіт! Як справи?» (Hi! How are things?)
>
> Учень: «Добре.» (Good.)
>
> Учениця: «Чудово.» (Great.)
>
> Учень: «Нормально. А у тебе?» (Fine. And you?)
>
> Вчитель: «Добре.» (Good.)

Use **«привіт»** (hi) for friends, family, and classmates. In the question **«Як справи?»** (How are things?), **«справи»** means “affairs” or “things,” but learn the whole greeting as one useful chunk.

> Марко: «Привіт! Як тебе звати?» (Hi! What is your name?)
>
> Софія: «Мене звати Софія. А тебе?» (My name is Sofiia. And you?)
>
> Марко: «Мене звати Марко.» (My name is Marko.)
>
> Марко: «Радий тебе бачити!» (Glad to see you!)
>
> Софія: «Рада тебе бачити!» (Glad to see you!)

A man says **«Радий тебе бачити!»** (Glad to see you). A woman says **«Рада тебе бачити!»** (Glad to see you); this is your first small look at gender forms, which become a bigger topic later.

note
For polite first contact, keep these as indivisible phrases: **«Добрий день!»** (Good afternoon!), **«Доброго ранку!»** (Good morning!), **«Добрий вечір!»** (Good evening!), **«До побачення!»** (Goodbye!), and **«На все добре!»** (All the best!). Listen for stress from audio, keep the final soft sound in **«день»** (day), and keep clean [о] in **«Доброго»** (good).

Ukrainian also has **«милозвучність»** (euphony), a kind of politeness between sounds. It alternates short forms to avoid heavy clusters: **«була в школі»** (was at school), **«пішов у школу»** (went to school), **«мама й тато»** (mom and dad), **«мама і тато»** (mom and dad).

Now read **«Привіт»** (hi) by sound: П [п], р [р], и [и], в [в], і [і], т [т]. It has two **«голосний»** (vowel) sounds and four **«приголосний»** (consonant) sounds.

## Підсумок

Use these questions as your final self-check. How many letters are in the Ukrainian alphabet? 33. How many sounds are counted here? 38. Why are the numbers different? Some letters can do more than one job: **«я»** (letter ya), **«ю»** (letter yu), **«є»** (letter ye), and **«ї»** (letter yi) may point to two sounds; **«щ»** (letter shcha) points to [shch]; **«ь»** (soft sign) has no sound of its own. What do you hear and pronounce? A **«звук»** (sound). What do you see and write? A **«літера»** (letter). What is a **«голосний»** (vowel sound)? A sound made with voice and no blockage: [a], [o], [u], [e], [y], [i]. What is a **«приголосний»** (consonant sound)? A sound with blockage from lips, teeth, or tongue. Can you say **«голосна літера»** (vowel letter)? No: vowel sounds, not letters.

:::note
For first contact, keep these chunks ready: **«привіт»** (hi), **«Добрий день»** (Good day), **«Доброго ранку»** (Good morning), **«Добрий вечір»** (Good evening), **«До побачення»** (Goodbye), **«як справи»** (how are things), **«добре»** (fine), and **«чудово»** (great). Also recognize friendly anchor words: **«мама»** (mother) and **«молоко»** (milk).
:::
