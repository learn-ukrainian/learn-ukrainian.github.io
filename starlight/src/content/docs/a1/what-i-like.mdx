When you review these two conversations, you will notice a distinct grammatical boundary. In English, you might say "I like reading" or "I like this book." You use the exact same word, "like", for both your actions and the objects around you. In Ukrainian, we have two distinct patterns for expressing this idea — one that pairs naturally with actions (things you actively do) and another that works well with objects (things around you). In this module, we will focus on when to use each one.

Look closely at the first dialogue. Olenka asks, **Що ти любиш робити?** (What do you like to do?). Maksym replies with the phrase **Я люблю...** (I love/like...) followed by action words like **читати** (to read) and **слухати** (to listen). He is talking about his activities.

Now look at the second dialogue. Anna holds up an object and asks, **Тобі подобається ця книга?** (Do you like this book?). Dmytro answers with **Так, мені подобається** (Yes, I like it). They are not talking about performing an action; they are talking about their reaction to a physical thing in the world.

Understanding when to use each pattern is an important step in sounding natural. In later modules, you will learn that these patterns can sometimes overlap — for example, **Я люблю каву** (I love coffee) is perfectly natural Ukrainian. But at this stage, we will focus on the clearest use of each: **люблю** with actions and **подобається** with things. This gives you two reliable tools to talk about your hobbies, your favorite things, and what you do not enjoy at all.

## Я люблю... (I Like...)

When you want to talk about your hobbies, your daily habits, or the activities that bring you joy, you will use the verb **любити** (to love/like). Specifically, when you are talking about yourself and your own preferences, you use the form **я люблю** (I love/like).

But you cannot just stop there. You have to specify *what* action you like doing. To do this, you need the "dictionary form" of a verb. In grammatical terms, we call this the infinitive.

The concept of the infinitive is universal in European languages, but in Ukrainian, it is visually very distinct. Think about how you look up a word in an English dictionary. You do not look up "running" or "ran"; you look up "run". In Ukrainian dictionaries, you look up the infinitive form. Every single infinitive you will learn at the beginner level ends in the two letters **-ти**. This suffix is the grammatical marker that tells you the verb is in its raw, unconjugated state — what Ukrainian grammarians call the неозна́чена фо́рма (indefinite form). You can think of the **-ти** ending as the exact equivalent of placing the word "to" before a verb in English, such as "to read" or "to walk".

Here are some of the most common infinitives you will use to talk about your free time. Notice how the **-ти** marker appears in every single one. One verb in this list, **дивитися**, adds the reflexive particle **-ся** after **-ти** — you will learn more about reflexive verbs later, but the core marker **-ти** is still there:

*   **читати** (to read)
*   **гуляти** (to walk)
*   **готувати** (to cook)
*   **слухати** (to listen)
*   **дивитися** (to watch)
*   **грати** (to play)
*   **малювати** (to draw)
*   **подорожувати** (to travel)
*   **співати** (to sing)

When you combine **я люблю** with one of these infinitives, you create a complete, natural Ukrainian sentence describing your hobby. The pattern is simple and consistent: Subject + **люблю** + Infinitive.

*   **Я люблю читати.** (I like to read.)
*   **Я люблю гуляти.** (I like to walk.)
*   **Я люблю готувати.** (I like to cook.)
*   **Я люблю слухати музику.** (I like to listen to music.)

You do not need to alter or conjugate the infinitive at all. You simply plug it into the equation. This makes talking about your interests one of the easiest sentence patterns to master in the Ukrainian language.

When you speak Ukrainian, the rhythm and melody of the words are just as important as the letters themselves. While every single infinitive in this list ends in **-ти**, the stress—the syllable that receives the most emphasis when spoken aloud—is entirely unpredictable. There is no underlying phonetic rule that dictates where the stress must fall based on the spelling. In the word **читати** (to read), the stress lands firmly on the middle syllable. However, in the word **співати** (to sing), the stress is also on the middle vowel, but in the longer word **подорожувати** (to travel), it falls near the end. You must memorize the specific stress pattern of each new verb individually, just as you memorize its spelling.

## Мені подобається... (I Like...)

In English, the verb "to like" is incredibly versatile and forgiving. You can say "I like swimming" and "I like this chair," using the exact same verb for both an action you perform and a static object you observe. Ukrainian requires much more precision and forces you to divide the world into different categories. So far we have used **я люблю** with action words (infinitives). But what happens when you want to express that you like a physical object, a place, or a piece of art? Ukrainian gives you a second, very common construction for exactly this purpose.

For things that simply exist in the world, you must use an entirely different construction: **мені подобається**.

Although this phrase translates smoothly to "I like" in English, the literal grammatical mechanics behind it are quite different from the English "subject-verb" structure. For now, it is best to treat **мені подобається** as a fixed, unbreakable chunk of vocabulary. Do not try to analyze the grammar behind the word **мені** or why it changes depending on who is speaking; simply memorize the entire phrase as a single, unified block that means "I like [a thing]".

At this stage, use this chunk when you are reacting to nouns — it is the most natural fit. Here is how it looks in practice:

*   **Мені подобається музика.** (I like music.)
*   **Мені подобається ця книга.** (I like this book.)
*   **Мені подобається Ки́їв.** (I like Kyiv.)

Notice the practical divide. If your friend asks what you want to do this weekend, you use an action pattern: **Я люблю гуляти** (I like to walk). But if your friend hands you a cup of coffee and asks how it tastes, you are reacting to a thing: **Мені подобається** (I like it). Using the right pattern for the right situation will make your Ukrainian sound natural from the very beginning.

### Saying No: Negation

Expressing your dislikes is just as important as sharing your interests. Fortunately, negation in Ukrainian is delightfully straightforward and consistent. You do not need to worry about complex auxiliary verbs like "do not" or "does not". You simply take the negative particle **не** (not) and place it directly in front of the verb you wish to negate.

If you want to state that you do not enjoy a particular activity, you insert **не** immediately before **люблю**:

*   **Я не люблю готувати.** (I do not like to cook.)
*   **Я не люблю співати.** (I do not like to sing.)

The exact same rule applies when you want to say that you do not like a specific thing. You place the **не** right before the verb **подобається**:

*   **Мені не подобається цей фільм.** (I do not like this film.)
*   **Мені не подобається музика.** (I do not like music.)

### Asking Questions

To turn these statements of preference into questions, the spoken language relies entirely on intonation. You do not rearrange the sentence structure. You simply raise the pitch of your voice at the end of the sentence to indicate that you are asking a question, rather than making a statement.

However, when you direct a question at your conversation partner, the pronouns and verb forms must change to reflect that you are speaking to "you" rather than talking about "I". The verb **любити** changes its form based on the person performing the action. Instead of **я люблю** (I love), you must use the form **ти любиш** (you love).

*   **Ти любиш читати?** (Do you like to read?)
*   **Ти любиш подорожувати?** (Do you like to travel?)

Similarly, when asking about objects, the fixed chunk **мені подобається** (pleasing to me) shifts to **тобі подобається** (pleasing to you).

*   **Тобі подобається ця книга?** (Do you like this book?)
*   **Тобі подобається Київ?** (Do you like Kyiv?)

We will explore the complete conjugation tables for verbs in a later module. For now, your only task is to recognize **я люблю** when speaking about yourself, and **ти любиш** when asking a question to your partner.

## Підсумок — Summary

You have now learned the two most fundamental ways to express your preferences in the Ukrainian language. The key to this module is learning a reliable starting framework: use **люблю** when talking about activities, and **подобається** when reacting to things around you. As your Ukrainian grows, you will discover that these patterns can overlap — but this division gives you a solid foundation to build on.

1.  **Я люблю + infinitive (-ти):** You use this pattern exclusively when you want to talk about activities and things you enjoy doing. The verb must always be in its dictionary form, recognizable by its ending.
2.  **Мені подобається + noun:** You use this fixed chunk when you want to express that you like a specific thing, place, or object. You do not analyze the grammar behind it; you simply deploy it as a complete unit.

When you want to express a negative preference, the rule is identical and straightforward for both patterns: simply place the word **не** directly before the main verb (**не люблю** or **не подобається**).

**Self-Check:**
Before moving on to the next module, take a moment to test yourself. Can you answer these prompts aloud in Ukrainian?
*   Name 3 things you like doing (using **Я люблю...**).
*   Name 2 things you like (using **Мені подобається...**).
*   Name 1 thing you do not like doing (using **Я не люблю...**).
*   Name 1 thing you do not like (using **Мені не подобається...**).

If you can confidently complete these sentences aloud, you are ready to move forward and expand your ability to describe actions.
