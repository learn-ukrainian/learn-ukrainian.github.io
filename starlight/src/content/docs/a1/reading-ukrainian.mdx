## –°–Ї–ї–∞–і–Є (Syllables)

There is a golden rule taught to every Ukrainian first-grader, straight from the pages of –С–Њ–ї—М—И–∞–Ї–ЊћБ–≤–∞'s –±—Г–Ї–≤–∞ћБ—А on page 25: **¬Ђ–£ —Б–ї–ЊћБ–≤—Ц —Б—В—ЦћБ–ї—М–Ї–Є —Б–Ї–ї–∞–і—Ц–≤, —Б–Ї—ЦћБ–ї—М–Ї–Є –≥–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ–ЄћБ—Е –Ј–≤—ГћБ–Ї—Ц–≤¬ї** вАФ a word has as many syllables as it has vowel sounds. This rule never breaks. Look at the word **–Љ–∞ћБ–Љ–∞** вАФ it has two vowels, **–Р** and **–Р**, so it splits into two syllables: **–Љ–∞-–Љ–∞**. The word **–Љ–Њ–ї–Њ–Ї–ЊћБ** has three vowels, all **–Ю**: **–Љ–Њ-–ї–Њ-–Ї–Њ** вАФ three syllables. What about **–±–∞–љ–Ї**? One vowel, **–Р** вАФ one syllable. No splitting needed. And **—Б–Њ–љ**? One vowel, **–Ю** вАФ also one syllable. This rule is absolute. No exceptions exist in the entire Ukrainian language. If you can find the vowels, you can count the syllables.

Now for the method. –С–Њ–ї—М—И–∞–Ї–Њ–≤–∞'s page 29 outlines the **–Ј–≤—Г–Ї–Њ–≤–ЄћБ–є –∞–љ–∞ћБ–ї—Ц–Ј** вАФ the sound analysis method that Ukrainian children use to read any unfamiliar word. It works in four steps. First, find all the vowels вАФ they are the cores of each syllable. Second, split the word at syllable boundaries. Ukrainian follows the **—Б–Ї–ї–∞–і–Њ–њ–ЊћБ–і—Ц–ї** (open-syllable principle), which means consonants prefer to start new syllables rather than end old ones. Third, sound out each syllable slowly and clearly. Fourth, blend the syllables together at natural speed until the word flows. Walk through it with **–∞–њ—В–µћБ–Ї–∞**: the vowels are **–Р**, **–Х**, **–Р** вАФ three syllables. Split: **–∞-–њ—В–µ-–Ї–∞**. Sound each syllable, then blend: **–∞–њ—В–µ–Ї–∞**. A pharmacy вАФ and you just read it.

Practice the method on progressively harder words. **–Ю—Б–∞ћБ** вАФ two vowels, **–Ю** and **–Р** вАФ two syllables: **–Њ-—Б–∞**. **–®–Ї–ЊћБ–ї–∞** вАФ vowels **–Ю**, **–Р** вАФ two syllables: **—И–Ї–Њ-–ї–∞**. **–Ь–Њ–ї–Њ–Ї–Њ** вАФ three –Ю's, three syllables. Now try **—Г–љ—Ц–≤–µ—А—Б–Є—В–µћБ—В** вАФ five vowels: **–£**, **–Ж**, **–Х**, **–Ш**, **–Х** вАФ five syllables: **—Г-–љ—Ц-–≤–µ—А-—Б–Є-—В–µ—В**. Single-vowel words like **–ї—Ц—Б**, **–і—Г–±**, and **—Е–ї—Ц–±** are one syllable each вАФ no splitting needed, just read them whole. This is exactly how –С–Њ–ї—М—И–∞–Ї–Њ–≤–∞'s classroom exercise works: *¬Ђ–Т–ЄћБ–Ј–љ–∞—З, —Б–Ї—Ц–ї—М–Ї–Є —Б–Ї–ї–∞–і—Ц–≤ —Г –Ї–ЊћБ–ґ–љ–Њ–Љ—Г —Б–ї–Њ–≤—Ц¬ї* вАФ determine how many syllables each word has. You are now doing what Ukrainian first-graders do.

## –У–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ—ЦћБ –ї—ЦћБ—В–µ—А–Є (Vowel Letters)

You learned in M01 that Ukrainian has 6 vowel sounds but 10 vowel letters. Now it is time to meet each one individually. The six simple vowels each produce one consistent sound вАФ no surprises, no variations. **–Р** sounds like the "a" in "father" (**–Љ–∞–Љ–∞**). **–Ю** is a pure, rounded "o" (**–Љ–Њ–ї–Њ–Ї–Њ**). **–£** sounds like the "oo" in "moon" (**–≤—ГћБ–ї–Є—Ж—П**). **–Х** is an open "e" as in "bed" (**–і–µ–љ—М**). **–Ш** is lower and more central, without an exact English match (**–Ї–Є—В**). **–Ж** is higher and fronter, closer to "ee" in "see" (**–Ї—Ц—В**). Each letter maps to exactly one sound, every time. These six are the syllable-building sounds you just learned to count.

Now meet the iotated vowels вАФ the four letters that can represent two sounds at once. **–ѓ** equals [–є] + [–∞] at the start of a word (**—ПћБ–±–ї—Г–Ї–Њ** вАФ apple) or after another vowel (**–Љ–Њ—ПћБ**). But after a consonant, **–ѓ** softens that consonant and adds [–∞]: in **–њ—ЦћБ—Б–љ—П** (song), the **–Э** before **–ѓ** becomes soft. **–Ѓ** works the same way: [–є] + [—Г] at a word start (**—О–љ–∞ћБ–Ї** вАФ young man) or after a vowel; after a consonant, it softens + [—Г] (**–ї—О–±–ї—ОћБ**). **–Д** follows the pattern too: [–є] + [–µ] at word start (**—Ф–љ–ЊћБ—В** вАФ raccoon) or after a vowel (**—Б–ЄћБ–љ—Ф**); after a consonant, softening + [–µ]. Then there is **–З** вАФ the uniquely Ukrainian letter. **–З** always represents [–є] + [—Ц], without exception. It never softens a consonant. It appears only at a word start (**—Ч–ґ–∞ћБ–Ї** вАФ hedgehog), after a vowel (**–Љ–Њ—ЧћБ**), or after an apostrophe (**–Ј'—Ч–≤**). No other Slavic language has **–З**.

Why does precision with vowels matter so much? Because one vowel letter can change the entire meaning of a word. Consider these critical minimal pairs: **–Ї–Є—В** (whale) vs **–Ї—Ц—В** (cat), **–і–Є–Љ** (smoke) vs **–і—Ц–Љ** (house), **–ї–Є—Б** (fox) vs **–ї—Ц—Б** (forest), **–±–Є–Ї** (bull) vs **–±—Ц–Ї** (side). The **–Ш** vs **–Ж** distinction does not exist in English вАФ both sound "ee"-adjacent, but they differ in tongue position. **–Ш** is lower and more central; **–Ж** is higher and fronter. Listen to Anna's pronunciation videos for audio examples of each pair. Also compare **–±–ЄћБ—В–Є** (to hit) vs **–±—ЦћБ—В–Є** (beats) вАФ the vowel alone changes the meaning. This is why reading each vowel carefully matters from day one.

## –І–Є—В–∞ћБ–љ–љ—П —Б–ї—Ц–≤ (Reading Words)

The single most important reading habit to build right now: never read letter-by-letter вАФ read syllable-by-syllable. Apply the method from Section 1 to real words immediately. Take **–Ї–љ–Є–≥–∞**: find the vowels **–Ш** and **–Р** вАФ two syllables. Split: **–Ї–љ–Є-–≥–∞**. Blend: **–Ї–љ–Є–≥–∞** вАФ a book. Now **—И–Ї–Њ–ї–∞**: vowels **–Ю** and **–Р** вАФ split **—И–Ї–Њ-–ї–∞** вАФ blend **—И–Ї–Њ–ї–∞** вАФ a school. Try **—Б—В–Њ–ї–ЄћБ—Ж—П**: vowels **–Ю**, **–Ш**, **–Р** вАФ three syllables, **—Б—В–Њ-–ї–Є-—Ж—П**. **–Ъ–ЄћБ—Ч–≤** вАФ **—Б—В–Њ–ї–Є—Ж—П**. The faster your eye spots vowels, the faster you read. And here is welcome news: Ukrainian spelling is highly regular. Once you know the letters, reading is predictable. There are no silent letters, no surprise pronunciations. Unlike English, where "though," "through," and "thought" all sound different, Ukrainian letters behave consistently.

Building reading fluency depends on recognizing common word patterns. Start with the easiest: CVCV words (consonant-vowel-consonant-vowel), where every syllable ends in a vowel. These are open syllables, and they flow naturally: **–Љ–∞–Љ–∞**, **—В–∞ћБ—В–Њ**, **–Ї–∞—И–∞**, **–≤–Њ–і–∞ћБ**, **—А—Г–Ї–∞ћБ**, **—Е–∞ћБ—В–∞**, **–Ї–Њ–Ј–∞ћБ**, **–љ–Њ–≥–∞ћБ**. Read each one вАФ they practically read themselves. Next, CVCCV words with a consonant cluster: **—И–Ї–Њ–ї–∞**, **–Ї–љ–Є–≥–∞**, **–њ–∞ћБ—А—В–∞** вАФ the cluster stays together at the start of the second syllable. Then CVC words вАФ closed, single-syllable: **–і—Ц–Љ**, **—Б–Њ–љ**, **–ї—Ц—Б**, **–і—Г–±**, **—Е–ї—Ц–±**, **–±–∞–љ–Ї**. No splitting needed at all. Practice reading each group aloud, starting slow, then faster. The more patterns your eye recognizes, the less you need to consciously split syllables.

Three special letter combinations appear in many common words. Full treatment comes in M03, but here is a preview. **–©** always sounds like [—И—З] вАФ as in **—Й–Њ** (what), **—Й–µ** (more), **—Й–∞ћБ—Б—В—П** (happiness). Do not try to pronounce it as a single sound; it is always the two-sound combination [—И—З]. **–ђ** (the soft sign) has no sound of its own вАФ it softens the consonant that comes before it: **–і–µ–љ—М** вАФ the **–Э** becomes soft; **—Б—Ц–ї—М** вАФ the **–Ы** becomes soft; **–Ї—Ц–љ—М** вАФ again, a soft **–Э**. Then the apostrophe (**'**) separates a consonant from an iotated vowel, preserving the [–є] sound: **—Б—Ц–Љ'—ПћБ** (family) вАФ without the apostrophe, the **–ѓ** would soften **–Ь** instead of keeping its full [–є–∞] sound. Same with **–Љ'—ПћБ—Б–Њ** (meat) and **–њ'—П—В—М** (five). Think of the apostrophe as a wall вАФ the consonant stays hard on one side, and the vowel keeps both of its sounds on the other.

## –І–Є—В–∞ћБ—Ф–Љ–Њ —А–∞ћБ–Ј–Њ–Љ (Reading Together)

Time to put everything together with a progressive reading ladder вАФ from simple to ambitious. **Level 1** (2 syllables): **–Љ–∞–Љ–∞**, **—В–∞—В–Њ**, **–≤–Њ–і–∞**, **—А—Г–Ї–∞**, **—Е–∞—В–∞**, **–Ї–∞—И–∞**. All CVCV, all open syllables вАФ the gentlest entry point. **Level 2** (3 syllables): **–∞–њ—В–µ–Ї–∞**, **–Љ–Њ–ї–Њ–Ї–Њ**, **–ї—О–і–Є–љ–∞**, **–≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—П**. Longer, but every word follows the same regular patterns. **Level 3** (4+ syllables): **—Г–љ—Ц–≤–µ—А—Б–Є—В–µ—В**, **–±—Ц–±–ї—Ц–Њ—В–µ–Ї–∞**, **—Д–Њ—В–Њ–≥—А–∞ћБ—Д—Ц—П**, **—И–Њ–Ї–Њ–ї–∞–і**. These look intimidating at first glance, but they follow the same rules as every word before them. Find the vowels, split into syllables, sound out, blend. Try reading each word three times: once slowly, once at medium speed, once at natural conversational pace.

Now read a connected text вАФ all using the **–¶–µ** + noun pattern, no conjugated verbs required yet.

> **–¶–µ –Ъ–Є—Ч–≤. –¶–µ —Б—В–Њ–ї–Є—Ж—П. –Ґ—Г—В –∞–њ—В–µ–Ї–∞ —Ц –±–∞–љ–Ї. –Ґ–∞–Љ —И–Ї–Њ–ї–∞. –©–Њ —Ж–µ? –¶–µ –Ї–∞—Д–µћБ. –Р —Ж–µ –њ–ЊћБ—И—В–∞. –Ю—Б—М –±—Ц–±–ї—Ц–Њ—В–µ–Ї–∞. –Ґ–∞–Љ –Ї–љ–ЄћБ–≥–Є.**

Every word in this text uses letters and patterns from M01 and this module. Read it once slowly, syllable by syllable, then again at natural speed. Notice how Ukrainian sentences are short and direct вАФ noun after noun, no articles. There is no "a" or "the" in Ukrainian. **–¶–µ –Ї–∞—Д–µ** means "This is a caf√©" вАФ three words instead of four. If you can read this entire paragraph aloud without stopping to decode any word, you are reading Ukrainian. Not letter-by-letter, not guessing вАФ actually reading.

## –Я—Ц–і—Б—Г–Љ–Њ–Ї вАФ Summary

Four key skills now belong to you. First, **syllable counting** вАФ **¬Ђ–£ —Б–ї–Њ–≤—Ц —Б—В—Ц–ї—М–Ї–Є —Б–Ї–ї–∞–і—Ц–≤, —Б–Ї—Ц–ї—М–Ї–Є –≥–Њ–ї–Њ—Б–љ–Є—Е –Ј–≤—Г–Ї—Ц–≤¬ї** вАФ a rule that works for every Ukrainian word without exception. Count the vowels and you know the syllables. Second, the **vowel letter system** вАФ 6 simple vowels (**–Р**, **–Ю**, **–£**, **–Х**, **–Ш**, **–Ж**), each making one consistent sound; 4 iotated vowels (**–ѓ**, **–Ѓ**, **–Д**, **–З**), which can produce two sounds or soften a preceding consonant. **–З** always stays [–є—Ц] вАФ uniquely Ukrainian, never softening. Third, a **reading strategy** that works on any word: find vowels вЖТ split into syllables вЖТ sound out вЖТ blend. Never read letter-by-letter. Fourth, a **preview of special signs** вАФ **–©** always equals [—И—З], **–ђ** softens the consonant before it, and the apostrophe separates consonant from iotated vowel. These will be explored fully in M03.

Self-check: read **–±—Ц–±–ї—Ц–Њ—В–µ–Ї–∞** aloud. How many syllables? Five: **–±—Ц-–±–ї—Ц-–Њ-—В–µ-–Ї–∞**. Now spot the vowels in **—Г–љ—Ц–≤–µ—А—Б–Є—В–µ—В**: **–£**, **–Ж**, **–Х**, **–Ш**, **–Х** вАФ five vowels, five syllables. If you can do this quickly and confidently, you are ready for the next module: **Special Signs** вАФ where **–ђ**, the apostrophe, and the full consonant system take center stage.
