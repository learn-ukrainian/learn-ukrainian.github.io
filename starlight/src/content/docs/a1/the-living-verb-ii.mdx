> **Чому це важливо?** (Why is this important?)
>
> Verbs are the engine of any sentence. While Class I verbs allowed you to begin describing actions, Class II verbs unlock the core of human interaction. By mastering these patterns, you gain the ability to express needs, discuss daily routines, and participate in the foundational rituals of Ukrainian hospitality.

## Вступ: Тріада гостинності

Imagine entering a Ukrainian home. The first thing that happens is a chair being pulled out for you. Next, food appears on the table. Then, the tea or coffee is poured. Finally, the deep conversation begins. In Ukrainian culture, connection is not just about sharing the same physical space. True connection is forged actively through feeding and speaking.

We call this the **Triad of Hospitality**. The verbs that power this triad are **ї́сти** (to eat), **пи́ти** (to drink), and **говори́ти** (to speak). Without these three actions, social interaction in Ukraine is incomplete. They form the core of how people relate to one another. The moment you cross the threshold, this triad is activated. The host will invite you to sit, and then immediately assess whether you need to eat or drink. Only after these physical needs are addressed does the conversation truly begin. This sequence is almost ritualistic.

**Ситуація:** (Situation)
Ми вдома. Це наш дім. Я бачу тебе. Ти бачиш мене. Ми сидимо разом. Я говорю. Ти говориш. Ми робимо чай. Я п'ю чай. Ти п'єш чай. Ми п'ємо чай разом. Це традиція. Це наша культура. Ми завжди так робимо. Ти любиш чай? Так, я люблю чай. Я дуже люблю гарячий чай. А ти любиш каву? Ні, я не люблю каву. Я люблю тільки чай. Ми сидимо, ми п'ємо чай, і ми говоримо. Ми говоримо довго.
(We are at home. This is our home. I see you. You see me. We sit together. I speak. You speak. We make tea. I drink tea. You drink tea. We drink tea together. This is a tradition. This is our culture.

**Маленька історія: Ранок**
Це ранок. Я не сплю. Я сиджу. Я роблю каву. Я п'ю каву. Кава гаряча. Кава дуже смачна. Я дивлюся у вікно. Я бачу місто. Я бачу дерева. Я бачу парк. Люди ходять у парк. Вони не сплять. Вони теж роблять каву. Ми всі робимо каву. Це нормальний ранок. Ми живемо і ми працюємо. Я люблю ранок. Я завжди люблю цей час.
 We always do this. Do you love tea? Yes, I love tea. I really love hot tea. And do you love coffee? No, I do not love coffee. I only love tea. We sit, we drink tea, and we talk. We talk for a long time.)

To master these actions and participate in these cultural rituals, we need to understand the second major pillar of Ukrainian verbal action. In previous lessons, we learned about Class I verbs. Those verbs typically end in **-ати** in their dictionary form, like **читати** (to read) or **знати** (to know). When we conjugate Class I verbs, we rely on the vowels **-е-** or **-є-** in the endings to connect the stem to the pronoun.

Now, we introduce Class II verbs. These verbs typically end in **-ити** or **-іти** in their dictionary form. They are the second major family of verbs in the Ukrainian language. When we conjugate Class II verbs, the dominant vowels in their endings are **-и-** or **-і-**. This simple shift in the connective vowel is the primary mechanical difference between the two conjugation classes. By mastering Class II, you unlock thousands of new verbs, effectively doubling your ability to describe the world around you.

Before we look at the specific endings, let's establish what these verbs actually do. In the present tense, Ukrainian verbs focus on the *process* of an action, not its completion. The present tense describes ongoing and habitual meanings. When you say you are speaking or doing something, you are focusing on the activity itself as it unfolds in time.

:::tip[🔍 Pattern Discovery]
**Процес, а не результат.** (Process, not result.)
The Ukrainian present tense focuses on the action happening right now, or happening regularly as a habit. It does not focus on finishing the action. It describes the flow of an event.
:::

This difference between "doing" and "completing" is a crucial grammatical concept. English has multiple present tenses to capture different nuances: 'I speak', 'I am speaking', 'I do speak'. Ukrainian simplifies this conceptually. The single present tense form covers all of these English meanings. Whether you are speaking right now in this exact second, or you speak generally as a lifelong habit, you use the same verb form. The context of the conversation dictates the exact nuance. The verbs we will learn in this lesson describe actions that fundamentally take time. Eating takes time. Speaking takes time. Doing your homework takes time. We use the imperfective aspect to describe these unfolding processes.

Let's look at a short interaction to see these verbs in action.

**Діалог:**
<div className="conversation">

— Що ти робиш?

— Я сиджу і п'ю каву. А ти?

— А я роблю домашнє завдання.

</div>

(— What are you doing?)
(— I am sitting and drinking coffee. And you?)
(— And I am doing homework.)

In just three short lines of casual conversation, we see four essential verbs from this lesson. Let's break down exactly how they work, how their stems change, and how you can use them to accurately describe your own daily life.

## Презентація: Моделі та мутації

In this section, we will systematically break down how Class II verbs behave. They follow highly reliable patterns, but they also possess some physical phonetic quirks that actually make them easier to pronounce once you understand the mechanics behind them.

### Порівняння моделей: Клас I та Клас II

To truly understand Class II verbs, it is incredibly helpful to see them side-by-side with the Class I verbs you already know. Let's compare the verb **читати** (Class I) with the verb **говори́ти** (Class II). The critical difference lies in the vowels used for the endings. Class I relies heavily on **е/є**, while Class II relies exclusively on **и/і**.

Here is the complete paradigm for a regular Class II verb. Notice how the vowel **-и-** dominates almost every single form, acting as the glue between the stem and the personal ending, except for the "I" and "They" forms.

**Таблиця: Дієвідміна**
- **Я** (I) — говор**ю**
- **Ти** (You, singular) — говор**иш**
- **Він/Вона/Воно** (He/She/It) — говор**ить**
- **Ми** (We) — говор**имо**
- **Ви** (You, plural/formal) — говор**ите**
- **Вони** (They) — говор**ять**

Notice that the first person singular (Я) takes the ending **-ю**, and the third person plural (Вони) takes the ending **-ять**. For every other pronoun in the paradigm, the ending starts with **-и-**. This consistency makes Class II verbs very predictable.

The verb **говорити** (to speak) is not just a grammatical example; it is the cornerstone of human connection. In Ukrainian, there are multiple verbs for talking, such as «розмовляти» (to converse) or «казати» (to say). However, **говорити** serves as the broad, foundational verb for the physical act of producing speech and conveying information.

**Приклади:**
1. Я завжди говорю правду. (I always speak the truth.)
2. Ми говоримо українською мовою. (We speak the Ukrainian language.)
3. Вони тихо говорять. (They speak quietly.)
4. Він говорить дуже швидко. (He speaks very fast.)

This same exact pattern applies to many other high-frequency verbs, such as **вчи́ти** (to teach/learn).

**Діалог:** (Dialogue)
<div className="conversation">

— Привіт. Що ти робиш зараз? (Hello. What are you doing now?)

— Я роблю домашнє завдання. Я вчу нові слова. А ти? (I am doing homework. I am learning new words. And you?)

— Я не роблю нічого. Я відпочиваю. Я дивлюся у вікно. (I am not doing anything. I am resting. I am looking out the window.)

— Ти бачиш там парк? (Do you see the park there?)

— Так, я бачу парк. Я бачу дерева. (Yes, I see the park. I see trees.)

— Ти часто ходиш у цей парк? (Do you often walk to this park?)

— Так, я ходжу туди кожного дня. Я дуже люблю цей парк. (Yes, I walk there every day. I really love this park.)

— Ми теж любимо гуляти там. Ми ходимо туди разом. (We also love to walk there. We walk there together.)

— Це чудово. Ви платите за каву? (That is great. Are you paying for the coffee?)

— Так, я плачу за каву сьогодні. Ти платиш завтра. (Yes, I pay for the coffee today. You pay tomorrow.)

</div>

**Маленька історія: День**
Це день. Ми не вдома. Ми працюємо. Ти працюєш тут. Він працює там. Вони працюють добре. Я теж працюю. Я роблю роботу. Я говорю багато. Я часто говорю і часто слухаю. Я бачу людей. Люди купують чай. Вони платять за чай. Я теж плачу за чай. Це смачний чай. Ми п'ємо чай вдень. Ми не п'ємо каву вдень. Ми говоримо, ми слухаємо, ми працюємо. Ми дуже любимо день.

### Зміна приголосних: Мутації

Sometimes, adding a **-ю** directly to a consonant is physically awkward for the tongue. To solve this friction, Ukrainian uses consonant mutations in the first person singular (**Я** form). According to the Ukrainian State Standard, mastering these specific mutations is a core competency for A1 learners.

Let's look at the verbs **сиді́ти** (to sit) and **ходи́ти** (to walk). The stems of both verbs end in the dental consonant **-д-**. Saying «сидю» or «ходю» requires your tongue to perform an uncomfortable acrobatic jump against your teeth. To make the word flow naturally and effortlessly, the **-д-** softens and mutates into a smooth **-дж-**.

Crucially, this mutation **only** happens in the **Я** form. All other forms follow the regular pattern without any stem changes.

**Verb: сидіти**
- **Я** си**дж**у
- **Ти** сид**иш**
- **Він** сид**ить**

**Приклади:**
1. Я сиджу на дивані. (I am sitting on the sofa.)
2. Ти сидиш у парку. (You are sitting in the park.)
3. Я часто ходжу до школи. (I often walk to school.)

:::tip[💡 Tip]
**Тільки форма «Я».** (Only the "I" form.)
Remember that consonant mutations in Class II verbs usually only affect the first person singular. The rest of the paradigm remains completely stable and regular. Do not mutate the "He" or "We" forms.
:::

Another common mutation is **-т-** changing to **-ч-**, which happens frequently in the practical verb **плати́ти** (to pay).
- **Я** пла**ч**у — I pay
- **Ти** плат**иш** — You pay

### Губний звук: Лабіальне «Л»

Another very common type of mutation involves lip consonants, known phonetically as labial consonants. These are the sounds **б, п, в, м, ф**.

Let's look at two of the most important and frequently used verbs in the entire language: **роби́ти** (to do) and **люби́ти** (to love). The stems of both verbs end in **-б-**. Try saying «робю» or «любю» quickly. It feels physically constrained because your lips have to snap from a tightly closed "b" position directly into a rounded "yu" sound in a fraction of a second.

To fix this muscular tension, the language elegantly inserts a phonetic bridge: the **Labial L**. The letter **-л-** acts as a comfortable stepping stone between the lip consonant and the vowel.

If we look closer at the mechanics of speech, the Labial L is a brilliant phonetic solution. By inserting the **-л-** — which is produced by touching the tip of the tongue to the roof of the mouth behind the teeth — the mouth is forced to open slightly. This releases the pressure and allows the **-ю** to flow effortlessly. This phonetic bridge demonstrates how languages naturally evolve toward efficiency and physical ease.

**Verb: робити**
- **Я** роб**л**ю
- **Ти** роб**иш**
- **Він** роб**ить**
- **Вони** робл**ять**

*Notice a special rule here: for labial mutations, the **-л-** bridge also appears in the 'They' form (Вони).*

**Приклади:**
1. Я дуже люблю каву. (I really love coffee.)
2. Що ти робиш сьогодні? (What are you doing today?)
3. Вони роблять домашнє завдання. (They are doing homework.)

:::info[ℹ️ Fact]
**Фізичний комфорт.** (Physical comfort.)
The Labial L is not an arbitrary grammatical rule designed to confuse learners. It is a natural, physical bridge that makes the word significantly easier to say out loud.
:::

### Неправильні дієслова: Їсти та пити

Some verbs are so incredibly old and so universally used every single day that they preserve ancient, irregular conjugation patterns. The foundational hospitality verbs **ї́сти** (to eat) and **пи́ти** (to drink) belong exactly to this category. They do not neatly fit into Class I or Class II rules. You simply must memorize their specific forms.

The irregularity of **їсти** extends deep into history. Unlike almost every other verb in the modern language, the first person singular form does not end in a vowel. The form is simply **я їм**. This isolated '-м' ending is a relic of an ancient conjugation class that has otherwise completely disappeared from standard Ukrainian. Furthermore, the second person singular form, **ти їси** (you eat), is equally distinctive, dispensing completely with the standard '-ш' ending. Because these words are spoken constantly, they have resisted the grammatical leveling that standardizes newer words.

**Verb: їсти**
- **Я** їм
- **Ти** їси
- **Він/Вона/Воно** їсть
- **Ми** їмо
- **Ви** їсте
- **Вони** їдять

**Verb: пити**
- **Я** п'ю
- **Ти** п'єш
- **Він/Вона/Воно** п'є
- **Ми** п'ємо
- **Ви** п'єте
- **Вони** п'ють

**Приклади:**
1. Я їм борщ. (I am eating borsch.)
2. Вона п'є воду. (She is drinking water.)
3. Ми смачно їмо. (We are eating deliciously.)

### Семантична різниця: Бачити чи дивитися

A common challenge for language learners is distinguishing between verbs of visual perception. In English, we have "to see" and "to watch" or "to look at". Ukrainian makes a very similar, strict distinction between the Class II verbs **ба́чити** and **дивитися**.

**Бачити** describes the biological faculty of sight or the result of visual perception. It means "to see". You use it when light enters your eyes and you mentally register an image, often without any deliberate, planned effort. It is an instantaneous result.

**Дивитися** describes a deliberate, intentional, ongoing process. It means "to watch" or "to look at". It requires active attention over a period of time.

Let's explore this distinction through a real-world scenario. Imagine you are sitting in a quiet cafe. You are looking out the large window, observing the cars driving past. In this scenario, you are engaged in the prolonged process of watching. Therefore, you would say «Я дивлюся у вікно» (I am looking out the window). Notice the Labial L mutation here: див**л**юся.

Suddenly, across the busy street, you notice a close friend you haven't seen in years. The act of noticing them, the sudden registration of their presence, is not a deliberate process; it is an immediate result. In that exact moment, you would say «Я бачу друга» (I see a friend).

You can «дивитися» without «бачити». For example, if you stare into a pitch-black room, you might say: «Я дивлюся в темряву, але нічого не бачу» (I am looking into the darkness, but I see nothing). Grasping this distinction early prevents confusion and adds immense precision to your descriptions.

## Практика: Помилки та автоматизація

Now that we have comprehensively explored the rules, mutations, and exceptions, we need to focus heavily on building correct speaking habits. The cognitive transition from Class I to Class II verbs often causes friction for learners. Let's look at the most common learner errors and discuss how to automate the correct patterns in your brain.

### Змішування дієвідмін

The absolute most common mistake early learners make is **Conjugation Mixing**. Because Class I verbs (-ати) are usually taught first, the human brain conveniently treats the **-е/-є** endings as the default setting for all verbs. When you encounter a Class II verb like **робити**, your brain might automatically reach for the familiar, deeply ingrained ending and produce the incorrect form «ти робеш».

This is a totally natural neurological shortcut, but it is grammatically incorrect. We must actively train the brain to recognize **-ити** stems and immediately trigger the secondary **-и/-і** vowel pathway.

**Аналіз помилок:**
- ❌ Неправильно: Ти робеш. (Incorrect: You do.)
- ✅ Правильно: Ти роб**и**ш. (Correct: You do.)
- ❌ Неправильно: Він говоре. (Incorrect: He speaks.)
- ✅ Правильно: Він говор**и**ть. (Correct: He speaks.)

To fix this ingrained habit, practice contrastive drills. Pair a Class I verb with a Class II verb and say them out loud back-to-back, physically exaggerating the vowel difference with your voice.

**Дриль:**
1. Ти чита**є**ш, але ти роб**и**ш. (You read, but you do.)
2. Вона зна**є**, але вона говор**и**ть. (She knows, but she speaks.)
3. Ми гуля**є**мо, але ми сид**и**мо. (We walk, but we sit.)

### Майстерність мутацій: Пастка форми «Я»

The second major hurdle for learners is forgetting the consonant mutations in the first person singular. Learners often drop the mutation entirely, creating words that are physically hard to say and grammatically incorrect, such as «я ходю» or «я сидю».

This stubborn error, which we call the **'Я'-form trap**, happens because the learner logically tries to apply the standard **-ю** ending to the raw stem without successfully softening the preceding consonant.

Let's look at common errors:
- ❌ Неправильно: Я ходю. -> ✅ Правильно: Я хо**дж**у.
- ❌ Неправильно: Я сидю. -> ✅ Правильно: Я си**дж**у.

To permanently eliminate omission errors like this, we must deliberately isolate the first person singular and drill it intensively in realistic contexts.

**Міні-діалоги для практики:**
<div className="conversation">

— Де ти? (Where are you?)

— Я си**дж**у вдома. (I am sitting at home.)

</div>

<div className="conversation">

— Що ти робиш? (What are you doing?)

— Я пла**ч**у за каву. (I am paying for coffee.)

</div>

:::warning[⚠️ Warning]
**Уникайте гіперкорекції.** (Avoid hypercorrection.)
When learning irregular verbs like **їсти**, eager learners sometimes try to force a regular Class II mutation, resulting in the highly incorrect form «я їджу» or «я їстю». Remember that **їсти** is completely irregular: the correct form is simply **я їм**. Do not try to make it fit the rules.
:::

### Розпізнавання голосних

To build reliable structural intuition, you need to be able to look at a new dictionary infinitive and instantly predict its vowel pattern. You can categorize most verbs just by looking at their final letters.

If the dictionary form ends in **-ати** (читати, знати), your brain should prepare to use the **-е/-є** endings.
If the dictionary form ends in **-ити** (говорити, робити) or **-іти** (сидіти), your brain should prepare to use the **-и/-і** endings.

**Сортування:**
1. **проси́ти** (to ask) -> ends in -ити -> Class II. (Я прошу, ти просиш)
2. **стоя́ти** (to stand) -> Class II exception. (Я стою, ти стоїш, вони стоять)
3. **бачити** (to see) -> ends in -ити -> Class II. (Я бачу, ти бачиш)

By mentally sorting verbs into columns before you speak, you give your brain a crucial millisecond to load the correct conjugation pathway. This drastically reduces hesitation and errors in spontaneous speech.

**Читання:** (Reading)
Я сиджу вдома. Це мій старий диван. Я сиджу тут часто. Я люблю сидіти тут і читати. Я читаю цікаву книгу. Я дуже люблю читати. Мій брат не любить читати. Він любить дивитися кіно. Він зараз дивиться нове кіно. Він сидить на стільці. Він сидить і дивиться. Ми різні. Але ми живемо разом і ми любимо один одного. Я роблю чай. Я роблю гарячий чай для нас. Він любить пити чай. Я теж люблю пити чай. Ми п'ємо чай разом. Це наша щоденна рутина. Ми сидимо, п'ємо чай і говоримо.
(I sit at home. This is my old sofa. I sit here often. I love to sit here and read. I am reading an interesting book. I really love to read. My brother does not love to read. He loves to watch movies. He is watching a new movie now. He sits on a chair. He sits and watches. We are different. But we live together and we love each other. I make tea. I make hot tea for us. He loves to drink tea. I also love to drink tea. We drink tea together. This is our daily routine. We sit, drink tea and talk.)

**Маленька історія: Вечір**
Це вечір. Я вже вдома. Ти теж вдома. Ми сидимо. Ми відпочиваємо. Ми нічого не робимо. Ми просто сидимо і ми говоримо. Ми говоримо довго. Ти бачиш нове кіно. Ти любиш старе кіно, але сьогодні ти дивишся нове кіно. Я не дивлюся кіно. Я читаю. Я завжди читаю ввечері. Ми їмо вечерю. Ми їмо борщ. Борщ дуже смачний. Ми їмо і ми говоримо. Вони також їдять. Усі люди їдять ввечері. Усі люди відпочивають ввечері. Це добрий вечір.

## Продукування та культурний контекст

Grammar rules are only a theoretical framework; language truly comes alive through meaningful context. Let's use our newly acquired Class II verbs to build complex sentences about daily routines and explore the deep cultural concepts embedded within them.

### Щоденна рутина: Сполучення слів

Instead of memorizing isolated verbs floating in a vacuum, focus your energy on learning high-frequency collocations. A collocation is a group of words that naturally and frequently belong together. By practicing complete chunks of meaning, you improve your overall fluency and sound significantly more natural to native speakers.

**Корисні сполучення:**
- **робити домашнє завдання** (to do homework)
- **робити покупки** (to do shopping)
- **говорити українською** (to speak Ukrainian)
- **говорити правду** (to speak the truth)
- **просити допомоги** (to ask for help)
- **любити природу** (to love nature)

Let's combine these foundational collocations into a short narrative describing a typical daily routine.

**Текст:**
Кожного дня я роблю домашнє завдання. Я сиджу і вчу мову. Я люблю читати книги. Ввечері ми сидимо разом, їмо і говоримо українською. Я завжди говорю правду.

### Навчання та розвиток

As a dedicated language learner, the verb **вчи́ти** (to teach/learn) is highly relevant to your daily experience. It is a completely regular Class II verb that allows you to easily describe your educational process.

Notice that the stem of this verb ends in **-ч-**. Because this sound is already naturally soft, the first person singular does not need a complex mutation; it simply takes the ending **-у**.

**Verb: вчити**
- **Я** вчу — I learn
- **Ти** вчиш — You learn
- **Він** вчить — He learns
- **Вони** вчать — They learn

**Корисні фрази:**
1. Я вчу українську мову. (I am learning the Ukrainian language.)
2. Ми вчимо нові слова. (We are learning new words.)
3. Вони вчать граматику. (They are learning grammar.)

Using this powerful verb allows you to talk confidently about your progress and engage with native speakers about your genuine efforts to learn their language. It is an excellent icebreaker.

### Етимологія любові: Корінь слова

One of the most profound and frequently used verbs in the Ukrainian language is **любити** (to love). This verb is used universally—you can love a person, you can love drinking coffee, and you can love reading a good book.

However, its ancient origins reveal a deep and beautiful cultural perspective. The root of the word «любити» descends directly from the Proto-Indo-European linguistic root **\*lewdh-**. This is the exact same ancient root that forms the modern Ukrainian word **люди** (people) and the concept of **людство** (humanity).

:::note[🏺 Culture]
**Зв'язок із людством.** (Connection to humanity.)
In the Ukrainian linguistic worldview, love is not an isolated, solitary, or purely abstract emotion. It is intrinsically tied to community, belonging, and the very essence of being human. To love is to be fundamentally connected to the people around you.
:::

When you say «Я люблю тебе» (I love you) or «Я люблю цю країну» (I love this country), you are utilizing a word that fundamentally connects the concept of deep affection with the concept of shared humanity. It is a beautiful linguistic reflection of community values.

### Етика гостинності: Соціальна вага їжі

We began this comprehensive lesson by discussing the Triad of Hospitality: **їсти**, **пити**, and **говорити**. Now that we know how to conjugate them, let's examine the profound social ethics surrounding these actions in real life.

In Ukrainian culture, hosting a guest is a serious social responsibility. Offering food and drink is never merely a polite, perfunctory gesture; it is the primary, tangible method of showing respect, warmth, and genuine acceptance. When a host offers you something to eat, they are quite literally offering a piece of their home and a demonstration of their goodwill.

Consequently, the act of refusing a meal carries significant cultural weight. Simply saying «Я не хочу їсти» (I don't want to eat) when someone has painstakingly prepared a table for you can be deeply perceived as a rejection of the host's hospitality. It creates an immediate, awkward social distance between you and the host.

**Правила поведінки:**
1. Always accept at least a small, symbolic portion of what is offered. You absolutely do not need to eat everything on the table, but participating in the shared physical act of the meal is crucial.
2. Even if you are genuinely not hungry, accepting a simple drink—whether it is tea, coffee, or even just water—allows the social interaction to proceed smoothly without friction.
3. Use the time spent eating and drinking to engage in meaningful conversation (**говорити**). The shared meal is simply the foundation for the personal connection.

:::danger[🛡️ Myth Buster]
**Не тільки ввічливість.** (Not just politeness.)
The intense pressure to eat when visiting a Ukrainian home is often viewed by foreigners as overwhelming or invasive. It is important to understand that this is not aggressive over-politeness. It is an ancient cultural mechanism designed specifically for building trust. Sharing food breaks down emotional barriers.
:::

By mastering the verbs **їсти**, **пити**, and **говорити**, you are doing much more than just learning grammar rules. You are actively acquiring the linguistic tools strictly necessary to navigate the most fundamental, beautiful aspects of Ukrainian social life.
