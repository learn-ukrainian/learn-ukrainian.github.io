## Що ми зна́ємо? (What Do We Know?)

Welcome to the first major checkpoint of your Ukrainian learning journey! You have successfully navigated the foundational phonetic systems, learned to decipher the Cyrillic alphabet, and acquired the essential vocabulary to make your first contact with native speakers. This is a profound moment to pause, reflect, and consolidate everything you have absorbed over the past six modules. You are transitioning from passive recognition to active, confident production.

Before we move forward into new territory, let us ask a few critical self-assessment questions. Can you read any Ukrainian word you see? Think back to your very first lessons. You learned that the Ukrainian alphabet is highly phonetic. Do you remember the golden rule taught to every student in a Ukrainian first-grade classroom? We hear and pronounce **зву́ки** (sounds), but we see and write **лі́тери** (letters). In Ukrainian textbooks, young learners use specific visual symbols to map these abstract sounds to reality: a bullet point `[•]` represents vowels, a single dash `[–]` represents hard consonants, and a double dash `[=]` represents soft consonants. Sometimes, a vertical line `|` is used to divide a word into individual syllables. This structural approach makes reading logical and predictable. 

Do you remember the tricksters of the alphabet? The letter **В** looks like an English "B", but it produces a "V" or "W" sound. The letter **Н** looks like an "H", but it makes an "N" sound. The letter **Р** is a rolled "R", never a "P". And the letter **С** is strictly an "S" sound. Overcoming these visual false friends is a massive milestone in your reading fluency.

Do you know what the soft sign and the apostrophe do? The soft sign, written as **ь**, is not an independent sound. It is a silent modifier that softens the preceding consonant. The apostrophe acts as a hard barrier, preventing a consonant and a vowel from bleeding into one another. Can you place stress correctly? Remember that stress in Ukrainian is dynamic. It must be memorized with each word because it dictates the entire rhythm of the sentence.

Can you introduce yourself? You have learned the conversational formulas to say your name and ask for the names of others. Can you talk about your family? You know the foundational words for your immediate relatives and how to claim them. If you can confidently answer "yes" to these questions, you are ready to combine these skills into real conversations.

## Чита́ння (Reading Practice)

Reading connected text is where your phonetic knowledge meets your growing vocabulary. When you read full sentences, you are not just decoding letters; you are processing meaning in real time. We are going to read a short Ukrainian text consisting of ten simple statements. This text uses only the vocabulary you have already encountered in the previous modules. There are no hidden grammatical structures.

Your task is to read this text aloud. Reading aloud is a mandatory practice because it forces your mouth to articulate the physical sounds of Ukrainian while your brain manages sentence intonation. Do not rush. Take your time with every single word. 

> **Привіт!** (Hi!)
> **Мене звати А́нна.** (My name is Anna.)
> **Моє прі́звище — Кова́ль.** (My surname is Koval.)
> **Я з України.** (I am from Ukraine.)
> **Я — студе́нтка.** (I am a student.)
> **Це моя сім'я́.** (This is my family.)
> **Моя мама — лі́кар.** (My mother is a doctor.)
> **Мій та́то — інжене́р.** (My father is an engineer.)
> **У мене є брат.** (I have a brother.)
> **Йо́го звати Анто́н.** (His name is Anton.)

Let us break down what you just read, analyzing how the language functions. The text begins with a standard informal greeting. Instantly, Anna introduces her first name and her surname. The word **ім'я́** means "first name", and the word **прізвище** means "surname". We highly recommend adding these to your active vocabulary. They are ubiquitous on official forms and in formal introductions.

Next, she states her origin using the standard chunk we practiced, and then she clearly identifies her profession. Notice how seamlessly she transitions to talking about her family. She uses the basic identifying phrase to introduce the group as a whole. Then, she lists the professions of her parents using the zero copula pattern. Finally, she states that she possesses a sibling and gives his name. This sequence is a perfectly natural, perfectly correct Ukrainian introduction. It is simple, clear, and highly effective.

## Грама́тика (Grammar Summary)

To build your own sentences with total confidence, we must review the core grammatical patterns of this phase. You have learned these not as abstract, intimidating rules, but as highly functional tools. Let us summarize them now.

1. The identification pattern: **це** (this is / these are).
We use the word **це** to point to and identify objects and people. It is incredibly versatile because it never changes shape. Whether you are pointing at a masculine house, a feminine mother, or a plural group, the word remains exactly the same.
**Це мама.** (This is mother.)
**Це дім.** (This is a house.)
**Це мій кіт.** (This is my cat.)

2. The zero copula: Subject — Noun.
In the present tense, Ukrainian completely ignores linking verbs like "is" or "am". You might feel a strong urge from your English brain to insert a verb here. Resist that urge. You simply place the subject and the noun together, often separated by a written dash for visual clarity.
**Я — студе́нт.** (I am a student.)
**Мама — лікар.** (Mother is a doctor.)
**Антон — інженер.** (Anton is an engineer.)

3. The possession pattern: **У мене є** (I have).
To say that you possess something, you use this specific structural chunk, which literally translates to "by me there is". Do not try to translate it word for word. Treat it as a single, unbreakable unit of meaning. 
**У мене є брат.** (I have a brother.)
**У мене є сестра́.** (I have a sister.)
**У мене є дім.** (I have a house.)

4. Asking names: **Як тебе звати?** (What is your name? - informal) / **Як вас звати?** (What is your name? - formal).
These are your fixed phrases for asking names. You choose the informal or formal pronoun based on the social context of the interaction.
**Як тебе звати?** (What is your name?)
**Мене звати Марк.** (My name is Mark.)

5. Possessive pronouns: **мій** / **моя** / **моє** (my).
The concept of grammatical gender is crucial here. In Ukrainian, every noun belongs to a gender category. When you claim something as your own, your possessive pronoun must physically change its shape to match the noun it describes.
**Мій брат.** (My brother - masculine.)
**Моя сім'я.** (My family - feminine.)
**Моє мі́сто.** (My city - neuter.)

6. Expressing origin: **Зві́дки ти?** (Where are you from?)
This is the standard chunk for inquiring about origin and providing the answer.
**Звідки ти?** (Where are you from?)
**Я з України.** (I am from Ukraine.)
**Я з Кана́ди.** (I am from Canada.)

## Діало́г (Capstone Dialogue)

Now it is time for the capstone dialogue. This is the ultimate test of your current abilities. It combines absolutely everything you have learned in this phase into a single, cohesive conversation. The setting is universally understood: two people meeting for the very first time in a casual environment.

Read through the dialogue carefully. Observe the full cycle of the social interaction. It moves from the initial greeting, to the exchange of names, asking about origins, discussing professions, introducing family members from a photograph, and finally, bringing the conversation to a polite close.

<div class="dialogue">


**Марк:** Привіт! *(Hi!)*


**Окса́на:** Добрий день! *(Good day!)*


**Марк:** Як тебе звати? *(What is your name?)*


**Оксана:** Мене звати Оксана. А тебе? *(My name is Oksana. And you?)*


**Марк:** Мене звати Марк. *(My name is Mark.)*


**Оксана:** Звідки ти? *(Where are you from?)*


**Марк:** Я з Канади. А ти? *(I am from Canada. And you?)*


**Оксана:** Я з України. *(I am from Ukraine.)*


**Марк:** Я — студент. А ти? *(I am a student. And you?)*


**Оксана:** Я — лікар. *(I am a doctor.)*


**Марк:** Це твоя́ сім'я? *(Is this your family?)*


**Оксана:** Так, це моя сім'я. *(Yes, this is my family.)*


**Марк:** Хто це? *(Who is this?)*


**Оксана:** Це мій брат. *(This is my brother.)*


**Марк:** Ду́же приє́мно! *(Very nice to meet you!)*


**Оксана:** Навза́єм! Бувай! *(Likewise! Bye!)*


**Марк:** До побачення! *(Goodbye!)*


</div>


Notice the polite exchanges embedded within the dialogue. When Mark and Oksana finish their basic introductions, Mark says **Дуже приємно!** (Very nice to meet you!). This is an essential phrase for social harmony. Oksana replies with **Навзаєм!** (Likewise!). These small conversational markers make your Ukrainian sound entirely natural and culturally aware. If you can follow this dialogue, understand the questions being asked, and produce similar answers, you have successfully completed the core communication goals of this phase. 

Now, let us turn this conversational skill into a connected monologue. This is your graduation speech for the A1.1 level. It is a continuous block of text where you introduce yourself fully and proudly, without needing any prompts from a partner. When constructing your monologue, pay attention to the flow of information. You start with the most personal facts, expand outward to your geographic origin, state your social role, and finally introduce your immediate family. 

Here is the master template for your self-introduction monologue:

**Привіт!** (Hi!)
**Мене звати...** (My name is...)
**Моє прізвище...** (My surname is...)
**Я з...** (I am from...)
**Я — ...** (I am a...)
**Моя мама — ...** (My mother is a...)
**Мій тато — ...** (My father is a...)
**У мене є...** (I have a...)

Practice filling in your own specific details. Speak this entire monologue aloud multiple times until it feels entirely natural and effortless. This speech is your personal linguistic passport.

## Підсумок — Summary

We have reached the end of our first major checkpoint. Take a moment to honestly assess your own knowledge. 

Ask yourself these final, defining self-check questions. How many letters and sounds are in the Ukrainian language? Can you clearly articulate the functional difference between a letter and a sound? Can you say hello appropriately in both a formal and an informal setting? Can you introduce yourself confidently using five continuous, connected sentences? Can you name your immediate family members and correctly apply the possessive pronouns based entirely on their grammatical gender?

If you can perform all of these tasks, you have built an incredibly solid, unbreakable foundation. You have mastered the mechanics of reading Cyrillic, internalized the rhythm of basic speech, and grasped the fundamental patterns of identification and possession. You are no longer just looking at strange characters on a screen; you are reading, thinking, and speaking in Ukrainian.
