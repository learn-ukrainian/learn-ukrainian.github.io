## Діало́ги (Dialogues)

We express desires, abilities, and obligations every single day. Whether you are making weekend plans with a friend, navigating a menu at a local café, or explaining why you cannot attend an event, you need a specific set of tools. In Ukrainian, these tools are modal verbs.

Modal verbs are special action words that express a speaker's attitude toward an action. They tell us if someone wants to do something, has the ability to do it, or is required to do it. Before we look at the grammar rules, let us observe how native speakers use these verbs in everyday conversations. Pay close attention to the bolded words.

Our first situation involves two friends trying to coordinate their schedules. Notice how quickly the conversation shifts from a desire to an obligation.

<div class="dialogue">


**Олег:** Приві́т! Що ти **хочеш** роби́ти? *(Hi! What do you want to do?)*


**Марія:** Я **хочу** гуля́ти. А ти? *(I want to walk. And you?)*


**Олег:** Я не **можу**, я **мушу** працюва́ти. *(I cannot, I must work.)*


**Марія:** **Шкода**! *(Pity!)*


</div>


In just four lines, we see a complete narrative: a question about a desire, a statement of that desire, a lack of ability, and a hard obligation. The word **шкода** (pity / unfortunately) is a highly natural response when plans fall through.

Now, let us look at a different scenario. You are at a restaurant, and you need to communicate your needs to the waiter. This dialogue gives you a preview of how you will order food.

<div class="dialogue">


**Офіціант:** До́брий день! *(Good day!)*


**Клієнт:** Я **хочу** **каву**. *(I want coffee.)*


**Офіціант:** Велику чи мале́ньку? *(Large or small?)*


**Клієнт:** Велику. І ще я **хочу** **їсти**. Що ви **можете** **порекомендувати**? *(Large. And also I want to eat. What can you recommend?)*


**Офіціант:** **Можу** **порекомендувати** **борщ**! *(I can recommend borscht!)*


</div>


In this exchange, the customer uses the same verb to express a desire for an object (**каву**, coffee) and a desire to perform an action (**їсти**, to eat). The customer also asks about the waiter's ability to suggest a good dish using **порекомендувати** (to recommend), leading to the classic suggestion of **борщ** (borscht).

These conversations highlight the three core verbs we will master in this module: **хотіти** (to want), **могти** (to be able to / can), and **мусити** (to must / have to).

## Хотіти (To Want)

The verb **хотіти** (to want) is arguably the most common modal verb you will use. It is the direct way to express a desire. While polite requests later in your learning journey will use a conditional form (like "I would like"), right now, stating exactly what you want is perfectly acceptable and widely used.

There is a slight grammatical catch with this verb. If you look at the infinitive ending **-іти**, you might logically assume that it belongs to Group II (the "-ити / -іти" verbs). However, **хотіти** is a famous exception. It belongs strictly to Group I. 

Furthermore, **хотіти** features a consonant mutation. The root consonant **т** changes to **ч** in all present tense forms. You drop the **-іти**, change the **т** to **ч**, and add the standard Group I endings.

| Pronoun | Form | English |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Я | **хочу** | I want |
| Ти | **хочеш** | You want (informal) |
| Він / Вона́ / Воно́ | **хоче** | He / She / It wants |
| Ми | **хочемо** | We want |
| Ви | **хочете** | You want (formal / plural) |
| Вони́ | **хочуть** | They want |

This mutation (**т** → **ч**) makes the verb much smoother to pronounce. 

There are two primary ways to use **хотіти** in a sentence. The first way is to pair it with an infinitive verb. When you want *to do* something, you conjugate **хотіти** to match the subject and leave the second action verb in its dictionary form.

*   Я **хочу** чита́ти. — I want to read.
*   Він **хоче** працювати. — He wants to work.
*   Вони **хочуть** гуляти. — They want to walk.

The second way is to pair it directly with a noun. When you want *a thing*, you conjugate **хотіти** and place the object right after it. (Note that the object will often take the accusative case, which you will learn systematically later, but for now, observe the pattern).

*   Я **хочу** **каву**. — I want coffee.
*   Вона **хоче** чай. — She wants tea.
*   Ми **хочемо** хліб. — We want bread.

To make a negative statement, simply place the negative particle **не** directly before the conjugated verb.

*   Я не **хочу**. — I do not want to.
*   Ти не **хочеш**? — Do you not want to?
*   Вона не **хоче**. — She does not want to.

## Могти і мусити (Can and Must)

Now that we know how to express desires, we need to talk about abilities and obligations. Life is rarely just about what we want to do; it is heavily dictated by what we are capable of doing and what we are forced to do. 

The verb **могти** means "to be able to" or "can". It describes physical ability, availability, or permission. Like our previous verb, **могти** is irregular and belongs to Group I. It also features a consonant mutation to make pronunciation easier. In this case, the root consonant **г** changes to **ж** in all present tense forms. 

| Pronoun | Form | English |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Я | **можу** | I can |
| Ти | **можеш** | You can (informal) |
| Він / Вона / Воно | **може** | He / She / It can |
| Ми | **можемо** | We can |
| Ви | **можете** | You can (formal / plural) |
| Вони | **можуть** | They can |

Just like with desires, you pair the conjugated form of **могти** with the infinitive of the main action. 

*   Я **можу** говори́ти украї́нською. — I can speak Ukrainian.
*   Ти **можеш** **допомогти**? — Can you help?
*   Вони не **можуть** читати. — They cannot read.

On the other end of the spectrum is **мусити**, which translates to "must" or "have to". You use this verb when there is a strong obligation, duty, or unavoidable necessity. It implies that you do not have a choice in the matter. This is much stronger and more personal than the impersonal word **треба** (need to), which you will study in detail in a future module.

Grammatically, **мусити** is much better behaved. It is a regular Group II verb. It has one small quirk: a consonant mutation (**с** → **ш**) that only happens in the "я" (I) form. For all other pronouns, the root remains exactly as it is in the dictionary.

| Pronoun | Form | English |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Я | **мушу** | I must |
| Ти | **мусиш** | You must (informal) |
| Він / Вона / Воно | **мусить** | He / She / It must |
| Ми | **мусимо** | We must |
| Ви | **мусите** | You must (formal / plural) |
| Вони | **мусять** | They must |

Here are examples of strong obligations in action:

*   Я **мушу** працювати. — I must work.
*   Ти **мусиш** вчи́ти слова́. — You must study words.
*   Ми **мусимо** йти. — We must go.

## Підсумок — Summary

You now have a powerful trio of verbs at your disposal. These three verbs form the foundation of expressing personal intent and limits. Let us review the structural formula that unites them.

The standard pattern is: **Modal Verb + Infinitive**. 

1.  **Хочу** + infinitive = I want to (desire)
2.  **Можу** + infinitive = I can (ability)
3.  **Мушу** + infinitive = I must (obligation)

What makes these verbs so useful is that they handle all the complex conjugation. The secondary action—the actual thing you are doing—stays safely in its dictionary form. You only have to remember the forms for **хотіти**, **могти**, and **мусити**.

The real magic happens when you combine them to describe realistic situations. Life is often a conflict between what we desire and what we are obligated to do. You can express this conflict beautifully in a single sentence:

*   Я **хочу** гуляти, але́ не **можу** — **мушу** працювати. — I want to walk, but I cannot — I must work.

This sentence uses all three modal concepts to tell a complete, relatable story in just one line.

Let us put all three verbs side by side for a final comparison.

| Modal | Meaning | Pattern | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|----------|
| **Хотіти** | Want (desire) | Хочу + infinitive / noun | Я **хочу** читати. Я **хочу** каву. |
| **Могти** | Can (ability) | Можу + infinitive | Я **можу** допомогти. |
| **Мусити** | Must (obligation) | Мушу + infinitive | Я **мушу** працювати. |

Notice that **хотіти** is the only modal that can take a noun directly (**хочу каву**). With **могти** and **мусити**, you always need an infinitive after the conjugated form.

Here are more combined examples to show how these verbs work together in everyday life:

*   Він **хоче** спати, але **мусить** працювати. — He wants to sleep, but he must work.
*   Ми **хочемо** допомогти, але не **можемо**. — We want to help, but we cannot.
*   Вони не **хочуть** вчити слова, але **мусять**. — They do not want to study words, but they must.
*   Ти **можеш** читати, якщо́ **хочеш**. — You can read, if you want.

### Self-Check

Now it is your turn. Try to answer these questions out loud in Ukrainian, using the modal verbs from this module.

1.  Що ти **хочеш** робити сього́дні? — What do you want to do today? *(Example: Я хочу гуляти.)*
2.  Що ти **можеш** робити українською? — What can you do in Ukrainian? *(Example: Я можу читати.)*
3.  Що ти **мусиш** робити за́втра? — What must you do tomorrow? *(Example: Я мушу працювати.)*

If you can answer all three, you have the tools to express desire, ability, and obligation — the three pillars of everyday communication.
