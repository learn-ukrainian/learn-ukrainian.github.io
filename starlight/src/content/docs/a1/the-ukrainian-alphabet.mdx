## Warm-up

Welcome to the beautiful Ukrainian language. Before we can start building sentences, having conversations, or ordering a coffee in a local café, we need to unlock the foundation of the language: the Ukrainian alphabet. In Ukrainian, we call it **українська абетка**, **алфавіт**, or **азбука**. These are three valid synonyms, and you'll hear all of them used by native speakers.

The Ukrainian alphabet uses the Cyrillic script and consists of exactly 33 letters. Unlike English, where spelling can sometimes feel like a guessing game with silent letters and unpredictable rules, Ukrainian is incredibly phonetic.

:::note[Fun-Fact]
**The golden rule of Ukrainian phonetics**
In Ukrainian schools, children learn this from day one: "We hear and pronounce sounds, but we see and write letters." {/**/} What you see is almost always exactly what you say. Every single letter has a purpose, and every letter is pronounced.
:::

As a beginner, you might look at a page of Ukrainian text and feel a little intimidated. But here's the secret: you already know more than you think. You don't have to learn 33 completely alien shapes. We can divide the alphabet into three very manageable groups: the letters that are your true friends, the letters that are false friends, and the completely new letters. By tackling them in these groups, you'll be reading your very first real Ukrainian words in just a few minutes.

Before we dive into the specific letters, take a moment to watch this excellent pronunciation overview. Hearing the sounds is just as important as seeing the shapes.

## Знайомі літери (Familiar Letters)

We begin with the easiest group. These are six letters that look exactly like Latin letters, and the wonderful news is that they sound exactly the same, too. You don't have to learn anything new here — just trust your eyes.

The letters are **А**, **О**, **К**, **М**, **Т**, and **Е**.

Here's what each one sounds like:
- **А** sounds like the "a" in "father."
- **О** sounds like the "o" in "more."
- **К** sounds like the "k" in "kitten."
- **М** sounds like the "m" in "moon."
- **Т** sounds like the "t" in "table."
- **Е** sounds like the "e" in "met."

With just these six letters, you can already read real Ukrainian words. You don't need any English crutches to sound these out. Just read them exactly as you would in English. Let's build your confidence right now. Read these words aloud:

> **мама** — mom
> **тато** — dad
> **кома** — comma
> **мак** — poppy
> **око** — eye

Did you read them? Five real Ukrainian words using only six letters! Let's try more — these are also words you might recognize instantly because they're similar to English:

> **атом** — atom
> **метро** — metro
> **тема** — theme
> **мета** — goal
> **том** — volume (of a book)
> **Тома** — a name

That's already 11 words, and you've only learned six letters. These letters appear in hundreds of Ukrainian words. By mastering just these six, you already have a solid anchor in the language. When reading, use them as your safe zones. If you see **мама**, you know exactly what it says.

Time for a tiny context exercise. In Ukrainian, to say "This is..." we use the word **це**.

> Це мама.
> Це тато.
> Мама тут.
> Тато там.

You're officially reading Ukrainian sentences.

## Фальшиві друзі (False Friends)

Now let's face the biggest trap for English speakers. This group contains six letters that look exactly like Latin letters, but they represent completely different sounds. Your brain will instinctively want to read them the English way. You've got to actively break this reflex. When you see these letters, your mouth needs to do something unexpected.

The false friends are **В**, **Н**, **Р**, **С**, **У**, and **Х**.

Watch how the letter **В** actually sounds — this is the single biggest trap for English speakers:

>

<YouTubeVideo client:only="react" url="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aFcvYfvQ2X4" label="Anna Ohoiko — The letter В" />

Here is what they actually sound like in Ukrainian:
- **В** looks like a "B", but it sounds like the English "v" in "van."
- **Н** looks like an "H", but it sounds like the English "n" in "nest."
- **Р** looks like a "P", but it sounds like a rolled "r" in Spanish or Italian.
- **С** looks like a "C", but it ALWAYS sounds like the "s" in "sun."
- **У** looks like a "Y", but it sounds like the "oo" in "boot."
- **Х** looks like an "X", but it sounds like the heavy "h" in the Scottish word "loch" or simply a strong exhale.

Time to drill through some common words. Remember, don't let your English eyes trick you! Read these carefully:

> **вода**
> **ніс**
> **рука**

The first word is water. It starts with **В**, so it's a "v" sound. The second word is nose. It starts with **Н**, so it's an "n" sound. The third word is hand. It starts with **Р**, so it's a rolled "r" sound. Let's try three more:

> **сон**
> **хата**
> **кухня**

These are dream, traditional house, and kitchen. Notice how **хата** uses the heavy breath sound for **Х**, and **сон** starts with the "s" sound of **С**.

Now for the ultimate reading challenge. We're going to mix the familiar letters with the false friends. Take your time. Sound them out letter by letter. If you can read these correctly, you're successfully breaking the false friend habit.

> **сорок**
> **норма**
> **мороз**
> **ворон**

You just read the words for forty, norm, frost, and raven. Excellent work! Now try a couple of these in our simple sentence structure.

> Це вода.
> Це рука.
> Вода тут.
> Хата там.

## Нові літери (New Letters)

We've covered the letters you know and the letters that trick you. Now it's time to meet the strangers. These are letters with shapes that don't exist in the Latin alphabet. They might look unusual at first, but their shapes are distinct, making them easy to remember once you associate them with their sounds.

The new letters are: **Б**, **Г**, **Ґ**, **Д**, **Ж**, **З**, **И**, **Й**, **Л**, **П**, **Ф**, **Ц**, **Ч**, **Ш**, **Щ**.

Instead of drilling isolated letters, let's meet them through high-frequency words you'll use often.

- **Б** sounds like "b" in "book."
- **Г** sounds like a soft "h" in "home."
- **Д** sounds like "d" in "dog."
- **З** sounds like "z" in "zoo."
- **И** sounds somewhat like the "i" in "bit."
- **Й** sounds like "y" in "yes." It's the shortest consonant in Ukrainian.
- **Л** sounds like "l" in "lamp."
- **П** sounds like "p" in "pen."
- **Ф** sounds like "f" in "fun."
- **Ш** sounds like "sh" in "ship."

Practice reading these essential words:

> **банк**
> **гарно**
> **дім**
> **зима**
> **книга**
> **школа**

These mean bank, nicely, modern house, winter, book, and school.

Ukrainian also has some special sounds that English speakers often find interesting.
- **Ж** sounds like the "s" in "pleasure" or "measure."
- **Ц** sounds like the "ts" in "cats."
- **Ч** sounds like the "ch" in "church."
- **Ш** sounds like the "sh" in "ship."
- **Щ** is a unique sound. It's a combination of "sh" and "ch" said together smoothly.

This video demonstrates the **Щ** sound — one of the trickiest for English speakers:

>

<YouTubeVideo client:only="react" url="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QmBLieIuf6Q" label="Anna Ohoiko — The letter Щ" />

Try reading these common words that use the unique **Щ** sound:

> **що**
> **ще**

These mean "what" and "still" or "more."

Finally, let's talk about the unique letter **Ґ**. This letter has a fascinating history. It looks just like the letter **Г**, but it has a little hook on the top right. While **Г** is a soft, breathy sound, **Ґ** is a hard, solid "g" sound, like in the English word "go." This hard **Ґ** only appears in a handful of native Ukrainian words, but it's deeply cherished. Here are three wonderful examples:

> **ґанок**
> **ґудзик**
> **ґречний**

These mean porch, button, and polite. Keep an eye out for this rare and beautiful letter!

## Голосні (Vowels)

Ukrainian phonetics relies heavily on its vowels. They give the language its melodic, flowing quality. Ukraine has exactly 10 vowel letters: **А**, **Е**, **И**, **І**, **Ї**, **О**, **У**, **Ю**, **Я**, **Є**.

We divide these ten letters into two groups. Six of them are considered "simple" vowels because they represent just one sound. We've already met most of them: **А**, **Е**, **И**, **І**, **О**, **У**.

The remaining four are called "iotated" vowels. They're special because they carry a hidden "y" sound (like the "y" in "yes") right before the main vowel sound.
- **Я** sounds like "ya" in "yard."
- **Ю** sounds like "you" in "youth."
- **Є** sounds like "ye" in "yes."
- **Ї** sounds like "yi" in "yield."

:::info[💡 Did You Know?]
**The letter Ї**
This letter **Ї** is completely unique to the Ukrainian alphabet. You won't find it in any other Slavic language. It's a powerful symbol of Ukrainian linguistic identity.
:::

One of the most important vowel distinctions you need to master is the difference between **И** and **І**. To an English speaker, both sound a bit like "i," but they're fundamentally different.

- **И** is a lower, more relaxed sound. It sits somewhere between the English "i" in "bit" and the "ee" in "feet."
- **І** is a higher, tighter sound, much closer to a sharp English "ee."

To hear the contrast, look at these minimal pairs—words that change their entire meaning just by swapping these two vowels:

> **кит**
> **кіт**

The first word means whale, and the second means cat.

> **бик**
> **бік**

The first word means bull, and the second means side. Hearing and producing the difference between **И** and **І** is a key milestone for beginners.

As a quick preview for our next lesson: those four iotated vowels (**Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, **Ї**) have a superpower. When they follow a consonant, they soften that consonant. This softening is the heartbeat of correct Ukrainian pronunciation, and we'll explore it in detail in Module 2.

## Діалоги (Dialogues)

Now it's time to put all this theory into practice. Imagine you're walking down a vibrant street in the center of modern Kyiv with a friend. You're looking around, taking in the sights, and reading the signs above the doors. This is the best way to practice reading, as each sign uses letters from all three of our groups—the familiar, the false friends, and the new ones.

Read these signs aloud as you walk past them:

> **аптека**
> **банк**
> **кафе**
> **метро**
> **пошта**
> **школа**
> **зупинка**

You just identified a pharmacy, a bank, a café, the metro station, a post office, a school, and a bus stop. Notice how **аптека** uses a mix of familiar letters and the false friend **П** (which sounds like "p"). Notice how **зупинка** combines the new **З** with the false friends **У**, **Н**, and the familiar **К** and **А**.

Next, let's practice reading names. Ukrainian names are beautiful and reading them is fantastic alphabet practice. Here are some common masculine names. Read them slowly:

> **Олег**
> **Тарас**
> **Андрій**
> **Богдан**
> **Михайло**

Now, read these common feminine names:

> **Олена**
> **Марія**
> **Софія**
> **Катерина**
> **Наталія**

:::note[🏺 Cultural Context]
**Calling people by name**
When you call out to someone directly in Ukrainian, their name changes its ending. This is called the vocative case. For example, if you want to get Olena's attention, you don't just say her name — you call out:

**Олено!**

If you're calling out to Taras, you say:

**Тарасе!**
:::

We'll learn much more about this later, but it's a delightful feature of the language to notice early on.

Finally, let's take a quick geography tour. Reading Ukrainian city names exercises every possible letter combination. Challenge yourself to read these aloud:

> **Київ**
> **Львів**
> **Одеса**
> **Харків**
> **Дніпро**
> **Запоріжжя**
> **Полтава**
> **Чернігів**

If you can sound out these cities, you're well on your way. You've taken your first major step into the Ukrainian language.

## 📋 Підсумок — Summary

Congratulations! You've just navigated the entire 33-letter Ukrainian Cyrillic alphabet. Here's a recap of our strategy. We conquered the alphabet by breaking it into three manageable groups:
1. **The Familiar Letters** that look and sound like English (like А, О, М).
2. **The False Friends** that try to trick your brain (like В, Р, Н).
3. **The New Letters** with unique shapes and sounds (like Б, Ж, Щ).

We also learned that Ukrainian has simple vowels and special iotated vowels, and we practiced the crucial difference between the vowels **И** and **І**. You have already read real words, signs, names, and cities.

**Self-Check Questions:**
1. Can you name three "false friend" letters and explain what sound they actually make?
2. What is the golden rule of Ukrainian phonetics regarding letters and sounds?
3. Can you read the words **вода**, **школа**, and **місто** aloud without relying on English letters?
4. What is the difference between the letters **Г** and **Ґ**?
