## Множина іменників (Noun plurals)

So far, you have learned how to talk about one thing at a time: one cat, one city, one book, one student. But the world is full of multiples! Today, you are taking a big and exciting step forward. You are adding plurals to your toolkit. The good news is that creating plurals in Ukrainian is quite logical and pattern-based. Once you understand the basic rules, you will be able to talk about groups of people, collections of objects, and multiples of anything you see around you.

Take a look at the most common and predictable pattern: masculine nouns. As you remember, most masculine nouns in Ukrainian end in a consonant. To make them plural, you simply add the ending **-и** (or **-і** if the stem ends in a soft consonant or if it falls under certain spelling rules). For example, the word **студе́нт** (student) becomes **студе́нти** (students). The word **хло́пець** (boy) ends in a soft sign, so it takes an **-і** and becomes **хло́пці** (boys). Here are a few more examples of this very regular pattern:

- **студе́нт** (student) → **студе́нти** (students)
- **брат** (brother) → **брати́** (brothers)
- **стіл** (table) → **столи́** (tables)
- **хло́пець** (boy) → **хло́пці** (boys)

Now, move on to feminine nouns. Most feminine nouns end in the vowels **-а** or **-я**. To make them plural, you do not just add a letter; you replace the final vowel. The **-а** is replaced with **-и**, and the **-я** is replaced with **-і**. For example, the word **кни́га** (book) replaces its final **-а** with **-и**, becoming **кни́ги** (books). The word **земля́** (land, earth) replaces its final **-я** with **-і**, becoming **землі́** (lands). This pattern is incredibly reliable and applies to the vast majority of feminine nouns you will encounter.

- **кни́га** (book) → **кни́ги** (books)
- **ма́ма** (mom) → **ма́ми** (moms)
- **сестра́** (sister) → **се́стри** (sisters)
- **ву́лиця** (street) → **ву́лиці** (streets)

Consider neuter nouns. They have a very clear and distinct pattern. In their singular form, they typically end in **-о** or **-е**. To form the plural, you replace this characteristic ending with **-а** or **-я**. It is almost as if neuter plurals borrow the singular feminine endings! The word **мі́сто** (city) drops its **-о** and adds an **-а** to become **міста́** (cities). The word **мо́ре** (sea) drops its **-е** and adds a **-я** to become **моря́** (seas). This simple vowel swap is all you need to remember for neuter nouns.

- **мі́сто** (city) → **міста́** (cities)
- **вікно́** (window) → **ві́кна** (windows)
- **яйце́** (egg) → **я́йця** (eggs)
- **мо́ре** (sea) → **моря́** (seas)

Finally, just like in English, there are a few irregular plurals that you simply must memorize. These words do not follow the standard rules. However, they are often high-frequency words, so you will see them a lot and learn them quickly. For example, the word **люди́на** (person) completely changes its stem to become **лю́ди** (people) in the plural. The word **дити́на** (child) undergoes a similar transformation and becomes **ді́ти** (children). And the word **о́ко** (eye) changes its ending unexpectedly to become **о́чі** (eyes). These exceptions are ancient and very common in everyday speech, so it is best to learn them right away as single units.

## Чергування (Alternation)

Now that you know the basic endings for creating plurals, it is time to explore a fascinating and very characteristic feature of the Ukrainian language. Sometimes, when you add a plural ending to a word, the vowel inside the word stem changes! This phenomenon is called vowel alternation. The most common and important alternation you will encounter at this stage is when the letter **і** changes to **о** or **е**.

Observe the word **кіт** (cat). It is a masculine noun ending in a consonant, so you might expect the plural to simply be "кіти". But it is actually **коти́** (cats). The **і** in the stem has reverted to **о**. Similarly, the word **піч** (oven) becomes **печі́** (ovens), and the word **ніж** (knife) becomes **ножі́** (knives). The vowel inside the word shifts when the ending is added.

Why does this alternation happen? It goes back to historical vowel changes and what linguists call the "fleeting **і**". In older forms of the language, certain vowels were pronounced differently depending on the structure of the word. Today, the rule of thumb is that the letter **і** often appears in closed syllables. A closed syllable is a syllable that ends in a consonant, like the single syllable in the word "кіт". However, when you add a plural ending, the syllable opens up. Now the word is divided into two syllables: "ко-ти", and the first syllable ends in a vowel. When the syllable opens, the **і** reverts to its original, historical **о** or **е**. This makes the word flow more smoothly in spoken Ukrainian.

- **кіт** (cat) → **коти́** (cats)
- **ніж** (knife) → **ножі́** (knives)
- **сік** (juice) → **со́ки** (juices)
- **річ** (thing) → **ре́чі** (things)
- **дім** (house) → **доми́** (houses)

You should also be aware of another type of change: consonant alternation. You will not need to use this specific rule for plurals right now, but it will be very important later on in your learning journey. Sometimes, consonants like **к**, **г**, and **х** change to **ц**, **з**, and **с** before the ending **-і**. For example, the word **рука́** (hand) becomes **руці́** (in the hand) when you talk about location. Similarly, **нога́** (leg) becomes **нозі́** (on the leg). This pattern returns when you learn the locative case, so just keep it in the back of your mind for now!

Here is a great pattern recognition strategy for you as you learn new vocabulary: if a word has the letter **і** in the last syllable of its stem, you should always check whether it alternates in the plural. This is most common with short, monosyllabic masculine nouns. If you see a word like **дім** (house) or **ніс** (nose), be prepared for the **і** to change when you make it plural! (**дім** → **доми́**, **ніс** → **носи́**). It becomes second nature very quickly.

## Множина прикметників (Adjective plurals)

You already know that adjectives must agree with the noun they describe in gender (masculine, feminine, neuter). That meant learning three different endings for every single adjective in the singular. But here is some fantastic news that will make your life much easier: when you use adjectives in the plural, things get incredibly simple!

In the plural, all three genders collapse into one single form. Whether you are describing masculine students, feminine books, or neuter cities, the plural adjective ending is exactly the same: **-і** (or **-ї** after a vowel or a soft sign). This simplification is a huge relief and makes building complex sentences much easier for beginners.

Take a look at the adjective "new". In the singular, you learned **нови́й** (masculine), **нова́** (feminine), and **нове́** (neuter). You had to match the gender perfectly. In the plural, all of them become **нові́** (new for all genders). It does not matter what the original gender of the noun was.

- **нови́й** (m) / **нова́** (f) / **нове́** (n) → **нові́** (plural)
- **стари́й** (m) / **стара́** (f) / **старе́** (n) → **старі́** (plural)
- **га́рний** (m) / **га́рна** (f) / **га́рне** (n) → **га́рні** (plural)
- **молоди́й** (m) / **молода́** (f) / **молоде́** (n) → **молоді́** (plural)

This rule applies to soft-stem adjectives too. The adjective "blue" is **си́ній** (masculine), **си́ня** (feminine), and **си́нє** (neuter). In the plural, it simply becomes **си́ні**. The pattern remains exactly the same, just with the soft variant of the ending. It is a very consistent rule.

The most important rule here is agreement. The adjective must always match the noun in number. If the noun is plural, the adjective must absolutely be plural too. You cannot mix a singular adjective with a plural noun.

- **нові́ студе́нти** (new students)
- **старі́ кни́ги** (old books)
- **вели́кі міста́** (big cities)
- **молоді́ лю́ди** (young people)
- **си́ні о́чі** (blue eyes)
- **га́рні коти́** (beautiful cats)

Now practice forming some simple noun phrases and sentences. Combining plural adjectives with plural nouns is straightforward. Remember our little word **це** (this is / these are)? It works perfectly with plurals too! You can also use the plural demonstrative **ті** (those).

- Це **нові́ кни́ги**. (These are new books.)
- Ті **вели́кі** будинки старі. (Those big buildings are old.)
- Це **молоді́ студе́нти**. (These are young students.)
- Ті **ціка́ві кни́ги** тут. (Those interesting books are here.)

## Винятки та особливості (Exceptions and special cases)

Just like in English, not every single noun can be neatly packaged into a clear singular and a distinct plural form. Language is messy and organic! There are some exceptions and special cases that you need to be aware of. They are very natural once you get used to them, and they work very similarly to how they work in English.

First, consider uncountable nouns. These are substances, liquids, materials, or abstract concepts that cannot be counted individually. Because they cannot be counted one by one, they have no plural form. They exist entirely in the singular. For example, **молоко́** (milk) is uncountable. You cannot say "one milk" or "two milks" (you would count glasses or bottles of milk instead). Similarly, **цу́кор** (sugar), **вода́** (water in a general sense), and **пові́тря** (air) are uncountable nouns. You will always use them with singular adjectives and verbs.

- Це смачне **молоко́**. (This is tasty milk.)
- Де **цу́кор**? (Where is the sugar?)
- Тут вода́. (Here is water.)

On the other hand, there are nouns that are plural-only, known as *pluralia tantum*. These words exist exclusively in the plural form, even when they refer to a single physical object or a singular concept. The word **гро́ші** (money) is a great example. It is always plural in Ukrainian! You will always say "big money" using plural adjectives. Another common example is **две́рі** (door). Even if you have just one single door in front of you, you refer to it with the plural word. **Но́жиці** (scissors) and **окуля́ри** (glasses) follow this exact same pattern, which should feel familiar since they are also plural-only in English.

- Де **гро́ші**? (Where is the money?)
- Це нові **две́рі**. (This is a new door.)
- Ті **окуля́ри** старі. (Those glasses are old.)
- Де великі **но́жиці**? (Where are the big scissors?)

Finally, remember that Ukrainian is a language with mobile stress. You learned about this before in a previous lesson! Sometimes, the stress shifts to a different syllable when a noun is pluralized. The emphasis moves, which can change how the word sounds. For example, the singular **рука́** (hand) has stress on the last syllable, but it changes its stress to become **ру́ки** (hands). The word **сестра́** (sister) becomes **се́стри** (sisters). And **нога́** (leg/foot) becomes **но́ги** (legs/feet). This stress mobility is completely natural, and you will pick it up naturally as you hear these words spoken by native speakers.

## Практика (Practice)

You have learned a lot of important rules today! Put all of these new patterns and concepts into practice. The best way to master plurals and vowel alternation is through repetition, active recall, and reading in context. Start with some plural formation drills. Pay close attention to the ending changes and remember to watch out for the "fleeting **і**" we discussed earlier. Read these pairs out loud.

- **студе́нт** (student) → **студе́нти** (students)
- **кіт** (cat) → **коти́** (cats) — *Did you catch the alternation?*
- **кни́га** (book) → **кни́ги** (books)
- **мі́сто** (city) → **міста́** (cities)
- **ніж** (knife) → **ножі́** (knives)
- **стіл** (table) → **столи́** (tables)
- **брат** (brother) → **брати́** (brothers)
- **мо́ре** (sea) → **моря́** (seas)

Now, practice matching singular nouns to their plural forms, including those high-frequency irregular pairs that you simply have to memorize. This is a great way to solidify your active vocabulary and train your brain to recognize the exceptions instantly.

- **люди́на** (person) → **лю́ди** (people)
- **дити́на** (child) → **ді́ти** (children)
- **о́ко** (eye) → **о́чі** (eyes)
- **я́блуко** (apple) → **я́блука** (apples)

Finally, practice reading and understanding simple sentences with plural noun phrases. See if you can identify the noun, identify the adjective, and ensure they match in number perfectly. This is where everything comes together and you start to see the language working as a system!

- У мене є **нові́ кни́ги**. (I have new books.)
- Це **вели́кі міста́**. (These are big cities.)
- Ті **молоді́ студе́нти** там. (Those young students are there.)
- Де старі **окуля́ри**? (Where are the old glasses?)
- Це **малі́ ді́ти**. (These are small children.)
- Тут **до́брі лю́ди**. (There are good people here.)
- Ті **га́рні коти́** великі. (Those beautiful cats are big.)
- Де **нові́ гро́ші**? (Where is the new money?)
- Це вода́. Тут **бі́лий цу́кор**. (This is water. Here is white sugar.)

Great job! You are now equipped to talk about multiples in Ukrainian. You understand the basic endings, the vowel alternations, and the simplified adjectives. Keep practicing these patterns, and soon they will feel completely natural and effortless.

## 📋 Підсумок — Summary

Today, you unlocked the very important world of plurals in Ukrainian. You learned that masculine nouns typically take the ending **-и** (or **-і**), feminine nouns replace their final **-а/-я** with **-и/-і**, and neuter nouns replace their final **-о/-е** with **-а/-я**. You also explored the fascinating vowel alternation where the "fleeting **і**" reverts to its historical **о** or **е** when a syllable opens up, such as when **кіт** becomes **коти́**.

You saw that adjectives become incredibly simple in the plural, using the single ending **-і** (or **-ї**) for all genders regardless of the noun (**нові́ кни́ги**, **вели́кі міста́**). And you discovered that some nouns are uncountable (**молоко́**, **цу́кор**) while others exist exclusively in the plural (**гро́ші**, **две́рі**, **окуля́ри**).

Test yourself with these questions before moving on:

- What happens to the vowel in the word **кіт** when it becomes a plural noun?
- What is the universal plural form of the adjective **нови́й** for all three genders?
- What is the irregular plural form of the singular noun **люди́на**?
