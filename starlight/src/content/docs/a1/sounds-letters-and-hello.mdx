## Зву́ки і лі́тери (Sounds and Letters)

Look at the text on this page. What you see are letters — symbols on paper or on a screen. Now say a word out loud. What you just produced are sounds. This distinction is the absolute foundation of the Ukrainian language, and every Ukrainian child learns it in the first grade. There is a golden rule:

> **Ми чу́ємо і вимовля́ємо звуки, а ба́чимо і пи́шемо літери.**

We hear and pronounce sounds. We see and write letters. A **звук** is a sound. A **лі́тера** is a letter. The Ukrainian alphabet has 33 **літери**, but the language has 38 **зву́ків**. Why the mismatch? Because some letters represent more than one sound, and one special letter — **Ь** — represents no sound at all. More on that soon.

Sounds split into two families. The first family: **голосні́** — vowels. These are sounds made with voice alone. Your mouth stays open, air flows freely, nothing blocks it. Ukrainian children learn them through a poem from their very first textbook:

> **Голосні почу́єш в пісні́.**

"You'll hear vowels in a song." There are 6 vowel sounds in Ukrainian, but 10 vowel letters:

| Vowel sounds (6) | Vowel letters (10) |
|---|---|
| **а, о, у, е, и, і** | **А, О, У, Е, И, І, Я, Ю, Є, Ї** |

Why 10 letters for 6 sounds? Four letters — **Я, Ю, Є, Ї** — do double duty. They can represent two sounds at once, or they can soften the consonant before them. That is a story for Module 2.

The second family: **при́голосні** — consonants. These sounds are made when something in your mouth — lips, teeth, or tongue — blocks the airflow. Voice plus obstruction, or just obstruction alone. There are 32 consonant sounds. Another textbook poem:

> **Приголосні деренча́ть і тихе́нько шелестя́ть.**

"Consonants rattle and quietly rustle." Some are loud and buzzing, others are soft whispers. Together, **голосні** and **приголосні** build every Ukrainian word.

## Пе́рші слова (First Words)

Some Cyrillic letters are friendly. They look like Latin letters and sound similar enough to read immediately. Meet your first five: **А**, **О**, **К**, **М**, **Т**. With just these five letters, you can already read real Ukrainian words:

| Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **ма́ма** | mother |
| **та́то** | father |
| **ко́ма** | comma |
| **а́том** | atom |
| **мак** | poppy |
| **о́ко** | eye |

Try it: look at **мама**. М sounds like "m," А sounds like the "a" in "father." Put them together: **ма-ма**. You just read your first Ukrainian word.

:::tip
Ukrainian **Т** is dental — your tongue touches your teeth, not the gum ridge behind them. And both **К** and **Т** have no puff of air after them, unlike English. Do not worry about perfecting this now. Just notice the difference.
:::

### False Friends

Now for the trap. Six Cyrillic letters look exactly like Latin letters but make completely different sounds. These are the "false friends":

| Letter | Looks like | Actually sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| **В** | English B | "v" as in "very" |
| **Н** | English H | "n" as in "no" |
| **Р** | English P | a rolled "r" (like in Spanish) |
| **С** | English C | "s" as in "sun" |
| **У** | English Y | "oo" as in "moon" |
| **Х** | English X | "ch" as in Scottish "loch" |

Read these words, and fight every instinct to read the letters as English:

| Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **вода́** | water |
| **рука́** | hand |
| **сон** | dream |
| **ніс** | nose |
| **ха́та** | house |

That word **вода** is not "boda." It is "voda" — water. **Рука** is not "puka." It is "ruka" — hand. Every false friend is a small battle with your English brain. You will win.

### New Shapes

Some Ukrainian letters have no Latin lookalike at all: **Б**, **Г**, **Ґ**, **Д**, **Ж**, **З**, **И**, **Й**, **Л**, **П**, **Ф**, **Ц**, **Ч**, **Ш**, **Щ**. You will learn each one through words. Here are a few to start:

| Word | Meaning | New letter |
|---|---|---|
| **банк** | bank | **Б** |
| **дім** | home | **Д** |
| **зима́** | winter | **З** |
| **кни́га** | book | **Г** |
| **шко́ла** | school | **Ш** |

Two special characters deserve attention. **Щ** always makes two sounds together — like "sh" followed immediately by "ch." And **Ь** — the soft sign — has no sound of its own. It softens the consonant before it, like the invisible difference between a hard "l" and a soft "l." The word **день** (day) ends with a softened **н**. You will explore soft consonants fully in Module 3.

One more: **Ї** is unique to Ukrainian. No other Slavic language has it. It always sounds like "y" plus "ee" together — two sounds in one letter. The word **Ки́їв** (Kyiv) ends with this letter.

## Приві́т! (Hello!)

Two friends meet on the street:

> — **Привіт!**
> — **Привіт! Як спра́ви?**
> — **До́бре. А у тебе́?**
> — **Чудо́во!**

This is your first Ukrainian conversation. **Привіт** means "hi" — informal, for friends, family, and people your age. **Як справи?** means "how are you?" And the answers:

| Response | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Добре.** | Fine. Good. |
| **Чудово!** | Great! Wonderful! |
| **Норма́льно.** | Okay. So-so. |

The follow-up question is **А у тебе?** — "And you?" Simple, natural, exactly what a Ukrainian would say.

:::caution
Ukrainian has gendered forms. A woman says **Ра́да тебе ба́чити!** (Glad to see you!), while a man says **Ра́дий тебе бачити!** This is your first encounter with grammatical gender in Ukrainian — it will become a major topic starting in Module 8. For now, just notice that **рада** and **радий** are two forms of the same word.
:::

Now look at **Привіт** letter by letter. This single word uses letters from every group you just learned:

| Letter | Type |
|---|---|
| **П** | new shape |
| **р** | false friend! (not "p") |
| **и** | new vowel |
| **в** | false friend! (not "b") |
| **і** | vowel |
| **т** | familiar |

Read it slowly: **При-віт!** You know every letter. You can read this word.

## Чита́ємо (Reading Practice)

Walk down any street in Ukraine and you will see signs everywhere. Each one is a reading exercise. Sound out every letter, blend them into syllables, then read the whole word:

| Sign | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Апте́ка** | pharmacy |
| **Банк** | bank |
| **Кафе́** | café |
| **Метро́** | metro |
| **По́шта** | post office |
| **Школа** | school |
| **Зупи́нка** | bus stop |

Some are almost identical to English — **банк**, **метро**, **кафе**. Others require your new skills: **Зупинка** uses **З** (new shape) and **У** (false friend — it is "oo," not "y"). Sound it out: **Зу-пин-ка**.

### Ukrainian Cities

Every Ukrainian knows these names. Try reading them:

**Київ** · **Львів** · **Оде́са** · **Харків** · **Дніпро́** · **Полта́ва**

Notice **Київ** — the capital. К is familiar, и is the new vowel, ї is uniquely Ukrainian (two sounds!), в is a false friend ("v," not "b"). Four letters, three different categories.

### First Sentences

Ukrainian uses the word **це** to mean "this is." With **це** and the nouns you already know, you can build your first sentences:

> **Це мама.**

> **Це банк.**

> **Це Київ.**

And two question words: **Що це?** (What is this?) and **Хто це?** (Who is this?)

> **Що це? — Це школа.**

> **Хто це? — Це тато.**

**Хто** is for people. **Що** is for everything else. **Школа** — що це? **Мама** — хто це?

## Підсумок — Summary

This module covered the foundation of everything that follows. The Ukrainian alphabet has 33 **літери** and the language has 38 **звуків** — because letters and sounds are not the same thing. You learned the golden rule: **Ми чуємо і вимовляємо звуки, а бачимо і пишемо літери.**

Sounds divide into two families: **голосні** (6 vowel sounds, 10 vowel letters) and **приголосні** (32 consonant sounds). You read your first words using familiar letters (**мама**, **тато**), survived the false friends (**В** is "v," **Р** is a rolled "r," **Н** is "n"), and met letters with entirely new shapes (**Б**, **Д**, **Ш**).

You learned your first greeting — **Привіт! Як справи? — Добре!** — and built your first sentences with **це**: **Це школа. Хто це? Що це?**

Self-check: Can you answer these questions?

- How many letters? How many sounds? Why different?
- What are **голосні**? What are **приголосні**?
- What does **Привіт** mean?
- How do you respond to **Як справи?**

If yes — you are ready for Module 2: Reading Ukrainian.
