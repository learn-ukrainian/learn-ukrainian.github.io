> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> In Ukrainian, movement and destination require specific grammatical structures to be clearly understood. The way you express "I am going to the store" is fundamentally different from "I am in the store". Furthermore, prepositions in Ukrainian do much more than indicate physical location; they express gratitude, timeframes, topics of conversation, and reasons. Mastering accusative prepositions allows you to effectively navigate cities, make weekend plans, thank people correctly, and discuss your thoughts accurately.

## Куди? з Acc — Direction with Acc

### Статика чи динаміка? — Static or Dynamic?

In the English language, we often use the word "where" for both location and destination. We say "Where are you?" to ask about a static location. We also say "Where are you going?" to ask about a dynamic destination. Ukrainian makes a very strict cognitive distinction between these two concepts. You cannot use the same question word for both situations.

Де ти? (Where are you?)
Я у магази́ні. (I am in the shop.)

Куди́ ти іде́ш? (Where are you going?)
Я іду́ в магази́н. (I am going to the shop.)

The question "Де?" asks about your current, non-moving location. The question "Куди́?" asks about the direction of your movement toward a destination. Whenever you answer the question "Куди́?", you must use the Accusative case. This is a fundamental rule of Ukrainian grammar. The Accusative case is not just the case of the direct object; it is also the case of motion toward a target.

### Прийменники в та у — Prepositions v and u

When your destination is an enclosed space, a building, a room, or a clearly defined container, you use the preposition "в" or "у". This preposition translates to "to" or "into" in English when combined with verbs of motion and the Accusative case.

Я іду́ в магази́н. (I am going to the shop.)
Він іде́ в кімна́ту. (He is going into the room.)
Ми ї́демо в Ки́їв. (We are going to Kyiv.)
Вона́ йде у шко́лу. (She is going to the school.)

In these examples, the shop, the room, the city, and the school are seen as enclosed or defined spaces. Notice that the noun "магази́н" and "Ки́їв" are masculine inanimate nouns. Therefore, their Accusative form is identical to their Nominative form. The word "кімна́та" is feminine, so its ending changes from "-а" to "-у" in the Accusative case.

:::tip[💡 Tip]
Think of "в/у" as entering a box. If you have to pass through a door, cross a boundary, or enter a defined territory to get inside, you will most likely use "в" or "у" to indicate your destination.
:::

### Евфонія: в чи у? — Euphony: v or u?

You might be wondering why we have two different prepositions, "в" and "у", that mean exactly the same thing. This is because of a beautiful feature of the Ukrainian language called euphony. Euphony means "pleasant sound". Ukrainian naturally avoids awkward clusters of consonants and strives for a smooth flow of speech.

Вона́ іде́ в магази́н. (She is going to the shop.)
Він іде́ у парк. (He is going to the park.)

If the previous word ends in a vowel, and the next word starts with a consonant, we prefer to use the consonant "в". This creates a smooth rhythm. However, if the previous word ends in a consonant, or if the next word starts with a cluster of consonants, we use the vowel "у". This acts as a vocalic bridge, preventing the sounds from crashing together. This is a natural, rhythmic feature of the language, designed to make speaking easier.

### Прийменник на — Preposition na

While "в/у" is used for enclosed spaces, the preposition "на" is used for open surfaces, territories, and events. When combined with verbs of motion and the Accusative case, it also translates to "to" in English.

Я іду́ на по́шту. (I am going to the post office.)
Вони́ ї́дуть на конце́рт. (They are going to the concert.)
Ми ідемо́ на стадіо́н. (We are going to the stadium.)
Студе́нти йдуть на ле́кцію. (The students are going to the lecture.)

A concert and a lecture are events, not physical buildings. Even if the concert is held inside a building, you are attending the event itself. Therefore, you must use "на". A stadium is traditionally an open-air surface, so it takes "на". The post office is an interesting historical exception. Traditionally, post was delivered by horses to open relay stations, so the word "по́шта" kept the preposition "на" throughout the centuries.

:::info[ℹ️ Fact]
Historically, the choice between "в/у" and "на" was shaped by how people interacted with the space. Open areas, fields, and events naturally attracted "на", while enclosed fortresses or buildings attracted "в/у".
:::

### Винятки та спеціальні місця — Exceptions and Special Places

Some locations simply have to be memorized because they belong to the "на" category based on tradition, their nature as open areas, or geographical conventions.

Ми ї́демо на о́стрів. (We are going to the island.)
Вона́ іде́ на пло́щу. (She is going to the square.)
Я іду́ на пляж. (I am going to the beach.)
Він іде́ на робо́ту. (He is going to work.)

An island (о́стрів), a city square (пло́ща), and a beach (пляж) are all considered open surfaces. Work (робо́та) is treated as an activity or an event rather than just a building, so it always takes "на".

| Enclosed Spaces (в / у) | Open Surfaces & Events (на) |
| :--- | :--- |
| в магази́н (to the shop) | на по́шту (to the post office) |
| у шко́лу (to the school) | на конце́рт (to the concert) |
| в о́фіс (to the office) | на стадіо́н (to the stadium) |
| у кімна́ту (into the room) | на пло́щу (to the square) |
| в парк (to the park) | на пляж (to the beach) |

Сьогодні ми йдемо в парк. Парк дуже великий. Ми йдемо в магазин. Магазин там. Ми купуємо воду. Ми йдемо в кімнату. Кімната велика. Ми йдемо на роботу. Робота тут. Я йду на площу. Площа красива. Ми бачимо новий будинок.

## За + Acc — Behind/For

### Подяка та обмін — Gratitude and Exchange

One of the most frequent uses of the preposition "за" with the Accusative case is to express gratitude or an exchange. In English, you say "thank you for something" or "pay for something". In Ukrainian, you use "за".

Дя́кую за допомо́гу. (Thank you for the help.)
Я плачу́ за квито́к. (I am paying for the ticket.)
Дя́кую за ка́ву. (Thank you for the coffee.)
Ви́бачте за запі́знення. (Sorry for the delay.)

Notice that the nouns follow the preposition "за" and are in the Accusative case. "Допомо́га" becomes "допомо́гу". "Ка́ва" becomes "ка́ву". "Квито́к" is a masculine inanimate noun, so it remains "квито́к". This structure is the only correct way to express what you are thankful for. In Ukrainian communication, expressing specific gratitude ("Дякую за...") is essential for politeness and shows warmth and sincerity.

:::warning[⚠️ Warning]
A very common mistake for English speakers is to translate the English word "for" directly into the Ukrainian word "для" when expressing gratitude. Never say "Дякую для допомогу". The preposition "для" means "for the benefit of" and takes the Genitive case. When you are thanking someone, you are exchanging your gratitude for their action. This exchange always requires "за + Accusative".
:::

### Просторовий напрямок — Spatial Direction

The preposition "за" can also indicate a physical direction. When it is used with verbs of motion and the Accusative case, it means moving "behind" an object.

Він іде́ за буди́нок. (He is going behind the building.)
Со́нце сіда́є за го́ру. (The sun is setting behind the mountain.)
Ми йдемо́ за де́рево. (We are going behind the tree.)
Я покла́в сумку за стіл. (I put the bag behind the table.)

This represents a dynamic movement. You are actively relocating yourself or an object to a position situated behind something else. Once you are already standing behind the building, you will use a different case (the Instrumental case) to describe your static location. But as long as you are moving toward that position, you use the Accusative.

### Часовий вимір — Temporal Dimension

In addition to space and gratitude, "за" is heavily used in time expressions. When combined with a unit of time in the Accusative case, it indicates the duration it takes to complete an action. It translates to "in" or "within" in English.

Я зроблю́ це за годи́ну. (I will do this in an hour.)
Він прочита́в кни́гу за ти́ждень. (He read the book in a week.)
Ми закінчи́ли проє́кт за мі́сяць. (We finished the project in a month.)
Вона́ написа́ла лист за хвили́ну. (She wrote the letter in a minute.)

In these examples, "за годи́ну" means that the entire process from start to finish required exactly one hour of effort. The focus is on the amount of time consumed by the task to reach completion. This is usually paired with verbs that indicate a finished result.

Let us see how "за" is used in a real-life situation. This short interaction shows how naturally this preposition fits into daily life.

## Через + Acc — Through/Because of

### Рух крізь простір — Movement Through Space

The preposition "че́рез" with the Accusative case is highly versatile. Its most literal meaning is spatial: moving "through" or "across" an area, an obstacle, or a pathway.

Ми йдемо́ че́рез парк. (We are walking through the park.)
Вона́ перехо́дить че́рез доро́гу. (She is crossing the road.)
Вони́ ї́дуть че́рез міст. (They are driving across the bridge.)
Птах лети́ть че́рез ліс. (The bird is flying through the forest.)

Whether you are walking through a dense park, crossing an open street, or driving over a bridge, "че́рез" indicates that you are traversing a space from one side to the other. Notice that "доро́га" changes to "доро́гу" (feminine Accusative), while "парк" and "міст" remain the same because they are masculine inanimate nouns.

### Через у часі — Cherez in Time

Like "за", the preposition "че́рез" also has a very important temporal meaning. It indicates how much time will pass before an event happens in the future. It also translates to "in" in English, which requires careful attention.

Конце́рт почне́ться че́рез годи́ну. (The concert will start in an hour.)
Я бу́ду вдо́ма че́рез ти́ждень. (I will be home in a week.)
Ми зустрі́немося че́рез мі́сяць. (We will meet in a month.)
По́їзд прибува́є че́рез хвили́ну. (The train arrives in a minute.)

In these sentences, the action is not happening right now. You have to wait for an hour, a week, or a month to pass, and then the event will occur. The time period is the waiting time, not the working time.

:::tip[🔍 Pattern Discovery]
The English phrase "in an hour" can mean two completely different things in Ukrainian.
**За годи́ну**: I will spend one hour working on it, and then it will be done. — Duration of effort
**Че́рез годи́ну**: I am not doing anything now, but sixty minutes from now, the event will happen. — Elapsed time until event
Mixing these up is a common source of confusion. Always ask yourself: am I talking about the time it takes to complete a task, or the time I have to wait?
:::

### Причина та наслідок — Cause and Reason

The third major meaning of "че́рез" is causal. It introduces an external reason, a factor, or an obstacle that causes something else to happen. It translates to "because of" or "due to".

Я запізни́вся че́рез дощ. (I was late because of the rain.)
Ми не ї́демо че́рез робо́ту. (We are not going because of work.)
Він не спить че́рез стрес. (He is not sleeping because of stress.)
Доро́га закри́та че́рез ава́рію. (The road is closed because of an accident.)

When you use "че́рез" in this way, you are usually pointing to an external circumstance that disrupted your plans or caused a specific state. The rain caused the delay. The work prevented the trip. The nouns following "че́рез" are all in the Accusative case: дощ (masculine inanimate), робо́ту (feminine), стрес (masculine inanimate), ава́рію (feminine).

### Комунікація та посередництво — Communication and Mediation

Sometimes, "че́рез" indicates the medium or the channel through which an action is performed or an object is sent.

Я відпра́вив ли́ст че́рез по́шту. (I sent the letter through the post office.)
Ми говоримо́ че́рез інтерне́т. (We are talking through the internet.)
Він дізна́вся новину́ че́рез дру́га. (He found out the news through a friend.)

In these contexts, the post office, the internet, and the friend act as intermediaries. The action passes "through" them to reach its final destination. It is a metaphorical extension of walking through a physical space.

Я запізнився через дощ. Дощ дуже сильний. Ми не йдемо через роботу. Робота важлива. Дорога закрита через аварію. Аварія там. Ми говоримо через інтернет. Інтернет тут. Ми йдемо через ліс. Ліс дуже гарний. Ми бачимо річку. Ми йдемо через міст. Ми йдемо на стадіон. Там буде концерт. Ми йдемо на пошту. Я маю лист. Я відправляю лист через пошту.

## Про + Acc — About

### Тема розмови — Topic of Conversation

When you want to discuss a topic, share your thoughts, or mention what a book is about, you need the preposition "про" with the Accusative case. It translates directly to "about" in English and is essential for all intellectual communication.

Це кни́га про істо́рію. (This is a book about history.)
Це фі́льм про коха́ння. (This is a film about love.)
Я розповіда́ю про Украї́ну. (I am telling about Ukraine.)
Він пи́ше статтю́ про культу́ру. (He is writing an article about culture.)

Every time you introduce a subject matter, you will use "про". For instance, "істо́рія" becomes "істо́рію". Similarly, "Украї́на" takes the form "Украї́ну". Since the word "коха́ння" is neuter and inanimate, so its Accusative form is the same as its Nominative form.

:::note[🏺 Culture]
Ukrainians deeply value meaningful conversations about history, culture, and current events. Knowing how to correctly use "про" allows you to engage in these discussions. It opens the door to sharing stories about your own experiences and learning about the rich heritage of Ukraine.
:::

### Дієслова з про — Verbs with Pro

Certain verbs naturally pair with the preposition "про". Whenever you use these verbs to indicate the topic of your mental or verbal activity, "про" must follow.

Ми говоримо́ про робо́ту. (We are talking about work.)
Вона́ ду́має про ма́му. (She is thinking about mom.)
Він чита́є про Ки́їв. (He is reading about Kyiv.)
Я зна́ю про це. (I know about this.)
Вони́ мрі́ють про відпу́стку. (They are dreaming about a vacation.)

These verbs — говори́ти (to speak), ду́мати (to think), чита́ти (to read), зна́ти (to know), and мрі́яти (to dream) — are the most common triggers for this grammatical structure. Notice how "ма́ма" changes to "ма́му" because it is the direct object of her thoughts, marked by the preposition "про".

### Як ставити питання? — How to Ask Questions?

When you want to ask what someone is talking or thinking about, you need to form an interrogative phrase using "про". Because the preposition requires the Accusative case, the question words themselves must also be in the Accusative case.

The Nominative question word for things is "що" (what). The Accusative form is exactly the same: "що".
The Nominative question word for people is "хто" (who). The Accusative form changes to "кого́".

Про що це? (What is this about?)
Це про конце́рт. (This is about the concert.)

Про кого́ ви говорите́? (Who are you talking about?)
Ми говоримо́ про бра́та. (We are talking about the brother.)

In English, the preposition often dangles at the end of the sentence ("What are you thinking *about*?"). In Ukrainian, the preposition must always come immediately before the question word ("*About* what are you thinking?").

### Розповідь про себе — Telling About Yourself

Using "про" is also essential when you introduce yourself or share personal details with new acquaintances.

Я хо́чу розповісти́ про се́бе. (I want to tell about myself.)
Вона́ пи́ше про свою́ сім'ю́. (She is writing about her family.)
Він розповіда́є про свій день. (He is telling about his day.)

It creates a frame for your narrative. Master this small preposition, and you will be able to express a vast range of ideas and topics confidently.

Це книга про історію. Історія цікава. Це фільм про кохання. Кохання там. Я розповідаю про Україну. Україна велика. Ми говоримо про роботу. Ми думаємо про маму. Ми читаємо про Київ. Я пишу про місто. Це місто має історію. Ми говоримо про історію міста.

### Короткий підсумок у контексті — Short Summary in Context

Let us bring all these prepositions together. Seeing them in a continuous text helps your brain recognize the patterns naturally. Read the following paragraph carefully and identify the prepositions.

Сього́дні субо́та. Я іду́ в магази́н, а по́тім я іду́ на по́шту. Я хо́чу відпра́вити лист че́рез по́шту. Мій брат за́раз іде́ на стадіо́н. Він гра́є у футбо́л. Уве́чері ми ї́демо на конце́рт. Ми купи́ли квитки́ за ти́ждень. Я ду́же дя́кую бра́ту за квитки́. Конце́рт почне́ться че́рез годи́ну. Це конце́рт про любо́в і про Украї́ну. Ми йдемо́ че́рез парк на стадіо́н. Це ду́же шви́дко.

Today is Saturday. I am going to the shop, and then I am going to the post office. I want to send a letter through the post office. My brother is currently going to the stadium. He is playing football. In the evening we are going to a concert. We bought the tickets in a week. I thank my brother very much for the tickets. The concert will start in an hour. It is a concert about love and about Ukraine. We are walking through the park to the stadium. It is very fast.

Notice how every preposition serves a specific purpose. "В" and "на" show our destinations. "Че́рез" shows the medium of sending the letter, our path through the park, and the time until the concert. "За" shows the time it took to buy the tickets, and it shows gratitude. "Про" introduces the topic of the concert.

### Аналіз контексту — Context Analysis

When you read a dialogue like this, do not just translate the words. Analyze why a specific preposition was chosen.

Why did the person say "в магази́н" but "на по́шту"? Because the shop is viewed as an enclosed space, while the post office takes "на" by tradition. Why "на о́стрів"? Because an island is an open surface. Why "про цей фестива́ль"? Because the festival is the topic of the reading. Why "че́рез нови́й міст"? Because they are driving across the physical space of the bridge. Why "за вихідні́"? Because the weekend is the duration of time needed to finish reading the book. Why "за розмо́ву"? Because they are expressing gratitude for the exchange of words. Why "че́рез ти́ждень"? Because a full week will pass before they meet again.

Mastering these distinctions is the key to speaking natural, accurate Ukrainian.

In this module, you have learned how to navigate space, time, topics, and social interactions using Accusative prepositions.
You now know that:
- **в/у** denotes motion toward an enclosed destination.
- **на** denotes motion toward an open surface, a territory, or an event.
- **за** is essential for expressing gratitude, paying for items, indicating duration of effort, and moving behind objects.
- **через** is used for moving across a space, expressing the time elapsed before a future event, indicating a medium, and stating a cause.
- **про** is used to introduce the topic of your thoughts, words, and reading.
