## Мені подобається (I like)

It is time to learn how to express that something is pleasing to you. In English, you say "I like coffee". The person "I" is the one doing the action. Ukrainian grammar is different. Instead of you liking the object, you say that the object is pleasing to you.

Ми використовуємо нову фразу:

**мені́** — to me
**подо́бається** — appeals / pleases

Мені́ подо́бається ка́ва.
I like coffee.

Тобі подобається музи́ка.
You like music.

А мені подобається чита́ти.
And I like to read.

This structure might feel a bit strange at first because you are not the main character of the sentence. The thing you like is the actual star. It is the subject doing the action of pleasing you. Це цікавий факт.

:::warning[⚠️ Обережно!]
Watch out for a very common visual trap! English speakers naturally want to say "Я подо́баюсь ка́ва" because they automatically translate "I" as "Я". It is important to remember the rule: we use the set phrase "Мені́ подо́бається" instead. Це правило!
:::

What if you like many things? If the thing you like is plural, the verb must change slightly to match. Ми додаємо літеру -ю-. Це також важливо.

Мені́ подо́бається кві́тка.
I like the flower.

Тобі подобаються кві́ти.
You like flowers.

Мені́ подо́бається ціка́ва кни́га.
I like the interesting book.

А тобі подобаються ціка́ві книжки́.
And you like interesting books.

This construction works beautifully with actions, too. If you want to say you like doing a certain activity, simply add the action word right after our set phrase. The action word stays in its basic form.

Мені́ подо́бається чита́ти.
I like to read.

Тобі подобається ї́сти.
You like to eat.

Також мені подобається слу́хати музи́ку.
Also I like to listen to music.

Тобі подобається працювати.
You like to work.

It is very natural to use this structure to express your general aesthetic preferences or how you feel about a specific thing in the moment. Це дуже популярно.

## Я люблю (I love)

Sometimes simply saying that something appeals to you is not enough. When you have a strong, active enjoyment of something, or when you are talking about deep affection, you need a much stronger word. Ми знаємо це слово!

Ми використовуємо слово **люби́ти**.

Тут логіка як в англійській. You are the one doing the action. Це слово має свої форми.

Я люблю́.
I love.

Ти лю́биш.
You love.

Він лю́бить.
He loves.

Вона́ лю́бить.
She loves.

Ми лю́бимо.
We love.

Ви лю́бите.
You love.

Вони́ лю́блять.
They love.

:::tip[💡 Порада]
What is the difference? Use "подо́бається" when something appeals to your taste, catches your eye, or provides a mild pleasure. Use "люблю́" for active, passionate enjoyment or deep emotional connection. You can easily say "Мені́ подо́бається ця кни́га", but if it is your absolute favorite, you say "Я люблю́ цю кни́гу".
:::

Тут є важливе правило. When you actively love an object, that object is directly receiving the action of your love. For feminine words ending in **-а**, this means their ending changes to **-у**. Це Знахідний відмінок. This is the Accusative case for direct objects.

Ка́ва.
Coffee.

Я люблю́ ка́ву.
I love coffee.

Музи́ка.
Music.

Ти лю́биш музи́ку.
You love music.

You can also use this word with actions. Це дуже просто.

Я люблю́ чита́ти.
I love to read.

Ми лю́бимо ї́сти.
We love to eat.

Вони люблять слухати музику.
They love to listen to music.

Смачни́й борщ.
Tasty borscht.

Він лю́бить смачни́й борщ.
He loves tasty borscht.

Ціка́ва кни́га.
Interesting book.

Ми лю́бимо ціка́ву кни́гу.
We love the interesting book.

This word is very useful. You can use it for your favorite food, your hobbies, and the people you care about. Воно показує емоції. Це супер!

## Я хочу (I want)

Now we know how to talk about what we love. Ми також вчимо нові слова. We also need to learn how to express our desires and plans. Ми використовуємо слово **хоті́ти** for this.

This word is a little rebellious, which means it does not perfectly follow the standard conjugation rules. Воно має свої правила. We simply need to memorize them. Наприклад:

Я хо́чу.
I want.

Ти хо́чеш.
You want.

Він хо́че.
He wants.

Вона́ хо́че.
She wants.

Ми хо́чемо.
We want.

Ви хо́чете.
You want.

Вони́ хо́чуть.
They want.

We often combine this word with an infinitive to state our plans or desires for the day.

Я хо́чу ї́сти.
I want to eat.

Він хо́че піти́.
He wants to go.

Ми хо́чемо чита́ти ціка́ву кни́гу.
We want to read an interesting book.

You can also use it with objects. Just like with the word for love, if you want an object directly, and that object is a feminine word ending in **-а**, you must change that ending to **-у**. Це дуже логічно.

Я хо́чу ка́ву.
I want coffee.

Ти хо́чеш музи́ку.
You want music.

:::note[🏺 Культура]
In some languages and cultures, simply stating "I want" can sound a bit demanding or rude. In Ukrainian culture, saying "Я хо́чу" is a completely standard and polite way to express your desire. Whether you are relaxing at home with family or ordering a drink in a cafe, you do not need to soften it. Це стандарт!
:::

This is very simple for students. You just speak clearly and confidently:

Я хо́чу смачни́й борщ.
I want tasty borscht.

Вона хоче чай.
She wants tea.

## Порівняння (Comparing Likes)

Спілкування — це діалог. Ви говорите про себе. Потім ви запитуєте інших. Це важливо.

You can ask short questions to keep the conversation flowing smoothly. Це популярно:

А ти?
And you?

А ви?
And you?

Here is how we ask full questions using the phrases we have just learned. Remember that your voice should go up slightly at the end of the sentence to show it is a question.

Ти лю́биш чита́ти?
Do you love to read?

Ви хо́чете піти́?
Do you want to go?

Тобі подобається ця кава?
Do you like this coffee?

When we compare what you like with what someone else likes, the little word **а** is extremely useful. It creates a soft, natural contrast between two different statements.

Я люблю́ ка́ву, а він лю́бить чай.
I love coffee, and he loves tea.

Я хо́чу піти́, а ти хо́чеш чита́ти.
I want to go, and you want to read.

Мені подобається музика, а їй подобається кіно.
I like music, and she likes cinema.

This is a typical dialogue. Ми говоримо про себе. We can ask questions and then answer them to build a conversation.

<div className="conversation">

— Я люблю́ чита́ти. А ти?

— Мені́ подо́бається музи́ка.

— Ти хо́чеш піти́ в кафе?

— Ні, я хо́чу піти́ в кіно.

</div>

These small exchanges are the absolute building blocks of real communication. Asking questions shows your interest and keeps the dialogue alive and engaging. Це круто!

## 📋 Підсумок

We have learned three important phrases to express our inner world and our desires. We can express gentle appeal, strong passion, and direct, clear wants. Ми знаємо багато!

It is important to remember the key differences in how these phrases are built and when to use them.

| Phrase | Meaning & Use | Grammar Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Мені́ подо́бається** | Appeals to me (aesthetic, mild pleasure) | Subject is the object liked. | Мені́ подо́бається ка́ва. |
| **Я люблю́** | I love (strong active enjoyment, passion) | You are the subject. Feminine -а becomes -у. | Я люблю́ ка́ву. |
| **Я хо́чу** | I want (desire, plans, culturally polite) | You are the subject. Feminine -а becomes -у. | Я хо́чу ка́ву. |

This is a small test to check what we remember from this lesson. Try to answer these questions:

1. Which phrase should you use to tell someone "I love music"?
Я люблю́ музи́ку.

2. How do you say "I like coffee" using the grammatical structure that means "appeals to me"?
Мені́ подо́бається ка́ва.

3. If you want to confidently state "He wants to eat", which form of the verb is correct?
Він хо́че ї́сти.
