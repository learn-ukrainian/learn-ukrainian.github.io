> **Чому це важливо?**

> You need the imperative mood to give instructions. In Ukrainian, the way you give a command changes depending on who you are talking to — a friend or a stranger.

## Наказовий спосіб (Imperative Mood)

:::info[🌍 Діалог: Урок (Dialogue: The Lesson)]
<div className="conversation">

— Ти, читай це слово.

— Я читаю.

— Ти, читай також.

— Я також читаю.

— Ви, читайте ці слова.

— Ми читаємо ці слова.

— Люди, слухайте мене.

— Ми слухаємо.

— Ти, пиши тут.

— Я пишу.

— Ви, чекайте там.

— Ми чекаємо.

— Я тут.

— Добре, слухай.

</div>
:::

When you tell someone to do something, you use the imperative mood (наказовий спосіб). In Ukrainian, you cannot just use the dictionary form (the infinitive) to give a command to a person. Instead, the verb changes depending on whether you are talking to one person informally, or to a group/formally.

If you are speaking to one person informally (a friend, family member, or child), you use the **singular command**. For many regular verbs, you drop the final **-ти** from the dictionary form and add **-й** or **-и**.

| Dictionary Form | Singular Command | English Translation |
|---|---|---|
| **читати** | **читай** | read (informal) |
| **писати** | **пиши** | write (informal) |
| **слухати** | **слухай** | listen (informal) |
| **чекати** | **чекай** | wait (informal) |

If you are speaking to multiple people, or formally to an adult you don't know well, you use the **plural/formal command**. You create this simply by adding **-те** or **-ть** to the singular command form.

| Singular Command | Plural/Formal Command | English Translation |
|---|---|---|
| **читай** | **читайте** | read (plural/formal) |
| **пиши** | **пишіть** | write (plural/formal) |
| **слухай** | **слухайте** | listen (plural/formal) |
| **чекай** | **чекайте** | wait (plural/formal) |

:::warning[⚠️ A Common Mistake]
Never use the dictionary form to give a personal command. If you say ~~**Читати**~~ to a friend, it sounds like a robot or a written public rule on a wall. Always use the specific command form, like **читай** або **читайте**.
:::

Remember the golden rule of Ukrainian politeness: match the verb to the pronoun you would use. If you would call the person **ти** (you, informal), use the singular command. If you would use **ви** (you, formal/plural), use the plural command. Using the informal form with a stranger can sound rude!

| Українською | English Translation |
|---|---|
| Ти, пиши тут. | You, write here. |
| Ви, пишіть там. | You (plural), write there. |
| Ти, чекай мене. | You, wait for me. |
| Ви, чекайте мене. | You (plural), wait for me. |
| Ти, читай це слово. | You, read this word. |
| Ви, читайте ці слова. | You (plural), read these words. |
| Ти, слухай мене. | You, listen to me. |
| Ви, слухайте мене. | You (plural), listen to me. |
| Ти, дивись там. | You, look there. |
| Ви, дивіться там. | You (plural), look there. |

## Вісім обов'язкових дієслів (Eight Required Verbs)

:::info[🌍 Діалог: Робота (Dialogue: Work)]
<div className="conversation">

— Пиши це слово тут.

— Я пишу. Де ще?

— Дивись там. Іди до мене.

— Я іду. Дай це.

— Візьми це. Стій там.

— Я стою там.

— Люди, допоможіть також.

— Так, ми тут.

— Покажіть це.

— Я показую.

</div>
:::

Here are eight essential verbs you will use constantly in daily life. Some follow regular patterns, while others have slight stem changes in the imperative mood. When you learn these eight verbs, you can handle almost any basic daily interaction. You can ask for an item in a store, tell someone where to go, or ask someone to look at something important.

First, let's look at verbs that simply take **-й** and **-йте**. These are very common in classroom settings and daily conversations. You will hear these constantly when your teacher is speaking to the class.

| Dictionary Form | Command (Informal) | Command (Formal) |
|---|---|---|
| **читати** | **читай** | **читайте** |
| **слухати** | **слухай** | **слухайте** |
| **чекати** | **чекай** | **чекайте** |

The next group of verbs takes the endings **-и** (informal) and **-іть** (formal/plural). Notice how the stress shifts to the ending in words like **пиши́** and **іді́ть**. This change in stress makes the command sound stronger and clearer.

| Dictionary Form | Command (Informal) | Command (Formal) |
|---|---|---|
| **писати** | **пиши** | **пишіть** |
| **дивитися** | **дивись** | **дивіться** |
| **іти** | **іди** | **ідіть** |

:::tip[💡 Listening for Context]
The **-те** / **-ть** ending is a strong auditory cue. When you hear it, you know immediately that someone is either addressing a group of people or speaking formally to an individual. Pay attention to this sound when you are in public spaces like markets or train stations.
:::

Finally, here are some highly frequent verbs with irregular or unpredictable command forms. You will use these constantly when asking for items in a shop, getting someone's attention on the street, or asking for help with a task.

| Dictionary Form | Command (Informal) | Command (Formal) |
|---|---|---|
| **дати** | **дай** | **дайте** |
| **взяти** | **візьми** | **візьміть** |
| **сказати** | **скажи** | **скажіть** |
| **стояти** | **стій** | **стійте** |

Pay special attention to **дайте** (give me) and **скажіть** (tell me) — these are the absolute foundation of polite requests in Ukrainian shops, cafes, and streets. You cannot buy a coffee or ask for directions without knowing these two exact words.

| Українською | English Translation |
|---|---|
| Ти, скажи слово. | You, say the word. |
| Ви, дайте це. | You (plural), give this. |
| Ти, дивись там. | You, look there. |
| Ви, ідіть там. | You (plural), go there. |
| Ти, візьми це. | You, take this. |
| Ви, стійте тут. | You (plural), stand here. |
| Люди, слухайте! | People, listen! |
| Ти, покажи це. | You, show this. |
| Ви, допоможіть! | You (plural), help! |
| Ти, іди тут. | You, go here. |
| Ви, візьміть це. | You (plural), take this. |
| Ти, дай це. | You, give this. |
| Ви, скажіть це. | You (plural), say this. |
| Ти, чекай тут. | You, wait here. |
| Ви, чекайте там. | You (plural), wait there. |

## Ввічливе прохання (Polite Requests)

:::info[🌍 Діалог: Допомога (Dialogue: Help)]
<div className="conversation">

— Людино, допоможіть, будь ласка.

— Так, що це?

— Скажіть, будь ласка, де це слово.

— Покажіть це, будь ласка.

— Дивіться, ось це слово.

— Я слухаю. Це так. Дуже добре.

— Дайте це, будь ласка.

— Так, візьміть.

— Скажіть також це слово.

— Так, кажу.

</div>
:::

A direct command on its own can sound blunt or even aggressive, especially if you are speaking to a stranger. To soften a command into a polite request, simply add the phrase **будь ласка** (please). This is the easiest and most effective way to be polite in Ukrainian.

You have a lot of flexibility with word order. You can place **будь ласка** at the beginning, in the middle, or at the end of your request. The meaning remains exactly the same.

| Українською | English Translation |
|---|---|
| Будь ласка, читайте ці слова. | Please, read these words. |
| Дайте, будь ласка, це. | Give this, please. |
| Покажіть це, будь ласка. | Show this, please. |
| Допоможіть, будь ласка! | Please help! |
| Чекайте, будь ласка. | Wait, please. |
| Скажіть, будь ласка. | Tell me, please. |
| Візьміть, будь ласка, це. | Take this, please. |
| Дивіться, будь ласка, там. | Look there, please. |
| Ідіть, будь ласка, там. | Go there, please. |
| Слухайте, будь ласка, мене. | Listen to me, please. |
| Пишіть, будь ласка, тут. | Write here, please. |
| Стійте, будь ласка, там. | Stand there, please. |

:::note[🏺 Cultural Context]
The phrase **будь ласка** translates literally to "be kind." Ukrainians use it very generously in all situations. When you are asking a stranger for directions, buying food at a market, or speaking to a teacher, you should always include this phrase. It shows that you deeply respect the person you are speaking to.
:::

For the most polite and respectful request — ideal for strangers, older adults, and professionals — combine the formal command (ending in **-те** / **-ть**) with **будь ласка**. This is the standard formula for public interactions.

| Direct Command | Polite Request | English Translation |
|---|---|---|
| **Дайте** це. | **Дайте**, **будь ласка**, це. | Please give this. |
| **Скажіть** це. | **Скажіть**, **будь ласка**, це. | Please say this. |
| **Покажіть** це. | **Покажіть**, **будь ласка**, це. | Please show this. |
| **Допоможіть**! | **Допоможіть**, **будь ласка**! | Please help! |

Using this combination ensures you sound polite and respectful in any public situation. Never use the informal command form with a stranger without adding **будь ласка**, as it will sound like an order rather than a request.

## Заборони (Prohibitions)

:::info[🌍 Діалог: Заборона (Dialogue: Prohibition)]
<div className="conversation">

— Ти, читай це.

— Ні, не читай це.

— Що? Не дивись там.

— Так... я не дивлюсь.

— Не іди там. Стій тут.

— Я стою тут.

— Не слухай це.

— Я не слухаю.

</div>
:::

To tell someone *not* to do something, simply place the negative word **не** (not) directly in front of the imperative verb. The verb itself does not change its form.

| Positive Command | Negative Command | English Translation |
|---|---|---|
| **Іди** там. | **Не іди** там. | Do not go there. |
| **Слухайте** це. | **Не слухайте** це. | Do not listen to this. |
| **Дивись** там. | **Не дивись** там. | Do not look there. |
| **Чекай** тут. | **Не чекай** тут. | Do not wait here. |

:::info[ℹ️ Public Signs]
You will use these personal negative commands when speaking directly to people in conversations. However, public signs often use the dictionary form instead. A printed sign might use the dictionary form to say "Do not walk," but you would use the personal command form when speaking directly to your friend.
:::

This structure works exactly the same way for both informal and formal commands: **не** + verb. You can mix it with requests too.

| Українською | English Translation |
|---|---|
| Не пишіть там, будь ласка. | Do not write there, please. |
| Не чекай мене. | Do not wait for me. |
| Не ідіть там. | Do not go there. |
| Не дивіться на це. | Do not look at this. |
| Не беріть це. | Do not take this. |
| Не читайте ці слова. | Do not read these words. |
| Не слухай мене. | Do not listen to me. |
| Не стійте там. | Do not stand there. |

The imperative mood is your key to active communication in Ukrainian. You now have the tools to give commands, ask for help, and tell people what not to do. Remember these core principles for successful daily interactions:

1. **Match the formality:** Use singular forms (like **читай**) for informal situations with friends or children, and plural forms (like **читайте**) for groups and strangers.
2. **Be polite:** Always add **будь ласка** to soften a command into a polite request. This is especially important with formal commands.
3. **To forbid:** Just put the word **не** directly in front of the verb (**не читай**, **не пишіть**). The verb ending does not change.

## 📋 Підсумок

You now know how to use the imperative mood to give commands and make requests. You can distinguish between informal and formal situations, and you can soften your requests to be polite. You can also form negative commands to tell people what not to do.

1. What is the formal command form of **читати**?
   **Читайте**. (Read - formal/plural)
2. How do you say "Please give"?
   **Дайте**, **будь ласка**.
3. What is the negative command for "Do not write"?
   **Не пиши** або **Не пишіть**.
4. Which form do you use for friends?
   The singular command form, like **читай**.
5. How do you soften a command to make it polite?
   Add the phrase **будь ласка**.
