## Діало́ги (Dialogues)

Navigating a new city can feel like a puzzle, but with the right words, it becomes an adventure. In Ukraine, public transport is the lifeblood of urban life. Whether you are rushing to a meeting in Kyiv or exploring the cobblestone streets of Lviv, you will need to ask for directions and buy tickets. Let us look at two very common situations you will experience.

First, imagine you are standing on the street with your bags. You need to find the main train station, known as the **вокзал** (train station). You see a friendly passerby and decide to ask for help.

<div class="dialogue">


**Турист:** Ви́бачте, як діста́тися до вокза́лу? *(Excuse me, how do I get to the train station?)*


**Перехожий:** Ї́дьте авто́бусом або́ на метро́. *(Take a bus or the metro.)*


**Турист:** Яки́й авто́бус? *(Which bus?)*


**Перехожий:** Но́мер сім. Зупи́нка ось там. *(Number seven. The stop is right there.)*


**Турист:** Дякую! *(Thank you!)*


**Перехожий:** На здоро́в'я! *(You're welcome!)*


</div>


This short conversation is packed with practical tools. Notice the phrase **як дістатися до** (how to get to). This is the most polite, natural, and common way to ask for directions in Ukrainian. It always pairs with the preposition **до** (to) and the genitive case. Because **вокзал** is a masculine noun, it takes an **-у** ending here, becoming **до вокзалу** (to the station). 

The passerby answers with a direct instruction: **їдьте** (go / ride). This is a formal command, perfect for speaking to strangers. They offer two travel options: **автобусом** (by bus) or **на метро** (by metro). We will explore these two specific grammar patterns in the next section. Finally, they point to the **зупинка** (stop).

Now, imagine you have arrived at the station. You find the ticket counter, called the **каса** (ticket office). You need to buy a ticket for a journey across the country.

<div class="dialogue">


**Пасажи́р:** Оди́н квито́к до Льво́ва, будь ла́ска. *(One ticket to Lviv, please.)*


**Касир:** В один бік чи туди́ й наза́д? *(One-way or round-trip?)*


**Пасажир:** Туди й назад. Скі́льки ко́шту́є? *(Round-trip. How much does it cost?)*


**Касир:** П'ятсо́т гри́вень. *(Five hundred hryvnias.)*


**Пасажир:** О котрі́й відпра́влення? *(At what time is the departure?)*


**Касир:** О дев'я́тій ра́нку. *(At nine in the morning.)*


</div>


A **квиток** (ticket, m) is essential for any journey. When buying one, you use the exact same destination structure we saw earlier: **до** + genitive case. So, "to Lviv" becomes **до Львова**.

The cashier asks a very specific transport question: **в один бік** (one-way, literally "in one side") or **туди й назад** (round-trip, literally "there and back"). Once you clarify your route, you ask the golden question for any traveler: **скільки коштує** (how much does it cost). Finally, you confirm the schedule. The word **відправлення** means "departure". The cashier answers using the time format you already know from previous modules: **о дев'ятій** (at nine).

## Транспорт (Transport Types)

Ukraine has an extensive and varied public transport system. To navigate it smoothly, you need to recognize the different vehicles and know exactly how to say you are traveling by them. Let us build your transport vocabulary.

For getting around within a city, you have several primary options:
- **автобус** (bus, m)
- **тролейбус** (trolleybus, m)
- **трамвай** (tram, m)
- **метро** (metro, n)
- **маршрутка** (minibus, f)
- **таксі** (taxi, n)

The **автобус** is standard in every city. A **тролейбус** looks like a bus but runs quietly on electricity drawn from overhead wires; they are incredibly common across the country. A **трамвай** runs on steel rails through the city streets, adding a historic charm to cities like Lviv, Kyiv, and Odesa. The **метро** is the rapid underground subway system, currently operating in Kyiv, Kharkiv, and Dnipro. 

The **маршрутка** is a true cultural icon. These yellow minibuses operate on specific routes, but unlike regular municipal buses, they often stop exactly where you ask them to along the road. Finally, you can always take a **таксі** for a private, direct ride. 

Note that the words **метро** and **таксі** are borrowed from other languages and end in vowels. In Ukrainian grammar, this means they are indeclinable—their endings never change, no matter what grammatical case you use them in.

For traveling longer distances between cities, you will rely on:
- **потяг** (train, m)
- **автобус** (bus, m)
- **літак** (plane, m)

The **потяг** is the classic, romantic way to travel across Ukraine. The national railway network is vast and reliable. For international distances, you might take a **літак**.

When you want to say "by bus" or "by metro", Ukrainian uses two distinctly different grammatical patterns. You must memorize which transport uses which pattern. They both mean exactly the same thing in English ("by..."), but they are constructed differently.

**Pattern 1: The Instrumental Chunk**
Most standard, declinable vehicles take an instrumental case ending (usually **-ом** or **-єм**) without any preposition at all. Treat these as fixed chunks for now.
- **їхати автобусом** (to go by bus)
- **їхати троле́йбусом** (to go by trolleybus)
- **їхати трамваєм** (to go by tram)
- **їхати по́тягом** (to go by train)

Examples in action:
- Я ї́ду **автобусом**. *(I am going by bus.)*
- Ми ї́демо **потягом**. *(We are going by train.)*
- Вона́ ї́де **трамваєм**. *(She is going by tram.)*

**Pattern 2: На + Locative Chunk**
A few specific vehicles use the preposition **на** followed by the locative case. This pattern is strictly required for indeclinable borrowed words, and it is also the standard way to talk about traveling by a personal car.
- **їхати на метро** (to go by metro)
- **їхати на таксі** (to go by taxi)
- **їхати на маши́ні** (to go by car)

Examples in action:
- Він їде **на метро**. *(He is going by metro.)*
- Ти ї́деш **на таксі**. *(You are going by taxi.)*
- Вони́ ї́дуть **на машині**. *(They are going by car.)*

Always link the transport type with its specific pattern in your mind. When you want to say "by bus" (means of transport), use **автобусом** — not "на авто́бусі." The form "на автобусі" does exist, but it means "on the bus" (a physical location). For the means of getting somewhere, stick to the instrumental: **автобусом**.

## Ко́ри́сні фра́зи (Useful Phrases)

You know the vehicles, and you know how to talk about riding them. Now, let us look at the essential, practical phrases you need at the station, on the street, and during your actual journey.

When you are trying to find your transport, your most important word is **зупинка** (stop or station, f).
- Де **зупинка**? *(Where is the stop?)*
- Де **зупинка** авто́буса? *(Where is the bus stop?)*

Once you arrive, you need a **квиток** (ticket, m). Buying one at a station counter or from a driver is straightforward. You state the quantity, the destination, and add the polite "please".
- Один **квиток**, будь ласка. *(One ticket, please.)*
- Один **квиток** до Оде́си. *(One ticket to Odesa.)*

If you do not know the price beforehand, simply ask:
- Скільки коштує **квиток**? *(How much is a ticket?)*

If you are waiting at the railway station, watching the large departure boards, you might need to ask someone nearby about the schedule:
- Ко́ли насту́пний **потяг**? *(When is the next train?)*

Once you are actually sitting on the bus or the tram, you might feel a bit lost as the city rolls by. It is completely normal and expected to ask fellow passengers for help. If you do not recognize the street outside the window, you can ask:
- Яка це **зупинка**? *(What stop is this?)*

If you are unsure whether it is your turn to leave the vehicle, you can ask a specific verification phrase. We use the word **мені** (to me) here as part of a fixed chunk.
- **Мені** виходити тут? *(Do I get off here?)*

Sometimes, you need broader directions before you even find the transport. As we saw in the very first dialogue, the best way to ask is using the phrase **як дістатися до** (how to get to).
- Вибачте, **як дістатися до** парку? *(Excuse me, how do I get to the park?)*
- Вибачте, **як дістатися до** це́нтру? *(Excuse me, how do I get to the center?)*

When someone answers your question, listen carefully for these three directional words. They are absolutely crucial for your navigation.
- **прямо** (straight)
- **направо** (right)
- **наліво** (left)

You will often hear a combination of these words in a single response.
- Йдіть **прямо**, а по́тім **наліво**. *(Go straight, and then left.)*
- Зупинка там, **направо**. *(The stop is there, to the right.)*

Mastering these phrases gives you immense freedom. You no longer need to rely entirely on your phone's digital map application. You can interact with people, ask for guidance, and navigate the city like a true local.

## Підсумок — Summary

Let us review the essential tools you have gained for moving around in Ukrainian. You are now fully equipped to navigate cities, buy tickets, and find your way with confidence.

First, you learned the vocabulary for different types of transport. You know that an **автобус**, **тролейбус**, and **трамвай** will take you around the city above ground. If you want to travel rapidly underground, you look for the **метро**. For a fast, private ride, you call a **таксі**. And if you want to experience the true local flavor of a bustling Ukrainian city, you can hop on a yellow **маршрутка**. For longer journeys between cities, the **потяг** or the **літак** are your main options.

Next, we covered the two distinct grammatical patterns for saying "by transport". This is a key feature of natural Ukrainian speech.
You use the instrumental chunk pattern without a preposition for most large, declinable vehicles: **автобусом**, **тролейбусом**, **трамваєм**, **потягом**.
You use the **на** + locative pattern for indeclinable borrowed words and personal cars: **на метро**, **на таксі**, **на машині**.

We also explored the practical language of buying tickets and asking for directions. You can step up to a ticket counter and confidently say, "Один **квиток** до Києва, будь ласка" (One ticket to Kyiv, please). You can ask "Скільки коштує?" (How much does it cost?) to prepare your money.

When you are lost, you know exactly how to get help. You can stop someone politely and ask, "Вибачте, **як дістатися до** центру?" (Excuse me, how do I get to the center?). And most importantly, you will understand the answer when they point and say **прямо** (straight), **направо** (right), or **наліво** (left).

Finally, you know how to navigate the journey itself. You can locate your departure point by asking "Де **зупинка**?" (Where is the stop?). Once on board, you can confirm your location with "Яка це **зупинка**?" (What stop is this?) and ensure you do not miss your destination by asking "**Мені** виходити тут?" (Do I get off here?).

These phrases are the foundational building blocks of independent travel in Ukraine. Practice them aloud until they feel completely natural.

To test yourself, try these real-life scenarios out loud:
- You need to get to work tomorrow morning. How do you travel? (Я їду на робо́ту **автобусом** / **на метро**.)
- You are at the Kyiv railway station. Buy a round-trip ticket to Lviv. (Один **квиток** до Львова, **туди й назад**, будь ласка.)
- You are lost in the city center. Ask someone how to get to the park. (Вибачте, **як дістатися до** парку?)
