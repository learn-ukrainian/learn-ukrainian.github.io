> **Чому це важливо?**
> Numbers unlock the ability to shop, travel, and handle daily tasks independently. Knowing how to ask for prices and pay for things is one of the most useful skills you will learn.

## Вступ: Навіщо потрібні числа та гроші?

Те́ма сього́дні — чи́сла та гро́ші. (The topic today is numbers and money.) Це ду́же важли́ва те́ма. (This is a very important topic.) Ми купу́ємо проду́кти, о́дяг та ка́ву. (We buy groceries, clothes, and coffee.) Ми пла́тимо за квитки́ на авто́бус. (We pay for bus tickets.) Скрізь потрі́бні чи́сла. (Numbers are needed everywhere.)

Imagine you are in a bakery. You see a fresh loaf of bread. You want to buy it, but you need to know the price.

<div className="conversation">

— Скі́льки ко́штує хліб? (How much does the bread cost?)

— Два́дцять п'ять гри́вень. (Twenty-five hryvnias.)

</div>

This simple exchange happens millions of times a day across Ukraine. This module prepares you to confidently handle these situations. You get to count from zero to one hundred. You also learn to ask for the price. The final step is combining numbers with the word for the Ukrainian currency, the hryvnia. It is time to make your Ukrainian practical and ready for the real world!

Numbers are not just for shopping. You need them for telling time, reading bus schedules, and giving your phone number. By mastering numbers now, you are building a solid foundation for everything else you will do in Ukraine. «Скі́льки це ко́штує?» is your magic key. You will hear numbers everywhere: at the supermarket checkout, at the train station ticket office, and when paying for a taxi. We use numbers constantly.

Time to count!

## Презентація: Від нуля до ста

Let's start with the basics: numbers from zero to ten. These are the building blocks for all other numbers. They are essential to memorize.

Нуль. (Zero.)
Оди́н. (One.)
Два. (Two.)
Три. (Three.)
Чоти́ри. (Four.)
П'ять. (Five.)
Шість. (Six.)
Сім. (Seven.)
Ві́сім. (Eight.)
Де́в'ять. (Nine.)
Де́сять. (Ten.)

:::warning[⚠️ Warning]
Pay special attention to the apostrophe in **п'ять** and **де́в'ять**. In Ukrainian, the apostrophe means you must make a tiny pause before the next vowel. Do not blend the sounds together. It is like the slight pause in the English phrase «uh-oh». Pronounce the sounds clearly.
:::

Now, let us look at the numbers from eleven to nineteen. They are actually very logical. You take the base number, add **на** (on), and then add **дцять** (which is an old form of ten). So, eleven is literally «one on ten».

Одина́дцять. (Eleven.)
Двана́дцять. (Twelve.)
Трина́дцять. (Thirteen.)
Чотирна́дцять. (Fourteen.)
П'ятна́дцять. (Fifteen.)
Шістна́дцять. (Sixteen.)
Сімна́дцять. (Seventeen.)
Вісімна́дцять. (Eighteen.)
Дев'ятна́дцять. (Nineteen.)

:::tip[💡 Tip]
Notice where the stress falls. The stress is consistently on **-на́-** for all teen numbers! Одина́дцять, двана́дцять, трина́дцять. Keep that rhythm in your mind when you say them out loud.
:::

Next, we count by tens. These are formed by combining a base number with the word for ten.

Два́дцять. (Twenty.)
Три́дцять. (Thirty.)
Со́рок. (Forty.)
П'ятдеся́т. (Fifty.)
Шістдеся́т. (Sixty.)
Сімдеся́т. (Seventy.)
Вісімдеся́т. (Eighty.)
Дев'яно́сто. (Ninety.)
Сто. (One hundred.)

:::warning[⚠️ Warning]
Do you see how **п'ять** has a soft sign at the end? When you build **п'ятдеся́т** (fifty), that soft sign disappears! The same happens with **шістдеся́т**, **сімдеся́т**, and **вісімдеся́т**. Writing a soft sign in the middle of these words is a very common spelling mistake. Do not fall into this visual trap.
:::

Now that we have our numbers, we need to put them together. To make a number like twenty-one, simply say «twenty» and «one». It works just like English.

Два́дцять оди́н. (Twenty-one.)
Со́рок п'ять. (Forty-five.)
Сто. (One hundred.)

Finally, we need to talk about the «1-2-5 Rule». In English, you say «one dollar» and «two dollars». In Ukrainian, the word changes depending on the number before it. We will use the Ukrainian currency, **гри́вня**, to practice this rule. It is a very important lexical pattern.

Якщо́ число́ закі́нчується на оди́н. (If the number ends in one.)
Ми ка́жемо: одна́ гри́вня. (We say: one hryvnia.)

Якщо́ число́ закі́нчується на два, три, чоти́ри. (If the number ends in two, three, four.)
Ми ка́жемо: дві гри́вні. (We say: two hryvnias.)

Якщо́ число́ закі́нчується на п'ять і бі́льше. (If the number ends in five and more.)
Ми ка́жемо: п'ять гри́вень. (We say: five hryvnias.)

| Число́ (Number) | Фо́рма сло́ва (Word form) | При́клад (Example) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | гри́вня | одна́ гри́вня |
| 2, 3, 4 | гри́вні | дві гри́вні, три гри́вні |
| 5... 20, 0 | гри́вень | п'ять гри́вень, де́сять гри́вень |

Why did we use **одна́** instead of **оди́н**, and **дві** instead of **два**? The word **гри́вня** is feminine. The numbers one and two change to match the gender of the noun.

Оди́н до́лар. (One dollar.)
Одна́ гри́вня. (One hryvnia.)
Два до́лари. (Two dollars.)
Дві гри́вні. (Two hryvnias.)

## Практика та Культура: Рахуємо гроші

Now it is time to put our knowledge into practice. You are at the bazaar or a local cafe. You want to buy some things. Here are the most important phrases you will need.

Скі́льки це ко́штує? (How much does this cost?)
Яка́ ціна́? (What is the price?)
Скі́льки з ме́не? (How much do I owe you?)

Let us imagine a conversation at a cafe.

<div className="conversation">

— До́брий день! (Good afternoon!)

— До́брий день! Що бажа́єте? (Good afternoon! What would you like?)

— Ка́ву й торт, будь ла́ска. (A coffee and a cake, please.)

— До́бре. (Okay.)

— Скі́льки з ме́не? (How much do I owe you?)

— Ка́ва ко́штує со́рок гри́вень. (Coffee costs forty hryvnias.) Торт ко́штує шістдеся́т гри́вень. (The cake costs sixty hryvnias.) Ра́зом — сто гри́вень. (Together — one hundred hryvnias.)

</div>

Sometimes, prices might surprise you. You can express your opinion using these words.

Це де́шево. (This is cheap.)
Це ду́же до́рого! (This is very expensive!)

When paying with cash, you might hand the seller a large bill. It is always polite to ask if they have change.

У вас є зда́ча? (Do you have change?)
Так, у ме́не є зда́ча. (Yes, I have change.)

:::note[🏺 Culture]
The word **гри́вня** has a rich history. It comes from the word «грива», which means neck or mane. Back in the 11th century during the times of Kyivan Rus, a «hryvnia» was a valuable gold or silver ornament worn around the neck. Later, it became a standard measure of weight for precious metals, and eventually, the name of the national currency.
:::

Let us practice some simple math with our new vocabulary.

Де́сять плюс п'ять дорі́внює п'ятна́дцять. (Ten plus five equals fifteen.)
Сто мі́нус два́дцять дорі́внює вісімдеся́т. (One hundred minus twenty equals eighty.)
Три́дцять плюс де́сять дорі́внює со́рок. (Thirty plus ten equals forty.)
П'ятдеся́т мі́нус п'ять дорі́внює со́рок п'ять. (Fifty minus five equals forty-five.)

Now, let us apply the «1-2-5 Rule» with some random prices you might see at a local market.

Скі́льки ко́штує вода́? (How much does water cost?)
Вода́ ко́штує два́дцять одну́ гри́вню. (Water costs twenty-one hryvnias.)

Notice we used **одну́** and **гри́вню** because it is the object of the sentence. But for now, just memorize the chunks.

Скі́льки ко́штує сік? (How much does juice cost?)
Сік ко́штує три́дцять дві гри́вні. (Juice costs thirty-two hryvnias.)

Скі́льки ко́штує чай? (How much does tea cost?)
Чай ко́штує п'ятдеся́т гри́вень. (Tea costs fifty hryvnias.)

:::info[💡 Did You Know?]
Ukrainians have a popular proverb about saving money: «Копі́йка гри́вню береже́». It literally translates to «A kopeck saves a hryvnia», which is very similar to the English saying «A penny saved is a penny earned». The **копі́йка** is the smaller subunit of the currency. There are 100 kopecks in one hryvnia.
:::

## 📋 Підсумок

You have successfully learned how to count from zero to one hundred in Ukrainian! You also learned how to use these numbers in a practical setting, like shopping at a market or ordering at a cafe.

The most important takeaway from this lesson is the «1-2-5 Rule». Remember that the form of the noun changes based on the last digit of the number.

Одна́ гри́вня. (One hryvnia.)
Дві гри́вні. (Two hryvnias.)
П'ять гри́вень. (Five hryvnias.)

Also, do not forget to match the gender! It is **одна́** and **дві** for feminine words like **гри́вня**, but **оди́н** and **два** for masculine words. And keep an eye out for those spelling traps—remember that **п'ятдеся́т** and **шістдеся́т** do not have a soft sign in the middle! Practice reading prices aloud whenever you can.

Check yourself before moving on:
1. How do you say «2 hryvnias» versus «5 hryvnias»?
2. Where does the stress fall in the number «11» (одинадцять)?
3. How do you correctly ask «How much does it cost?» in Ukrainian?
