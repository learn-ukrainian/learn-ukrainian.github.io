> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> Grammar is the engine that drives your ability to communicate in the real world. Cases are not just abstract rules found in textbooks; they are the precise coordinates that help you navigate streets, find your friends, and express exactly what you need in a bustling city environment.

## Огляд (Overview)

Welcome to this critical checkpoint in your language learning journey. You have absorbed a significant amount of vocabulary and grammar over the past modules. Now, it is time to deliberately pause and consolidate your knowledge. We are going to comprehensively review how you form words and build logical sentences using the fundamental Ukrainian case system. Think of this lesson as a diagnostic tool. We want to ensure your grammatical foundation is absolutely solid before we introduce more complex sentence structures in the next phase of the course.

As your patient and supportive tutor, I want to emphasize that making mistakes with cases is a completely normal part of the process. Every language learner experiences moments of hesitation when trying to remember the right noun ending mid-conversation. However, understanding the underlying logic behind these changes makes the process much smoother and less intimidating. Ukrainian uses cases to create a precise map of relationships between words. Without these specific endings, a sentence is just a chaotic list of dictionary terms. With the correct endings, your sentences become clear, logical, and incredibly easy for a native speaker to follow and understand.

In this module, we will focus intensely on three specific grammatical tools. First, we will examine the Accusative case (Знахідний відмінок). This case allows you to interact with direct objects and talk confidently about your destination. Second, we will review the Locative case (Місцевий відмінок). This is your primary, indispensable tool for describing static physical locations. Third, we will look at a very specific, highly frequent use of the Genitive case (Родовий відмінок): expressing the complete absence of something.

To make this extensive review practical and immediately useful, we will anchor all our grammar examples in the context of city navigation. Ukrainian cities are fundamentally designed for walking. Whether you are strolling through the historic cobblestone center of Lviv, finding a specific monumental square in Kyiv, or simply meeting a friend at a local neighborhood cafe, you absolutely need to know how to ask for and understand directions. Navigation inherently relies on distinguishing between where you are right now and where you are going.

:::note[🏺 Орієнтація в місті]
Ukrainians are generally very hospitable and helpful when you ask for directions on the street. If you stop someone and ask «Де тут кафе?» (Where is a cafe here?), be prepared for a very detailed, enthusiastic response. Understanding the case endings they use will help you visualize the exact route they are describing, rather than just guessing based on hand gestures.
:::

Take your time working through this material. Read the Ukrainian examples out loud, pay close attention to the structural patterns, and trust the learning process. You are building a permanent linguistic skill that will serve you every single time you speak the language.

## Навичка 1: Відмінки (Skill 1: Cases)

### Модель: Огляд

The core functional purpose of a case is to change the ending of a noun to accurately reflect its role in the sentence. Let's break down the three cases you need for everyday navigation and social interaction. We will examine each category step by step to ensure total clarity.

### Знахідний відмінок: Неживі об'єкти

The Accusative case is fundamentally known as the case of the direct object. When an action is directly performed upon a thing, that thing must take the Accusative case. For inanimate objects (items, physical places, concepts that are not biologically alive), the grammatical rules are quite forgiving and follow a highly predictable pattern.

If you are dealing with a masculine or neuter inanimate noun, you do not need to change anything at all. The word remains exactly as it appears in the dictionary (the Nominative case). The real grammatical transformation happens exclusively with feminine nouns. If a feminine noun ends in «-а», that ending shifts definitively to «-у». If the feminine noun ends in «-я», the ending shifts to «-ю».

Let's look at how this works in practice when you interact with common objects in a city environment. Notice how the feminine nouns clearly change their shape.

- Я бачу нови́й парк. (I see a new park. — Masculine, no change)
- Він шукає вели́кий музей. (He is looking for a big museum. — Masculine, no change)
- Я замовляю мале́ньку каву. (I order a small coffee. — Feminine, changes to «-у»)
- Вона читає цікаву книгу. (She is reading an interesting book. — Feminine, changes to «-у»)

Whenever you buy a transit ticket, order a meal at a restaurant, or spot a famous landmark, you are utilizing this specific grammatical mechanism. The action transfers from you directly onto the object, and the case ending proves it.

### Знахідний відмінок: Живі об'єкти

The grammatical system shifts slightly when the direct object of your sentence is a living being. In Ukrainian grammar, "animate" refers primarily to humans and animals. Understanding this structural distinction is crucial for clear, unambiguous communication.

For feminine animate nouns, the transformation process is exactly the same as for inanimate objects we discussed previously. However, masculine animate nouns behave entirely differently. Instead of retaining their basic dictionary form, masculine animate nouns adopt the endings typically associated with the Genitive case, which means they take an «-а» or «-я».

This specific structural change helps listeners instantly recognize that a person or animal is receiving the action, preventing any confusion when the sentence word order is flexible.

- Я бачу студента. (I see a student. — Masculine animate, takes «-а»)
- Вона зустрічає лікаря. (She meets the doctor. — Masculine animate, takes «-я»)
- Брат обіймає сестру. (The brother hugs the sister. — Feminine animate, takes «-у»)
- Ми годуємо собаку. (We feed the dog. — Feminine animate, takes «-у»)

If you are waiting for a friend at a busy intersection and finally spot them in the crowd, you will absolutely need this animate form to describe the event accurately.

:::warning[⚠️ Живе чи неживе?]
A very common mistake for beginners is forgetting to apply the genitive-style ending to masculine animate nouns. Remember, saying "Я бачу студент" is grammatically incorrect and sounds very unnatural. You must say "Я бачу студента". Always ask yourself if the masculine direct object is breathing!
:::

### Місцевий відмінок: Локація

While the Accusative case expertly manages dynamic actions and direction, the Locative case is completely static. It serves one singular, unwavering purpose: to answer the question **де** (where). This case is essentially a linguistic pin dropped firmly onto a map.

The Locative case is distinctive among Ukrainian cases because it strictly requires a preposition. You will never see it floating alone in a sentence. It must always be attached to a preposition, most commonly «у», «в», or «на». The word endings typically involve adding an «-і» or «-у» to the noun base, depending on its gender and final consonant.

Let's observe the Locative case performing its job of establishing a static position in physical space.

- Де ти зараз? (Where are you right now?)
- Я працюю у вели́кому місті. (I work in a big city.)
- Вона сидить у мале́нькому кафе. (She sits in a small cafe.)
- Твій телефон лежить на столі. (Your phone is lying on the table.)

Every single time you need to inform someone of your current location or find where something is stored, you must activate the Locative case and its required prepositions.

### Родовий відмінок: Відсутність

The Genitive case is a massive grammatical category with dozens of functions, but at the A1 level, we focus intensely on its most frequent and practical application: expressing absolute absence. Whenever you want to state that something or someone is simply not present, you use the powerful trigger word **нема́є** (there is no), immediately followed by the Genitive case.

The Genitive case answers the questions: Кого немає? Чого немає? (Who is not here? What is not here?). This construction is essential for describing what a place lacks, or when a store has run out of your favorite item.

- Тут нема́є ка́ви. (There is no coffee here.)
- У ме́не нема́є часу. (I have no time.)
- На вулиці нема́є машини. (There is no car on the street.)
- Сьогодні нема́є дощу. (There is no rain today.)

:::info[ℹ️ Походження слова]
The critical word «немає» literally translates to "does not have." Over centuries of use, the space between the negation particle «не» and the verb «має» completely disappeared, creating a single, unified word dedicated entirely to expressing absence.
:::

## Навичка 2: Прийменники (Skill 2: Prepositions)

### Модель: Прийменники

Prepositions are tiny words that carry immense grammatical authority. They strictly dictate exactly which case the following noun must assume. Let's thoroughly analyze how different prepositions interact with the cases we just reviewed.

### Напрямок чи Місце?

One of the deepest and most persistent traps for English speakers is the concept of spatial questioning. In English, we rely heavily and lazily on the single word "where" for both location ("Where are you?") and direction ("Where are you going?"). Ukrainian demands absolute precision and splits this concept into clear, distinct categories: static location, direction toward a goal, and origin.

When you ask about a static, unchanging location, you use the question word **де** (where at). This strictly requires the Locative case with the prepositions «в», «у», or «на».
When you ask about a dynamic destination, you use the question word **куди́** (where to). This triggers the Accusative case, also utilizing «в», «у», or «на».
When you ask about the origin of a movement, you use the question word **зві́дки** (from where). This requires the Genitive case with the prepositions «з» or «із».

Let's carefully compare these fundamental spatial concepts to see how the grammar shifts entirely based on movement.

**Статика (Static Location): Де? + Місцевий відмінок**
- Де ти працюєш? (Where do you work?)
- Я працюю в школі. (I work in the school. — Locative case)

**Динаміка (Direction Toward): Куди? + Знахідний відмінок**
- Куди́ ти йдеш? (Where are you going?)
- Я йду в школу. (I am going to the school. — Accusative case)

**Походження (Origin From): Звідки? + Родовий відмінок**
- Зві́дки ти йдеш? (Where are you coming from?)
- Я йду зі школи. (I am coming from the school. — Genitive case)

:::danger[🛡️ Пастка перекладу]
Do not try to translate the English word "where" directly into Ukrainian without thinking deeply about the context. Always ask yourself first: Is there movement? If there is movement toward a goal, use «куди». If there is absolutely no movement, use «де». This single mental habit will fix the vast majority of your prepositional errors.
:::

### Інші прийменники зі Знахідним відмінком

The Accusative case is not arbitrarily limited to physical destinations. It also closely partners with several other highly frequent prepositions. We need to look very closely at three specific words: **че́рез** (through/across), **за** (for/behind), and **про** (about).

The preposition «через» is indispensable for describing the specific path you take through a city or landscape. It forces the following noun into the Accusative case.
- Ми йдемо че́рез краси́вий парк. (We are walking through a beautiful park.)
- Вона обережно переходить че́рез вулицю. (She carefully crosses through/across the street.)
- Я бачу тебе че́рез вікно. (I see you through the window.)

The preposition «за» has multiple meanings depending on the context, but it is most commonly used to express gratitude or an exchange of value in a transaction.
- Дякую за каву. (Thank you for the coffee.)
- Він платить за квиток. (He is paying for the ticket.)
- Ми дякуємо за допомогу. (We say thank you for the help.)

The preposition «про» is used to clearly indicate the subject of a thought, a deep conversation, or a written text.
- Я часто думаю про нове місто. (I often think about the new city.)
- Ми читаємо цікаву книгу про Київ. (We are reading an interesting book about Kyiv.)
- Вона розповідає про брата. (She is telling about the brother.)

### Зведена таблиця прийменників

To assist your memory and provide a quick reference, here is a consolidated summary table. Review how each preposition systematically demands a specific case and answers a specific question.

| Прийменник (Preposition) | Питання (Question) | Відмінок (Case) | Приклад (Example) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| у, в, на | Де? (Where at?) | Місцевий (Locative) | Я зараз у парку. |
| у, в, на | Куди? (Where to?) | Знахідний (Accusative) | Я йду в парк. |
| через | Як? Куди? (Through where?) | Знахідний (Accusative) | Я йду через парк. |
| про | Про що? (About what?) | Знахідний (Accusative) | Я думаю про парк. |
| за | За що? (For what?) | Знахідний (Accusative) | Дякую за парк. |

## Навичка 3: Узгодження (Skill 3: Agreement)

### Модель: Узгодження

Words in a Ukrainian sentence behave exactly like members of a highly synchronized team. When the main noun changes its case to fit a new role, all the supporting words—like adjectives and pronouns—must fundamentally change their forms to match. We call this critical grammatical process agreement (узгодження).

*(Observe how the adjective and pronoun endings match their nouns in the examples below).*

### Самоперевірка: Узгодження

*(Check if you can form a complete agreement chain correctly).*

### Узгодження прикметників

Adjectives are fiercely loyal to their nouns. They must mirror the noun's gender, number, and case perfectly, without exception. Let's practice this strict synchronization using four common adjectives: **нови́й** (new), **вели́кий** (big), **краси́вий** (beautiful), and **мале́нький** (small).

When a noun enters the Accusative case (inanimate), masculine and neuter adjectives hold their ground and do not change their spelling. Feminine adjectives, however, must change their ending to «-у» or «-ю» to match the noun.

- Я бачу вели́кий будинок. (I see a big building. — Masculine, no change)
- Він купує краси́ву книгу. (He buys a beautiful book. — Feminine, takes «-у»)
- Ми любимо мале́нький театр. (We love the small theater. — Masculine, no change)

When a noun enters the Locative case, the adjective must transform significantly to signal the static location. Masculine and neuter adjectives typically adopt the «-ому» ending, while feminine adjectives usually take the «-ій» ending.

- Ми багато гуляємо у вели́кому місті. (We walk a lot in a big city. — Neuter, takes «-ому»)
- Кіт зараз спить на нові́й вулиці. (The cat is sleeping right now on a new street. — Feminine, takes «-ій»)
- Вона працює у мале́нькому кафе. (She works in a small cafe. — Neuter, takes «-ому»)

To make this completely clear, here is a comparison table of adjective endings.

| Відмінок (Case) | Чоловічий (Masc.) | Жіночий (Fem.) | Середній (Neut.) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Знахідний (Accusative Inanimate) | нови́й | нову́ | нове́ |
| Місцевий (Locative) | ново́му | нові́й | ново́му |

### Відмінювання займенників

Pronouns—the useful words that replace nouns—also undergo complete phonetic transformations depending on their grammatical role. The most vital pronouns for daily social interaction are **я** (I), **ти** (you), and **він/вона/воно** (he/she/it).

When you become the direct object of an action (Accusative case), the word «я» transforms entirely into **ме́не**, «ти» becomes **тебе́**, and «він» becomes **його́**.

- Ти слухаєш ме́не? (Are you listening to me?)
- Я дуже добре розумію тебе́. (I understand you very well.)
- Вона щиро любить його́. (She sincerely loves him.)

There is an extremely important, non-negotiable phonetic rule regarding the third-person pronouns. Whenever the pronouns «його», «її», or «їх» directly follow any preposition, you must attach the prefix «н-» to the beginning of the word. This rule exists strictly to ensure euphony (милозвучність) — it actively prevents awkward vowel clashes and makes the language flow musically.

- Я йду до ньо́го. (I am going to him. — Preposition triggers «н-»)
- Це гарний подарунок для неї. (This is a nice gift for her. — Preposition triggers «н-»)
- Я бачу його́. (I see him. — No preposition, therefore no «н-»)

:::note[📝 Милозвучність]
Ukrainian deeply values the melodic flow of speech. The mandatory addition of the "н-" prefix is just one prominent example of euphony. You will also notice native speakers naturally alternating between «у» and «в», or «і» and «й», simply to avoid placing too many heavy consonants or identical vowels together. Trust your ear; if a combination is hard to pronounce smoothly, there is usually a more melodic alternative form available.
:::

### Ланцюжки узгодження

The ultimate, undeniable test of your grammatical control is building a complete agreement chain. A chain occurs when you purposefully lock a preposition, an adjective, and a noun together into a single cohesive unit of meaning. All three elements must operate in exactly the same case.

Observe carefully how these chains operate in different contexts to deliver precise, unambiguous meaning.

- Ми йдемо че́рез вели́кий ліс. (We are walking through a big forest. — Accusative chain)
- Вони щасливо живуть у мале́нькому селі. (They happily live in a small village. — Locative chain)
- Я щиро дякую за краси́ву пісню. (I sincerely give thanks for a beautiful song. — Accusative chain)
- Тут, на жаль, нема́є нового́ кафе. (Here, unfortunately, there is no new cafe. — Genitive chain)

Constructing these complex chains requires intense focus initially, but with consistent, dedicated practice, your brain will start treating these grammatical clusters as single, automated chunks of language.

## Інтеграційне завдання (Integration Task)

We have systematically analyzed the individual, isolated components of the case system. Now, it is time to integrate them fully. We will examine a realistic dialogue scenario, perform a deep analysis of the grammar used, and then you will actively reflect on your own readiness to advance.

### Навігаційний сценарій

Please read the following dialogue carefully. Two friends, Oksana and Maksym, are trying to locate each other in the busy center of town. Pay close attention to every preposition and case ending they naturally employ.

Оксана: Привіт! Де ти зараз? Я тебе́ не бачу.
Максим: Привіт! Я зараз у вели́кому парку. А ти де?
Оксана: Я йду че́рез краси́ву площу. Куди́ ми йдемо сьогодні?
Максим: Давай підемо в нове́ кафе. Вони мають дуже смачну каву.
Оксана: Добре. Але де це кафе?
Максим: Вони відкрили його́ біля старого театру. Я чекаю на тебе́.
Оксана: О, я бачу вели́кий театр. Але тут нема́є кафе.
Максим: Подивись за кут. Я йду до те́бе!

### Аналіз тексту

Let's systematically break down the exact grammatical choices Oksana and Maksym made during their conversation. Every single ending serves a specific communicative purpose.

When Oksana says, «Я тебе́ не бачу», she correctly uses the Accusative form of the pronoun «ти» because Maksym is the direct object of her inability to see.

Maksym establishes his static physical location by saying, «Я зараз у вели́кому парку». Because he is answering the conceptual question «де?», he uses the preposition «у» followed immediately by the Locative agreement chain «великому парку» (both words actively taking Locative endings).

Oksana describes her current forward trajectory: «Я йду че́рез краси́ву площу». The specific preposition «через» strictly demands the Accusative case. Therefore, both the adjective and the noun adopt the feminine Accusative «-у» ending seamlessly.

When Maksym suggests a future destination, he says, «Давай підемо в нове́ кафе». This sentence inherently involves movement toward a goal. He asks the conceptual question «куди?», which perfectly requires the preposition «в» and the Accusative case. The neuter adjective «нове» and the foreign noun «кафе» do not change their spelling, but they are functioning grammatically in the Accusative case.

Oksana encounters a navigational problem and states, «Тут нема́є кафе». The presence of the absolute trigger word «немає» instantly forces the noun into the Genitive case. (Although the loan word "кафе" does not change its form, its grammatical role here is undeniably Genitive).

Finally, look closely at the pronoun interaction. Maksym says, «Я чекаю на тебе́». The preposition «на» triggers the Accusative form of the pronoun. Later he reassures her, «Я йду до те́бе!». The preposition «до» triggers the Genitive form of the pronoun, which also happens to be spelled «тебе».

:::info[🤔 Ваша черга]
When you read the dialogue the very first time, did you naturally understand the profound grammatical difference between Maksym standing still IN the park and Oksana walking dynamically THROUGH the square? That instantaneous, intuitive comprehension is the ultimate goal of learning cases.
:::
