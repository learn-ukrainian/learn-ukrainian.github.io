## Діало́ги (Dialogues)

Numbers unlock the real Ukraine. You need them at the Бессарабський ри́нок to haggle for cheese, at a кав'я́рня to pay for your ка́ва, or when a new friend asks for your phone number. 

Imagine you are standing in a vibrant, bustling Ukrainian market. You see a beautiful bag you want to buy. Unlike in a supermarket where prices are printed on barcodes, here you must talk to the seller. Let us look at a typical interaction.

<div class="dialogue">


**Олена:** До́брого дня! Скі́льки ко́шту́є ця су́мка? *(Good day! How much does this bag cost?)*


**Продавець:** Дві́сті гри́вень. *(Two hundred hryvnias.)*


**Олена:** А ця? *(And this one?)*


**Продавець:** Сто п'ятдеся́т. *(One hundred fifty.)*


**Олена:** До́бре, я беру́ цю. *(Okay, I'll take this one.)*


</div>



The word **скільки** is incredibly versatile. It literally means "how much" or "how many". You will see it constantly. The word **коштує** is the verb "costs", which comes from the infinitive **коштувати**. Together, **Скільки коштує?** is your essential phrase for shopping anywhere in Ukraine. 

The seller replies with prices in **гривня** (hryvnia), the Ukrainian currency. He says **двісті** (two hundred) and **сто п'ятдесят** (one hundred fifty). 

Notice that Olena says **я беру цю** (I take this one). You already know **ця** (this) for feminine nouns like **сумка** (bag). Here it changes to **цю** because it is the object of her action. Do not worry about the grammar rules for this right now. Just memorize **я беру цю** as a highly useful shopping chunk.

Numbers are also essential for talking about age. In Module 5, you learned how to introduce yourself. Now let us add one more detail to those introductions — your age. 

<div class="dialogue">


**Максим:** Скільки тобі́ ро́кі́в? *(How old are you?)*


**Анна:** Ме́ні два́дцять п'ять. А тобі? *(I am twenty-five. And you?)*


**Максим:** Мені три́дцять два. А твоя́ сестра́? *(I am thirty-two. And your sister?)*


**Анна:** Їй вісімна́дцять. *(She is eighteen.)*


</div>



In Ukrainian, we do not say "I am 25 years old." The structure is literally "To me is 25 years." 

The question is **Скільки тобі років?** (How old are you?). 

The answer uses a set formula. You say **Мені** (to me), add the number, and finish with the word for years. 
*   **Мені двадцять п'ять.** (I am twenty-five.)

For asking someone else, use **тобі** (to you). For talking about a woman or girl, use **їй** (to her). You just plug the number into this formula. The word for "years" changes depending on the exact number. Here is the pattern to memorize:
*   1, 21, 31... → **рік**: Мені двадцять оди́н **рік**.
*   2, 3, 4, 22, 23... → **роки**: Мені тридцять два **роки**.
*   5–20, 25, 30... → **років**: Мені двадцять п'ять **років**.

Ukrainians learn these combinations naturally as memorized chunks, and you should do the same. Start with the most common ones — most ages you will hear end in **років**.

## Чи́сла 1-20 (Numbers 1-20)

Let us start with the foundation. The numbers from one to ten are the building blocks for all other numbers. Take your time to memorize these first ten numbers perfectly. If you know them well, all the higher numbers will feel completely natural and logical.

*   1 — **один** (one)
*   2 — **два** (two)
*   3 — **три** (three)
*   4 — **чотири** (four)
*   5 — **п'ять** (five)
*   6 — **шість** (six)
*   7 — **сім** (seven)
*   8 — **вісім** (eight)
*   9 — **дев'ять** (nine)
*   10 — **десять** (ten)

Pronunciation is crucial here. Pay special attention to the apostrophe in **п'ять** and **дев'ять**. The apostrophe means the consonant before it is hard, and the vowel after it sounds like a distinct "ya" sound. It creates a tiny separation, almost like a micro-pause. 

For the number **сім** (seven), make sure you use the soft Ukrainian "і" sound, like the vowel in the English word "machine". Do not pronounce it as "sem". 

When you count physical objects, you will notice that the noun often changes its ending. Do not try to memorize complex grammar rules right now. It is much more effective to observe and learn these common phrases as complete patterns. 

*   **один стіл** (one table)
*   **два стільці** (two chairs)
*   **три книги** (three books)

Now let us look at the numbers from eleven to twenty. In English, we add "-teen" to the base number. Ukrainian does exactly the same thing. We add the suffix **-надцять** to the base number. Historically, this suffix comes from the phrase "на десять" (on ten). So, "one on ten" becomes eleven.

*   11 — **одинадцять** (eleven)
*   12 — **дванадцять** (twelve)
*   13 — **тринадцять** (thirteen)
*   14 — **чотирнадцять** (fourteen)
*   15 — **п'ятнадцять** (fifteen)
*   16 — **шістнадцять** (sixteen)
*   17 — **сімнадцять** (seventeen)
*   18 — **вісімнадцять** (eighteen)
*   19 — **дев'ятнадцять** (nineteen)
*   20 — **двадцять** (twenty) — literally "two tens".

:::tip
There is a golden rule for pronouncing numbers from 11 to 19. The stress ALWAYS falls on the syllable **на** in the suffix **-надцять**. 
Listen carefully: оди-НА-дцять, два-НА-дцять, три-НА-дцять. This regular stress pattern makes these numbers easy to say naturally.
:::

## Деся́тки і со́тні (Tens and Hundreds)

Counting by tens is very straightforward. Most of them are just the base number plus **-дцять** or **-десят**. Both suffixes simply mean "ten".

*   20 — **двадцять** (twenty)
*   30 — **тридцять** (thirty)
*   40 — **сорок** (forty)
*   50 — **п'ятдесят** (fifty)
*   60 — **шістдесят** (sixty)
*   70 — **сімдесят** (seventy)
*   80 — **вісімдесят** (eighty)
*   90 — **дев'яносто** (ninety)
*   100 — **сто** (one hundred)

Notice the two major exceptions! The word for forty is not "чотиридесят", it is **сорок**. Historically, this word came from the name of a large bag or bundle containing forty animal skins. People used these skins as a form of currency centuries ago. The word for ninety is **дев'яносто**, not "дев'ятдеся́т". You must simply memorize these two rebels: **сорок** and **дев'яносто**.

Combining tens and units is exactly like English. You just say the ten and then the unit.

*   21 — **двадцять один** (twenty-one)
*   35 — **тридцять п'ять** (thirty-five)
*   47 — **сорок сім** (forty-seven)

Now let us talk about hundreds. These are absolutely essential for shopping, eating at restaurants, and asking **Скільки коштує?** (How much does it cost?). They follow a very logical pattern, combining the base number with the word for hundred.

*   100 — **сто** (one hundred)
*   200 — **двісті** (two hundred)
*   300 — **триста** (three hundred)
*   400 — **чотириста** (four hundred)
*   500 — **п'ятсот** (five hundred)
*   1000 — **тисяча** (one thousand)

In the *Ukrainian Lessons Podcast* Episode 9, Anna teaches these numbers by taking you to a real Ukrainian market. You hear sellers stating prices. This is the best way to train your ear. When you go to a market in Ukraine, you will hear prices in **гривня** (hryvnia). The word for the currency changes its ending depending on the number. 

*   **одна гривня** (one hryvnia)
*   **дві гривні** (two hryvnias)
*   **п'ять гривень** (five hryvnias)

You will also encounter the **копійка** (kopek). This is the smaller coin. There are one hundred kopeks in one hryvnia. However, when shopping, sellers often drop the currency words completely. If a coffee is 80 hryvnias, they might just say **вісімдесят**.

## Підсумок — Summary

You can now handle three essential daily situations in Ukrainian. Let us review these highly practical uses for numbers.

First, asking for prices at the store or the local market. You can confidently inquire about costs and understand the answers.
*   **Скільки коштує?** (How much does it cost?)
*   **Двісті гривень.** (Two hundred hryvnias.)
*   **Сто п'ятдесят гривень.** (One hundred fifty hryvnias.)

Second, talking about your age when meeting new people. You use the formula combining **Мені**, the specific number, and the word for years.
*   **Скільки тобі років?** (How old are you?)
*   **Мені двадцять три роки.** (I am twenty-three years old.)

Third, sharing your contact information. The word for "number" is **номер**, and the word for "zero" is **нуль**. Phone numbers in Ukraine are usually read in groups of two or three digits.
*   **Мій номер — нуль дев'яносто сім, три два один, сорок п'ять, шістдесят сім.** (My number is 097-321-45-67.)

Before you finish, perform a quick self-check out loud. 
1.  Say your own age in Ukrainian. Start with **Мені**, add your number, and try to guess the correct ending for years.
2.  Imagine you are working at a coffee shop. A customer points to a cake and asks **Скільки коштує?**. Tell them it costs 250 hryvnias (**двісті п'ятдесят гривень**).
3.  Read the following phone number out loud in Ukrainian: 050-123-89-44. Break it down into pairs and triplets just like a native speaker would.
