In your previous lessons, you learned how to describe where things are located using the Locative case. Now, it is time to move. In this module, we shift our focus from static location to movement, **напрямок** (direction), and **мета** (purpose). We will explore the preposition **до**, which is one of the most versatile and frequently used words in the Ukrainian language.

Прийменник «до» завжди вимагає після себе родового відмінка. Він показує напрямок руху до певного місця або особи. Також ми використовуємо це слово, щоб позначити час. Це дуже важливе правило для щоденного спілкування.

> *The preposition "до" always requires the Genitive case after it. It shows the direction of movement toward a specific place or person. We also use this word to indicate time. This is a very important rule for everyday communication.*

Whether you are traveling toward a destination like a **музей** (museum), going to see a **лікар** (doctor), or setting a time limit like **ранок** (morning) or **вечір** (evening), **до** is the preposition you need. It corresponds to the English "to," "toward," or "until," and it exclusively pairs with the Genitive case.

:::info
**Grammar box** — The preposition **до** (to/until) is a strict trigger for the Genitive case. Whether you are talking about space, time, or purpose, the noun that follows must always change its ending.
:::

## Куди ти йдеш? До + родовий для напрямку

When you travel to a specific city or country, you need to use the preposition **до** followed by the Genitive case. This is the most common and natural way to express your destination when you are planning a trip or commuting across the map.

Ми часто подорожуємо до інших міст та країн. Улітку я планую поїхати до Києва, а потім до Львова або до Харкова. Моя сестра зараз їде до Польщі. Наступного року ми всі хочемо полетіти до Франції. Коли ти повернешся до України?

> *We often travel to other cities and countries. In the summer, I plan to go to Kyiv, and then to Lviv or to Kharkiv. My sister is currently going to Poland. Next year we all want to fly to France. When will you return to Ukraine?*

Notice the endings. Masculine cities usually take the **-а** or **-я** ending in the Genitive case. Feminine countries follow the standard rules for feminine nouns, changing their final vowel to match the case.

* Київ → до Києва
* Львів → до Львова
* Україна → до України
* Польща → до Польщі
* Франція → до Франції

In English, you might say you are going "to the doctor" or "to a friend's place." In Ukrainian, expressing a **напрямок** (direction) toward a person always requires **до** plus the Genitive case. You absolutely cannot use the preposition **в** (in or into) with people, as this sounds very unnatural to a native speaker.

Сьогодні я йду до лікаря, бо погано почуваюся. Після цього я поїду до бабусі на обід. Увечері мій брат іде до сусіда. Ми ніколи не кажемо, що йдемо в лікаря або в друга. Це велика помилка.

> *Today I am going to the doctor because I feel unwell. After that I will go to grandma's for lunch. In the evening, my brother is going to the neighbor's. We never say that we are going "in" the doctor or "in" a friend. That is a big mistake.*

Using **до** with a person implies you are going to see them, visit their home, or attend their office. Remember that **лікар** (doctor) becomes **до лікаря**, and **бабуся** (grandmother) becomes **до бабусі**. 

:::note
**Quick tip** — When talking about moving toward a person, always use **до** plus the Genitive case. Never use **в** or **на** with people!
:::

You already know that you can use **в** or **на** with the Accusative case to show direction. So, what is the difference between using **до** and using **в** or **на**? The preposition **до** emphasizes the journey or movement toward the vicinity of a place, while the Accusative prepositions emphasize crossing the boundary and physically entering the destination. Both are completely natural and correct choices in daily conversation.

Я йду до магазину, щоб купити хліб. Мій друг іде в магазин і вже відчиняє двері. Коли ми їдемо до університету, ми ще в дорозі. Коли ми йдемо в університет, ми заходимо всередину.

> *I am going to the store to buy bread. My friend is going into the store and is already opening the door. When we travel to the university, we are still on the way. When we go into the university, we step inside.*

In everyday speech, Ukrainians use both constructions interchangeably for many places. Emphasizing the journey with the Genitive case is just as standard as emphasizing the final destination.

Let's review the Genitive endings for different types of words. For masculine inanimate objects and places, the ending is often **-у** or **-ю**, especially for buildings, abstract locations, and institutions. For feminine words, the ending is typically **-и** or **-і**, depending on whether the stem is hard or soft.

Студенти йдуть до університету або до великого будинку. Туристи часто ходять до музею. Діти щоранку йдуть до школи. Минулого тижня ми їздили до нової станції та до центральної площі.

> *Students go to the university or to the large building. Tourists often go to the museum. Children go to school every morning. Last week we traveled to the new station and to the central square.*

Hard masculine stems take **-у**, while soft stems like **музей** (museum) take **-ю**. Institutions also take **-у**. For feminine words, hard stems take **-и**, words ending in **-ія** take **-ії**, and sibilants take **-і**.

* будинок → до будинку
* університет → до університету
* музей → до музею
* школа → до школи
* станція → до станції
* площа → до площі

When you add adjectives to describe your destination, they must also change to match the Genitive case. The preposition **до** affects the entire noun phrase, forcing every word connected to the destination to take a new ending.

Ми йдемо до нового будинку на нашій вулиці. Вони їдуть до старої станції біля річки. Я поспішаю до найкращого друга. Цей автобус їде до центрального парку.

> *We are going to the new building on our street. They are traveling to the old station near the river. I am hurrying to my best friend. This bus goes to the central park.*

For masculine adjectives, the ending becomes **-ого**. For feminine adjectives, the ending becomes **-ої**. This agreement is essential, as it makes your sentences sound fluent, connected, and precise.

There is one very special destination word that you use every day: **додому** (home, homeward). You will hear this word constantly in Ukraine.

Після роботи я завжди йду додому. Надворі холодно, тому ми йдемо додому.

> *After work I always go home. It is cold outside, so we are going home.*

Historically, this was a two-word phrase meaning "to the house", but over time it merged into a single adverb indicating direction homeward. Because the direction is built into the word itself, you do not need to add an extra preposition here.

## До якого часу? До + родовий для часу (~650 words)

We use the preposition **до** with the Genitive case to talk about time limits. When you want to say that an action continues "until" a certain point, this is the exact structure you need to use. It sets a clear boundary for how long something happens.

Я працюю до вечора, а потім відпочиваю. Мій брат читає книгу до пізнього ранку. Студенти чекають на результати до вівторка. Ми маємо вільний час до літа.
> *I work until evening, and then I rest. My brother reads a book until late morning. The students wait for the results until Tuesday. We have free time until summer.*

Notice the endings for the time words. Masculine words for specific days and units of time usually take the **-а** or **-я** ending in the Genitive case. For example, the word **вівторок** (Tuesday) becomes **до вівторка**, and the word **день** (day) becomes **до дня**. The words **вечір** (evening) and **ранок** (morning) also take the **-а** ending.

Він спить до обіду. Цей магазин працює до вечора. Моя сестра гуляє до ночі.
> *He is sleeping until noon. This store works until evening. My sister walks until night.*

You also use **до** to set a strict deadline. In English, this translates to doing something "by" a certain time. It shows the exact moment when an action must be finished.

Тобі треба зробити це завдання до п'ятниці. Будь ласка, зателефонуй мені до обіду. Вони мають підготувати проєкт до завтра.
> *You need to do this task by Friday. Please call me by lunchtime. They have to prepare the project by tomorrow.*

When talking about clock time, Ukrainian uses ordinal numbers like "first" or "second" in the Genitive case. Because the word **година** (hour) is feminine, the ordinal number must have a feminine Genitive ending, which is **-ої**. The noun **година** changes to **години**.

Я буду в офісі до восьмої години. Цей концерт триває до десятої години. Ми повинні прийти до п'ятої.
> *I will be in the office by eight o'clock. This concert lasts until ten o'clock. We must arrive by five.*

:::info
**Grammar box** — In everyday conversations, Ukrainians often drop the word **година** when the context is clear. Saying **до п'ятої** (by five) sounds completely natural and means exactly the same thing as **до п'ятої години**.
:::

To express a continuous duration or a time range like "from ... to", you pair the preposition **до** with the preposition **з** (or its phonetic variations **із**, **зі**) or **від**. Both prepositions require the Genitive case. This structure clearly marks both the start and the end of the action.

Цей супермаркет працює з ранку до вечора. Моя сестра навчається в університеті з понеділка до п'ятниці. Наш лікар приймає пацієнтів з дев'ятої до шостої.
> *This supermarket is open from morning to evening. My sister studies at the university from Monday to Friday. Our doctor sees patients from nine to six.*

This paired construction is the most common way to talk about schedules. The starting point takes **з** or **від**, and the ending point takes **до**. You must change the endings of the words on both sides of the range to match the Genitive case.

There are many fixed expressions related to time that use the preposition **до**. These are incredibly useful phrases to memorize for your everyday conversations. You will hear them constantly in Ukraine.

До завтра! До зустрічі! Ми маємо багато роботи до початку уроку. Виставка в музеї триває до кінця липня.
> *See you tomorrow! See you soon! We have a lot of work before the start of the lesson. The exhibition in the museum lasts until the end of July.*

:::tip
**Quick tip** — The common phrase **До побачення!** literally translates as "Until the seeing". The noun **побачення** (meeting, date) is in the Genitive case.
:::

It is helpful to contrast how we use **до** with other prepositions of time, such as **перед** (before) and **після** (after). While the preposition **перед** requires the Instrumental case, both **до** and **після** require the Genitive case. Understanding the difference makes your speech more precise.

Я завжди п'ю каву до сніданку. Моя мама п'є чай після сніданку. Студенти читають текст перед уроком.
> *I always drink coffee before breakfast. My mom drinks tea after breakfast. The students read the text before the lesson.*

In these situations, **до** effectively means "before" an event happens. It emphasizes the period of time leading directly up to the deadline rather than just the sequence of events.

## До + родовий: решта значень та узагальнення (~650 words)

Beyond physical movement and time, the preposition **до** also expresses a **мета** (goal, purpose) or an abstract relationship. You use it when talking about readiness, suitability, or connection to an event. Just like with physical destinations, the focus is on looking ahead toward a target. For example, if you are preparing for a difficult test or buying a ticket to travel, you are aiming your effort toward that point. 

Мій брат дуже серйозно готується до екзамену. Він читає багато книжок і робить усі завдання. Ця нова програма ідеально підходить до нашого комп'ютера. Я купив квиток до Києва на завтра. До речі, нам треба поговорити про нашу спільну мету.

> *My brother is preparing very seriously for the exam. He reads many books and does all the tasks. This new program is perfectly suited for our computer. I bought a ticket to Kyiv for tomorrow. By the way, we need to talk about our shared goal.*

Notice how Ukrainian uses **до** where English might use "for", "to", or "by". The phrase **готовий до** means "ready for" an action. The construction **квиток до** literally means a "ticket to" a specific place. We also use this preposition in extremely common conversational markers. The classic greeting **до побачення** (goodbye) literally translates as "until the meeting", and the phrase **до речі** means "by the way" or "to the point".

Another common and very useful application of this preposition is to show addition, attachment, or your personal attitude toward something or someone. When you add an item to a physical collection, or when you express how you feel about a situation, the preposition points your action or emotion toward that specific object. 

Вчитель попросив додати нові слова до списку на дошці. Моє ставлення до роботи завжди було дуже відповідальним. Нові студенти мають великий інтерес до української мови. Вона відчуває глибоку повагу до своїх викладачів.

> *The teacher asked to add new words to the list on the board. My attitude toward work has always been very responsible. The new students have a great interest in the Ukrainian language. She feels deep respect for her instructors.*

Using abstract words like **ставлення** (attitude) or **інтерес** (interest) naturally requires **до** to clearly show the target of those feelings. In English, you might have "interest in" or "attitude toward", but Ukrainian standardizes these connections with the Genitive case. Similarly, the verb **додати** (to add) visually and logically moves one thing toward another. The object you are adding to must end in the Genitive case.

Now that we have seen the various roles of **до**, it is helpful to look at the bigger picture of the Genitive case. You already know **від** (from) and **після** (after). Together with **до**, these three prepositions create a complete and logical system for navigating space and time. They all require the Genitive case, but they move your focus in completely different directions. 

Сьогодні з самого ранку ми їздили від одного місця до іншого. Спочатку ми пішли від готелю до музею в центрі. Після музею ми поїхали до лікаря, а потім в гості до бабусі. Ми гуляли містом від обіду до пізнього вечора. Після такої довгої поїздки ми всі дуже втомилися.

> *Today, from the very morning, we traveled from one place to another. First, we walked from the hotel to the museum in the center. After the museum, we went to the doctor, and then to visit grandma. We walked around the city from lunch until late evening. After such a long trip, we were all very tired.*

You can see how beautifully these prepositions work together in a single narrative. The preposition **від** sets the starting point or origin of an action. The preposition **до** marks the destination, the time limit, or the general **напрямок** (direction) of your movement. Finally, **після** describes what happens next in a sequence of events. This consistency across different situations is the real beauty of the Ukrainian case system.

:::info
**Grammar box** — The natural, authentic Ukrainian way to express movement toward a person or a deadline is strictly **до** + Genitive. You might sometimes hear people use the preposition "к" with the Dative case (like "к мамі" instead of "до мами"). Whether you are going to see a friend, scheduling an appointment with a doctor, or trying to finish a difficult task by Friday, always use the preposition **до** followed by the correct form of the Genitive case. This structure is a core feature of the Ukrainian language. Relying on it rather than translating from other Slavic languages ensures that your speech sounds authentic, polite, and structurally perfect.

## Підсумок — Summary (~150 words)

Ми вивчили всі основні функції прийменника **до** з родовим відмінком. Використовуйте цю таблицю, щоб швидко згадати правила.

> *We have learned all the main functions of the preposition **до** with the Genitive case. Use this table to quickly recall the rules.*

| Функція (Function) | Приклад (Example) | Переклад (Translation) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Напрямок** (Direction) | до Києва, до мами | to Kyiv, to mom's |
| **Час** (Time limit) | до вечора, до п'ятниці | until evening, by Friday |
| **Мета** (Purpose) | до побачення, до речі | goodbye, by the way |

У цій темі ми розглянули різні ситуації. Ваш **напрямок** (direction) може бути дуже різним. Наприклад, ви можете йти до **музею** (museum) або до **лікаря** (doctor).

> *In this topic, we looked at different situations. Your direction can be very different. For example, you can go to the museum or to the doctor.*

Часові межі — це також важлива частина граматики. Ви можете працювати від **ранку** (morning) до **вечора** (evening). А іноді ви просто їдете до **бабусі** (grandmother) на вихідні.

> *Time limits are also an important part of grammar. You can work from morning to evening. And sometimes you just go to your grandmother's for the weekend.*

Прийменник **до** чудово показує, яка у вас **мета** (goal, purpose). Ви можете готуватися до **екзамену** (exam) або додати нові слова до **списку** (list). 

> *The preposition **до** perfectly shows what your goal is. You can prepare for an exam or add new words to a list.*

:::note
**Quick tip** — The preposition **до** always requires the Genitive case. Never mix it with the Dative or Accusative cases.
:::

Кожна зустріч чи **побачення** (meeting, date) має свій кінець. Тепер ви розумієте граматику фрази «до побачення». Спробуйте відповісти на питання: як сказати "by 5 o'clock"? Чому ми кажемо "до лікаря", а не "в лікар"?
