## Для кого це? Для + родовий (Who Is It For? Для + Genitive)

Welcome to a new grammar topic. In Ukrainian, when you want to express the intended recipient of an object or the purpose of an action, you use the preposition **для** (for). Unlike English, which uses "for" in many different contexts, the Ukrainian word is specifically focused on who gets something or what something is intended to do. For example, you might look for time for **відпочинок** (rest, relaxation). And here is the most important rule: this preposition ALWAYS triggers the Genitive case. This means the noun that follows it must change its ending.

:::note
**Quick tip** — When you learn the word **для**, immediately associate it with the question words **кого?** (who?) and **чого?** (what?) in the Genitive case.
:::

Прийменник «для» показує призначення або мету. Ми часто використовуємо це маленьке слово щодня. Наприклад, ми купуємо подарунок для мами. Або ми шукаємо час для відпочинку.

> *The preposition "для" shows purpose or goal. We often use this little word every day. For example, we buy a gift for mom. Or we look for time for rest.*

Let's focus on the idea of **призначення** (purpose, destination). When you attach the preposition to abstract nouns or concepts, it explains what something is intended for. This is very common when talking about the function of everyday objects or actions. You will hear this constantly in shops, in a **бібліотека** (library), or when organizing your day. Remember that for masculine abstract nouns, the Genitive ending is usually **-у**.

Свіжі фрукти корисні для здоров'я. Ми купуємо зручний одяг для роботи. Кожна річ має своє призначення. Наприклад, у цій бібліотеці є цікаві книги для навчання. Вечір — це найкращий час для відпочинку.

> *Fresh fruit is good for health. We buy comfortable clothes for work. Every thing has its purpose. For example, in this library, there are interesting books for studying. Evening is the best time for rest.*

Now let's look at recipients. When the recipient is a person, we need to change their name or title into the Genitive case. For hard-stem nouns, masculine words take the **-а** ending, and feminine words take the **-и** ending. This is the most common pattern you will see when buying gifts or doing favors for family members. Let's see how this looks in practice with some everyday examples.

Я маю квиток для брата на концерт. Це великий сюрприз для сестри. Сьогодні я готую смачну вечерю для тата. Мій друг шукає подарунок для сина.

> *I have a ticket for my brother to the concert. This is a big surprise for my sister. Today I am cooking a tasty dinner for dad. My friend is looking for a gift for his son.*

If the noun has a soft stem or ends in **-ія**, the endings change to match the softness. Soft masculine nouns take **-я**. Soft feminine nouns, like a **лікарня** (hospital), take **-і**, while names ending in **-ія** take **-ії**. It is important to pronounce these endings clearly so the listener knows exactly who the recipient is. Notice how the endings glide smoothly when spoken.

У класі стоїть новий стілець для вчителя. Цей новий комп'ютер для лікарні. Я купив ці красиві квіти для Марії. Ця яскрава іграшка призначена для Софії.

> *In the classroom stands a new chair for the teacher. This new computer is for the hospital. I bought these beautiful flowers for Maria. This bright toy is intended for Sophia.*

Finally, let's talk about neuter nouns and plural recipients. Neuter nouns often end in **-я** in the Genitive case, just like soft masculine nouns. For plural recipients, the rules are a bit more complex, but a very common ending for masculine plural nouns is **-ів**. For example, in a large **будинок** (building, house), you might find a room for children. The word for "children" is special and becomes **дітей**.

Ми хочемо зробити все для щастя нашої родини. У цьому великому будинку є світла кімната для дітей. У мене є дуже хороші новини для друзів. Цей проєкт потрібен для міста.

> *We want to do everything for the happiness of our family. In this big building, there is a bright room for children. I have very good news for friends. This project is needed for the city.*

Let's see how this works in a real conversation. Ігор and Марта are packing for a camping trip. Pay attention to how they use the Genitive case to talk about who needs what and the purpose of the items they are packing. They also talk about how their **допомога** (help, assistance) is important. This is a very natural way to use the grammar in daily life.

## Без чого? Без + родовий (Without What? Без + Genitive)

Now that we know how to say who something is for, let's look at how to express the absence of something. In Ukrainian, we use the preposition **без** (without) followed by the Genitive case. This is an incredibly common word, especially when ordering food or drinks. You will hear it every day when people ask for coffee or tea.

Я люблю пити теплий чай без молока. Мій старший брат завжди замовляє каву без цукру. У цьому новому ресторані є дуже смачна вода без газу. Ми часто їмо свіжий салат без олії.

> *I like to drink warm tea without milk. My older brother always orders coffee without sugar. In this new restaurant, there is very delicious still water (water without gas). We often eat fresh salad without oil.*

The preposition **без** is also frequently used with abstract nouns to create common conversational phrases. These phrases act like adverbs, describing how something is done. For example, if you want to say that something is certain, you say it is without a **сумнів** (doubt). 

Цей новий проєкт буде працювати абсолютно без проблем. Наш розумний колега зробив це складне завдання без допомоги. Ми можемо зробити цю роботу вчасно без сумніву. На жаль, мій друг шукав нову роботу без успіху.

> *This new project will work absolutely without problems. Our smart colleague did this difficult task without help. We can do this work on time without doubt. Unfortunately, my friend looked for a new job without success.*

:::note
**Quick tip** — The phrase **без проблем** (no problem) is used exactly like its English equivalent. It is a great way to respond when someone asks for a favor!
:::

Let's look closer at masculine nouns after **без**. Just like we saw before, masculine nouns in the Genitive case can take either an **-а** or a **-у** ending. Concrete, physical objects that you can count usually take the **-а** ending. However, substances, materials, and abstract concepts generally take the **-у** ending.

Я не можу нормально різати велике яблуко без ножа. Цей гарячий суп дуже несмачний без свіжого хліба. Вона завжди любить пити зелений чай без цукру. У великій бібліотеці всі студенти читають без шуму.

> *I cannot normally cut a large apple without a knife. This hot soup is very tasteless without fresh bread. She always likes to drink green tea without sugar. In the large library, all students read without noise.*

For feminine nouns, the rule is straightforward: hard stems take **-и**, and soft stems take **-і** or **-ї**. Neuter nouns behave similarly to masculine concrete objects, taking **-а** for hard stems and **-я** for soft stems. When you combine these with **без**, you can describe the exact state of objects or places.

Жодна людина не може довго жити без чистої води. Ця традиційна їжа зовсім несмачна без солі. У старій кімнаті дуже темно без великого вікна. Вони просто не уявляють своє тепле літо без моря.

> *No person can live long without clean water. This traditional food is completely tasteless without salt. In the old room, it is very dark without a large window. They simply do not imagine their warm summer without the sea.*

You will often hear **без** paired with the phrase **не можу** (I cannot) to express a strong preference or a necessity. You can also use it with pronouns, just remember that personal pronouns also change their form in the Genitive case. Let's see how these look in complete sentences.

Рано-вранці я просто не можу нормально працювати без кави. Вчора вона прийшла на роботу абсолютно мокра без парасольки. Чому ви поїхали в центральний магазин без нього? Маленькі діти не хочуть іти гуляти без неї.

> *Early in the morning, I simply cannot work normally without coffee. Yesterday she came to work absolutely wet without an umbrella. Why did you go to the central store without him? Little children do not want to go for a walk without her.*

Let's rejoin Ігор and Марта as they finish packing for their camping trip. They are double-checking their gear and realizing what they cannot afford to leave behind. Notice how they use **без** with different types of nouns.

## Де це? Біля, навпроти, коло + родовий (Where Is It? Біля, навпроти, коло + Genitive) (~850 words total)

When you want to describe where something is located in relation to another object, Ukrainian relies heavily on the Genitive case. The most common and essential preposition for spatial relationships is **біля** (near, next to). Think of it as establishing a primary reference point: you are stating that one thing exists in the immediate vicinity of another. Because the reference point acts as the anchor for the location, it must be in the Genitive case. This spatial logic applies whether you are talking about large city structures or small items in a room. You will hear this preposition constantly in everyday conversations when people give directions.

Моя нова школа знаходиться біля старого парку. Кожного ранку я п'ю каву біля великого вікна. Наш улюблений ресторан стоїть біля головного банку. Вони залишили свою машину біля високого дерева.

> *My new school is located near the old park. Every morning I drink coffee by the large window. Our favorite restaurant stands near the main bank. They left their car near the tall tree.*

Let us look at how **біля** interacts with different genders. The grammatical rules you have already learned for the Genitive case apply perfectly here. For masculine nouns ending in a consonant, you will typically add **-а** or **-у**. For instance, a physical **будинок** (building, house) becomes **будинку**. Feminine nouns ending in **-а** change to **-и**, such as a river becoming **річки**. Neuter nouns like the sea change from **море** to **моря**. Because giving directions is such a fundamental part of daily life, you will find yourself using these specific gender endings with **біля** very frequently.

Великий супермаркет знаходиться біля мого будинку. Ми любимо гуляти біля широкої річки кожного вечора. Цей дорогий готель знаходиться прямо біля синього моря. Туристи завжди фотографуються біля історичного музею.

> *A large supermarket is located near my house. We love to walk near the wide river every evening. This expensive hotel is located right by the blue sea. Tourists always take pictures near the historical museum.*

Another highly useful spatial preposition is **навпроти** (opposite, across from). This word is used when two objects are facing each other, usually with some space or a street between them. It paints a very clear picture of the urban layout. If you stand at the door of one building and look straight across the street, you are looking at the building **навпроти**. Just like our previous prepositions, it strictly requires the Genitive case. This is incredibly helpful when you need to specify a location more precisely than just saying it is "near" something else.

Нова кав'ярня відкрилася навпроти центрального банку. Я чекаю тебе навпроти залізничного вокзалу. Старий театр стоїть навпроти красивої церкви. Мій офіс знаходиться навпроти великого торгового центру.

> *A new coffee shop opened opposite the central bank. I am waiting for you opposite the train station. The old theater stands opposite the beautiful church. My office is located opposite the large shopping center.*

You might also encounter the preposition **коло** (near, by). In modern urban speech, it is essentially a synonym for **біля**. However, it carries a slightly different stylistic flavor. While **біля** is neutral and universal, **коло** often feels a bit more traditional, cozy, or literary. You will frequently hear it in folk songs, poetry, or when people describe rural settings and childhood memories. It operates grammatically in the exact same way, demanding the Genitive case for the noun that follows it.

Вона читає цікаву книгу коло своєї хати. Старий чоловік довго стояв коло порожньої дороги. Діти цілий день гралися коло теплої печі. Ми знайшли маленьке кошеня коло нашого двору.

> *She is reading an interesting book by her house. The old man stood for a long time by the empty road. The children played all day by the warm stove. We found a little kitten by our yard.*

When describing city infrastructure, you will often use soft-stem feminine nouns. For example, a **станція** (station) changes to **станції**, a **площа** (square (city)) becomes **площі**, and a **лікарня** (hospital) becomes **лікарні**. These nouns require the ending **-і** or **-ї** in the Genitive case. Mastering these specific endings will make your directions sound natural and grammatically correct.

Головна пошта знаходиться біля автобусної станції. Вони зустрілися навпроти міської лікарні. Ми домовилися пообідати біля центральної площі. Мій улюблений ресторан стоїть коло старої будівлі.

> *The main post office is located near the bus station. They met opposite the city hospital. We agreed to have lunch near the central square. My favorite restaurant stands by the old building.*

:::note
**Quick tip** — Remember that nouns ending in **-ія** (like **станція**) change to **-ії** (like **станції**) in the Genitive case, while soft feminine nouns ending in **-я** (like **лікарня**) change to **-і** (like **лікарні**).
:::

Let us put all of this together to describe a typical neighborhood layout. Being able to create a clear mental map using these prepositions is a highly practical everyday skill. You can link multiple locations together to guide a friend to a **бібліотека** (library) or explain where the nearest bus **зупинка** (stop (bus/tram)) is located. Notice how the reference points constantly use the Genitive case to anchor the descriptions firmly in space.

Наша нова квартира дуже зручна. Маленька аптека знаходиться біля місцевого ринку. Затишне кафе стоїть прямо навпроти великої бібліотеки. А трамвайна зупинка знаходиться біля станції метро. Ми часто гуляємо біля цього парку.

> *Our new apartment is very convenient. A small pharmacy is located near the local market. A cozy cafe stands right opposite the large library. And the tram stop is located near the subway station. We often walk near this park.*

Let's rejoin Ігор and Марта. They have finally arrived at the campsite and now need to make a practical decision: where exactly should they set up their camp for their **відпочинок** (rest, relaxation)? They use these spatial prepositions to discuss the best location.

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марта:</span> Я думаю, найкраще місце — біля річки. *(I think the best place is near the river.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Ігор:</span> Але біля річки може бути дуже холодно вночі. *(But near the river it can be very cold at night.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марта:</span> Тоді давай поставимо його навпроти великого дерева. *(Then let's put it opposite the large tree.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Ігор:</span> Добра ідея. А коло вогню ми поставимо наші стільці. *(Good idea. And by the fire we will put our chairs.)*</div>

It is helpful to know that these prepositions are part of a much larger system of spatial words that use the Genitive case. We will explore prepositions like **до** (to), **з** (from), and **від** (from/away from) in later modules. The key difference right now is that **біля**, **навпроти**, and **коло** describe a static location—where something currently rests—rather than the direction of movement toward or away from a place.

## Підсумок — Summary (~150 words)

Ви успішно вивчили три важливі функції родового відмінка. Прийменник «для» показує призначення об'єкта. Також ми говорили про відсутність чогось за допомогою слова «без». Нарешті, ми описали місцезнаходження за допомогою слів «біля», «навпроти» та «коло».

> * The preposition "for" shows the purpose of an object. Also we talked about the absence of something using the word "without". Finally, we described location using the words "near", "opposite", and "by".*

These prepositions are incredibly useful for discussing **призначення** (purpose, destination) or finding a **будинок** (building, house). You can confidently ask for directions to a **лікарня** (hospital).

You will easily locate a **станція** (station) or **площа** (square (city)). You can also find a quiet **бібліотека** (library) for studying.

These words help you navigate to the nearest bus **зупинка** (stop (bus/tram)) or plan your weekend **відпочинок** (rest, relaxation). Most importantly, you can communicate without any **сумнів** (doubt).

You will do this without extra **допомога** (help, assistance). Remember that all these prepositions strictly require the Genitive case.
