## Дієслова з давальним відмінком (Verbs That Take the Dative) (~660 words)

In English, when you help, thank, or call someone, the person receiving the action is treated as a direct object. You simply say "I help my brother," "She thanks the teacher," or "They call the manager." Ukrainian grammar looks at these everyday actions quite differently. Instead of doing something directly *to* the person, you are conceptually directing the action *towards* them. They are the recipient, the addressee, or the beneficiary of your action. Because of this sense of direction, Ukrainian uses the Dative case, which answers the questions **кому?** (to whom?) and **чому?** (to what?). Understanding this fundamental difference is key to sounding natural when you interact with others in Ukrainian.

Let's start with the common verb **допомагати** (to help). When you help someone, you are offering your time and assistance to them. Therefore, the person receiving the help must always be in the Dative case. This applies to both pronouns and nouns.

Я часто допомагаю родині вдома. Уранці я завжди допомагаю мамі готувати сніданок. Увечері я допомагаю татові ремонтувати машину. Моя молодша сестра допомагає братові робити домашнє завдання. У вихідні ми всі допомагаємо бабусі в саду.

> *I often help my family at home. In the morning, I always help my mom prepare breakfast. In the evening, I help my dad repair the car. My younger sister helps my brother do homework. On weekends, we all help our grandmother in the garden.*

Notice the specific noun endings for masculine family members, like **татові** and **братові**. While you can sometimes use the shorter **-у** ending for masculine nouns, Ukrainian strongly prefers the **-ові** or **-еві** endings for male persons. These specific endings clearly signal the Dative case to the listener and make the meaning of the sentence completely unambiguous.

The verb **дякувати** (to thank) is a critical word where learners frequently make mistakes. It strictly requires the Dative case because you are giving your words of thanks *to* a person. This is a very important decolonization point and a major difference from the Russian language, which uses the Accusative case for its equivalent verb. You must never say «дякую вас» in Ukrainian. This is a severe grammatical error and a direct, unnatural translation from Russian grammar. You must always respect the Ukrainian rule and use the Dative case, such as **дякую вам** or **дякую тобі**.

Учні завжди дякують вчителеві за цікавий урок. Ми часто дякуємо друзям за гарний подарунок. Вдячний пацієнт дякує лікарю за допомогу. Я дуже дякую тобі за постійну підтримку в цій ситуації.

> *The students always thank the teacher for an interesting lesson. We often thank our friends for a beautiful gift. The grateful patient thanks the doctor for the help. I thank you very much for your constant support in this situation.*

:::info
**Grammar box**
Always ask yourself **кому?** (to whom?) when using **дякувати**. You are directing your gratitude towards a person, not acting upon them. This simple mental check will help you avoid using the wrong grammatical case.
:::

When you make a phone call, you will use the verbs **дзвонити** (to call, to phone) or **телефонувати** (to phone). Just like helping and thanking, calling is an action conceptually directed at an addressee. You do not use the preposition «до» (to) when you call a person on the phone. Instead, you create a direct grammatical relationship using the Dative case.

Кожного вечора я дзвоню подрузі Марії. Вона живе в іншому місті, і ми довго розмовляємо. Мій колега зараз телефонує Андрієві, щоб обговорити новий проєкт. Удень я дзвоню мамі, щоб дізнатися про її здоров'я.

> *Every evening I call my friend Mariia. She lives in another city, and we talk for a long time. My colleague is currently phoning Andrii to discuss a new project. During the day I call my mom to find out about her health.*

Several other very common verbs follow this exact same grammatical pattern. These useful verbs include **радити** (to advise), **заважати** (to bother, to disturb), and **відповідати** (to answer (someone)). You give advice *to* someone, you are a bother *to* someone, and you provide an answer *to* someone. This consistent logic applies to regular statements as well as polite commands and requests.

Будь ласка, не заважай мені працювати! Я раджу тобі подивитися цей новий фільм про історію України. Студент швидко відповідає вчителеві на уроці. Мати завжди радить синові тепло одягатися взимку.

> *Please, do not bother me while I work! I advise you to watch this new film about the history of Ukraine. The student quickly answers the teacher during the lesson. A mother always advises her son to dress warmly in winter.*

Let's see how these important Dative verbs work together in a realistic everyday scenario. Imagine a local volunteer talking to a friend about their busy day helping the community.

## Мені подобається: Давальний відмінок досвідника (The Experiencer Dative with подобатися) (~550 words)

Let's explore one of the most common and important verbs in Ukrainian: **подобатися** (to be pleasing to, to like (reversed syntax)). When you want to say that you like something, Ukrainian uses a completely different grammatical logic than English. In the English sentence "I like this book", the person ("I") is the active subject doing the liking. The Ukrainian language flips this perspective entirely. The thing that you like becomes the active grammatical subject in the Nominative case. The person who experiences the feeling of liking is placed in the Dative case, acting as a recipient. Literally translated, the Ukrainian structure means "This book is pleasing to me". This reversed syntax takes a little practice to get used to, but it is incredibly natural for native speakers.

Because the object you like is the actual grammatical subject, the verb must agree with it, not with you. In the present tense, you will almost exclusively use two forms of this verb. If you like a single item or an uncountable concept, you use the singular form «подобається». If you like multiple items, you must use the plural form «подобаються». The ending of the verb changes based on what is being liked, while the Dative person remains exactly the same.

Мені дуже подобається цей гарячий чай. Мені також подобається українська музика. Але мені подобаються ці великі квіти. Моєму братові подобаються старі фільми.

> *I really like this hot tea. I also like Ukrainian music. But I like these big flowers. My brother likes old films.*

Ми часто розповідаємо, що подобається іншим людям. Тобі подобається новий викладач в університеті. Йому подобається смачна піца в цьому кафе. Їй подобається читати цікаві книжки ввечері. Нам подобається гуляти в парку навесні. Вам подобається ця маленька собака. Їм подобаються високі гори і чисті річки. Усі ці люди отримують приємні емоції від різних речей.

> *We often talk about what other people like. You like the new teacher at the university. He likes the delicious pizza in this cafe. She likes reading interesting books in the evening. We like walking in the park in the spring. You all like this small dog. They like high mountains and clean rivers. All these people receive pleasant emotions from different things.*

:::info
**Grammar box**
Notice how the Dative pronouns (**тобі**, **йому**, **їй**, **нам**, **вам**, **їм**) stay locked in place at the beginning of the sentence. The structure is always: [Dative Person] + [Verb] + [Nominative Thing].
:::

This reversed logic also applies to the past tense. Since the thing you liked is the grammatical subject, the past tense verb must agree with its gender and number. If the liked object is masculine, you use «подобався». For a feminine object, you use «подобалася». For a neuter object, the verb becomes «подобалося». For plural objects, you will use «подобалися».

У дитинстві мені подобався футбол. Також мені подобалася класична музика. Моїй сестрі дуже подобалося тепле молоко. А моїм батькам завжди подобалися спокійні вечори вдома.

> *In childhood, I liked football. I also liked classical music. My sister really liked warm milk. And my parents always liked calm evenings at home.*

Forming questions and negative statements is straightforward. To make a negative sentence, simply place the word «не» directly before the verb. The rest of the sentence structure remains unchanged. When asking a question, you usually raise your intonation or start with a question word like «що» (what).

Тобі не подобається ця пісня? Ні, мені вона зовсім не подобається. Що вам найбільше подобається в Україні? Нам дуже подобаються люди та природа. Тобі подобається вивчати українську мову? Так, мені подобається наша група.

> *Do you not like this song? No, I do not like it at all. What do you like most in Ukraine? We really like the people and the nature. Do you like studying the Ukrainian language? Yes, I like our group.*

## Скільки тобі років? Вік у давальному відмінку (Age in the Dative) (~500 words)

In English, you "are" a certain age. You say "I am twenty years old." In Ukrainian, expressing age uses a completely different logic. Age is treated as a measure of time that has accumulated or happened "to" someone. Because this is an experience received by a person, the person must be in the Dative case. The core pattern is always: [Person in Dative] + [Number] + [Year word]. You do not use the verb "to be" in the present tense for age. Instead of saying "I am twenty", you literally say "To me is twenty of years."

Сьогодні мені двадцять п'ять років. Моєму старшому братові вже тридцять років. Нашій маленькій сестрі тільки вісім років. Моєму дідусеві сімдесят років, і він дуже активний. Нашому собаці п'ять років. Для всіх цих людей і тварин ми використовуємо давальний відмінок.

> *Today I am twenty-five years old. My older brother is already thirty years old. Our little sister is only eight years old. My grandfather is seventy years old, and he is very active. Our dog is five years old. For all these people and animals, we use the Dative case.*

When stating an age, the word for "year" changes depending on the number that immediately precedes it. This is a fundamental rule of Ukrainian numbers. There are three forms of the word "year" that you must learn: **рік** (year), **роки** (years), and **років** (years). The choice depends entirely on the last digit of the number, with a special exception for the "teen" numbers.

:::info
**Grammar box**
1. Use **рік** for numbers ending in 1 (1, 21, 31, 41...), except for 11.
2. Use **роки** for numbers ending in 2, 3, or 4 (2, 3, 4, 22, 23, 24...), except for 12, 13, and 14.
3. Use **років** for numbers 5-20, and all numbers ending in 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, or 0.
:::

| Число | Форма | Приклади |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1, 21, 31... (крім 11) | **рік** | Мені один рік. Йому двадцять один рік. |
| 2, 3, 4, 22... (крім 12-14) | **роки** | Тобі два роки. Братові двадцять три роки. |
| 5-20, 25-30, 35-40... | **років** | Мамі п'ятдесят років. Дідусеві сімнадцять років. |

To ask someone their age, you use the question word «скільки» (how many). When you ask "how many", the noun that follows must be in the Genitive plural form. This is why the question always uses the form «років». The person you are asking about remains in the Dative case because the age is still happening "to" them. The standard question is «Скільки тобі років?» (How old are you?). If you are asking about someone else, you simply change the Dative pronoun or noun.

Ми часто запитуємо нових знайомих про їхній вік. Скільки тобі років? Скільки років вашому синові? Скільки років цій старій школі? Скільки їй зараз років? Це дуже корисні питання для щоденного спілкування.

> *We often ask new acquaintances about their age. How old are you? How old is your son? How old is this old school? How old is she now? These are very useful questions for daily communication.*

Now let's see how these questions and answers work in a natural conversation. Notice how the speakers use the Dative case for the family members being discussed, and how the form of the word "year" changes depending on the exact number given in the answer.

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Антон:</span> Привіт! Моєму татові п'ятдесят два роки. *(Hi! My dad is fifty-two years old.)*</div>

## Давальний чи знахідний? Порівняння (Dative vs. Accusative with Verbs) (~490 words)

Understanding when to use the Dative case versus the Accusative case is all about the "logic" of the action. The Accusative case is for direct actions where the object takes a direct hit from the verb. For example, when you see someone, the action lands directly on the target. The Dative case represents a hand-off or a beneficiary. When you help someone, you direct your energy *to* them as a recipient.

Ми використовуємо знахідний відмінок для прямої дії. Наприклад, я бачу маму або я знаю мову. Дія переходить прямо на об'єкт. Але ми використовуємо давальний відмінок для адресата дії. Я допомагаю мамі або я дякую вчителеві. Тут людина є отримувачем нашої дії.

> *We use the accusative case for direct action. For example, I see mom or I know the language. The action transfers directly to the object. But we use the dative case for the addressee of the action. I help mom or I thank the teacher. Here the person is the recipient of our action.*

Let's look at minimal pairs to see the difference clearly. When the verb changes the relationship with the person, the case ending must also change. This is especially visible with feminine nouns, which typically change to **-у** in the Accusative and **-і** in the Dative.

Я дуже люблю свою маму, тому я завжди допомагаю мамі. Вранці я бачу мого друга, а ввечері я дзвоню другові. На зупинці я чекаю подругу, а потім я дякую подрузі за допомогу. Це різні ситуації та різні відмінки. Ти мусиш уважно вибирати правильне закінчення.

> *I love my mom very much, so I always help mom. In the morning I see my friend, and in the evening I call my friend. At the bus stop I wait for my friend, and then I thank my friend for the help. These are different situations and different cases. You must carefully choose the correct ending.*

Some verbs actually require both cases in the same sentence. These are verbs of giving or communicating, like **давати** (to give) and **розповідати** (to tell). The structure is logical: the verb is followed by the person receiving the item (Dative) and then the item itself (Accusative). 

На свято я даю мамі гарні квіти. Мама отримує квіти, тому вона стоїть у давальному відмінку. Ввечері дідусь розповідає нам цікаву історію. Ми є слухачами, а історія — це прямий об'єкт. Вчителька часто дає студентам нове завдання.

> *For the holiday I give mom beautiful flowers. Mom receives the flowers, so she stands in the dative case. In the evening grandpa tells us an interesting story. We are the listeners, and the story is the direct object. The teacher often gives the students a new task.*

When you learn a new verb, ask yourself about the nature of the action. If the person is the *destination* of the action—like communication or giving help—use the Dative case. If the person is the literal *object* of the action—like seeing or loving them—use the Accusative case. One tricky pair to remember: to call someone on the phone is **дзвонити** (to call, to phone), which requires the Dative, but to summon someone is **кликати** (to call/summon), which requires the Accusative.

:::info
**Grammar box**
Think of the Dative case as the "receiver" case. Verbs like **радити** (to advise), **відповідати** (to answer (someone)), and **заважати** (to bother) all point *to* a person, making them the receiver of your advice, answer, or annoyance. Therefore, they always take the Dative case.
:::

Син дзвонить батькові по телефону. Це давальний відмінок, бо батько отримує дзвінок. Але мати голосно кличе сина додому. Це знахідний відмінок, бо дія спрямована прямо на нього. Я раджу тобі запам'ятати це правило.

> *The son calls the father on the phone. This is the dative case, because the father receives the call. But the mother loudly calls the son home. This is the accusative case, because the action is directed right at him. I advise you to remember this rule.*

## Підсумок (~150 words)

In this module, we explored three pillars of the Dative case. First, certain verbs always direct their action toward a receiver. You must memorize that **допомагати** (to help), **дякувати** (to thank), and **дзвонити** (to call, to phone) require the Dative case.

Other verbs in this group include **радити** (to advise), **заважати** (to bother, to disturb), and **відповідати** (to answer (someone)). You are giving advice, causing trouble, or giving an answer *to* someone. 

Second, the verb **подобатися** (to be pleasing to, to like (reversed syntax)) flips the typical English structure. The person experiencing the feeling is in the Dative case, while the object is in the Nominative case.

Third, expressing age always requires the Dative case for the person. Remember the number rules: you use **рік** (year) for one, **роки** (years (2-4)) for numbers ending in two through four, and **років** (years (5+)) for five and above.

:::note
**Quick tip**
Always ask yourself "to whom?" when deciding whether to use the Dative case with a new verb. If the person is the recipient of the action, the answer is usually yes!
:::

Тепер перевір свої знання. Як правильно сказати: «дякую тебе» чи «дякую тобі»? Правильно говорити «дякую тобі», бо це давальний відмінок. Яке закінчення має дієслово, якщо мені подобаються книги? Ми використовуємо множину: «подобаються». Яку форму ми вживаємо для числа двадцять два? Це форма «роки», тому ми кажемо «двадцять два роки».

> *Now check your knowledge. How is it correct to say: "thank you" (accusative) or "thank you" (dative)? It is correct to say "thank you" (dative), because it is the dative case. What ending does the verb have if I like books? We use the plural: "like". What form do we use for the number twenty-two? It is the form "years", so we say "twenty-two years".*
