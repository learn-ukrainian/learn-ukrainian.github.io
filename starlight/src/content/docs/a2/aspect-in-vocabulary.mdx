## Чо́му дієслова́ потрі́бно вчи́ти па́рами? (Why Verbs Must Be Learned in Pairs)

When you learn a new verb in Ukrainian, you are often only learning half of a word. In English, a single verb can describe both the ongoing process of an action and its completed result. In Ukrainian, these two ideas require two different verbs. Think of Ukrainian verbs like a pair of shoes. You need both the left shoe and the right shoe to walk correctly. The left shoe is the **недоко́наний вид** (imperfective aspect), which describes the process, habit, or continuous action. The right shoe is the **доко́наний вид** (perfective aspect), which describes the result, achievement, or completion of that action. If you only learn one form, your vocabulary will always feel incomplete.

Here is a common kitchen scenario that shows the difference. When cooking, the difference between doing something and finishing it is very important.
<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Бабу́ся:</span> Що ти за́раз ро́биш? *(What are you doing right now?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Ону́чка:</span> Я **ліплю́** варе́ники. *(I am forming varenyky.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Бабуся:</span> До́бре. Ко́ли **злі́пиш** усі, скажи́ мені́. *(Good. When you finish forming all of them, tell me.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Онучка:</span> Я вже **зліпи́ла** де́сять штук! *(I have already formed ten pieces!)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Бабуся:</span> Молоде́ць! Тепе́р тре́ба їх **вари́ти**. *(Well done! Now we need to boil them.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Онучка:</span> Скі́льки ча́су? *(How much time?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Бабуся:</span> Хвили́н десять. Коли **зва́риш**, ми бу́демо ї́сти. *(About ten minutes. When you have boiled them until ready, we will eat.)*</div>

</div>

Notice the difference in meaning. We use the imperfective verbs **ліпи́ти** (to form) and **варити** (to boil) to focus on the ongoing process of cooking. We use the perfective verbs **зліпи́ти** (to finish forming) and **звари́ти** (to finish boiling) to focus on the final result.

Because these two forms are so closely connected, textbooks and dictionaries always present them together as an aspectual pair. The standard notation you will see is a slash separating the two verbs, like this: **роби́ти / зроби́ти** (to do / to have done). The first verb listed before the slash is almost always the imperfective form. The second verb listed after the slash is the perfective form. When you learn new vocabulary, you must memorize both parts of the pair together. The dictionary form, or the base lemma, is usually the imperfective one. If you want to know how to say the verb for writing, you should learn the entire pair **писа́ти / написа́ти** (to write / to have written) right from the start.

When you use a Ukrainian **словни́к** (dictionary) like Slovnyk.ua or ULIF, you will see this relationship clearly. If you look up an imperfective verb like **чита́ти** (to read), the dictionary definition will usually include a link to its perfective partner. You will see the abbreviation "док. вид", which stands for perfective aspect, pointing you directly to the result verb **прочита́ти** (to have read). Make it a habit to always check for this abbreviation when you look up a new action word so you can add both forms to your vocabulary list.

Ukrainian aspect pairs are not random. There are three highly predictable patterns that will help you group these pairs together and memorize them much faster.

## Спо́сіб 1: Додава́ння пре́фікса (Method 1: Adding a Prefix)

The most common way to create a perfective verb in Ukrainian is by adding a prefix to the beginning of an imperfective verb. Think of a prefix as a special cap that you place on an action to signal that it is completely finished. Without this cap, the action is open, ongoing, or repeating. Once you add the prefix, you close the action and focus entirely on the final result. The clearest example is the verb **писати** (to write). This is the imperfective form, used when you are in the process of writing or if you write regularly. When you want to say that you have finished writing something, like completing a letter, you add the prefix **на-** to create its perfective partner, **написати** (to finish writing). The core meaning of the action remains exactly the same. You are still dealing with the act of writing. However, the grammatical focus has shifted from the ongoing process to the successful completion of the task. This simple addition is the primary mechanism for forming aspectual pairs.

While adding a prefix is the standard method, there is a small catch. There is no single universal prefix that makes every verb perfective. Instead, different verbs require different prefixes to form their pure aspectual pairs. You have already seen **на-** used to create **написати**. The same prefix works with **малюва́ти** (to draw), creating the perfective **намалюва́ти** (to finish drawing). Another very common prefix is **про-**, which often gives the sense of reading or going through something completely. For example, the imperfective verb **читати** (to read) pairs with the perfective **прочитати** (to finish reading).  You will also frequently encounter the prefix **по-**. This prefix is incredibly versatile and pairs with many everyday actions. It creates perfective verbs like **поба́чити** (to catch sight of), **почу́ти** (to catch a sound), and **пообі́дати** (to have lunch). It is important to understand that in other contexts, these prefixes might change the actual meaning of a word. But here, their primary job is simply to mark the action as completed.

Another important prefix for forming perfective verbs is **з-** (or its variant **с-**). This prefix is often called the resultative prefix because it strongly emphasizes the successful completion of a final product. The most fundamental example is the verb **робити** (to do / to make). To say that you have finished doing or making something, you add the prefix **з-** to create **зробити** (to have done / to have made). You will also see this prefix in **їсти** (to eat), which becomes **з'ї́сти** (to eat up completely). Sometimes, other prefixes step in to serve the exact same resultative function. For instance, the imperfective verb **готува́ти** (to cook / to prepare) uses the prefix **при-** to form its perfective partner **приготува́ти** (to finish cooking). Because there is no strict rule dictating which prefix pairs with which verb, you cannot simply guess. You must memorize which specific prefix goes with which base verb to form the correct aspectual pair.

This prefix rule also applies to verbs of sensory perception, which behave in a specific way. The verbs for seeing and hearing behave in a specific way. The imperfective verbs **ба́чити** (to see) and **чу́ти** (to hear) describe the ongoing state of observing something. We use them when our senses are actively engaged over a period of time. However, when we want to describe the exact moment we first notice something or successfully catch a sound, we use their perfective partners: **побачити** and **почути**. Compare these two examples to feel the difference.
<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Окса́на:</span> Я **ба́чу** си́нє не́бо. *(I see the blue sky.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Степа́н:</span> А я що́йно **поба́чив** вели́кий літа́к! *(And I just caught sight of a big airplane!)*</div>

</div>

In the first sentence, the observation is continuous. In the second sentence, the perfective verb highlights the sudden, completed event of noticing the airplane.

As you learn to recognize these pairs, there is one common pitfall you must avoid. You cannot simply add any prefix to a verb and assume you have created its simple perfective partner. Adding a different prefix usually changes the meaning of the word entirely, creating a brand new verb. Return to our first example, **писати**. We know that **написати** is its pure perfective partner. But what if we add the prefix **під-**? We get the verb **підписа́ти** (to sign). This is no longer just finishing the act of writing; it is a completely different action with its own specific meaning. Another example is adding the prefix **пере-** to get **переписа́ти** (to rewrite). For now, as you build your foundation, we will only focus on the specific, designated prefixes that create the pure, basic partner for each action without changing its core dictionary definition.

## Спосіб 2: Змі́на в ко́рені або́ су́фіксі (Method 2: Change in the Root or Suffix)

In the first method, we saw how adding a short prefix to a basic verb creates a perfective partner. The second method works in the exact opposite direction. We start with a base verb that is already perfective—usually one that inherently contains a prefix—and we intentionally "stretch" it to make it imperfective. This grammatical process is called imperfectivization. By inserting a noticeable suffix into the word, the language visually and acoustically stretches the verb to represent an action that takes time. Compare the perfective verb **відпові́сти́** (to give an answer) with its imperfective partner **відповіда́ти** (to be answering). The perfective form is shorter and more abrupt, perfectly suited for a quick, completed result. The imperfective form physically expands on the page and in the mouth, mimicking the ongoing, continuous nature of the process.

The most common and easily recognizable tools for stretching a perfective verb into an ongoing process are the suffixes **-ува-** and **-юва-**. When you see these syllables appearing in the middle of a verb, you are usually looking at an imperfective process. Let us look at the perfective verb **ви́рішити** (to decide / to solve). To show the long, sometimes difficult process of making that decision or actively solving that problem, we insert the suffix to create **вирі́шувати** (to be deciding / to be solving). Similarly, the quick, one-time action of asking a single question is **запита́ти** (to ask). If someone is repeatedly asking questions, the verb expands into **запи́тувати** (to be asking). This particular suffix gives the verbs a rolling rhythm that naturally sounds like a continuous, uninterrupted activity.

Sometimes, simply adding a suffix is not the only structural change that happens when stretching a verb. The Ukrainian language often reinforces this grammatical expansion by simultaneously changing a core vowel deep inside the root of the word. The most frequent internal change you will encounter in these pairs is replacing the vowel **о** with the vowel **а**. For example, the perfective verb **допомогти́** (to help successfully) transforms into the imperfective **допомага́ти** (to be helping). Notice how the tight, round **о** sounds in the root completely shift to the wide, open **а** sounds. You can see the exact same linguistic pattern in the perfective verb **перемогти́** (to win / to overcome) and its imperfective partner **перемага́ти** (to be winning). Here is a useful memory trick: pronouncing the **а** sound requires opening the mouth wider, which you can associate with the openness of an ongoing action.

In other verb pairs, the aspectual transformation happens entirely inside the root without adding long suffixes at the end. The most important of these internal changes is the specific shift from the vowel **і** to the vowel **и**. Consider the perfective verb **зібра́ти** (to gather / to collect successfully). To describe the ongoing process of collecting things over a period of time, the tight **і** sound drops down to a deeper **и**, creating the imperfective **збира́ти** (to be gathering). You will encounter the exact same internal vowel shift with the perfective verb **надісла́ти** (to send a message or package) and its imperfective counterpart **надсила́ти** (to be sending). While these paired words look and sound very similar, that single internal vowel change completely alters how we perceive the flow of time within the action.

A common communication verb shows this pattern clearly. The perfective verb **розпові́сти́** (to tell / to relate) pairs with the expanded imperfective verb **розповіда́ти** (to be telling). Telling a detailed story is inherently a long, sequential process, so we use the longer, imperfective form when we want to focus on the duration of the speech itself. However, if the conversational focus is merely on the successful delivery of a specific piece of information, we switch to the shorter, perfective form.
<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Оксана:</span> Я зараз **розпові́м** тобі́ оди́н ціка́вий секре́т. *(I will tell you one interesting secret now.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Степан:</span> Мій діду́сь ду́же до́вго **розповіда́в** цю стару́ ка́зку. *(My grandfather was telling this old fairy tale for a very long time.)*</div>

</div>

## Спосіб 3: Зо́всім і́нші слова́ (суплетиві́зм) (Method 3: Completely Different Words — Suppletion)

Some of the most basic verbs use completely different words for their partners. Just like in English we say "good" and "better" instead of "gooder", the Ukrainian language has its own historical rebels. This grammatical phenomenon is called suppletion. Instead of neatly adding a prefix or stretching out a root vowel, these verbs demand that you memorize an entirely different word to express the completed action. Because these verbs represent fundamental human actions performed for thousands of years, they appear in nearly every conversation. You will hear and use them every single day.

Here are the "Big Four" essential pairs that every beginner must master. The most common pair is **говори́ти** (to be speaking) and **сказа́ти** (to say successfully). Another vital pair is **бра́ти** (to be taking) and **взя́ти** (to take successfully). For physical objects, we use **кла́сти** (to be laying down / to be putting) and **покла́сти** (to put successfully). Finally, there is the dynamic pair **лови́ти** (to be catching / to hunt) and **пійма́ти** (to catch successfully). Notice how the imperfective forms focus on the ongoing physical process, while their perfective partners abruptly present the final result.
<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Оксана:</span> Степа́не, допоможи́ мені! Я не мо́жу **знайти́** свою́ су́мку. *(Stepan, help me! I can't find my bag.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Степан:</span> Де ти її зазвича́й **кладе́ш**? *(Where do you usually put it?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Оксана:</span> Зазвичай я **кладу́** її на стіле́ць, але́ вчо́ра **покла́ла** на поли́цю. *(Usually I put it on the chair, but yesterday I put it on the shelf.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Степан:</span> Зачека́й, я **візьму́** ліхта́рик. Треба **брати** ре́чі і диви́тися під ними. *(Wait, I'll take a flashlight. We need to take things and look under them.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Оксана:</span> Я хо́чу **сказати** тобі де́що — я вже годи́ну шука́ю! *(I want to tell you something — I've been searching for an hour!)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Степан:</span> Не хвилю́йся. Ти за́вжди́ довго **гово́риш** про пробле́му, за́мість **ловити** моме́нт. О, диви́сь — я **пійма́в** її! Ось твоя́ су́мка, під ку́рткою! *(Don't worry. You always talk about the problem for a long time instead of seizing the moment. Oh, look — I caught it! Here's your bag, under the jacket!)*</div>

</div>

There is another pair that learners often group together: **шука́ти** (to search / to look for) and **знайти** (to find). Logically, these two words represent the chronological halves of the same real-world task — you spend time searching, and the ultimate result of that process is finding the object. However, these are actually two different actions, not a true aspectual pair. The real perfective partner of **шукати** is **пошука́ти** (to search for a while) or **відшука́ти** (to track down successfully). Meanwhile, **знайти** is the perfective partner of **знахо́дити** (to find / to be finding). Because **шукати** and **знайти** appear together so naturally in everyday life, many learners treat them as a pair — but grammatically, they describe two separate processes: the act of looking and the act of discovering.
<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Оксана:</span> Я бу́ду довго **шукати** свої́ ключі́, по́ки не змо́жу їх **знайти**. *(I will look for my keys for a long time until I can find them.)*</div>

</div>

## Підсумок (Summary)

Understanding verb aspect is one of the most fundamental steps in mastering the Ukrainian language. You have learned that almost all verbs operate as pairs — one imperfective for the process, and one perfective for the result. Remember that you cannot truly learn a new verb without also memorizing its aspectual partner.

As a quick review, there are three primary ways these aspectual pairs are formed:
1. **Adding a prefix:** This is the most common method. The base imperfective verb receives a prefix to become perfective (for example, **писати** / **написати** — *to write*).
2. **Changing a suffix or a root vowel:** Often, a perfective verb alters its suffix to become imperfective. This can also trigger a vowel change inside the root (for example, **вирішити** / **вирішувати** — *to decide*).
3. **Using completely different words (suppletion):** Some of the most essential and frequently used verbs use entirely different roots for their aspectual forms and simply must be memorized (for example, **брати** / **взяти** — *to take*).

Before finishing this module, ask yourself these four self-check questions to test your understanding:
- Яка різни́ця між «пити» і «випити»? *(What is the difference between "пити" and "випити"?)*
- Як знайти па́ру в словнику́? *(How do you find a pair in a dictionary?)*
- Яка па́ра до дієслова «сказати»? *(What is the pair for the verb "сказати"?)*
- Чому важли́во зна́ти оби́два слова? *(Why is it important to know both words?)*
