## Яки́й? Яка? Яке́? Які́? (Which? What Kind?)

<div class="dialogue">


**Оксана:** Приві́т! Ти не ба́чив кни́жку, яка лежа́ла тут на столі́? *(Hi! Have you seen the book that was lying here on the table?)*


**Максим:** Яку книжку? Ту, яка в черво́ній обкла́динці? *(Which book? The one that is in the red cover?)*


**Оксана:** Ні, ту, яку я купи́ла вчо́ра. *(No, the one that I bought yesterday.)*


**Максим:** А, так. Хло́пець, який сиді́в тут рані́ше, взяв її почита́ти. *(Ah, yes. The guy who was sitting here earlier took it to read.)*


</div>


When we communicate, we rarely speak in short, isolated sentences. Instead of saying "I have a friend. He lives in Kyiv," we naturally combine these ideas: "I have a friend **who** lives in Kyiv." In Ukrainian grammar, the part of a **речення** (sentence, clause) that starts with "who," "which," or "that" and describes a noun is called an **означальний** (attributive, defining) clause. 

The most common way to link these clauses is using the relative pronoun **який** (which, who, that). Just like an adjective, **який** must perfectly match the noun it describes in gender and number. 

Let us look at how the endings change depending on what exactly you are describing.

If the noun is masculine, we use **який**:
- Це хлопець, **який** до́бре гра́є на гіта́рі. (This is the guy who plays the guitar well.)
- Де телефо́н, **який** лежа́в на дива́ні? (Where is the phone that was lying on the sofa?)
- Я зна́ю студе́нта, **який** вивча́є украї́нську мо́ву. (I know a student who is studying the Ukrainian language.)

If the noun is feminine, we use **яка**:
- Ось ді́вчи́на, **яка** працю́є в на́шому о́фісі. (Here is the girl who works in our office.)
- Це маши́на, **яка** ї́де ду́же шви́дко. (This is a car that drives very fast.)
- Ме́ні подо́бається пі́сня, **яка** за́раз грає. (I like the song that is playing right now.)

If the noun is neuter, we use **яке**:
- Це мі́сто, **яке** ніколи не спить. (This is a city that never sleeps.)
- Я чита́ю повідо́млення, **яке** ти мені надісла́в. (I am reading the message that you sent me.)
- Де молоко́, **яке** було́ в холоди́льнику? (Where is the milk that was in the fridge?)

For all plural nouns, regardless of their gender, we use **які**:
- Це лю́ди, **які** за́вжди́ гото́ві допомогти́. (These are people who are always ready to help.)
- Я люблю́ книжки́, **які** ма́ють щасли́вий кіне́ць. (I like books that have a happy ending.)
- Де гроші, **які** лежа́ли на столі? (Where is the money that was lying on the table?)

### The Case of Який

There is a very important rule about the case of **який**: it takes the case required by its specific role *inside the subordinate clause*, not the main clause. 

At the A2 level, you mostly need to focus on distinguishing between the Nominative case (when **який** is the subject doing the action) and the Accusative case (when **яку/якого/які** is the direct object receiving the action).

Look at the difference:
- Це люди́на, **яка** мене́ зустрі́ла. (This is the person who met me. — *The person did the meeting, so **яка** is Nominative.*)
- Це людина, **яку** я зустрі́в. (This is the person whom I met. — *I did the meeting, the person was met, so **яку** is Accusative.*)

Let us look at a few more examples to make this clear. Pay attention to who is performing the action in the second part of the sentence:
- Фільм, **який** ми диви́лися вчора, був дуже нудни́й. (The film that we watched yesterday was very boring. — *We watched the film, Accusative for inanimate masculine is the same as Nominative.*)
- Кни́жка, **яку** ти чита́єш, дуже популя́рна. (The book that you are reading is very popular. — *Feminine Accusative changes **яка** to **яку**.*)
- Студе́нти, **яких** я бачив, ішли́ до університе́ту. (The students whom I saw were going to the university. — *Plural animate Accusative changes **які** to **яких**.*)

### Commas and Questions

In English, you can often drop the word "that" (e.g., "The book I read"). In Ukrainian, you can **never** drop **який**, and you must **always** place a comma before it. This comma signals the start of the new clause. 
- Дім**, який** стої́ть на ро́зі, дуже стари́й. (The house that stands on the corner is very old.)

Finally, remember that you already know **який** as a question word meaning "which?" or "what kind?". It is the exact same word, but its function changes based on the context.
- **Question:** **Який** це фільм? (What kind of film is this?)
- **Relative clause:** Фільм, **який** ми дивилися, був чудо́вий. (The film that we watched was wonderful.)

You might also occasionally hear the word **котрий** (which). This is a formal synonym of **який**. While perfectly correct, **який** is much more common in everyday speech.

## Де, куди́, зві́дки — мі́сце (Where, Where To, Where From — Place)

<div class="dialogue">


**Анна:** Ти пам'ята́єш кафе́, де ми пили ка́ву мину́лого ти́жня? *(Do you remember the cafe where we drank coffee last week?)*


**Богдан:** Так, зві́сно. А ти зна́єш краї́ну, звідки вони́ приво́зять ці зе́рна? *(Yes, of course. Do you know the country where they bring these beans from?)*


**Анна:** Здає́ться, з Колу́мбії. До ре́чі, парк, куди ми хо́димо бі́гати, зо́всім по́руч із цим кафе. *(I think from Colombia. By the way, the park where we go running is right next to this cafe.)*


</div>


When we want to describe a place using a relative clause, we have special relative words that make our sentences sound much more natural and fluid. Instead of using **який**, we use words that express location, direction, or origin: **де** (where), **куди** (where to), and **звідки** (where from).

### Using ДЕ for Location

If the relative clause describes *where* something is located or *where* an action happens, we use **де**. This is the exact same word you use to ask "Where is...?", but here it connects two parts of a sentence.

- Кафе, **де** ми зустрі́лися, було дуже **затишним** (cozy, comfortable). (The cafe where we met was very cozy.)
- Місто, **де** я народи́вся, **знаходиться** (to be located) на заході Украї́ни. (The city where I was born is located in the west of Ukraine.)
- Це буди́нок, **де** живе́ моя́ бабу́ся. (This is the house where my grandmother lives.)
- Я забу́в на́зву ву́лиці, **де** розташо́ваний музе́й. (I forgot the name of the street where the museum is located.)

You might be wondering: could we use **який** instead? Yes, grammatically you could say "Будинок, **в яко́му** живе мій друг" (The house in which my friend lives). However, at the A2 level and in everyday spoken Ukrainian, saying "Будинок, **де** живе мій друг" is much simpler, more elegant, and more natural. 

### Using КУДИ for Direction

If the relative clause describes a destination—a place you are moving *toward*—we must use **куди** (where to). You will almost always see **куди** paired with verbs of motion like іти́ (to go on foot), ї́хати (to go by vehicle), or леті́ти (to fly).

- Парк, **куди** ми ходимо гуля́ти, дуже вели́кий. (The park where we go walking is very big.)
- Місто, **куди** він пої́хав працюва́ти, дале́ко зві́дси. (The city where he went to work is far from here.)
- Я не знаю мі́сця, **куди** вони пішли́. (I do not know the place where they went.)
- Університе́т, **куди** вона́ вступа́є, має га́рну репута́цію. (The university where she is applying has a good reputation.)

Notice that in English, we often just use "where" for both location ("the park where we walk") and direction ("the park where we are going"). In Ukrainian, the distinction between static location (**де**) and active direction (**куди**) is extremely strict.

### Using ЗВІДКИ for Origin

Finally, if the relative clause describes where something or someone comes *from*, we use **звідки** (where from).

- Краї́на, **звідки** вона приї́хала, — це Украї́на. (The country where she came from is Ukraine.)
- Місто, **звідки** ро́дом мій діду́сь, має ціка́ву істо́рію. (The city where my grandfather is from has an interesting history.)
- Я бачив село́, **звідки** привозять ці сві́жі о́вочі. (I saw the village where they bring these fresh vegetables from.)
- Кімна́та, **звідки** луна́ла му́зика, була зачи́нена. (The room where the music was coming from was closed.)

Using **де**, **куди**, and **звідки** as relative words allows you to bypass complex case endings. Instead of worrying about "в якому", "до якого", or "з якого", you can just use these straightforward place markers. Just remember the golden rule: always put a comma before them!

## Опи́суємо люде́й, речі та місця (Describing People, Things, and Places)

<div class="dialogue">


**Марія:** Розкажи́ мені про свою́ нову́ кварти́ру! *(Tell me about your new apartment!)*


**Денис:** Це чудо́ва кварти́ра, яка знаходиться в це́нтрі мі́ста. Будинок, де я живу́, дуже старий, але́ там га́рний ремо́нт. *(It is a wonderful apartment that is located in the city center. The house where I live is very old, but there is a good renovation there.)*


**Марія:** А сусі́ди які? *(And what are the neighbors like?)*


**Денис:** Жі́нка, яка живе поруч, працює лі́каркою. А хлопець, з яки́м ми і́ноді гово́римо, вивчає програмува́ння. *(The woman who lives next door works as a doctor. And the guy with whom we sometimes talk is studying programming.)*


</div>


The primary purpose of **означальні** clauses is to help us **описувати** (to describe) the world around us in a mature, flowing manner. When you first learn a language, you tend to speak in staccato, disconnected bursts. Relative clauses are the glue that turns those bursts into smooth, connected thoughts.

### Stacking Information Naturally

Look at how a beginner might describe a friend:
*Це мій друг. Він живе у Києві. Він працює програмі́стом. Він лю́бить гра́ти у футбо́л.*
(This is my friend. He lives in Kyiv. He works as a programmer. He likes to play football.)

While grammatically correct, it sounds robotic. Now, let us combine this using **який** and conjunctions to create a natural, sophisticated sentence:
*Це мій друг, **який** живе у Києві і працює програмістом, а у ві́льний час любить грати у футбол.*
(This is my friend, who lives in Kyiv and works as a programmer, and in his free time likes to play football.)

You can do the same when describing places:
*Рестора́н. Ми вчора вече́ряли там. Він знаходиться бі́ля парку. Там смачна́ ї́жа.*
(A restaurant. We had dinner there yesterday. It is located near the park. There is delicious food there.)

Becomes:
*Ресторан, **де** ми вчора вечеряли, знаходиться біля парку і готу́є дуже смачну́ ї́жу.*
(The restaurant where we had dinner yesterday is located near the park and prepares very delicious food.)

Notice how the relative clause ("де ми вчора вечеряли") sits right inside the main sentence, perfectly separating the subject ("Ресторан") from its main verb ("знаходиться").

### Avoiding Common Errors

As you start building these longer sentences, there are a few common pitfalls you need to actively avoid. Ukrainian grammar is strict about redundancy and agreement.

**Error 1: The Redundant Pronoun**
In some languages, speakers insert a personal pronoun inside the relative clause just to be safe. In Ukrainian, this is completely wrong. The word **який** already replaces the pronoun.
- ❌ WRONG: Книжка, **яка вона** ціка́ва... (The book, which it is interesting...)
- ✅ RIGHT: Книжка, **яка** дуже цікава... (The book, which is very interesting...)

**Error 2: Wrong Gender Agreement**
You must always look backwards at the specific noun you are describing to choose between який, яка, яке, or які. 
- ❌ WRONG: Хлопець, **яке** чита́є кни́гу... (The guy [masculine], which [neuter] reads a book...)
- ✅ RIGHT: Хлопець, **який** читає книгу... (The guy, who reads a book...)

**Error 3: The Missing Comma**
We mentioned this before, but it bears repeating. In Ukrainian syntax, the boundaries between clauses must be marked with punctuation. 
- ❌ WRONG: Машина яка стоїть там моя.
- ✅ RIGHT: Машина**, яка** **стоїть** (to stand, to be situated) там**,** моя. (The car that is parked there is mine.)

### Practical Production

To truly master this, try looking around your room right now and forming sentences in your head. 
- Find an object you use daily: *Це телефон, який я купив минулого ро́ку.* (This is the phone that I bought last year.)
- Think of a person you admire: *Вона людина, яка завжди ка́же пра́вду.* (She is a person who always tells the truth.)
- Think of your favorite place: *Це парк, де я люблю чита́ти.* (This is the park where I like to read.)

By actively connecting your thoughts with **який** and **де**, you will quickly start to think in Ukrainian rather than translating piece by piece from English.

## Підсумок

In this module, we learned how to build complex sentences using **означальні** relative clauses. You now know how to connect ideas naturally and **описувати** the world around you in greater detail.

Here are the key takeaways:
- Use **який**, **яка**, **яке**, and **які** to describe nouns. They must agree in gender and number with the noun they modify.
- The case of **який** depends on its function inside the relative clause (for example, Nominative for the subject, Accusative for the direct object).
- Always place a comma before the relative pronoun to separate the clauses in your **речення**.
- When describing places, use **де** for location (where it is), **куди** for direction (where it is going), and **звідки** for origin (where it is from).
- Avoid redundant pronouns (never say "який він") and focus on stacking information smoothly.

By practicing these patterns, your Ukrainian will become much more expressive and sound significantly closer to how native speakers actually converse in everyday life!
