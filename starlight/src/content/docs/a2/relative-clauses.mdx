## Який? Яка? Яке? Які? (Which? What Kind?) (~800 words total)

When we want to **описувати** (to describe) something in detail, a simple adjective is not always enough. Sometimes we need to describe a noun using a full action or a specific fact. That is when we use an **означальний** (attributive) clause. This is a type of subordinate **речення** (sentence, clause) that connects to the main sentence and acts like one big adjective. Instead of saying "the good book," we say "the book that I am reading." We call the first part the main clause, and the second part the subordinate clause.

В українській мові ми часто використовуємо такі речення, щоб дати більше інформації. Головне речення просто називає предмет або людину, а підрядне речення детально описує їх.

> *In Ukrainian, we often use such sentences to give more information. The main sentence simply names the object or person, and the subordinate clause describes them in detail.*

To connect the subordinate clause to the main sentence, we use the relative pronoun **який** (which, that — masc.). Just like a regular adjective, this connector must match the exact gender of the noun it describes. If the noun is masculine, we use *який*. If it is feminine, we switch to **яка** (which, that — fem.), and for a neuter noun, we must use **яке** (which, that — neut.). The connector perfectly mirrors the gender of the noun it refers back to.

Ось великий новий будинок, який стоїть у самому центрі міста. Це моя світла квартира, яка має балкон і великі вікна. А там є гарне маленьке місто, яке знаходиться прямо біля моря.

> *Here is a large new house that stands in the very center of the city. This is my bright apartment that has a balcony and large windows. And there is a beautiful small city that is located right near the sea.*

When we describe multiple people, places, or objects, the individual gender of those nouns no longer matters. We simply drop the singular forms and use the plural form **які** (which, that — pl.) for absolutely everything. This form is universal and makes it very easy to describe groups of things, lists of items, or crowds of people. You transition seamlessly from using *який* or *яка* for one item to using *які* for many items.

У цьому великому офісі є люди, які працюють тут щодня. На столі лежать мої старі ключі, які я завжди гублю вранці. Студенти, які уважно читають цей текст, добре знають нову граматику.

> *In this large office, there are people who work here every day. On the table lie my old keys, which I always lose in the morning. The students who carefully read this text know the new grammar well.*

We established that the relative pronoun takes its gender and number directly from the main noun it describes. However, its case is completely independent of the main sentence! The case of the connector is determined exclusively by its grammatical role inside the subordinate clause. 

Let us focus on the Nominative and Accusative cases. If the pronoun represents the subject doing the action inside the sub-clause, it stays in the Nominative case. But if it represents the object receiving the action, it must change to the Accusative case. If the main noun is feminine, we start with *яка*. Inside the sub-clause, if the item is the direct object, *яка* changes to its Accusative form, *яку*. If the noun is masculine animate, *який* changes to *якого* when it becomes the direct object.

Там стоїть високий хлопець, який прийшов до нас учора. Це той самий хлопець, якого я бачив учора в магазині. Я із задоволенням читаю нову книгу, яку ти мені дав.

> *There stands a tall boy who came to us yesterday. This is the same boy whom I saw yesterday in the store. I am reading with pleasure the new book that you gave me.*

In English, you can often drop the connecting word entirely or skip the punctuation, saying something fast like "the car I saw." In Ukrainian, the punctuation is strictly regulated and cannot be ignored. You must always place a comma immediately before the relative pronoun.

:::info
**The Grammar Gate**
Think of the comma as a necessary physical gate that opens to let the descriptive clause into the sentence. Without this comma, the sentence structure collapses, and it looks very unnatural to a native speaker. Always pause and place the comma.
:::

Я бачу нову машину яка стоїть там. Це речення написано абсолютно неправильно. Я бачу нову машину, яка стоїть там. Ось це речення написане правильно.

> *I see the new car that stands there. This sentence is written absolutely incorrectly. I see the new car, that stands there. Here this sentence is written correctly.*

While the word *який* is the standard formal connector you will learn and use, Ukrainian has a highly idiomatic and very natural alternative: the short word *що*. In the Nominative and Accusative cases, you can often replace *який* with *що*. Unlike *який*, *що* never changes its form for gender or number in these relative clauses. Historically, Russian grammar discouraged this specific use of *що*, favoring their own equivalent of *який*, but in natural, spoken Ukrainian, *що* is universally loved and extremely common. It makes speech flow faster.

Це дуже добра людина, що завжди охоче допомагає всім навколо. Ось той цікавий новий фільм, що ми разом дивилися вчора ввечері.

## Де, куди, звідки — місце (Where, Where To, Where From — Place)

We have just learned how to use *який* and its forms to add descriptive details about people and objects. But what happens when we want to describe a physical location? While you can technically use *який* combined with prepositions (like "the house in which I live" or "the city to which we traveled"), Ukrainian offers a much simpler and more elegant solution. When describing a location, a direction of movement, or a point of origin, we use the relative adverbs **де** (where), **куди** (where to), and **звідки** (where from). These short words act as seamless connectors that make your sentences flow smoothly, allowing you to bypass complex case endings entirely.

Коли ми **описуємо** місце, **де** щось **знаходиться** або де відбувається певна дія, ми використовуємо слово «де». Це дуже зручно для розмови, бо нам не потрібно думати про відмінки та прийменники. Замість того, щоб будувати складну конструкцію і казати «кав'ярня, в якій ми були», ми просто кажемо «кав'ярня, де ми були». Це звучить набагато коротше і природніше. Наприклад: «Ось той старий будинок, де живе мій найкращий друг». Або: «Це той **затишний** ресторан, де ми вчора дуже смачно вечеряли». Слово «де» завжди вказує на статичне місце. Воно показує фіксовану позицію без жодного напрямку чи руху.

> *When we describe a place where something is located or where a certain action happens, we use the word "де". It is very convenient for conversation because we do not need to think about cases and prepositions. Instead of building a complex structure and saying "the cafe in which we were", we simply say "the cafe where we were". This sounds much shorter and more natural. For example: "Here is that old building where my best friend lives." Or: "This is that cozy restaurant where we had a very delicious dinner yesterday." The word "де" always indicates a static place. It shows a fixed position without any direction or movement.*

:::info
**The "In Which" Shortcut**
In formal writing or literature, you might frequently see *в якому* (in which) or *на якому* (on which) used to describe places. However, in everyday spoken Ukrainian, **де** is heavily preferred by native speakers. It replaces the entire preposition and pronoun combination, making your speech much faster and highly idiomatic.
:::

Але якщо ми говоримо про рух або напрямок, слово «де» нам зовсім не підходить. Для таких ситуацій в українській мові є спеціальне слово «**куди**». Воно показує фінальну мету нашої подорожі або напрямок нашої дії. Порівняйте ці два дуже схожі **речення**: «місто, де я зараз живу» та «місто, куди ми поїдемо у відпустку наступного літа». У першому реченні немає жодного руху, а в другому є активний рух до нової локації. Ще один гарний приклад: «Це той новий магазин, куди вона зайшла п'ять хвилин тому». Пам'ятайте, що дієслова руху завжди вимагають слова «куди» в таких означальних реченнях.

> *But if we are talking about movement or direction, the word "де" does not suit us at all. For such situations, the Ukrainian language has a special word "куди". It shows the final destination of our journey or the direction of our action. Compare these two very similar sentences: "the city where I live now" and "the city where we will go on vacation next summer". In the first sentence, there is no movement at all, and in the second, there is active movement to a new location. Another good example: "This is that new store where she went in five minutes ago." Remember that verbs of motion always require the word "куди" in such attributive clauses.*

Третя важлива ситуація — це коли ми говоримо про походження чогось або пояснюємо, звідки хтось приїхав. Для цього ми використовуємо слово «**звідки**». Воно чітко показує точку старту або джерело. Наприклад: «Це те маленьке село, звідки вранці привезли це свіже молоко». Або: «Я дуже хочу побачити країну, звідки він приїхав до нас минулого року». Як і інші слова в цій граматичній групі, «звідки» завжди стоїть відразу після коми і починає нову, залежну частину речення, яка дає нам додаткову інформацію про місце.

> *The third important situation is when we talk about the origin of something or explain where someone came from. For this, we use the word "звідки". It clearly shows the starting point or source. For example: "This is that small village where this fresh milk was brought from in the morning." Or: "I really want to see the country where he came to us from last year." Like other words in this grammatical group, "звідки" always stands immediately after a comma and starts a new, dependent part of the sentence, which gives us additional information about the place.*

Let's see how these specific place connectors work together in a natural, everyday conversation. Our real estate agent is now showing the prospective buyer the neighborhood around the apartment. Notice how smoothly the agent and the buyer switch between static location, active direction, and points of origin depending on what they are discussing.

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Покупець:</span> Цей район виглядає дуже приємно. А де знаходиться великий парк, куди ми можемо ходити гуляти на вихідних? *(This neighborhood looks very pleasant. And where is the large park where we can go walking on the weekends?)*</div>

To quickly summarize how we describe places: if you are answering "at what place?" or describing a static location, use **де** (where). If you are answering "to what place?" with a verb of motion, use **куди** (where to). And if you are answering "from what place?" or discussing an origin, use **звідки** (where from). Mastering these three short words is the key to talking about locations fluently and naturally.

## Описуємо людей, речі та місця (Describing People, Things, and Places) (~600 words total)

Now that we know how to use the basic relative words, it is time to put everything together. The main reason we learn these structures is to naturally **описувати** (to describe) the people, objects, and places around us. In linguistics, a clause that describes a noun is called an **означальний** (attributive, defining) clause. Instead of speaking in short, choppy fragments, we can use a relative **речення** (sentence, clause) to combine our observations and create a smooth, sophisticated flow of information.

Ми часто використовуємо короткі фрази, коли тільки починаємо вивчати мову. Наприклад, ми кажемо: «Мій брат живе у Польщі. Він працює лікарем». Це правильно, але звучить трохи неприродно. Якщо ми використаємо слово «який», наша розмова стане набагато кращою. Ми можемо сказати: «Мій брат, який працює лікарем, живе зараз у Польщі».

> *We often use short phrases when we are just starting to learn a language. For example, we say: "My brother lives in Poland. He works as a doctor." This is correct, but it sounds a bit unnatural. If we use the word "який" (which), our conversation will become much better. We can say: "My brother, who works as a doctor, lives in Poland now."*

This combined structure allows you to give a vivid description of a person’s profession, habits, or characteristics without ever pausing the main story. However, English speakers often make a specific structural mistake when building these sentences. Because English grammar sometimes allows or even requires different phrasing, learners might unconsciously try to repeat the pronoun inside the relative clause.

Англомовні студенти часто кажуть: «Це нова книга, яку я читав її вчора». Вони додають слово «її», бо думають про англійську структуру. Але в українській мові слово «яку» вже замінює цю книгу. Тому правильно буде сказати просто: «Це нова книга, яку я читав вчора».

> *English-speaking students often say: "This is a new book, which I read it yesterday." They add the word "її" (it) because they are thinking about English structure. But in Ukrainian, the word "яку" already replaces this book. Therefore, it will be correct to say simply: "This is a new book, which I read yesterday."*

:::info
**The Redundant Pronoun**
Once you use a form of **який**, you do not need to add another pronoun (like *його*, *її*, *їх*) to represent the same object inside the clause. The relative word does all the heavy lifting!
:::

Another crucial aspect of mastering these descriptions is intonation. A comma in Ukrainian is not just a grammatical requirement for writing; it is a physical, auditory signal for your voice. It tells the listener that a descriptive addition is coming.

Коли ми читаємо складне речення, наша інтонація змінюється. Спочатку ми говоримо нормально, а перед комою робимо коротку паузу. Потім, коли ми читаємо підрядну частину, наш голос стає трохи нижчим. Після другої коми голос знову повертається до нормального тону.

This "breath pause" frames the relative clause as a parenthetical insert. It helps the listener process that you are adding extra details before you finish your main thought. As you become more comfortable, you can start stacking different types of information naturally. You can describe what an object is like, where it is located, and where it came from, all in one connected breath.

Це старий будинок, який стоїть на горі і де колись жив відомий поет. Поруч є річка, яка тече через місто. А ось кафе, яке ми знайшли вчора і звідки завжди пахне кавою. Ми любимо місця, куди хочеться повертатися і які мають свою історію.

> *This is an old house, which stands on the hill and where a famous poet once lived. Nearby is a river, which flows through the city. And here is a cafe, which we found yesterday and from where it always smells of coffee. We love places where we want to return and which have their own history.*

Now it is your turn to practice this descriptive skill. Think about your favorite object, a special place, or a person you truly admire. Try to describe them using a complex sentence. Consider adding interesting details about their history, their exact location, or the reasons why they are important to you.

## Підсумок (~150 words)

Let's recap how to form an **означальний** (attributive, defining) clause. When you need **описувати** (to describe) a noun, you use a complex **речення** (sentence, clause) to add details.

*   The pronouns **який** (which, that — masc.), **яка** (which, that — fem.), and **яке** (which, that — neut.) must match the gender of the noun.
*   For plural nouns, always use **які** (which, that — pl.).
*   Always place a comma before these relative words.
*   Use **де** (where — relative) for location and **куди** (where to — relative) for movement.
*   Use **звідки** (where from — relative) for origin.
*   Inside the sub-clause, the pronoun takes the case required by the verb (e.g., *яку я бачу* — Accusative).

:::tip
**Did you know?**
You can use these relative words to combine two or three simple ideas into one elegant sentence. It makes your Ukrainian sound much more natural!
:::
