## Давальний відмінок: Кому? (The Dative Case: To Whom?) (~550 words total)

Welcome to one of the most generous cases in the Ukrainian language. We are introducing the **давальний відмінок** (dative case). The very name of this case comes from the verb давати (to give). This perfectly illustrates its primary function. It marks the recipient of an action, the person or thing that benefits from what is happening. When you want to know who is receiving a gift, a message, or help, you ask the core questions of this case. For people, we ask «кому?» (to whom?). For objects or concepts, we ask «чому?» (to what?).

Українська мова має шість відмінків. Сьогодні ми вивчаємо новий відмінок. Давальний відмінок відповідає на питання «кому?» та «чому?». Ця назва походить від слова «давати». Ми використовуємо його, коли людина отримує предмет, інформацію або допомогу.

> *The Ukrainian language has six cases. Today we are learning a new case. The dative case answers the questions "to whom?" and "to what?". This name comes from the word "to give". We use it when a person receives an object, information, or help.*

To understand how this works, we need to contrast it with the cases you already know. The Nominative case is the doer of the action. The Accusative case is the direct object that is being affected or moved. The Dative case steps in as the indirect object. We will start using specific pronouns like **мені** (to me) and **тобі** (to you (informal)). The Dative marks the destination. Look at a simple foundational example: I am giving a book to you. The Dative case marks the person who benefits from the action or receives the information.

Я даю нову книгу. Слово «я» — це називний відмінок, бо я роблю дію. Слово «книгу» — це знахідний відмінок, бо це прямий об'єкт. Але кому я даю цю книгу? Я даю книгу тобі. Слово «тобі» стоїть у давальному відмінку. Це наш адресат.

> *I am giving a new book. The word "I" is the nominative case, because I am doing the action. The word "book" is the accusative case, because it is the direct object. But to whom am I giving this book? I am giving the book to you. The word "to you" is in the dative case. This is our addressee.*

:::info
**The Recipient Rule**
Always look for the destination of the action. If the action is moving toward someone, or if someone is benefiting from it, that person takes the Dative case.
:::

To start using this case immediately, you need a starter kit of verbs that naturally trigger it. These are high-frequency communication and interaction verbs. The most common ones are давати (to give), дарувати (to gift), and допомагати (to help). Whenever you use these verbs, the person receiving the action takes the Dative form. You will see forms like **йому** (to him, to it), **їй** (to her), and **нам** (to us) very often. Let's look at how these verbs work with our new pronouns.

Учитель допомагає мені читати текст. Я пишу тобі довгого листа. Бабуся завжди дарує йому солодощі. Дідусь часто каже їй приємні слова. Ми даємо вам ці зошити. Вони говорять нам усю правду. Я допомагаю їм робити ремонт.

> *The teacher helps me read the text. I am writing a long letter to you. Grandmother always gifts him sweets. Grandfather often says pleasant words to her. We are giving you these notebooks. They are telling us the whole truth. I am helping them do renovations.*

Let's see all these pronouns in a natural, repetitive context. Imagine a family gathering where birthday gifts are being distributed. This is the perfect situation to establish the Dative pronouns as recipients. Finally, pay attention to **вам** (to you (formal/plural)) and **їм** (to them) in the conversation below. Notice who gets what!

## Особові займенники у давальному відмінку (Personal Pronouns in the Dative) (~750 words total)

When you want to say "to me" or "to you" in Ukrainian, you cannot simply use the dictionary forms **я** (I) and **ти** (you). In the **давальний відмінок** (dative case), these pronouns change their shape completely. This phenomenon, where a word changes its root entirely, is called "suppletion". It is common in many languages, but in Ukrainian, the change is very distinct and vital for basic communication. The forms for the first and second person singular are **мені** (to me) and **тобі** (to you (informal)). They share a similar rhythmic ending with the letter «і», making them feel like a natural pair.

Ти ніколи не можеш сказати «Дай я книгу» або «Я допомагаю ти». Це дуже серйозна помилка, бо ці слова стоять у називному відмінку і є підметами. Якщо ти адресат дії, ти мусиш змінити форму. Скажи мені правду, і я обов'язково допоможу тобі. Тобі дуже пасує ця нова синя сорочка.

> *You can never say "Give I a book" or "I am helping you (Nominative)". This is a very serious mistake because these words are in the nominative case and are subjects. If you are the recipient of the action, you must change the form. Tell me the truth, and I will definitely help you. This new blue shirt suits you very much.*

:::tip
**Sounding Natural**
A very common mistake learners make is translating the English "for me" word-for-word as «для мене» when the Ukrainian verb naturally takes the Dative. If a verb involves communicating, giving, or recommending something, skip the preposition and stick to the simple Dative pronoun: **мені**, **тобі**.
:::

Moving to the third person singular, we find forms that might look slightly more familiar if you already know other Ukrainian cases, but they still require careful memorization. For both masculine and neuter pronouns (**він**, **воно**), the Dative form is exactly the same: **йому** (to him, to it). For the feminine pronoun (**вона**), the form is **їй** (to her). Notice the unique spelling of **їй** — it starts with the letter «ї», which always contains two distinct sounds. These short words are incredibly frequent in daily conversation.

Коли мій брат має проблеми на роботі, я завжди телефоную йому ввечері. Ми часто даруємо їй красиві квіти на свято. Я даю йому мій старий ноутбук, бо його комп'ютер зараз не працює. Вона завжди дуже рада, коли ми допомагаємо їй робити ремонт.

> *When my brother has problems at work, I always call him in the evening. We often gift her beautiful flowers for the holiday. I am giving him my old laptop because his computer does not work now. She is always very glad when we help her do renovations.*

The plural pronouns in the Dative case are often the easiest and most satisfying for learners to master. They feel highly symmetrical and follow a clear, predictable pattern. The pronoun **ми** (we) becomes **нам** (to us), **ви** (you) becomes **вам** (to you (formal/plural)), and **вони** (they) becomes **їм** (to them). All of these plural forms end in the letter «м». This consistent ending makes them very easy to group together in your memory and recall instantly during a fast conversation.

Вчитель на уроці щодня пояснює нам нове граматичне правило. Я щиро дякую вам за вашу велику допомогу вчора вранці. Мої друзі чекають на вулиці, тому потрібно їм зателефонувати прямо зараз. Ми даємо вам ці важливі документи для роботи.

> *The teacher in the lesson explains a new grammar rule to us every day. I sincerely thank you for your great help yesterday morning. My friends are waiting on the street, so it is necessary to call them right now. We are giving you these important documents for work.*

:::info
**The "Initial Н" Rule**
You might remember that in the Genitive and Accusative cases, third-person pronouns gain an extra «н» after prepositions (like **до нього**, **у неї**, or **на них**). In the Dative case, you will almost never use prepositions in basic speech because the Dative case itself acts like the preposition "to". The recipient forms are simply **йому**, **їй**, and **їм**. Even when a rare Dative preposition is used (like **завдяки** — thanks to), the pronoun usually does NOT gain an «н».
:::

It is crucial not to over-generalize the rules you learned for other cases. If you try to add an «н» to a Dative pronoun where it does not belong, it will sound like a confusing mix-up with the Genitive case. When it is **потрібно** (necessary, needed) to give a gift to someone, or when you tell someone a secret, you are directing the action straight to **йому** or **їй**, without any extra letters getting in the way. Let us look at a conversation where friends are planning a surprise party and using various Dative pronouns to figure out who knows what.

<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Олена:</span> У Павла завтра день народження. Я скажу йому наш великий секрет про вечірку. *(Pavlo has a birthday tomorrow. I will tell him our big secret about the party.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марко:</span> А нам ти скажеш цей секрет сьогодні, чи ми чекаємо? *(And will you tell us this secret today, or are we waiting?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Оксана:</span> Вам я скажу трохи пізніше, а їм — тільки завтра вранці. *(I will tell you a little later, and to them — only tomorrow morning.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марко:</span> Добре, тоді ми просто купимо йому дуже смачний торт. *(Good, then we will just buy him a very tasty cake.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Олена:</span> А його сестрі? Що ми подаруємо їй на свято? *(And to his sister? What will we gift her for the holiday?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Оксана:</span> Їй ми купимо червоні квіти, бо вона нам завжди допомагає. *(We will buy her red flowers because she always helps us.)*</div>

## Мені холодно: Безособові конструкції (Impersonal Constructions) (~550 words total)

In English, when you describe how you feel, you usually make yourself the subject of the sentence. The state is treated as something you are. In Ukrainian, however, many physical and emotional states are not something you are, but rather something that happens to you. These are called impersonal constructions because there is no traditional subject performing an action. Instead, the person experiencing the feeling becomes the logical subject, and we use the **давальний відмінок** (dative case) to show who is receiving that feeling. You can think of it as saying that a state is happening to someone. This creates a clear distinction between your core identity and your current, temporary state. If you describe yourself using the Nominative case, you are expressing a long-term state or a core part of who you are. But if you want to express a sudden burst of joy or a temporary mood, you use the Dative case.

Вчора ввечері ми дивилися дуже стару комедію. Спочатку я була втомлена, але потім мені стало дуже весело. Моєму братові теж було цікаво дивитися цей фільм. Коли ми закінчили, нам було так приємно згадувати смішні моменти.

> *Yesterday evening we watched a very old comedy. At first I was tired, but then it became very joyful to me. To my brother it was also interesting to watch this film. When we finished, to us it was so pleasant to remember the funny moments.*

To build these impersonal sentences, you simply combine a Dative pronoun with a state adverb. These adverbs almost always end in the letter «о», which gives them a neutral, descriptive feel. For example, the word for a cold environment is **холодно** (cold (impersonal state)). You can also express emotions like sadness, joy, pleasantness, and interest using similar words. Notice how the sentence structure never changes, no matter who is experiencing the feeling. You only change the pronoun at the beginning of the sentence to show who is currently feeling that way.

На вулиці йде сильний дощ, і мені холодно стояти тут. Я хочу запитати тебе: тобі цікаво читати цю нову книгу? Моїй сестрі зараз дуже сумно, тому що її найкраща подруга поїхала. Завтра ми йдемо в театр, і я знаю, що нам буде там дуже весело.

> *It is raining heavily outside, and to me it is cold standing here. I want to ask you: to you is it interesting reading this new book? To my sister right now it is very sad because her best friend left. Tomorrow we are going to the theater, and I know that to us it will be very joyful there.*

:::info
**Grammar box**
When using modal adverbs to express a need, permission, or advice, the person who experiences that need must always be in the Dative case. The action they need to perform is then expressed with an infinitive verb.
:::

This same impersonal structure is used to express necessity, permission, and advice. When you want to say that you need something, you do not use yourself as the subject. Instead, you say that it is necessary to you. The most common word for this is **потрібно** (necessary, needed). To say someone is allowed to do something, use a different adverb, and to give advice, use an adverb that means it is worth doing. These modal words are incredibly common in daily conversation, and mastering them will make your Ukrainian sound much more natural.

Мені треба йти додому, бо вже дуже пізно. Вибачте, вам можна тут сидіти, це місце вільне. Наш колега дуже багато працює, тому йому варто добре відпочити на вихідних. Їм потрібно купити нові квитки на ранковий потяг.

> *To me it is necessary to go home because it is already very late. Excuse me, to you it is allowed to sit here, this place is free. Our colleague works very much, therefore to him it is worth resting well on the weekend. To them it is necessary to buy new tickets for the morning train.*

Let us look at a conversation between two friends who have just met at a local cafe. They use these impersonal structures to check in on each other and discuss what they want to do next. Notice how seamlessly they flow between expressing physical comfort, emotional states, and basic needs.

<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Анна:</span> Тобі тут тепло? Я бачу, що на вулиці сильний вітер. *(Are you warm here? I see that there is a strong wind outside.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Олег:</span> Так, дякую, мені приємно тут сидіти. А тобі як? *(Yes, thank you, it is pleasant for me to sit here. And how about you?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Анна:</span> Мені цікаво, що ти замовиш на обід. *(It is interesting to me what you will order for lunch.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Олег:</span> Мені потрібно випити гарячої кави. *(It is necessary for me to drink hot coffee.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Анна:</span> Я теж хочу кави, бо мені сумно без неї вранці. *(I also want coffee because it is sad for me without it in the morning.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Олег:</span> Тоді нам варто покликати офіціанта прямо зараз. *(Then it is worth it for us to call the waiter right now.)*</div>

## Давальний чи знахідний? (Dative or Accusative?) (~350 words total)

One of the most common challenges for learners is choosing between the Accusative case and the **давальний відмінок** (dative case) for personal pronouns. In English, we use the same object pronoun whether we see someone or speak to someone. In Ukrainian, the choice depends entirely on the verb's underlying logic. Let us look at some minimal pairs where the only difference is the pronoun case.

Я бачу тебе кожного ранку на зупинці. Я кажу тобі дуже важливу інформацію. Він добре знає її вже багато років. Він часто дзвонить їй після роботи.

> *I see you every morning at the bus stop. I tell you very important information. He has known her well for many years already. He often calls her after work.*

Notice how the verbs for seeing and knowing take the Accusative forms "тебе" and "її". These verbs interact directly with the person as a target. Conversely, the verbs for telling and calling take the Dative forms **тобі** (to you) and **їй** (to her) because the person is the recipient of the message. 

When speaking, you can rely on a simple strategy to choose the correct case. If the action is directed *at* the person, or if they receive something like words or gifts, use the Dative forms like **нам** (to us) or **вам** (to you). If the action directly uses, sees, or affects the person as a direct object, use the Accusative case.

Ми запрошуємо вас на святкову вечерю сьогодні. Ми даємо вам наші нові робочі адреси. Вони розуміють нас дуже добре без слів. Вони купують нам великий і смачний торт.

> *We invite you to a festive dinner today. We give you our new work addresses. They understand us very well without words. They buy us a large and delicious cake.*

You must watch out for grammatical "false friends". For English speakers, the verb "to help" feels like it should take a direct object. However, the Ukrainian verb "допомагати" means "to give help to someone" and strictly requires the Dative case. You cannot help "його" (him, Accusative); you must help **йому** (to him). 

Let us look at how friends mix these cases in a natural conversation when organizing an event.

<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марко:</span> **Тобі** потрібна моя допомога з цим текстом? *(Do you need my help with this text?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Олена:</span> Так, допоможи **мені**, будь ласка. *(Yes, help me, please.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марко:</span> Я бачу тебе, але екран дуже темний. *(I see you, but the screen is very dark.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Олена:</span> Тоді я просто подзвоню **їм** і спитаю. *(Then I will simply call them and ask.)*</div>

</div>

:::info
**Grammar box** — Always memorize the case government when you learn a new verb. While "розуміти" (to understand) takes the Accusative case, "вірити" (to believe) and "допомагати" (to help) always require the Dative case.
:::

## Підсумок (~150 words)

Let us review the core functions of the **давальний відмінок** (dative case). When you talk about yourself as a recipient, you use **мені** (to me). For a direct friend, you say **тобі** (to you (informal)). 

For third-person subjects, the forms change completely. You give a gift **йому** (to him, to it) or you tell a secret **їй** (to her). 

Plural groups have distinct, memorable forms. A teacher gives homework **нам** (to us). We give instructions **вам** (to you (formal/plural)), and we give items **їм** (to them).

These pronouns act as logical subjects to express feelings or needs. This structure is used when a person is **холодно** (cold (impersonal state)). It is also essential when an action is **потрібно** (necessary, needed).

Перевірте себе перед новим уроком. Як сказати про інтерес жінки? Їй цікаво. Яке правильне слово для нашої групи? Це потрібно нам.

> *Check yourself before the new lesson. How do you talk about a woman's interest? To her it is interesting. What is the correct word for our group? It is necessary to us.*

:::note
**Quick tip** — Always ask «Кому?» (To whom?) to check if you need the dative case. If the action is directed at a person, the dative is the correct choice!
:::
