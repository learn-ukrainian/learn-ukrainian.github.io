## Частина 1: Вид дієслова — минулий і майбутній час (Part 1: Aspect in Past and Future)

This **контрольна точка** (checkpoint) for module A2.6 provides a comprehensive **перевірка** (review, check) of the "Verb Trinity": **вид дієслова** (verb aspect), tense, and motion. Understanding the **прагматика** (pragmatics) — the practical use — of these three elements is the final hurdle to intermediate fluency. Ukrainian grammar often relies on tiny details. A single prefix can change the entire meaning of a sentence from a long, ongoing process ("I was doing it") to a swift, guaranteed result ("I got it done"). By mastering this, you learn to think natively rather than translating word for word.

Look closely at the past tense. The fundamental choice here is between a process (imperfective) and a result (perfective). The imperfective aspect describes a process, a habit, or an action without a clear end. You use it when the duration of the action is important or when you are describing repeated actions in the background. The perfective aspect describes a completed action, a one-time event, or a clear result. You use it when the action is finished and the outcome is visible in the present. Most verbs exist as a **видова пара** (aspectual pair), where one verb is imperfective and the other is perfective. Sometimes there are rare exceptions, like a **двовидовий** (biaspectual) verb that acts as both depending on the context, or an **одновидовий** (monoaspectual) verb that only has one form, but the vast majority of Ukrainian verbs function in pairs.

> **Він довго писав довгий лист.** (He was writing a long letter for a long time.) — *Process, imperfective aspect.*
> **Він нарешті написав цей лист.** (He finally wrote this letter.) — *Result, perfective aspect.*
> **Оленка щовечора читала нову книгу.** (Olenka read a new book every evening.) — *Habit, imperfective aspect.*
> **Оленка прочитала всю книгу за ніч.** (Olenka read the whole book in one night.) — *Single event, perfective aspect.*

The future tense requires the exact same logic. When planning your future, you must decide: are you describing an intent or a process, or are you promising a specific result? The imperfective aspect uses a compound form: the auxiliary verb **буду** (I will) plus the infinitive. The perfective aspect uses a simple, single-word form, which is just the perfective verb conjugated with present tense endings. Consider how the choice of aspect completely alters the expectation in a professional setting:

> **Я буду робити домашнє завдання завтра о п'ятій.** (I will be doing homework tomorrow at five.) — *Process at a specific time.*
> **Я зроблю домашнє завдання до вечора.** (I will get the homework done by evening.) — *Guaranteed result by a deadline.*
> **Я буду писати звіт у понеділок.** (I will be writing the report on Monday.) — *The boss knows you are working, but does not expect the final document.*
> **Я напишу звіт у понеділок.** (I will write the report on Monday.) — *The boss expects the finished document on Monday.*

Ukrainian also features a unique, highly authentic future tense form: the synthetic future. Instead of using the separate word **буду**, we add specific endings directly to the infinitive: **-му** (I will), **-меш** (you will), **-ме** (he/she/it will), **-мемо** (we will), **-мете** (you will / plural), **-муть** (they will). Historically, this formed from the infinitive plus the verb "to have" (мати). It is a rhythmic, poetic alternative to the analytical "буду" construction and functions strictly as an imperfective process. To use it correctly, you must know your **дієвідміна** (conjugation group), the overall **дієвідмінювання** (conjugation process), and every **особове закінчення** (personal ending).

:::tip
The synthetic future tense is extremely common in spoken Ukrainian and literature. While you can always use **буду** + infinitive, mastering the **-му** endings will instantly make your Ukrainian sound more natural and authentic.
:::

> **Я працюватиму весь день без перерви.** (I will be working all day without a break.) — *Instead of "буду працювати".*
> **Ви знатимете усю правду про цю історію.** (You will know the whole truth about this story.) — *Instead of "будете знати".*
> **Вони читатимуть цей текст на уроці.** (They will be reading this text in class.) — *Instead of "будуть читати".*

## Частина 2: Дієслова руху та наказовий спосіб (Part 2: Motion Verbs and Imperatives)

Ukrainian **дієслова руху** (motion verbs) operate on a logic of direction and transport. The core concept is the "Two Pairs" distinction: unidirectional versus multidirectional motion. Are you moving in one specific direction right now, or are you making a round trip, a habit, or wandering without a specific goal? Furthermore, you must specify if you are moving on foot (**пішки** / on foot) or by transport (**транспортом** / by transport). Explore the forms in context:

> **Я зараз іду додому після роботи.** (I am walking home right now after work.) — *Unidirectional, on foot.*
> **Я щодня ходжу до парку з собакою.** (I walk to the park every day with the dog.) — *Multidirectional or habitual, on foot.*
> **Ми сьогодні їдемо в Карпати на машині.** (We are riding to the Carpathians today by car.) — *Unidirectional, by transport.*
> **Ми часто їздимо в Карпати відпочивати.** (We often ride to the Carpathians to rest.) — *Multidirectional or habitual, by transport.*

Motion verbs require precision with prepositions and cases to indicate your exact trajectory. The direction is crucial, and Ukrainian uses specific prepositions to map out the journey. When you are going toward a destination, use **до** (to) with the Genitive case. When leaving an origin, use **з/зі** (from) with the Genitive case. When moving onto an event, a conceptual space, or an open surface, use **на** (onto/to) with the Accusative case. When moving directly into a physical, enclosed space, use **в/у** (into/in) with the Accusative case. This logic helps quantify your trip, acting almost like a **неозначено-кількісний** (indefinite-quantitative) marker of your entire path, defining the exact boundaries of your movement.

> **Вона впевнено іде до Львова пішки.** (She is confidently walking to Lviv on foot.) — *Destination, Genitive case.*
> **Він швидко іде зі школи додому.** (He is walking quickly from school to home.) — *Origin, Genitive case.*
> **Ми щоранку їдемо на роботу автобусом.** (We ride to work every morning by bus.) — *Event or Surface, Accusative case.*
> **Вони разом йдуть у магазин за хлібом.** (They are walking together into the store for bread.) — *Enclosed space, Accusative case.*

When you need to give a command, use the **наказовий спосіб** (imperative mood). The imperative mood is incredibly common in daily life, from recipes to street signs to friendly advice. The forms depend entirely on who you are addressing and the level of formality. For the 2nd person singular (informal "you"), use the base imperative form: **читай** (read!) or **пиши** (write!). For the 2nd person plural or formal "you", add the plural suffix: **читайте** (read! / plural) or **пишіть** (write! / plural). For a collaborative "let's" action involving yourself and others in the 1st person plural, add the corresponding suffix: **читаймо** (let's read!) or **пишімо** (let's write!). If the verb is **зворотний** (reflexive), like **митися** (to wash oneself), add the reflexive suffix directly to the imperative: **мийся** (wash yourself!).

:::caution
Pay strict attention to stress in the imperative mood. Moving the stress to the wrong syllable often accidentally creates a present tense verb instead of a command.
:::

> **Ти завжди рОбиш домашнє завдання ввечері.** (You always do homework in the evening.) — *Present tense, stress on the first syllable.*
> **ЗробИ домашнє завдання негайно!** (Do the homework immediately!) — *Imperative, stress on the last syllable.*
> **Діти, швидко читайте цей текст!** (Children, quickly read this text!)
> **Олено, пиши обережно.** (Olena, write carefully.)

We also have indirect imperatives and wishes. For a 3rd person command (let him, let her, let them do it), use the particles **хай** (let) or **нехай** (let) followed by the present or future tense verb. When making a personal wish, use the Vocative case for the person's name, followed by **будь** (be) and the Instrumental case for the adjective.

> **Хай щастить вам у новій роботі!** (Good luck to you in the new job! / Let there be luck!)
> **Нехай він уважно читає цей важливий документ.** (Let him read this important document carefully.)
> **Маріє, будь завжди щасливою!** (Maria, always be happy!)
> **Друже, будь дуже обережним на дорозі!** (Friend, be very careful on the road!)

## Частина 3: Комплексні завдання (Part 3: Integrated Tasks)

Observe a typical oral review in class. The teacher is doing a quick **діагностика** (diagnostics) of the student's grammar. Notice how Marco naturally mixes his tenses and aspects to describe a weekend.

<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Пані Олена:</span> Марку, розкажи, що ти робив на вихідних? Ти відпочивав чи працював? *(Marko, tell us, what did you do on the weekend? Did you rest or work?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марко:</span> У суботу я **ходив** у кіно з друзями. Ми дивилися новий фільм. *(On Saturday I went to the cinema with friends. We watched a new movie.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Пані Олена:</span> Дуже добре. А в неділю ти теж відпочивав? *(Very good. And on Sunday did you also rest?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марко:</span> Ні, у неділю я **поїхав** до бабусі в село. Там я **написав** велике есе для університету. *(No, on Sunday I rode to my grandmother's in the village. There I wrote a large essay for the university.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Марко:</span> Пані Олено, **давай підемо** на екскурсію завтра? Це буде цікаво. *(Ms. Olena, let's go on an excursion tomorrow? It will be interesting.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Пані Олена:</span> Марку, це типова **помилка** (error, mistake). Нам треба **виправити** (to correct) це слово зараз. Ми кажемо правильно: "**ходімо** на екскурсію". *(Marko, this is a typical error. We need to correct this word now. We correctly say: "let's go on an excursion".)*</div>

</div>

English speakers often stumble on logic errors with Ukrainian verbs. The English concept "to go" is far too broad for Ukrainian, which demands knowing whether you are on foot or in a vehicle. Furthermore, forming the future tense with the imperfective auxiliary verb and a perfective infinitive — like "буду прочитати" — is grammatically impossible. You must choose either "буду читати" (the process) or "прочитаю" (the guaranteed result).

:::note
Decolonization note: Strictly avoid using "давай" (let's) or "давайте" (let's / plural) with an infinitive to make a suggestion. This is a common Russian calque. Instead, always use the authentic 1st person plural imperative ending **-мо** (e.g., **ходімо** (let's go), **зробімо** (let's do), **співаймо** (let's sing)).
:::

Observe a practical **завдання** (task, exercise) where two friends are planning a trip to the mountains. They use motion verbs to give precise directions, imperatives to suggest packing, and a wish construction to express excitement.

<div class="dialogue">

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Андрій:</span> Ми дуже скоро їдемо в гори! Що нам обов'язково треба взяти з собою? *(We are riding to the mountains very soon! What do we absolutely need to take with us?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Ірина:</span> Обов'язково **візьми** (take!) туристичну карту! Там дуже легко випадково загубитися в лісі. *(Definitely take a tourist map! It is very easy to accidentally get lost in the forest there.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Андрій:</span> Добре, я візьму її. А як ми підемо від залізничної станції до нашого табору? *(Good, I will take it. And how will we walk from the railway station to our camp?)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Ірина:</span> Від станції **ідіть** (walk! / plural) прямо до великого лісу. Потім одразу поверніть направо біля річки. *(From the station walk straight to the large forest. Then immediately turn right near the river.)*</div>

<div class="dialogue-line"><span class="speaker">Андрій:</span> Я все зрозумів. Наш маршрут готовий. Друзі, **будьмо готові** (let us be ready) до нових пригод! *(I understood everything. Our route is ready. Friends, let us be ready for new adventures!)*</div>

</div>

In an integrated narrative, you often have to use all these elements together to tell a cohesive story. The choice of verb changes how the listener visualizes the scene. Imagine a traveler visiting Kyiv for the first time. The narrator sets the scene by establishing the arrival, the ongoing exploration, and the specific plans for the next day:

> **Минулого тижня наш турист приїхав до Києва потягом.** (Last week our tourist arrived in Kyiv by train.)
> **Він дуже довго ішов через історичний центр міста.** (He walked for a very long time through the historical city center.)
> **Він часто заходив у старовинні київські церкви.** (He frequently went into ancient Kyiv churches.)
> **Він сказав: "Завтра я піду на Поділ і уважно оглядатиму архітектуру".** (He said: "Tomorrow I will walk to Podil and will be carefully inspecting the architecture.")

Notice the beautiful synthesis of aspect and motion prefixes. Prefixes like **по-** (start/perfective prefix) and **при-** (arrival prefix) not only change the specific direction of the verb but often force it into the perfective aspect. Compare the ongoing background process: **він ішов** (he was walking) with the sudden departure: **він пішов** (he set off / he left), or the final, completed arrival: **він прийшов** (he arrived). Integrating these subtly shifts the entire meaning of your story from background descriptions to foreground, completed actions.

## Підсумок

This checkpoint is your ultimate **самоперевірка** (self-check). Take a quiet moment to review everything you have learned and answer the following questions. If you can answer "yes", you can **впевнено** (confidently) move forward. You must be able to **обрати** (to choose) the right grammatical forms without hesitation.

*   Чи можу я обрати правильний вид у минулому часі? (Process vs. Result)
*   Чи розрізняю я "буду читати" та "прочитаю"?
*   Чи знаю я, коли використовувати "іти", а коли "ходити"?
*   Чи вмію я утворювати наказовий спосіб для всіх осіб?
*   Чи можу я побажати комусь здоров'я (Vocative + будь + Instrumental)?

If you answered "так" to all these questions, you are fully prepared and ready for module A2.7! Keep practicing these essential verbs in your daily conversations.
