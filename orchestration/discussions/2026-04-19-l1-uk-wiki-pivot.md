# Pre-commit review — L1-Ukrainian-first wiki architecture

## Context you need

- Curriculum spine is **already designed and stable**. A1 has 55 modules, A2 has 69 modules, B1–C2 + seminars all planned. Grounded in the 2024 Ukrainian State Standard as MINIMUM, seminar tracks go beyond.
- A2 already includes a deliberate **A2.9 Metalanguage Bridge** (6 modules, sequence 61–66): metalanguage-words-and-cases, metalanguage-verbs-and-time, metalanguage-sentences-and-classroom, metalanguage-phonetics, metalanguage-morphology, metalanguage-syntax-cases. These teach learners Ukrainian linguistic terminology (наголос, відмінок, голосний/приголосний, etc.) so B1+ immersion is viable.
- A2.10 (last 3 modules) is Refinement and Graduation.
- **From B1+ onward, the curriculum is already Ukrainian-only (immersion).**
- The wiki pipeline (COMPILE layer) has just been rebuilt in EPIC #1335: parent-section schema (#1337), section-level FTS5 + BGE-M3 dense re-ranker for modern tracks (#1338 in progress), metadata-first for archaic (#1341 parked).

## Proposal

Build the canonical wiki **in Ukrainian** for all levels, not English. Source: Ukrainian textbooks for teaching Ukrainian to foreigners (Заболотний, Литвінова, Вашуленко, Авраменко-recommended authors) + 2024 State Standard as the scope floor. No Russian-centric framing anywhere.

Then two published learner-facing tracks:

- **`l2-uk-en/`** (English-scaffolded) — A1 and A2 only, generated by LLM paraphrasing the Ukrainian wiki into English-scaffolded MDX modules
- **`l1-uk/`** (Ukrainian-only immersion) — A1 through C2 + all seminars

B1+ has ONE track (Ukrainian-only). A1/A2 have BOTH. Learners graduate from `l2-uk-en/a2-finale` to `l1-uk/b1-...`.

Two plans per A1/A2 module, same curriculum spine, different output-language targets.

## Why

Claim under test: **LLMs produce higher-quality English-scaffolded Ukrainian learning content when paraphrasing authoritative Ukrainian sources than when generating from English-described objectives.** Rationale: Gemini/Claude training data is English-heavy; when asked to "explain Ukrainian grammar in English" they hallucinate forms, miss native nuances, and pick Russian-leaning examples. When paraphrasing concrete Ukrainian text, they operate with a semantic anchor. Same logic that makes RAG better than generation.

Secondary claim: decolonization is more robust when baked into the source chain (the Ukrainian wiki, grounded in post-2022 State Standard) than when relied on at the presentation layer.

## Proposed A/B test (before committing)

Compare current pipeline (English wiki → English-scaffolded MDX) vs new pipeline (Ukrainian wiki → English-scaffolded MDX paraphrase) on:
- A1/M01, M02, M03 (early literacy, concrete)
- A1 mid (case introduction — accusative or nominative)
- A1 late (verb aspect — abstract)
- A2 grammar-heavy (conditional mood or instrumental)

Six articles total. ~3–4 days of work. Compare 4-dim review scores (source_grounding, factual_accuracy, ukrainian_perspective, register). Pivot if B wins by ≥1 point on any dim averaged across the six.

## gemini — round 1 — 2026-04-19T13:49:44.694928+00:00

The weakest assumption in the claim is that an LLM can effectively translate and adapt Ukrainian pedagogical metalanguage into accessible, scaffolded English for A1/A2 learners without losing pedagogical clarity. Ukrainian textbooks (even those for foreigners) rely on instructional traditions and baseline intuitions that do not map cleanly to an English speaker's mental model. We are assuming the LLM will act as an expert *pedagogical localized adapter*, rather than just a literal translator of grammatical rules.

**Concrete failure modes to watch for in the A/B test:**
1. **Pedagogical mismatch and Density:** The English scaffolding might become overly academic, dry, or dense, reading like a direct translation of a textbook rather than an accessible A1 guide. This would manifest as a drop in the `register` score.
2. **Loss of L2 comparative scaffolding:** Current English-driven prompts often explicitly guide the LLM to contrast Ukrainian concepts with English equivalents. A strict paraphrase of a Ukrainian-only source might drop these vital comparative lifelines.
3. **Translation artifacts in explanations:** The LLM might literally translate Ukrainian grammatical idioms or concepts, leading to confusing English explanations (e.g., failing the Semantic False Friends linter or creating awkward calques in the instruction text).

**What would make this hypothesis wrong:**
I would declare the hypothesis wrong if the A/B test reveals that while `factual_accuracy` and `ukrainian_perspective` improve, the overall accessibility and `register` collapse. If the resulting modules cannot explain *why* a grammatical rule exists in simple, intuitive English, the paraphrase approach fails the A1/A2 mandate, regardless of its source purity.

**Is the 6-article A/B sufficient?**
It is a good smoke test, but it is too narrow and risks the #1345 collapse. Your selected 6 modules focus heavily on early literacy, morphology (cases), and abstract concepts (aspect). It misses two critical content types where translation paraphrase often fails: 
1. **Lexical/Vocabulary:** Add one pure vocabulary module (e.g., A1 daily routines or shopping) to ensure the LLM doesn't generate overly formal dictionary definitions instead of natural usage notes.
2. **Syntax:** Add one A2 syntax module (e.g., word order or complex sentences) to ensure structural rules translate clearly.

Expanding to an 8-module test covering early literacy, cases, aspect, vocabulary, and syntax will provide the necessary coverage without over-testing.
