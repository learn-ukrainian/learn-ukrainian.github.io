# Exemplar: Foundation Band (A1 M01-10)

> Immersion 5-25% (M01-02: 5-15%, M03-05: 10-25%, M06-15: 15-35%). English carries ALL explanation. Ukrainian appears as individual letters and words bolded inline, in simple vocabulary tables, and in pattern boxes. No Ukrainian sentences in prose paragraphs. Ukrainian sentences max 10 words.

> PEDAGOGY: Discovery before rules. Visual mnemonics. Kinesthetic techniques (say it aloud, feel the vibration). Group letters by visual similarity to English, not alphabetical order.

## Example Section: Introducing New Letters (discovery approach)

Look at these three Ukrainian words. Can you read them using the letters you already know?

| Word | Try to read it |
|------|---------------|
| **мама** | ? |
| **нам** | ? |
| **он** | ? |

You could read **мама** because М and А are "true friends" — they look the same and sound the same in both alphabets. But **нам** might have tripped you up.

The letter **Н** is one of the Cyrillic alphabet's biggest visual traps. It looks exactly like the English H, but it makes the /n/ sound, like the N in "no."

| Letter | Looks like | Actually sounds like | Memory trick |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Н** | English H | N as in "no" | The two vertical lines are like two legs — you're saying "**n**o, I won't sit down" |
| **А** | English A | A as in "father" | True friend — same look, same sound |
| **М** | English M | M as in "mom" | True friend |
| **О** | English O | O as in "more" | True friend |

Now try again: **нам** = "nam" (to us). **Он** = "on" (he/that one, archaic).

> [!warning]
> Your brain will keep wanting to read **Н** as "h" for weeks. This is normal — you're fighting years of English reading habits. Every time you catch yourself, you're rewiring your brain. It gets easier.

Let's sort the letters you've learned so far into two groups:

**True friends** (safe — same look, same sound):
- **А** = A, **М** = M, **О** = O, **Т** = T, **К** = K

**False friends** (dangerous — familiar look, different sound):
- **Н** looks like H but = N
- **В** looks like B but = V
- **Р** looks like P but = R

> [!tip]
> Try covering the "Meaning" column in any vocabulary table and reading just the Ukrainian words aloud. If you can sound them out correctly, you've cracked the code for these letters.

## Example Section: Teaching Sounds (kinesthetic approach)

Put your hand on your throat and say the English sound "z." Feel the vibration? Now say "s." No vibration.

This difference — **voiced** (vibrating) vs. **voiceless** (silent) — works identically in Ukrainian:

| Voiced | Voiceless | Throat test |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Б** /b/ | **П** /p/ | buzz vs. pop |
| **Д** /d/ | **Т** /t/ | hum vs. tap |
| **Г** /h/ | **Х** /kh/ | open throat vs. friction |
| **З** /z/ | **С** /s/ | buzz vs. hiss |
| **Ж** /zh/ | **Ш** /sh/ | buzz vs. hush |

Try these minimal pairs — words that differ by only one sound:

| Voiced | Meaning | Voiceless | Meaning |
|--------|---------|-----------|---------|
| **зуб** | tooth | **суп** | soup |
| **жар** | heat | **шар** | ball/layer |
| **дім** | house | **тім** | (not a word) |

> [!did-you-know]
> The letter **Ж** looks like a beetle with spread wings. In Ukrainian, the word for beetle is **жук** — and it starts with **Ж**! The shape matches the meaning.

> [!culture]
> The word **хліб** (bread) starts with the voiceless **Х**. Bread is sacred in Ukrainian culture — guests are traditionally greeted with bread and salt (**хліб і сіль**). You'll hear this phrase at weddings, homecomings, and official welcomes.
