## –ѓ–Ї —Ж–µ –њ–Њ—П—Б–љ—О—О—В—М —Г —И–Ї–Њ–ї—Ц (How Schools Teach This)

The concept of using motion verbs figuratively is built upon the broader linguistic topic of **–±–∞–≥–∞—В–Њ–Ј–љ–∞—З–љ—Ц—Б—В—М (polysemy)** and the distinction between **–њ—А—П–Љ–µ (direct) and –њ–µ—А–µ–љ–Њ—Б–љ–µ (figurative) meaning**. This foundation is laid as early as the 2nd and 3rd grades in the Ukrainian school system.

The core pedagogical method is comparison and contrast. Textbooks present pairs of phrases where a word is used first in its literal sense and then in a metaphorical one, encouraging students to deduce the meaning from context.

1.  **Foundation in Primary School (Grades 2-4):**
    *   Students learn that a single word can have multiple meanings. The initial examples are often simple nouns (`–≥–Њ–ї–Њ–≤–∞ –ї—О–і–Є–љ–Є` vs. `–≥–Њ–ї–Њ–≤–∞ –њ–Њ—Ч–Ј–і–∞`) or adjectives (`–Ј–Њ–ї–Њ—В–∞ –Ї–∞–±–ї—Г—З–Ї–∞` vs. `–Ј–Њ–ї–Њ—В–∞ –Њ—Б—Ц–љ—М`) (Sources 9, 21, 32).
    *   Verbs of motion are introduced early as clear, intuitive examples of this phenomenon. A 3rd-grade textbook explicitly contrasts physical and abstract movement: "–Ф—Ц—Ф—Б–ї–Њ–≤–∞ –≤–ґ–Є–≤–∞—О—В—М—Б—П —Г –њ—А—П–Љ–Њ–Љ—Г —Ц –њ–µ—А–µ–љ–Њ—Б–љ–Њ–Љ—Г –Ј–љ–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П—Е: —Г—З–µ–љ—М —Ц–і–µ, —Г—З–µ–љ–Є—Ж—П –є–і–µ (–њ—А—П–Љ–µ –Ј–љ–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П) вАФ —З–∞—Б —Ц–і–µ, –≤–µ—Б–љ–∞ –є–і–µ (–њ–µ—А–µ–љ–Њ—Б–љ–µ –Ј–љ–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П)" [S45].
    *   Exercises involve identifying the "extra" phrase in a set, forcing students to distinguish between literal and figurative usage, for example: `–Ж–і–µ –і–Њ—Й, —Ц–і–µ –°–Њ—Д—Ц–є–Ї–∞, —Ц–і–µ –Ъ–Є—А–Є–ї–Ї–Њ`. Here, `—Ц–і–µ –і–Њ—Й` is the figurative one [S46]. Another example is `–Ы–µ—В–Є—В—М —З–∞—Б, –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М –ї–µ–ї–µ–Ї–∞, –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М –Љ–µ—В–µ–ї–Є–Ї` [S46].

2.  **Reinforcement in Middle School (Grades 5-7):**
    *   The terminology becomes more formal. Textbooks define *–њ—А—П–Љ–µ –Ј–љ–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П* as the "primary, usual meaning" and *–њ–µ—А–µ–љ–Њ—Б–љ–µ –Ј–љ–∞—З–µ–љ–љ—П* as an "imaginative, figurative name transferred from one object or phenomenon to another" (Sources 7, 14).
    *   The mechanism of transfer is explained as being based on similarity in form, color, function, etc. [S47].
    *   Verbs of motion continue to be a key example set. Zabolotnyi's 5th-grade textbook contrasts `—Б—Е–Њ–і–Є—В–Є –љ–∞ –Ј—Г–њ–Є–љ—Ж—Ц` (to get off at a stop) with `—Б—Е–Њ–і–Є—В—М —А–∞–љ–Њ–Ї` (the morning comes/rises) [S47].
    *   A 5th-grade textbook explicitly addresses the learner's potential confusion: "–Я—Ц–і–µ?! –Р —Е—Ц–±–∞ –і–Њ—Й –Љ–Њ–ґ–µ —Е–Њ–і–Є—В–Є?" This highlights that the learner's literal interpretation is a natural step in understanding the figurative extension [S48].

3.  **Advanced Analysis (Grades 10-11):**
    *   In higher grades, the focus shifts to the stylistic function of figurative language. Students analyze how polysemy and figurative meanings create imagery and expressiveness in literary texts [S49].
    *   The concept of **–ї–µ–Ї—Б–Є—З–љ–∞ –њ–Њ–Љ–Є–ї–Ї–∞ (lexical error)** is introduced, which includes using a word in an inappropriate context, often by misinterpreting its figurative vs. literal sense (Sources 10, 27). This solidifies the importance of mastering idiomatic usage.

In summary, the Ukrainian curriculum teaches the figurative use of motion verbs not as a special "grammar rule" but as a prime example of a fundamental lexical principle: words have core meanings that can be creatively extended to describe other phenomena based on perceived similarities.

## –Я–Њ–≤–љ–∞ –њ–∞—А–∞–і–Є–≥–Љ–∞ (Full Paradigm)

The verb **—Ц—В–Є (to go, unidirectional, by foot)** is the most productive verb for figurative motion. Its aspectual pair is **—Е–Њ–і–Є—В–Є (to go, multidirectional/habitual, by foot)**, which is rarely used in these figurative senses.

**Core Verb: —Ц—В–Є (Imperfective)**
*Present Tense Conjugation*
| –Ю—Б–Њ–±–∞ (Person) | –Ю–і–љ–Є–љ–∞ (Singular) | –Ь–љ–Њ–ґ–Є–љ–∞ (Plural) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1-—И–∞ (—П, –Љ–Є) | —П —Ц–і—ГћБ / –є–і—Г | –Љ–Є —Ц–і–µ–Љ–ЊћБ / –є–і–µ–Љ–ЊћБ |
| 2-–≥–∞ (—В–Є, –≤–Є) | —В–Є —Ц–і–µћБ—И / –є–і–µ—И | –≤–Є —Ц–і–µ—В–µћБ / –є–і–µ—В–µћБ |
| 3-—В—П (–≤—Ц–љ, –≤–Њ–љ–∞, –≤–Њ–љ–Њ, –≤–Њ–љ–Є) | –≤—Ц–љ / –≤–Њ–љ–∞ћБ / –≤–Њ–љ–ЊћБ —Ц–і–µћБ / –є–і–µ | –≤–Њ–љ–ЄћБ —Ц–і—ГћБ—В—М / –є–і—Г—В—М |

The perfective counterpart, **–њ—Ц—В–Є**, typically signifies the beginning of the action. Compare:
*   `–Ф–Њ—Й **—Ц—И–Њ–≤** —Ж—Ц–ї–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М.` (The rain **was falling** all day - *process, imperfective*)
*   `–†–∞–њ—В–Њ–Љ **–њ—Ц—И–Њ–≤** –і–Њ—Й.` (Suddenly, it **started to rain** - *inception, perfective*)

## –І–∞—Б—В–Њ—В–љ—Ц—Б—В—М —Ц –њ—А—Ц–Њ—А–Є—В–µ—В–Є (Frequency & Priorities)

For a B1-B2 learner, mastering the figurative uses of `—Ц—В–Є` is the highest priority. These expressions are ubiquitous in everyday speech.

1.  **Essential (Must-Know for B1):**
    *   **Precipitation:** `–і–Њ—Й —Ц–і–µ`, `—Б–љ—Ц–≥ —Ц–і–µ`. This is non-negotiable.
    *   **Time:** `—З–∞—Б —Ц–і–µ`, `–ґ–Є—В—В—П —Ц–і–µ`. Fundamental for expressing the passage of time.
    *   **Ongoing Events:** `—Д—Ц–ї—М–Љ —Ц–і–µ`, `–≤–Є—Б—В–∞–≤–∞ –є–і–µ`, `—Г—А–Њ–Ї —Ц–і–µ`, `–Ї–Њ–љ—Ж–µ—А—В —Ц–і–µ`. Used constantly to ask what's on or to describe an ongoing event.
    *   **Aboutness:** `–Љ–Њ–≤–∞ –є–і–µ –њ—А–Њ...` / `–є–і–µ—В—М—Б—П –њ—А–Њ...` (it is about...). A crucial structure for summarizing and discussing topics.

2.  **High-Frequency (Master for a strong B1/early B2):**
    *   **Time (fast):** `—З–∞—Б –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М`. Extremely common idiom. "–І–∞—Б —П–Ї–Њ—Б—М –і—Г–ґ–µ —И–≤–Є–і–Ї–Њ –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М –Њ—Б—В–∞–љ–љ—Ц–Љ —З–∞—Б–Њ–Љ" [S54].
    *   **Suitability:** `—В–Њ–±—Ц –њ–∞—Б—Г—Ф / –ї–Є—З–Є—В—М` (it suits you). While not a motion verb, it fills the semantic slot where learners might mistakenly use `—Ц—В–Є`. See Decolonization section.
    *   **Beginning of Action (Perfective):** `–њ—Ц—И–Њ–≤ –і–Њ—Й`, `–њ—Ц—И–Њ–≤ —Б–љ—Ц–≥`.

3.  **Lower Priority (Recognition at B1, Production at B2):**
    *   `–±—Ц–≥—В–Є` for time (`—А–Њ–Ї–Є –±—Ц–ґ–∞—В—М`). More poetic/expressive than `—Ц—В–Є`.
    *   `–њ–ї–Є–≤—В–Є` for time (`—З–∞—Б –њ–ї–Є–≤–µ`). More literary, implies slow, steady movement.
    *   Figurative uses of `–≤–µ—Б—В–Є` and `–љ–µ—Б—В–Є`. These are often taught as part of collocations (`–≤–µ—Б—В–Є –њ–µ—А–µ–≥–Њ–≤–Њ—А–Є`, `–љ–µ—Б—В–Є –≤—Ц–і–њ–Њ–≤—Ц–і–∞–ї—М–љ—Ц—Б—В—М`) rather than as extensions of motion verbs.

## –Ґ–Є–њ–Њ–≤—Ц –њ–Њ–Љ–Є–ї–Ї–Є L2 (Common L2 Errors)

Learners often struggle by either over-applying literal logic or by calquing (loan-translating) from their native language.

| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `–Ф–Њ—Й —Е–Њ–і–Є—В—М —Й–Њ–і–љ—П.` | `–Ф–Њ—Й —Ц–і–µ —Й–Њ–і–љ—П.` | The verb `—Е–Њ–і–Є—В–Є` (multidirectional/habitual motion) is not used for precipitation, even if it happens regularly. The process is always described with the unidirectional `—Ц—В–Є`. |
| `–¶–µ–є —Д—Ц–ї—М–Љ –±—Ц–ґ–Є—В—М –і–≤—Ц –≥–Њ–і–Є–љ–Є.` | `–¶–µ–є —Д—Ц–ї—М–Љ —Ц–і–µ –і–≤—Ц –≥–Њ–і–Є–љ–Є.` | This is a calque from English "the film is running". In Ukrainian, scheduled events like films, plays, and lessons always `—Ц–і—Г—В—М`, never `–±—Ц–ґ–∞—В—М`. |

## –Ф–µ–Ї–Њ–ї–Њ–љ—Ц–Ј–∞—Ж—Ц–є–љ—Ц –Ј–∞—Б—В–µ—А–µ–ґ–µ–љ–љ—П (Decolonization Notes)

While both Ukrainian and Russian use motion verbs figuratively, a learner relying on Russian knowledge will make predictable and noticeable errors. Ukrainian is not a dialect of Russian; it is a distinct language with its own idiomatic system.

1.  **The `–њ–∞—Б—Г–≤–∞—В–Є/–ї–Є—З–Є—В–Є` vs. `—Ц—В–Є` Trap:** This is the most significant point of divergence. In Russian, `—В–µ–±–µ –Є–і—С—В` is the standard way to say "it suits you." In Ukrainian, using `—В–Њ–±—Ц —Ц–і–µ` is a classic Russianism (—А—Г—Б–Є–Ј–Љ). The correct and natural Ukrainian verbs are `–њ–∞—Б—Г–≤–∞—В–Є` and `–ї–Є—З–Є—В–Є`. Insisting on this distinction is crucial for developing authentic-sounding Ukrainian.

2.  **Shared vs. Divergent Idioms:** Many basic idioms are shared across Slavic languages (e.g., `–і–Њ—Й —Ц–і–µ`). However, assuming all idioms are shared is a mistake. The writer should present each Ukrainian idiom as a feature of the Ukrainian language in its own right, not as something "similar to Russian."

3.  **Historical Perspective:** Some words may sound like Russianisms to the untrained ear but are in fact archaic Ukrainian words. For example, the old Ukrainian verb for "to search" was `—Ц—Б–Ї–∞—В–Є`, which was later replaced by the Polish borrowing `—И—Г–Ї–∞—В–Є`. A speaker from Lviv Oblast might say `–Ј–∞—З–∞–ї–Є —Ц—Б–Ї–∞—В–Є` (they started to search), which is not a Russianism (`–Є—Б–Ї–∞—В—М`) but a preserved dialectal form [S56]. While not a motion verb, this principle applies: similarity does not automatically equal a borrowing from modern Russian.

4.  **Avoiding Russian as a Reference:** The instructional materials must NEVER use Russian as the default or point of comparison. Explanations should be based on Ukrainian's internal logic. Instead of "unlike in Russian...", the brief should state "In Ukrainian, the verb for suitability is `–њ–∞—Б—Г–≤–∞—В–Є`."

## –Я—А–Є—А–Њ–і–љ—Ц –њ—А–Є–Ї–ї–∞–і–Є (Natural Examples)

These examples are drawn from authentic sources to illustrate natural usage.

**1. –Ю–њ–∞–і–Є (Precipitation)**
*   `–Ф—А—Г–≥–Є–є –і–µ–љ—М —Ц–і–µ —Б–љ—Ц–≥.` [S48]
*   `–Э–∞ –≤—Г–ї–Є—Ж—Ц –њ–Њ—Б—Ц—А—Ц–ї–Њ, –Ј—Ц—А–≤–∞–≤—Б—П –≤—Ц—В–µ—А, –Љ–∞–±—Г—В—М, –Ј–∞—А–∞–Ј –њ—Ц–і–µ –і–Њ—Й!` [S48]
*   `–У–Њ–≤–Њ—А–Є–ї–∞ вАФ –Љ–Њ–≤—З–∞–ї–∞, —А–Њ–Ј–Ї–∞–Ј—Г–≤–∞–≤ вАФ —А–Њ–Ј–њ–Њ–≤—Ц–і–∞–≤, –≤—Ц–і—З–Є–љ—П—В–Є вАФ –Ј–∞—З–Є–љ—П—В–Є, –њ—Ц–і–љ—П–≤ вАФ –Њ–њ—Г—Б—В–Є–≤, –њ–Њ—З–∞–ї–Є вАФ –Ј–∞–Ї—Ц–љ—З–Є–ї–Є, —Б–Љ—Ц—П–ї–Њ—Б—П вАФ —А–∞–і—Ц–ї–Њ.` [S45] This example from a textbook shows `—Ц–і–µ –і–Њ—Й`.

**2. –Я–ї–Є–љ —З–∞—Б—Г (Passage of Time)**
*   `–І–∞—Б —П–Ї–Њ—Б—М –і—Г–ґ–µ —И–≤–Є–і–Ї–Њ –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М –Њ—Б—В–∞–љ–љ—Ц–Љ —З–∞—Б–Њ–Љ.` [S54]
*   `–Ж –≤вАЩ—П–љ–µ—И —В–Є, –∞ –і–љ—Ц –ї–µ—В—П—В—М, –љ–µ—Б—Г—В—М –≤—Б–µ –і–Њ–±—А–µ –Ј–∞ —Б–Њ–±–Њ—О.` ([S57], quoted in [S58])
*   `–Т—Ц–Ї –њ—А–Њ–є—В–Є вАФ —П–Ї –±–∞—В–Њ–≥–Њ–Љ –ї—П—Б–љ—Г–≤.` [S59][S60]
*   `–Ы–µ—В–Є—В—М —З–∞—Б, –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М –ї–µ–ї–µ–Ї–∞, –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М –Љ–µ—В–µ–ї–Є–Ї.` [S46][S61]

**3. –Я—А–Њ—Ж–µ—Б–Є —В–∞ –њ–Њ–і—Ц—Ч (Processes and Events)**
*   `–£—А–Њ–Ї —Ц–і–µ (—В—А–Є–≤–∞—Ф).` [S62]
*   `–Ъ–Њ–ї–Є —Ц—Б—В–Њ—А–Є—З–љ—Ц —Г–Љ–Њ–≤–Є —Б–Ї–ї–∞–ї–Є—Б—П —В–∞–Ї, —Й–Њ –Љ–Є –љ–µ –Љ–∞–ї–Є –њ—А–Њ—Д–µ—Б—Ц–є–љ–Є—Е –њ–Њ–µ—В—Ц–≤, –љ–∞—А–Њ–і —Б—В–≤–Њ—А–Є–≤ —Б–Њ—В–љ—Ц —В–Є—Б—П—З –њ—Ц—Б–µ–љ—М...` [S63]. The phrase `—Г–Љ–Њ–≤–Є —Б–Ї–ї–∞–ї–Є—Б—П` is similar to things "going" a certain way.
*   `–ѓ–Ї —Б–њ—А–∞–≤–Є? –£ –Љ–µ–љ–µ –≤—Б–µ –≥–∞—А–∞–Ј–і.` [S54]. `–°–њ—А–∞–≤–Є –є–і—Г—В—М` is a common related phrase.

**4. –Р–±—Б—В—А–∞–Ї—В–љ–Є–є —А—Г—Е —В–∞ —Б—В–∞–љ (Abstract Movement and State)**
*   `–Ь–µ–љ–µ –≤–Њ–і–Є–ї–Њ –≤ –±–µ–Ј–≤—Ц—Б—В—М –ґ–Є—В—В—П, —В–∞ —П –≤–µ—А—В–∞–≤—Б—П –љ–∞ —Б–≤–Њ—Ч –њ–Њ—А–Њ–≥–Є.` [S64][S53]
*   `–Ь–Њ–≤–∞ –є–і–µ –њ—А–Њ —Г–Ї—А–∞—Ч–љ—Б—М–Ї—Г —В—А–∞–і–Є—Ж—Ц—О, –њ—А–Њ —А–Њ–і–Є–љ–љ—Ц —В—А–∞–і–Є—Ж—Ц—Ч.` (Paraphrased from [S53])
*   `–•–Љ–∞—А–Ї–Є –±—Ц–ґ–∞—В—М, –Љ–Є–ї—Г—О—Б—П.` [S65][S51]

## –†–µ–Ї–Њ–Љ–µ–љ–і–∞—Ж—Ц—Ч –і–ї—П –≤–њ—А–∞–≤ (Activity Concepts)

The learning progression should move from recognition to controlled production, and finally to free, idiomatic use.

*   **Phase 1: Recognition & Matching**
    *   **Multiple Choice:** `–°–љ—Ц–≥ ... (–∞) —Ц–і–µ, (–±) –±—Ц–ґ–Є—В—М, (–≤) —Ч–і–µ.`
    *   **Matching:** Match the noun to the most common figurative verb:
        *   `—З–∞—Б` вЖТ `—Ц–і–µ / –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М`
        *   `–і–Њ—Й` вЖТ `—Ц–і–µ`
        *   `—Д—Ц–ї—М–Љ` вЖТ `—Ц–і–µ`
        *   `–ґ–Є—В—В—П` вЖТ `–є–і–µ / –Љ–Є–љ–∞—Ф`
    *   **Odd One Out:** `–Ж–і–µ –ї—О–і–Є–љ–∞, —Ц–і–µ –њ–Њ—Ч–Ј–і, —Ц–і–µ —Б–љ—Ц–≥.` (Identify the figurative use).

*   **Phase 2: Controlled Production**
    *   **Fill-in-the-blanks:** Provide sentences with a blank where the learner must insert the correct form of `—Ц—В–Є`, `–ї–µ—В—Ц—В–Є`, etc. `–£ –Ї—Ц–љ–Њ—В–µ–∞—В—А—Ц –Ј–∞—А–∞–Ј ______ –љ–Њ–≤–Є–є —Д—Ц–ї—М–Љ –Ј –Љ–Њ—Ч–Љ —Г–ї—О–±–ї–µ–љ–Є–Љ –∞–Ї—В–Њ—А–Њ–Љ.`
    *   **Transformation:** Convert a statement from literal to figurative. `–Я–Њ—З–∞–≤—Б—П –і–Њ—Й.` вЖТ `–Я—Ц—И–Њ–≤ –і–Њ—Й.`
    *   **Correction:** Give sentences with common L2 errors (from the list above) and have the student correct them. E.g., `*–Ґ–Њ–±—Ц –і—Г–ґ–µ —Ц–і–µ —Ж—П —Б–Њ—А–Њ—З–Ї–∞.` вЖТ `–Ґ–Њ–±—Ц –і—Г–ґ–µ –њ–∞—Б—Г—Ф —Ж—П —Б–Њ—А–Њ—З–Ї–∞.`

*   **Phase 3: Free Production & Communication**
    *   **Sentence Building:** Ask students to create their own sentences describing the weather, the passage of time, or what's on TV, using the target structures.
    *   **Situational prompts:** "You are talking to a friend about how fast time flies during vacation. What would you say?" (Elicits `—З–∞—Б –ї–µ—В–Є—В—М`). "A friend tries on a new hat and asks for your opinion. It looks great. What do you say?" (Elicits `—В–Њ–±—Ц –њ–∞—Б—Г—Ф/–ї–Є—З–Є—В—М`).
