## –ú–Ķ—ā–ĺ–ī–ł—á–Ĺ–ł–Ļ –Ņ—Ė–ī—Ö—Ė–ī (Methodological Approach)

The core pedagogical goal at A1 is to empower learners to express personal preferences simply and confidently. Ukrainian textbooks for native children achieve this through a communicative and iterative approach, which should be adapted for L2 learners.

The primary structure is introducing a verb of preference followed by either a noun or another verb in the infinitive. The most fundamental verb, **`–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł`** (to love/like), is introduced very early. First-grade materials [S52] present it in simple, contrasting pairs: "**–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é** –ľ–į–Ľ—é–≤–į—ā–ł. **–Į –Ĺ–Ķ –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é** –≥—Ä–į—ā–ł –≤ —Ö–ĺ–ļ–Ķ–Ļ." This immediately establishes the verb and its negation as tools for expressing personal choice.

The second key structure, **`–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź`** (I like, lit. "To me it is pleasing"), is introduced as a parallel concept. While grammatically more complex (requiring the Dative case), it is frequently used for general likes and is presented as a chunk [S53]. Textbooks for slightly older children (2nd-3rd grade) begin to use it in questions like "–©–ĺ —ā–ĺ–Ī—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź –Ĺ–į–Ļ–Ī—Ė–Ľ—Ć—ą–Ķ —É —ą–ļ–ĺ–Ľ—Ė?" (What do you like most at school?) [S54].

The learning process is built around thematic vocabulary clusters related to personal life:
1.  **Food and Drink:** "–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é –Ņ–ł—Ä–ĺ–≥–ł –∑ –≤–ł—ą–Ĺ—Ź–ľ–ł" (I love pies with cherries) [S52].
2.  **Activities and Hobbies:** "–õ—é–Ī–Ľ—é —á–ł—ā–į—ā–ł –ļ–Ĺ–ł–∂–ļ—É —Ź" (I love to read a book) [S55], "–ö–į—ā–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź –Ĺ–į —Ä–ĺ–Ľ–ł–ļ–į—Ö, ... —á–ł—ā–į—ā–ł –ļ–Ĺ–ł–∂–ļ–ł, —Ā–Ľ—É—Ö–į—ā–ł –ľ—É–∑–ł–ļ—É" (Rollerblading, reading books, listening to music) [S56].
3.  **School and Learning:** "–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź –ī—Ė–∑–Ĺ–į–≤–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź –Ņ—Ä–ĺ –≤—É–Ľ–ļ–į–Ĺ–ł" (I like to learn about volcanoes) [S54].

Exercises are practical and action-oriented, moving from simple sentence construction to dialogues and short narratives about oneself [S57][S56]. The focus is always on what the learner can *do* with the language.

## –ü–ĺ—Ā–Ľ—Ė–ī–ĺ–≤–Ĺ—Ė—Ā—ā—Ć –≤–≤–Ķ–ī–Ķ–Ĺ–Ĺ—Ź (Introduction Sequence)

The introduction must be carefully staged to avoid cognitive overload, moving from grammatically simple structures to more complex ones.

-   **Step 1: The Verb `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` + Noun (Accusative Case)**
    Introduce `–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é` (I love/like) with simple, concrete nouns. This is the most direct parallel to the English "I like X." At this stage, use only feminine nouns that have a clear Accusative `-—É` ending or masculine inanimate nouns that don't change.
    -   `–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é **–ľ—É–∑–ł–ļ—É**.` (I like music.) [S58]
    -   `–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é **—Ā–≤–ĺ—é —Ä—Ė–ī–Ĺ—É –∑–Ķ–ľ–Ľ—é**.` (I love my native land.) [S59]
    -   Explain this as a strong, general liking, akin to "love" or a deeply held preference.

-   **Step 2: `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` + Verb (Infinitive)**
    Immediately expand on Step 1 by showing that `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` can be followed by an action. This is the gateway to talking about hobbies. The infinitive form, always ending in `-—ā–ł`, is presented as a fixed unit.
    -   `–Į –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é **–ľ–į–Ľ—é–≤–į—ā–ł**.` (I love to draw.) [S52]
    -   `–ö–į—ā–Ķ—Ä–ł–Ĺ–į –Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā—Ć **—á–ł—ā–į—ā–ł**.` (Kateryna loves to read.) [S60]
    -   Reinforce with the negative: `–Į –Ĺ–Ķ –Ľ—é–Ī–Ľ—é **–≥—Ä–į—ā–ł** –≤ —Ö–ĺ–ļ–Ķ–Ļ.` (I don't like to play hockey.) [S52].

-   **Step 3: The `–Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź` Construction (Dative + Nominative)**
    Introduce `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź` as a common alternative for "I like." It's crucial to present `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź` as a memorized chunk [S53]. Explain its logic: "To me, X is pleasing."
    -   Start with a noun: `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź **–ļ—Ė–ľ–Ĺ–į—ā–į** –ľ–ĺ—Ē—ó —Ā—ā–į—Ä—ą–ĺ—ó —Ā–Ķ—Ā—ā—Ä–ł.` (I like my older sister's room.) [S61]. The liked object (`–ļ—Ė–ľ–Ĺ–į—ā–į`) is in the Nominative case (the subject of the sentence).
    -   Then add an infinitive: `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź **–ī—Ė–∑–Ĺ–į–≤–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź** –Ņ—Ä–ĺ –≤—É–Ľ–ļ–į–Ĺ–ł...` (I like to learn about volcanoes...) [S54].

-   **Step 4: Distinguishing `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` and `–Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź`**
    Once both forms are familiar, clarify the nuance. `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` is for passions, deep affection, and established favorites. `–Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź` is for general liking, first impressions, and objective appeal. A podcast for learners explicitly makes this distinction, noting `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` is a "very strong phrase" whereas `–Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź` is the more general "like" [S53]. For A1, the rule of thumb is: use `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` for hobbies you are passionate about, and `–Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź` for things you generally enjoy.

-   **Step 5: Expanding Hobby Vocabulary with Prepositions**
    Introduce specific constructions for activities that require prepositions. These must be taught as fixed phrases.
    -   `**–≥—Ä–į—ā–ł —É** + [sport]`: `–≥—Ä–į—ā–ł —É —Ą—É—ā–Ī–ĺ–Ľ` (to play football) [S56].
    -   `**–≥—Ä–į—ā–ł –Ĺ–į** + [instrument]`: `–≥—Ä–į—ā–ł –Ĺ–į –≥—Ė—ā–į—Ä—Ė` (to play the guitar) [S56].
    -   `**–ļ–į—ā–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź –Ĺ–į** + [vehicle/equipment]`: `–ļ–į—ā–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź –Ĺ–į –≤–Ķ–Ľ–ĺ—Ā–ł–Ņ–Ķ–ī—Ė` (to ride a bicycle) [S62], `–ļ–į—ā–į—ā–ł—Ā—Ź –Ĺ–į —Ä–ĺ–Ľ–ł–ļ–į—Ö` (to rollerblade) [S56].

English speakers often map their native grammar onto Ukrainian, leading to predictable errors.

| `–Į –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—é—Ā—Ź —Ą—É—ā–Ī–ĺ–Ľ.` | `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź —Ą—É—ā–Ī–ĺ–Ľ.` | This is a direct translation of "I like football." | `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—é—Ā—Ź —Ą—Ė–Ľ—Ć–ľ–ł.` | `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—é—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź —Ą—Ė–Ľ—Ć–ľ–ł.` | The learner correctly uses the Dative `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė` but fails to make the verb agree with the plural subject `—Ą—Ė–Ľ—Ć–ľ–ł`. The verb must be in the 3rd person plural (`–Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—é—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź`), not singular. English "I like" doesn't change based on the object, causing this interference. |

## –Ē–Ķ–ļ–ĺ–Ľ–ĺ–Ĺ—Ė–∑–į—Ü—Ė–Ļ–Ĺ—Ė –∑–į—Ā—ā–Ķ—Ä–Ķ–∂–Ķ–Ĺ–Ĺ—Ź (Decolonization Notes)

**This is a mandatory section for all pedagogical briefs.** The goal is to build a learner's understanding of Ukrainian *from a Ukrainian foundation*, not as a derivative of another language, particularly Russian.

1.  **Teach Ukrainian on its Own Terms:** Never introduce Ukrainian grammar or vocabulary by comparing it to Russian. Avoid phrases like, "Ukrainian `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā–ł` is like Russian `–Ľ—é–Ī–ł—ā—Ć`," or "Ukrainian `–ł` is like Russian `—č`." This frames Ukrainian as a variant rather than a distinct language and builds an incorrect mental model.

2.  **Use Authentic Ukrainian Sources:** All examples, vocabulary, and pedagogical models should come from modern Ukrainian textbooks (e.g., –Ď–ĺ–Ľ—Ć—ą–į–ļ–ĺ–≤–į, –í–į—ą—É–Ľ–Ķ–Ĺ–ļ–ĺ, –£–≥–ĺ—Ä) and media created for Ukrainians or Ukrainian learners (e.g., Ukrainian Lessons Podcast). This ensures cultural and linguistic authenticity. The source material for this brief is exclusively Ukrainian [S67].

3.  **Correctly Frame Shared Vocabulary:** When encountering cognates (words that are similar in Ukrainian and Russian, like `—á–ł—ā–į—ā–ł`/`—á–ł—ā–į—ā—Ć`), present them as part of a shared Common Slavic heritage. Do not describe them as "Russian words used in Ukrainian" or "borrowings from Russian." A podcast source notes that about a third of Slavic vocabulary is shared, but this does not imply a parent-child language relationship [S68].

4.  **Emphasize Different Usage Patterns:** Even when words are cognates, their usage frequency and grammatical behavior can differ significantly. For example, the `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź` construction is central to expressing "like" in Ukrainian. While a similar structure exists in Russian (`–ú–Ĺ–Ķ –Ĺ—Ä–į–≤–ł—ā—Ā—Ź`), its idiomatic use, frequency, and social context are not identical. Teach the Ukrainian pattern `–ú–Ķ–Ĺ—Ė –Ņ–ĺ–ī–ĺ–Ī–į—Ē—ā—Ć—Ā—Ź` based on its own merits and prevalence in Ukrainian speech [S53].
