## Звуки і літери

A Ukrainian fifth-grade textbook (Заболотний, page 83) puts it in one line: «Звуки ми чуємо й вимовляємо, а букви бачимо й пишемо» — "We hear and pronounce sounds; we see and write letters." A **звук** (sound) is what your mouth produces — air, voice, lips, tongue. You hear it, you say it. A **літера** (letter) is the symbol on the page. You see it, you write it. When you press your lips together to make [m], that is a звук. When you draw the shape **М** with your hand, that is a літера. Ukrainian schoolchildren drill this distinction from Grade 1.

:::tip
When in doubt, ask yourself: do I HEAR it, or do I SEE it on the page? Heard and pronounced is a звук. Seen and written is a літера.
:::

Here is the surprise: Ukrainian has 33 літери but 38 звуки. More sounds than letters — why? Three reasons. First, four letters — **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, **Ї** — can each spell two sounds in certain positions (full rules come in Module 2). Second, one letter, **Ь** (the soft sign), spells no sound at all; it only softens the consonant before it. Third, the soft/hard distinction adds extra phoneme partners. A 5th-grade workbook (Літвінова, page 130) asks: «Чи можна говорити "голосна літера"?» — "Can we say 'a vowel letter'?" The answer is no. **Голосний** (vowel) and **приголосний** (consonant) describe sounds, not letters.

The Ukrainian alphabet — the **абетка** — is a sequenced inventory of 33 letters, each with its own name (Module 2 walks through them). Compare with English: *knife*, *knee*, *write* hide silent letters; *though*, *through*, *tough* share four letters and three different vowel sounds. Ukrainian is overwhelmingly phonetic. What you see is, with very few exceptions, what you hear. Once you master the sound-letter mapping, you can read aloud any Ukrainian word — even one you have never met before.
