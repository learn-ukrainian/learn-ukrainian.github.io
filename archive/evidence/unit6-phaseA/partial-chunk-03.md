## Приголосні звуки

A 1st-grade reader (Большакова, page 24) puts it this way: «Приголосні деренчать і тихенько шелестять, голосно свистять і шиплять» — "Consonants rattle and quietly rustle, loudly whistle and hiss." A **приголосний** звук (consonant sound) is made with voice and noise, or noise alone — lips, teeth, or tongue create an obstacle the air must push through. Try to sing [к] or [п] on a held breath — you cannot. Where a голосний flows freely, a приголосний blocks the air.

Ukrainian has **32 consonant sounds** spelled by **22 consonant letters**. The asymmetry comes from a feature English does not have — hard and soft pairs. Nine consonants pair up ([д]/[д′], [т]/[т′], [з]/[з′], and six others), and [й] is always soft. Захарійчук, page 15, marks them: hard = `[–]`, soft = `[=]`. Module 2 drills the contrast through pairs like **«стан»** (a state) versus **«стань»** (stand up!) — same consonants, different softness, different word.

The 22 consonant **літери** (letters) you will study: М, Н, С, К, Л, Р, Б, В, Д, П, Т, Г, Ґ, З, Ж, Ш, Х, Й, Ч, Щ, Ц, Ф. Three special facts. First, **Ґ** spells the hard plosive [g] — **«ґудзик»** (button), **«ґава»** (rook) — while **Г** spells the breathy pharyngeal [ɦ] — **«гора»** (mountain). Two separate Ukrainian phonemes. Second, **Щ** always equals two sounds, [шч] — **«борщ»** (borshch) is heard as [боршч]. Third, **Ь** (the soft sign) has no sound; it only softens the consonant before it — **«день»** (day) is heard as [ден′].

:::tip
If lips, teeth, or tongue block the air, you have a **приголосний**. If you can sing it on one breath, you have a голосний.
:::
