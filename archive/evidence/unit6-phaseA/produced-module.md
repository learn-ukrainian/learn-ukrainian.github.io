## Звуки і літери

A Ukrainian fifth-grade textbook (Заболотний, page 83) puts it in one line:
«Звуки ми чуємо й вимовляємо, а букви бачимо й пишемо» — "We hear and
pronounce sounds; we see and write letters." A **звук** (sound) is what your
mouth produces — air, voice, lips, tongue. You hear it, you say it. A
**літера** (letter) is the symbol on the page. You see it, you write it. When
you press your lips together to make [m], that is a звук. When you draw the
shape **М** with your hand, that is a літера. Ukrainian schoolchildren drill
this distinction from Grade 1.

:::tip
When in doubt, ask yourself: do I HEAR it, or do I SEE it on the page? Heard
and pronounced is a звук. Seen and written is a літера.
:::

Here is the surprise: Ukrainian has 33 літери but 38 звуки. More sounds than
letters — why? Three reasons. First, four letters — **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**,
**Ї** — can each spell two sounds in certain positions (full rules come in
Module 2). Second, one letter, **Ь** (the soft sign), spells no sound at all;
it only softens the consonant before it. Third, the soft/hard distinction adds
extra phoneme partners. A 5th-grade workbook (Літвінова, page 130) asks: «Чи
можна говорити "голосна літера"?» — "Can we say 'a vowel letter'?" The answer
is no. **Голосний** (vowel) and **приголосний** (consonant) describe sounds,
not letters.

The Ukrainian alphabet — the **абетка** — is a sequenced inventory of 33
letters, each with its own name (Module 2 walks through them). Compare with
English: *knife*, *knee*, *write* hide silent letters; *though*, *through*,
*tough* share four letters and three different vowel sounds. Ukrainian is
overwhelmingly phonetic. What you see is, with very few exceptions, what you
hear. Once you master the sound-letter mapping, you can read aloud any Ukrainian
word — even one you have never met before.

## Голосні звуки

A 1st-grade textbook (Большакова, page 24) opens with a verse: «Голосні почуєш
в пісні, і у темному у лісі, і коли дивуєшся, і коли милуєшся. Легко
вимовляються, весело співаються!» — "You hear vowels in a song, in the dark
forest, when you marvel, when you admire. Easily pronounced, joyfully sung!" A
**голосний** звук (vowel sound) is made with voice alone — air flows freely
through the mouth, meeting no obstacles. You can sing it. Try humming [а] on
one breath — easy.

:::tip
The test: if you can sing the sound on one held breath, it is a голосний. If
lips, teeth, or tongue block the air, it is not.
:::

Now the numbers. Ukrainian has **6 vowel sounds** — [а], [о], [у], [е], [и],
[і] — but **10 vowel letters**: А, О, У, Е, И, І, Я, Ю, Є, Ї. The four extras
(Я, Ю, Є, Ї) are called **йотовані** (iotated) and can spell two sounds at
once. Module 2 unpacks that rule. One structural fact to lock in: every
Ukrainian word contains at least one vowel sound. Vowels are the heart of every
syllable — without one, no syllable exists.

A tiny notation from Захарійчук, page 13: each голосний звук is marked in
sound-models with one dot — `[•]`. Three model words. **«мама»** (mom) splits
as ма-ма — two clean [а] sounds. **«молоко»** (milk) splits as мо-ло-ко —
three [о] sounds. **«Уля»** (Ulia, a name) — one [у]. Crucially: in
**«молоко»** the unstressed «о» stays a clean [о] — it does NOT slide toward
[а] as Russian unstressed «о» does. Anna Ohoiko's short video lessons cover
each vowel letter — watch, listen, imitate.

## Приголосні звуки

A 1st-grade reader (Большакова, page 24) puts it this way: «Приголосні
деренчать і тихенько шелестять, голосно свистять і шиплять» — "Consonants
rattle and quietly rustle, loudly whistle and hiss." A **приголосний** звук
(consonant sound) is made with voice and noise, or noise alone — lips, teeth,
or tongue create an obstacle the air must push through. Try to sing [к] or [п]
on a held breath — you cannot. Where a голосний flows freely, a приголосний
blocks the air.

Ukrainian has **32 consonant sounds** spelled by **22 consonant letters**. The
asymmetry comes from a feature English does not have — hard and soft pairs.
Nine consonants pair up ([д]/[д′], [т]/[т′], [з]/[з′], and six others), and
[й] is always soft. Захарійчук, page 15, marks them: hard = `[–]`, soft =
`[=]`. Module 2 drills the contrast through pairs like **«стан»** (a state)
versus **«стань»** (stand up!) — same consonants, different softness, different
word.

The 22 consonant **літери** (letters) you will study: М, Н, С, К, Л, Р, Б, В,
Д, П, Т, Г, Ґ, З, Ж, Ш, Х, Й, Ч, Щ, Ц, Ф. Three special facts. First, **Ґ**
spells the hard plosive [g] — **«ґудзик»** (button), **«ґава»** (rook) —
while **Г** spells the breathy pharyngeal [ɦ] — **«гора»** (mountain). Two
separate Ukrainian phonemes. Second, **Щ** always equals two sounds, [шч] —
**«борщ»** (borshch) is heard as [боршч]. Third, **Ь** (the soft sign) has no
sound; it only softens the consonant before it — **«день»** (day) is heard as
[ден′].

:::tip
If lips, teeth, or tongue block the air, you have a **приголосний**. If you can
sing it on one breath, you have a голосний.
:::
