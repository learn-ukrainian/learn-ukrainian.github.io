## Зву́ки і лі́тери

Ukrainian primary-school teachers drill one foundational distinction from Grade 1, and Заболо́тний's textbook (5th grade, p. 83) states it as a single golden rule:

> «Звуки ми чу́ємо й вимовля́ємо, а бу́кви ба́чимо й пи́шемо.»
> *(Sounds we hear and pronounce; letters we see and write.)*

A **звук** (sound) is something the mouth produces — a sound event in the air. A **лі́тера** (its everyday synonym is **бу́ква**) is a written symbol on paper. They are not the same thing. This module teaches both layers and how the two systems map onto each other.

Here is the puzzle every learner meets in week one: Ukrainian has 33 letters but 38 sounds. <!-- VERIFY: precise claim (33 letters; 38 sounds) --> Why the mismatch? Three reasons. First, certain letters can spell two sounds in some positions — **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, and **Ї** are called **йото́вані** (iotated), and you will meet them properly in module №2. Second, one letter — **Ь**, called **м'яки́й знак** (the soft sign) — represents no sound at all; its only job is to soften the consonant before it. Third, many consonants have both hard and soft paired sounds, so the sound inventory is larger than the set of consonant letters. Separately, the letter combinations **дж** and **дз** can spell single sounds.

Літвінова's 5th-grade textbook (p. 130) asks a sharp question: «Чи мо́жна говори́ти "голосна́ літера"?» — *can we say "vowel letter"?* The **ві́дповідь** (answer) is no. A **голосни́й** (vowel) or **при́голосний** (consonant) is a category that belongs to sounds, not to letters. Letters merely represent sounds. **На відмі́ну від** English orthography — where the same letter can be silent, doubled, or pronounced three different ways — Ukrainian's **голосні́** (vowels) and consonants map onto sounds with rare deviation.

The Ukrainian **алфа́ві́т** (also called **абе́тка**) has 33 letters in a fixed order, and each letter has its own name. <!-- VERIFY: precise claim (33 letters) --> Useful for reading: Ukrainian spelling is overwhelmingly **фонети́чний** — phonetic. What you see is, with very few exceptions, what you hear.

:::tip Good news for English-speaking learners
Ukrainian has no silent letters like the *k* in English *knife*, and no surprise pronunciations like English *though*, *through*, *cough*. Once you master the 38-sound inventory, you will be able to read aloud almost any Ukrainian word — even one you have never seen before. The alphabet is a stable, reliable map.
:::

## Голосні звуки

A **голосний** (vowel) is the easiest kind of sound in any language — a sound made by voice alone. Большако́ва's first-grade textbook (page 24) teaches Ukrainian children with this short verse:

> «Голосні почу́єш в пісні́, і у те́мному у лі́сі, і коли́ диву́єшся, і коли милу́єшся. Ле́гко вимовля́ються, ве́село співа́ються!»
> *(You'll hear vowels in a song, in the dark forest, when you marvel, when you admire. They are easy to pronounce, joyfully sung!)*

Articulatorily, a **звук** (sound) counts as a vowel when air flows freely through the mouth — no obstruction from the lips, teeth, or tongue. The concrete test: you can SING a vowel and hold it for as long as your breath lasts. You can shout it across a field. That freedom of airflow is the definition.

Ukrainian has **six vowel sounds**: [а], [о], [у], [е], [и], [і]. But there are **ten vowel letters** on the page: А, О, У, Е, И, І, Я, Ю, Є, Ї. The four extras — Я, Ю, Є, Ї — are **йотовані** (iotated) and can spell two sounds at once. The full story belongs to module №2; for now, fix the six core sounds in your ear. Here is the foundational rule: every Ukrainian word contains at least one vowel sound. Vowels are the heart of every **склад** (syllable). **Без голосни́х** нема́є сло́ва — without vowels, no word.

:::tip Drill the six sounds aloud
Say each one in turn, holding it for three seconds: [а-а-а], [о-о-о], [у-у-у], [е-е-е], [и-и-и], [і-і-і]. If you can sustain the sound, it is a vowel.
:::

Захарійчук's first-grade textbook (page 13) marks every vowel with a small dot **[•]** inside its sound-model diagrams. Try clapping these short words aloud: ма-ма (mom — two [а] sounds, two syllables); **молоко́** (milk), broken as мо-ло-ко (three [о] sounds, three syllables); У-ля (one [у], one syllable). For a video walkthrough of each vowel letter, the YouTube series by Anna Ohoiko is excellent — credit to her by name. Watch her lips, listen, and repeat aloud. ## При́голосні звуки

A **приголосний** (consonant) is the other main category of Ukrainian sounds. Большакова's first-grade textbook (page 24) introduces them with a short rhyme:

> «Приголосні деренча́ть і тихе́нько шелестя́ть, го́лосно свистя́ть і шипля́ть.»
> *(Consonants rattle and quietly rustle, loudly whistle and hiss.)*

Articulatorily, a consonant is produced by voice combined with noise, or by noise alone. The **гу́би** (lips), teeth, or tongue create a **перешко́да** (obstacle) that blocks the airflow. The test mirrors the vowel test in reverse: try to SING a pure [к] or [п] and hold it for three seconds. Impossible — you can only release a consonant, never sustain it. That obstruction is the definition.

Ukrainian has **32 consonant sounds** spelled with only **22 consonant letters** — more sounds than letters on the page. <!-- VERIFY: precise claim (32 consonant; 22 consonant) --> Nine consonants come in pairs: a **тверди́й** (hard) version and a **м'який** (soft) version of the same letter. Захарійчук's first-grade textbook (page 15) marks hard sounds with [–] and soft sounds with [=] inside its sound diagrams. Compare стан with стань — same letter Н, but the final **звук** is hard in the first word and soft in the second. This hard/soft contrast is a Slavic feature absent from **англі́йській мо́ві** (English).

Anna Ohoiko's video series walks through every consonant **літера**: М, Н, С, К, Л, Р, Б, В, Д, П, Т, Г, Ґ, З, Ж, Ш, Х, Й, Ч, Щ, Ц, Ф. Three points matter most from day one:

:::tip Three consonant facts to lock in early
- **Ґ** spells the hard plosive [g] (as in English *go*); **Г** spells the breathy [ɦ] (as in English *behind*) — both are equal Ukrainian phonemes, never interchangeable.
- **Щ** is always two sounds together, [шч]: борщ → [боршч].
- **Ь** — м'який **знак** — has no sound of its own; **він за́вжди́** softens the consonant standing before it (*it always softens the consonant in front of it*).
:::

One last Ukrainian pronunciation rule: voiced consonants stay voiced at the end of a word. **гриб** (mushroom) is pronounced [гриб], never [грип].

## Приві́т!

Your first Ukrainian conversation — three turns adapted from Anna Ohoiko's ULP Season 1, Episode 1. **Марко́** and **Софія** are two classmates who run into each other on the street after the school day:

> **Марко:** «Привіт, Софіє! Як спра́ви?» — Hi, Sofiia! How are things?
> **Софія:** «До́бре. А у тебе́, Марку?» — Good. And you, Marko?
> **Марко:** «Чудо́во.» — Wonderful.

First, exchange names as fixed chunks:

> **Марко:** «Привіт! Як тебе зва́ти?» — Hi! What is your name?
> **Софія:** «Ме́не звати Софія. А тебе?» — My name is Sofiia. And you?
> **Марко:** «Мене звати Марко. Ду́же приє́мно.» — My name is Marko. Nice to meet you.

For **«Як справи?»**, use **«А у тебе?»** as the correct reciprocal: **«у мене все добре»** pivots back as **«а у тебе?»**.

<!-- INJECT_ACTIVITY: fill-in-greeting-dialogue --> Notice that **Софія** becomes **Софіє** when Марко addresses her, and **Марко** becomes **Марку** when Софія answers — Ukrainian has a special **кли́чний відмі́нок** (vocative case) for calling someone by name. Module №8 unfolds this fully; for now, just memorize the two forms as fixed chunks.

Register flag: **«привіт»** is informal — exactly right for two classmates like **Марко** and **Софія**, but never for strangers or elders. If the day is average, **Софія** could answer **«Норма́льно»** (okay) instead of **«добре»** or **«чудово»**.

The classroom flips the register. The next morning, **Марко** and **Софія** sit at their desks as the **вчи́тель** (teacher) walks in and greets the **у́чні** (students) with the formal time-of-day chunk; the class — including **Марко** and **Софія** — answers in chorus:

> **Вчитель:** «До́брий день, ді́ти! Як справи?» — Good day, children! How are things?
> **Учні:** «Добрий день! Добре, дякуємо.» — Good day! Fine, thank you.

This classroom exchange — **вчитель** asking, **учні** answering together — is the textbook scenario every Ukrainian first-grader meets on day one of school. Notice how the formal **«До́брого ра́нку!»** replaces the informal **«Привіт!»** that **Марко** and **Софія** used a moment earlier on the street. Same question (**«Як справи?»**), different register.

For everyone else — strangers, elders, shopkeepers — Ukrainian uses time-of-day greetings as fixed chunks. Memorize all five:

- **«Добрий день!»** — daytime all-purpose greeting
- **«Доброго ранку!»** — morning greeting
- **«Добрий ве́чір!»** — late afternoon onward
- **«До поба́чення!»** — the standard farewell, built on **«побачення»** (a meeting)
- **«На все добре!»** — all the best

:::tip Decolonization — keep your vowels honest
In **«Доброго»**, the unstressed first **о** stays a clean [о]. Do NOT reduce it to [а] — that flattening is a Russian-interference habit Ukrainian rejects. In **«день»**, the final **-нь** is the soft [н′] from Section 3.
:::

When **Марко** and **Софія** meet face to face, they hit grammatical gender. **Софія** says **«Ра́да тебе ба́чити!»** (glad to see you); **Марко** says **«Ра́дий тебе бачити!»**. Same meaning, different ending tied to gender — Module №8 unfolds this fully.

Spell **«Привіт»** out by sound, the way **Марко** would in his first Ukrainian lesson: **П** [п] приголосний + **р** [р] приголосний + **и** [и] голосний + **в** [в] приголосний + **і** [і] голосний + **т** [т] приголосний. Two **голосні**, four **приголосні** — every category from this module lives in one word.

Final principle. Ukrainian smooths harsh consonant clusters. Before a consonant after a vowel, prepositions shorten: **«була́ в шко́лі»** (she was at school), **«ма́ма й та́то»** (mom and dad). After a consonant, the full form returns: **«пішо́в у шко́лу»**, **«мама і тато»** — при́нцип милозву́чності.

## Підсумок

Self-check time. Cover the right side of each line with your hand and try to recall the answer out loud. Every question pulls one Ukrainian term you met in this module — **звук**, **літера**, **голосний**, **приголосний**, **привіт**, **справи**.

- **Скі́льки лі́тер в украї́нській абе́тці?** (How many letters?) — 33 **літери** (letters).
- **Скільки зву́ків в українській мові?** (How many sounds?) — 38 **звуків** (sounds).
- **Чо́му звуків бі́льше, ніж літер?** (Why more sounds than letters?) — Because **Я**, **Ю**, **Є**, **Ї** can each spell two sounds in some positions, many consonants have hard and soft paired sounds, and **Ь** spells no sound of its own.
- **Що таке́ голосний?** (What is a vowel sound?) — A sound made with pure voice, no obstacle. You can sing it.
- **Що таке приголосний?** (What is a consonant sound?) — A sound with an obstacle from lips, teeth, or tongue. You cannot sing it.
- **Чи можна сказа́ти «голосна літера»?** (Can you say *vowel letter*?) — No. **Голосний** names a *sound*, never a letter. Letters only spell sounds.
- **Що означа́є «привіт»?** (What does *привіт* mean?) — Hi — informal, for friends and family (like **Марко** and **Софія**).
- **Як відпові́сти́ на «Як справи?»** (How do you answer *How are things?*) — **«Добре. А у тебе?»** (Fine. And you?) Or **«Чудово»** (Wonderful).

:::tip One last reminder
**Звук** is not **літера**. **Голосний** and **приголосний** describe sounds — the things your mouth makes — not the shapes on the page.
:::

One final pronunciation idea for the quiz: **на́голос** (stress) can move and can change meaning. **замок** can mean a castle or a lock depending on stress, and **бра́ти** can mean brothers or to take. That is why new Ukrainian words should be learned with stress.
