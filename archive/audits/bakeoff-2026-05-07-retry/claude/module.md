The Ukrainian way to say "I get up, wash, and dress" hides one small ending — **-ся** — that turns an ordinary verb into a reflexive one. The action turns back on the speaker. **Я вмиваю** ("I wash someone") versus **я вмиваюся** ("I wash myself") — same root, two different worlds.

English does the same job with a separate word — *I wash myself, she dresses herself* — but Ukrainian saves a syllable by tacking the reflexive marker straight onto the verb. The ending **-ся** is doing a lot of work. It tells you that the subject and the object of the action are the same person. Once your ear gets used to it, you start hearing it everywhere in everyday speech. Almost every routine at home — getting up, washing, getting dressed, getting ready, learning, hurrying — uses a reflexive verb where English would use a plain one. So learning the **-ся** pattern is the spine of every morning conversation.

## Діалоги

Ліна and Настя share a flat in Lviv. Both work, but their alarms go off at very different hours. Eavesdrop on a Tuesday morning, then on a lazy Saturday — every reflexive verb in this lesson appears here naturally.

**Будний ранок** (a weekday morning)

> **Настя:** Ліно, коли ти прокидаєшся?
> **Ліна:** Я прокидаюся о сьомій.
> **Настя:** Що ти робиш потім?
> **Ліна:** Вмиваюся, одягаюся і снідаю.
> **Настя:** А коли ти йдеш на роботу?
> **Ліна:** О восьмій.

Three reflexive verbs in three short turns — **прокидаюся**, **вмиваюся**, **одягаюся** — each one a regular verb plus the postfix **-ся** glued to the very end of every personal form. The verb **йдеш** (from **йти**) is plain. It has no reflexive partner because going somewhere is not an action you do to yourself.

**Вихідний ранок** (the weekend version)

> **Ліна:** У суботу я не поспішаю.
> **Ліна:** Прокидаюся пізно, лежу, дивлюся телефон.
> **Настя:** А я навчаюся вранці. Потім гуляю.

Same flat, two rhythms. **Навчаюся** (reflexive — "I study, I make myself learn") sits next to **гуляю** (non-reflexive — "I take a walk"). A real Ukrainian Saturday is told by mixing both kinds of verbs in a single short answer, and at A1 you can already do exactly that.

Notice the sequence trick that ties the whole weekday exchange together: question, answer, question, answer. Ліна never volunteers her routine in one long sentence — she replies one verb at a time, and Настя's questions pull the next reflexive form out of her. That is the natural shape of an A1 conversation. You do not need a paragraph to describe a morning; you need three or four short turns, each anchored on one reflexive verb. The weekend exchange drops the question pattern and leans on **не поспішаю** ("I'm not rushing") to set the lazier tone, then strings together two reflexive verbs and one regular one in a single quick line.

## Дієслова на -ся

The fundamental rule shows up the same way in every Ukrainian schoolbook. Захарійчук's Grade 4 textbook (page 119) puts it like this:

> Дієслова на -ся виражають дію, спрямовану на самого виконавця (на самого себе). Наприклад: миюся (мию себе); розчісуюся (розчісую себе).

That translates as: "Verbs in -ся express an action directed at the doer themselves. For example: миюся (I wash myself); розчісуюся (I comb myself)." Караман's Grade 10 textbook (page 187) frames the same idea for older students under the term **дієслова зворотного стану** ("reflexive-voice verbs"), and adds the historical note that the postfix **-ся** comes from the old short form of the reflexive pronoun **себе** (yourself) — the same logic as English *myself / yourself*, just glued to the verb.

The trick at A1 is mechanical: the regular verb keeps its normal personal endings, and **-ся** is glued onto the end of every form.

| Особа | Звичайне (regular) | Зворотне (reflexive) |
|---|---|---|
| я | вмиваю | вмиваюся |
| ти | вмиваєш | вмиваєшся |
| він/вона | вмиває | вмивається |

**Вимова** (pronunciation). Spelling and speech part ways here. Кравцова Grade 4 (page 113) gives the rule in one line:

> У дієсловах 2-ї особи однини -шся вимовляють як [с':а]. У дієсловах 3-ї особи однини та множини -ться вимовляють як [ц':а].

So **вмиваєшся** is written with -шся but said as [вмиваєс':а]. **Вмивається** is written with -ться but said as [вмиваєц':а]. Авраменко's Grade 7 textbook (page 67) confirms the orthography from the other direction: forms in -ться **always** carry the м'який знак, and forms in -шся **never** do, regardless of how the form sounds out loud.

Common A1 pairs always travel together. **Вмивати ↔ вмиватися** (to wash someone ↔ to wash up). **Одягати ↔ одягатися** (to dress someone ↔ to get dressed). **Будити ↔ будитися** (to wake someone ↔ to wake up — though **прокидатися** is the everyday choice for waking up on your own). **Збирати ↔ збиратися** (to gather things ↔ to get oneself ready). The pattern is dependable: take the regular verb you already know, glue **-ся** to every personal form, and you have a new reflexive verb without learning a new root.

## Мій ранок

Five reflexive verbs, two regular verbs, and four sequence words are enough to narrate any morning from start to finish. The vocabulary fits in one table:

| Дія (action) | Зворотне (reflexive) | Стандартне (non-reflexive) |
|---|---|---|
| wake up | прокидатися | — |
| wash up | вмиватися | — |
| get dressed | одягатися | — |
| get ready | збиратися | — |
| have breakfast | — | снідати |
| go (to work / school) | — | йти |

The verb **йти** is irregular. It does not follow either standard conjugation, so its present-tense forms have to be learned as a unit:

- я **йду**
- ти **йдеш**
- він/вона **йде**

Sequence words turn isolated verbs into a story:

- **спочатку** — first, at the start
- **потім** — then, next
- **після цього** — after that
- **нарешті** — finally

The Захарійчук Grade 4 textbook (page 120) packs the rhythm into a verse by Дмитро Білоус — a moral about getting to school on time, packed with reflexive forms:

> Раненько прокидаєшся, зарядкою займаєшся, водою обливаєшся, швиденько одягаєшся, в дорозі не спиняєшся.

A blank-template story uses the same chain. Try filling it for yourself:

- **Спочатку** я прокидаюся.
- **Потім** вмиваюся і одягаюся.
- **Після цього** снідаю.
- **Нарешті** йду на роботу.

You can extend the same pattern into the evening. **Лягати спати** ("to go to bed") is a closing verb — and notice that it is **not** reflexive, even though English uses *get into bed*. A short evening chain might run: **спочатку** вмиваюся, **потім** збираюся до сну, **нарешті** лягаю спати. The reflexive verbs do the same work at night that they do in the morning, because the action still turns back on the speaker. Each language draws the reflexive line in a slightly different place. At A1 you do not need a long list of routines — five reflexive verbs and four sequence words are enough to tell almost any ordinary morning, your own or someone else's, in three or four short Ukrainian sentences.

## Підсумок

A reflexive verb is the same regular verb with **-ся** glued to every personal form. The -ся never goes inside the verb — it always sits at the very end. Endings stay normal:

- я вмиваю**ся** (від вмивати)
- ти вмиває**шся**
- він/вона вмиває**ться**

Three takeaways for any morning narration:

1. The chain **прокидаюся → вмиваюся → одягаюся → снідаю → йду** covers a standard weekday morning. Mix in **спочатку**, **потім**, **після цього**, **нарешті** to glue the steps together. The same chain becomes a Saturday answer if you swap the order: **прокидаюся пізно → лежу → дивлюся телефон → нарешті снідаю**.
2. Speech is not spelling. Always say **-шся** as [с':а] and **-ться** as [ц':а]. Writing keeps the soft sign on -ться and drops it on -шся, regardless of how the form sounds. The asymmetry is fixed in the orthography, not in your accent.
3. Not every verb has a reflexive twin. **Снідати**, **пити**, **гуляти**, and **йти** never take **-ся** in normal usage; they describe actions that do not turn back on the speaker. Some verbs flip both ways: **вмивати** ("wash someone else") versus **вмиватися** ("wash up"); **одягати** ("dress someone") versus **одягатися** ("get dressed yourself"). The contrast is exactly the **миюся / мию себе** pair that Захарійчук anchors in Grade 4.

One more practical note. Some Ukrainian verbs exist only in the reflexive form — there is no non-reflexive partner. **Сміятися** (to laugh), **усміхатися** (to smile), and **подобатися** (the verb behind "I like" in Ukrainian) all carry **-ся** as a permanent part of their identity. It is worth knowing that **-ся** is sometimes a removable suffix and sometimes a fixed part of the verb. The morning verbs in this lesson are all of the removable kind.

**Самоперевірка** (self-check). Without scrolling up, write out your own morning routine in four to five sentences. Use at least three reflexive verbs and at least two sequence words. Read your answer aloud — and check that every -шся sounds like [с':а] and every -ться sounds like [ц':а].
