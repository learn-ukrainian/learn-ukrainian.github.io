# Imperative and Requests

> **Чому це важливо?**
>
> The imperative mood allows you to ask for help, give instructions, or make requests. It is the language of action, heavily used in classrooms, streets, and daily conversations. Adding simple polite words transforms direct commands into respectful requests.

## Introduction: The Power of Commands and Requests

We use the imperative mood when we want someone to do something. This includes giving direct instructions, asking for a favor, or seeking help. It is highly practical for everyday life and represents the core language of action. In Ukrainian, the imperative mood is dynamic and frequently appears in classroom instructions, street conversations, and casual gatherings.

> — **Слуха́й!** — Listen!
> — **Слуха́ю.** — I am listening.
> — **Диви́сь ту́т.** — Look here.
> — **Я дивлю́сь.** — I am looking.

The examples above show direct instructions. You do not just describe the action; you actively prompt the other person to act. The imperative sets an expectation that the listener will respond.

- **Да́й це́!** — Give this!
- **Допоможи́!** — Help!
- **Покажи́!** — Show!
- **Чека́й!** — Wait!
- **Сті́й!** — Stop!

Sometimes, you need to use the imperative mood for classroom instructions or when reacting to immediate, fast-paced events. The shortest commands are usually informal and direct.

To soften a command and make it polite, we frequently include the phrase **бу́дь ла́ска** (please). This single phrase instantly changes a strong order into a culturally appropriate request. You can place it at the beginning, middle, or end of a sentence.

- **Скажи́, бу́дь ла́ска, де́ ві́н?** — Please tell, where is he?
- **Допоможи́, бу́дь ла́ска.** — Help, please.
- **Да́й, бу́дь ла́ска, ча́с.** — Please give time.

The mood relies heavily on the specific situation and social context. When you say **чита́й** (read), you give an instruction to one person you know well. When you address a group or an unfamiliar adult, the form changes significantly to reflect social distance.

> [!culture] Politeness Matters
> Using **бу́дь ла́ска** is essential in Ukrainian society. Even if the grammatical form remains direct, adding this simple phrase makes your speech polite and respectful in a café, a store, or on the street. It demonstrates good manners.

By mastering these short, effective phrases, you gain a vital tool for interacting directly with others and navigating daily situations in Ukrainian.

## The Informal Imperative: Addressing 'Ти'

The informal singular imperative addresses someone you call **ти** (you, informal). You will use this form with friends, family members, children, and people your own age in casual settings. We form this imperative by taking the verb and applying the command ending. For many common verbs, you drop the infinitive ending and add **-й** or **-и**.

| Infinitive | Informal Command (**ти**) | English |
|---|---|---|
| **чита́ти** | **чита́й** | read |
| **слу́хати** | **слу́хай** | listen |
| **чека́ти** | **чека́й** | wait |

For verbs like **чита́ти**, **слу́хати**, and **чека́ти**, the stem ends in a vowel. In these cases, we simply attach the letter **-й**. This creates a smooth sound that is easy to pronounce.

> — **Чита́й сло́во.** — Read the word.
> — **Слу́хай!** — Listen!
> — **Чека́й ту́т.** — Wait here.

For other verbs, the stem ends in a consonant, and the ending becomes **-и**. This applies to verbs like **писа́ти** (to write) and **іти́** (to go). The **-и** ending ensures the word remains clear and distinguishable.

| Infinitive | Informal Command (**ти**) | English |
|---|---|---|
| **писа́ти** | **пиши́** | write |
| **іти́** | **іди́** | go |
| **сказа́ти** | **скажи́** | say |
| **допомогти́** | **допоможи́** | help |

You can form simple, direct sentences with these verbs. We often combine them with adverbs of place like **ту́т** (here) or **та́м** (there) to give clear directions.

- **Пиши́ ту́т.** — Write here.
- **Іди́ та́м.** — Go there.
- **Скажи́ сло́во.** — Say the word.
- **Допоможи́, бу́дь ла́ска.** — Help, please.

Let us see how people use these informal commands in everyday life. In this scenario, two friends are studying together and exchanging quick instructions.

> — **Чита́й це́.** — Read this.
> — **Я чита́ю.** — I am reading.
> — **Пиши́ сло́во ту́т.** — Write the word here.
> — **Та́к, я пишу́.** — Yes, I am writing.
> — **Та́к.** — Yes.

<!-- adapted from: textbook conversational scenarios -->

Notice how one person gives a command, and the other responds with a present tense verb describing their action. The form **пиши́** directly prompts the friend to act. It creates a natural, interactive dialogue.

> [!tip] Watch the Endings
> If the verb stem ends in a vowel, use **-й** (**чита́й**). If it ends in a consonant, use **-и** (**пиши́**). This phonetic rule helps maintain a natural flow of speech.

By remembering these two basic endings, you can build many commands for friends and family, allowing you to interact comfortably in relaxed environments.

## The Formal and Plural Imperative: Addressing 'Ви'

When speaking to a group of people or addressing a single person respectfully, you use the formal or plural command form. This form corresponds directly to the pronoun **ви** (you, formal/plural). You construct this formal imperative effortlessly by adding the suffix **-те** to the informal imperative you just learned.

| Informal Command (**ти**) | Formal/Plural Command (**ви**) | English |
|---|---|---|
| **чита́й** | **чита́йте** | read |
| **слу́хай** | **слу́хайте** | listen |
| **чека́й** | **чека́йте** | wait |
| **пиши́** | **пиші́ть** | write |
| **іди́** | **іді́ть** | go |

> — **Чита́йте, бу́дь ла́ска.** — Please read.
> — **Слу́хайте це́.** — Listen to this.
> — **Чека́йте та́м.** — Wait there.
> — **Пиші́ть сло́во.** — Write the word.
> — **Іді́ть ту́т.** — Go here.

Using the **-те** suffix adds an instant layer of politeness and formality to your speech. If you are in a new place, speaking to an older person, or talking to a stranger on the street, you must choose the **ви** form. Failing to do so can sound extremely rude and disrespectful.

- **Скажі́ть, де́ ві́н?** — Tell me, where is he?
- **Покажі́ть це́.** — Show this.
- **Допоможі́ть, бу́дь ла́ска!** — Help, please!

We also use this form when a teacher addresses a classroom full of students, as the teacher is speaking to multiple people simultaneously.

> — **Слу́хайте!** — Listen!
> — **Ми слу́хаємо.** — We are listening.
> — **Пиші́ть сло́во.** — Write the word.
> — **Ми пи́шемо.** — We are writing.
> — **Чита́йте та́кож.** — Read also.

Reflexive verbs maintain a unique pattern in the formal imperative. For example, the verb **диви́тися** (to look) is reflexive. For the informal form, we use **диви́сь**. For the formal or plural form, we say **диві́ться**. Notice how the reflexive ending shifts slightly to accommodate the plural marker.

| Pronoun | Command | English |
|---|---|---|
| **ти** | **диви́сь** | look (informal) |
| **ви** | **диві́ться** | look (formal/plural) |

- **Диви́сь ту́т.** — Look here.
- **Диві́ться, бу́дь ла́ска.** — Please look.
- **Диві́ться та́м.** — Look there.

> [!warning] A Common Learner Error
> Do not use the infinitive (like ~~чита́ти~~) when giving a personal command. English uses the base verb, but Ukrainian requires you to adapt the verb into the proper imperative form (**чита́й** or **чита́йте**) so it targets the person directly.

When you interact with native speakers in professional or public settings, paying close attention to the formal **-те** ending will greatly improve your communication skills and demonstrate your cultural awareness.

## Special Verbs and Reflexives

Some high-frequency verbs feature irregular command forms that do not perfectly follow the standard patterns. You simply need to memorize them because they appear so often. The verbs **да́ти** (to give), **взя́ти** (to take), and **стоя́ти** (to stand) fall into this special group.

| Infinitive | Command (**ти**) | Command (**ви**) | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| **да́ти** | **да́й** | **да́йте** | give |
| **взя́ти** | **візьми́** | **візьмі́ть** | take |
| **стоя́ти** | **сті́й** | **сті́йте** | stand / stop |
| **показа́ти** | **покажи́** | **покажі́ть** | show |

You will hear these commands constantly in daily life, especially when shopping, asking for directions, or navigating public spaces.

- **Да́й, бу́дь ла́ска.** — Give, please.
- **Візьмі́ть це́.** — Take this.
- **Сті́йте та́м.** — Stand there.
- **Да́йте сло́во.** — Give the word.
- **Візьми́ ча́с.** — Take time.

> — **Да́йте, бу́дь ла́ска, це́.** — Please give this.
> — **Візьмі́ть.** — Take it.
> — **А це́ яки́й ча́с?** — And what time is this?
> — **Чека́йте.** — Wait.

You can also use negative commands to establish clear prohibitions and tell someone not to do something. We simply add the negative word **не** (not) directly before the imperative verb. The verb form itself does not change.

- **Не чита́й це́.** — Do not read this.
- **Не пиші́ть.** — Do not write.
- **Не диви́сь.** — Do not look.
- **Не слу́хайте.** — Do not listen.

> [!did-you-know] Direct Action
> Ukrainian sometimes uses the perfective aspect in a command when requesting a single, completed action. For example, the perfective verb **сказа́ти** asks for a specific, immediate answer, so the command **скажи́** focuses on the final result of the action rather than the continuous process.

Understanding these special verbs and negative structures allows you to express precise needs and boundaries.

## Summary and Checkpoint

The imperative mood allows you to give direct instructions, make polite requests, and establish boundaries. The grammatical form changes depending on who you are addressing, reflecting the social context of the conversation. You use the shorter form for informal interactions with friends (**ти**) and attach the **-те** suffix for formal or plural situations (**ви**). Adding the phrase **бу́дь ла́ска** ensures you remain polite and respectful in any scenario.

- **Чита́й!** — Read! (informal)
- **Чита́йте!** — Read! (formal/plural)
- **Слу́хай!** — Listen! (informal)
- **Слу́хайте!** — Listen! (formal/plural)
- **Не пиши́.** — Do not write. (informal negative)
- **Не пиші́ть.** — Do not write. (formal negative)

Review these rules and practice using both the formal and informal forms. Always remember to assess your relationship with the listener before giving a command.
